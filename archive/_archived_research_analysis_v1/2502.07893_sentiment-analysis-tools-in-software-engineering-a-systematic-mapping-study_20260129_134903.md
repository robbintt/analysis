>
> The application of sentiment analysis within Software Engineering (SE) has grown significantly as researchers seek to understand developer emotions and team dynamics through text data found in commit messages, issue trackers, and communication forums. However, the field has become fragmented, populated by a wide array of tools, algorithms, and datasets with no standardized framework for comparison or selection.
>
> To address this fragmentation, the authors performed a **Systematic Mapping Study (SMS)** analyzing **106 primary studies**. The study introduces a rigorous classification framework based on six dimensions: application domain, purpose, datasets, approaches, existing tool usage, and difficulties.
>
> **Key Takeaways:**
> *   **Performance:** Neural Networks are the dominant and best-performing approach, with **BERT** identified as the highest-performing specific tool (RoBERTa achieved an F1-score of 0.83).
> *   **Scope:** The technology is predominantly applied to **Open Source Software (OSS)**, accounting for **74.5%** of analyzed papers.
> *   **Challenges:** The primary hurdles are "Adaptation to SE Domain" and the detection of complex linguistic nuances like "Irony/Sarcasm."
>
> This study provides a definitive benchmark for the SE community, recommending Neural Network-based approaches and highlighting the need to adapt general-purpose NLP models to the unique vernacular of software engineering.

## Key Findings

*   **Dominant Algorithms:** Neural networks and Support-vector Machines (SVM) are the most frequently utilized approaches for developing sentiment analysis tools in software engineering.
*   **Performance Leader:** Neural networks were identified as the best-performing approach overall. Specifically, **BERT** was identified as the highest-performing specific tool, followed closely by RoBERTa.
*   **Primary Application:** Sentiment analysis is predominantly applied within **open-source software projects** (OSS) rather than industry or academic settings.
*   **Linguistic Challenges:** Significant issues remain in accurately detecting complex linguistic nuances, with **irony and sarcasm** proving particularly difficult for current tools.
*   **Domain Adaptation:** Adapting general NLP tools to the specific vernacular of the Software Engineering domain remains a frequent technical challenge.

## Methodology

The researchers employed a **Systematic Mapping Study (SMS)** to review and synthesize existing literature. The study analyzed **106 papers** that focused on sentiment analysis tools developed for or applied in Software Engineering.

The analysis was structured around **six specific dimensions**:
1.  **Application Domain**
2.  **Purpose**
3.  **Datasets Used**
4.  **Development Approaches**
5.  **Usage of Existing Tools**
6.  **Difficulties Faced**
