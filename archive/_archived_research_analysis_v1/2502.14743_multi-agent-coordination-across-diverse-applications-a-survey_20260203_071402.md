> ## üìù Executive Summary
>
> The field of Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) has expanded rapidly to encompass diverse domains such as humanoid robotics, satellite constellations, and Large Language Models (LLMs). However, this expansion has created a fragmented landscape where achieving effective coordination remains a significant challenge due to the complexity of managing decentralized decision-making.
>
> The primary obstacles preventing widespread adoption and efficiency include **scalability** within exponential state and action spaces, the **heterogeneity** of agents, and the need for **robust learning mechanisms** in dynamic environments. Without a unified theoretical foundation, it is difficult to compare approaches or transfer solutions across these vastly different application areas.
>
> To unify this fragmented field, the authors introduce a conceptual framework anchored in four fundamental questions: **what, why, who, and how** to coordinate. Technically, the paper models coordination as an iterative sequential decision-making process involving three stages: evaluating system-level performance, social choice (clustering agents based on dependency graphs), and determining interaction mechanisms.
>
> The analysis reveals that **coordinated learning** demonstrates superior learning efficiency compared to independent learning by effectively solving non-stationarity problems. Furthermore, the review highlights that **event-triggered communication** provides improved efficiency, flexibility, and scalability over time-triggered methods, though often with a trade-off in raw coordination performance.
>
> This work significantly influences the field by providing a cohesive structure that bridges the gap between traditional industrial applications and cutting-edge frontiers like LLM-based agents. By systematically categorizing existing literature and identifying bottlenecks, the authors provide a strategic roadmap for future research, directing the community toward hybrid coordination architectures, human-in-the-loop systems, and the deep integration of LLMs.
