## üìã Executive Summary

The rapid emergence of LLM-based "Agentic AI" and "Multiagentic AI" is currently plagued by **terminological redundancy** and a **disconnect from historical scientific foundations**. This paper addresses the critical issue that the current hype cycle is effectively marketing decades-old concepts‚Äî"intelligent agents" and "multi-agent systems" (MAS)‚Äîas novel inventions.

Overlooking the genealogy of these fields creates a substantial risk of "reinventing the wheel." Consequently, modern development often neglects essential agent properties (autonomy, reactivity, proactivity, and social capability) and relies on ad-hoc implementations rather than established engineering standards.

The core innovation proposed is a framework to **retrofit modern LLM-based agents** with the rigorous engineering standards of classical MAS. This includes integrating legacy coordination mechanisms‚Äîsuch as automated negotiation, task assignment, consensus protocols, and trust models‚Äîinto current LLM orchestration frameworks. The paper calls for scientific rigor to prevent a fragmented landscape of incompatible agent standards, advocating for a convergence of generative capabilities and proven reliability.

## üîç Key Findings

*   **Terminological Redundancy:** The terms 'Agentic AI' and 'Multiagentic AI' are largely identified as marketing buzzwords that conceptually duplicate established 'intelligent agents' and 'multi-agent systems' (MAS).
*   **Historical Disconnect:** Current generative AI discourse frequently overlooks decades of foundational research, including theoretical origins in social sciences, philosophy, and classical agent architectures.
*   **Omission of Core Properties:** The hype surrounding LLM-based agents often neglects essential agent properties defined in classical AI, such as:
    *   Autonomy
    *   Reactivity
    *   Proactivity
    *   Social Capability
*   **Risk of Redundancy:** There is a significant risk of 'reinventing the wheel' in the development of LLM-agent platforms if existing standards, communication languages, and coordination algorithms are not integrated.
