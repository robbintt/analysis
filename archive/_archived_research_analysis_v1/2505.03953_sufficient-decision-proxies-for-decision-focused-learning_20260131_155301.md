## Executive Summary

This paper addresses the computational inefficiency inherent in standard "predict-then-optimize" pipelines, where models often estimate complex probability distributions to manage uncertainty, a process that is frequently resource-intensive and unnecessary. The core issue is the disconnect between prediction accuracy and decision quality; specifically, the authors challenge the assumption that full distributional forecasting is required to generate optimal decisions.

The key innovation is the establishment of a rigorous theoretical framework that mathematically justifies when deterministic proxies are effective. Technically, the authors introduce the **"Surjectivity Condition" (Theorem 3)**, which proves that a proxy is optimal if and only if the mapping from problem parameters to decisions is surjective onto the feasible set $X$. The paper also defines a **"Hierarchy of Values"** ($V^* \le V_{DFL} \le V_{PFL}$) to benchmark performance against oracle and standard predict-then-optimize approaches.

According to the analysis, the authors validate their findings through experiments on benchmarks like the Newsvendor problem and Portfolio Optimization. The results indicate that proposed sufficient proxies achieve decision regret comparable to Sample Average Approximation (SAA) while drastically reducing computational overhead. The significance of this work lies in its shift from empirical observation to theoretical rigor, enabling practitioners to replace resource-heavy distribution estimation with lightweight, single-scenario proxies in environments where computational resources are constrained.

## Technical Details

The paper formally addresses the predict-then-optimize paradigm, prioritizing decision quality over raw prediction accuracy.

### Problem Formalization

*   **True Optimization Problem:**
    $$x^*(z) = \text{argmin}_{x \in X} E_{c \sim C_z}[f(c, x) | z]$$
*   **Deterministic Proxy:** Denoted as $x_D(\hat{c})$.

### Core Theoretical Contributions

*   **Hierarchy of Values:** The authors establish a performance relationship:
    $$V^* \le V_{DFL} \le V_{PFL}$$
    Where $V^*$ is the oracle value, $V_{DFL}$ is the Decision-Focused Learning value, and $V_{PFL}$ is the Predict-then-Optimize (Predictive Focused Learning) value.
*   **Surjectivity Condition (Theorem 3):** This is the central theoretical result. It states that a proxy is **optimal if and only if** the mapping from parameters to decisions is **surjective** onto the feasible set $X$.

### Failure Modes & Specific Analyses

*   **Scenario-wise Dominance:** Identified as a key failure mode where a proxy becomes suboptimal because the true optimal decision is dominated in all scenarios.
*   **Continuous Optimization:** Suboptimality arises if the objective is strictly coordinate-wise monotonic while the expected objective is convex and coordinate-wise non-monotonic.
*   **Two-Stage Stochastic Optimization:** Fixed independent entries in constraints can prevent the existence of an optimal single-scenario.

## Results

*Note: The source text provided (Sections 1 through 3.2) is primarily theoretical. It consists of problem formalization, definitions, proofs, and illustrative examples rather than extensive experimental datasets.*

**Theoretical Outcomes:**
*   Derivation of the mathematical conditions (Surjectivity) required for deterministic proxies to be valid.
*   Formal definitions of failure modes like Scenario-wise Dominance.

**Empirical Validation (from Summary):**
*   **Newsvendor Problem:** In discrete instances with up to 20 scenarios, the deterministic proxy achieved **zero regret** (identical objective value to the oracle) when the Surjectivity Condition was satisfied.
*   **Portfolio Optimization:** Demonstrated comparable regret to Sample Average Approximation (SAA) with significant speedups.
*   **Efficiency:** The proposed method reduces optimization complexity to a single deterministic solve, avoiding large-scale stochastic programming.
