## Executive Summary

Current agentic AI systems suffer from significant reliability and brittleness issues, largely stemming from ad-hoc design processes that lack engineering rigor. This lack of a standardized architectural foundation makes it difficult to diagnose failures, scale systems, or ensure robustness in production environments. As AI agents move from research prototypes to critical real-world deployments, the absence of a principled methodology for their construction represents a major bottleneck for the field.

The authors introduce a novel system-theoretic framework that deconstructs agentic architectures into five core functional subsystems: **Reasoning & World Model**, **Perception & Grounding**, **Action Execution**, **Learning & Adaptation**, and **Inter-Agent Communication**. These subsystems are organized into a three-layer nested architectureâ€”comprising a Cognitive Core, Operational Interfaces, and an Adaptive Shellâ€”operating via a continuous cognitive cycle of perception, modeling, deliberation, and feedback.

From this structure, the authors derive 12 specific Agentic Design Patterns (ADPs), categorized into Foundational, Cognitive & Decisional, Execution & Interaction, and Adaptive & Learning groups, to address recurring architectural challenges. The framework and design patterns were validated through a case study on the ReAct framework, which successfully demonstrated the methodology's ability to identify and rectify systemic architectural deficiencies.

While the paper includes visualizations such as Sankey diagrams and summary tables to illustrate pattern relationships, it does not provide quantitative performance metrics (e.g., accuracy, latency, or success rates). Instead, the validation focuses on qualitative structural improvements, confirming that the proposed patterns can effectively expose and fix design flaws in existing agent systems. This work establishes a standardized design language and structured methodology for the engineering of robust AI agents, shifting the paradigm from experimental "hacking" to principled system design.
