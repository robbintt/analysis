## Executive Summary

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) frequently suffer from performance degradation when deployed in real-world scenarios involving distribution shifts or suboptimal data quality. While traditional approaches to improving robustness often focus on modifying model architectures or fine-tuning parameters, this paper addresses a critical gap: **the structural fragility of pre-trained GNNs at test time.**

Specifically, the authors investigate how the graph property of **homophily**â€”the tendency of connected nodes to share similar labelsâ€”impacts classification accuracy under varying conditions, arguing that static graph structures are a primary bottleneck for consistent test-time performance.

The paper introduces **GrapHoST (Graph Homophily Structure Transformation)**, a novel, training-free paradigm that shifts the focus from model design to structural transformation during inference. The key technical insight is that robustness requires adapting graph structure based on the specific graph type: GrapHoST dynamically increases homophily for homophilic graphs (by removing heterophilic edges) and decreases homophily for heterophilic graphs (by removing homophilic edges).

Evaluated across nine benchmark datasets, GrapHoST demonstrated significant improvements over existing baselinesâ€”achieving an increase in node classification accuracy of up to **10.92%** without any model training. This research establishes a theoretical and empirical link between homophily levels and test-time robustness, offering a cost-effective, plug-and-play solution for dynamic graph environments.

## Key Findings

*   **Structural Modulation:** Modifying the test graph structure by increasing homophily in homophilic graphs and decreasing it in heterophilic graphs significantly improves the robustness of pre-trained GNNs.
*   **Training-Free Improvement:** The improvements are achieved **without** model training or parameter updates, relying solely on structural transformation at test time.
*   **Root Cause Analysis:** Performance degradation is primarily driven by data quality issues and distribution shifts.
*   **Benchmark Success:** The proposed method outperforms existing baselines across nine benchmark datasets, yielding improvements of up to **10.92%**.

## Methodology

The proposed method, **GrapHoST**, operates through a distinct mechanism to optimize graph topology during inference:

1.  **Homophily Prediction:** It employs a homophily predictor to discriminate between edges within the test graph.
2.  **Adaptive Transformation:** It facilitates adaptive test-time graph structural transformation based on the confidence levels of the predicted homophily scores.
3.  **Dynamic Adjustment:** This allows the model to dynamically adjust the graph structure to suit the specific characteristics (homophilic or heterophilic) of the test data before inference begins.

## Technical Details

### Core Architecture
GrapHoST performs test-time graph structural transformation to optimize node classification using a **fixed pre-trained GNN** without parameter updates. Its architecture consists of three primary components:
*   A fixed pre-trained GNN classifier.
*   A learnable homophily predictor that classifies edges.
*   A structural filtering module.

### Mathematical Objective
Mathematically, the method minimizes classification loss subject to edge space constraints, utilizing a **Homophily Metric (HD)**.

### Structural Strategy
*   **Homophilic Graphs:** Strategy involves increasing homophily by removing heterophilic edges.
*   **Heterophilic Graphs:** Strategy involves decreasing homophily by removing homophilic edges.
*   **Validation:** Supported by theoretical analysis via the Contextual Stochastic Block Model.
