This research addresses the fundamental disconnect between the empirical success of Large Language Models (LLMs) and prevailing linguistic critiques rooted in structuralist theory. Critics often dismiss LLMs for lacking "deep structure" or grounding in reality, arguing they merely manipulate surface statistics without understanding. This position is problematic because it treats language as an abstract, idealized system rather than a historical artifact. The authors argue that these critiques are speculative and unproductive, creating a theoretical barrier to properly understanding why LLMs function as effective models of human communication.

The key innovation is the introduction of the **"Ma≈Ñczakian Framework,"** which applies the empiricist principles of 20th-century linguist Witold Ma≈Ñczak to modern NLP. This framework redefines language not as an abstract system of rules, but as the "material totality" of communication governed primarily by the frequency of use. Technically, the paper distinguishes between architectural approaches: N-grams rely on surface counting without analogy; Continuous Bag of Words (CBOW) utilizes dense vectors for external analogy; and modern LLMs leverage dynamic, high-dimensional embeddings that possess inherent analogy. This shift to high-dimensional spaces is identified as the critical technical advancement that allows models to capture grammatical patterns‚Äîdefined as high-frequency patterns‚Äîdistinguishing them from low-frequency lexicographical data.

As a theoretical position paper, this study does not present quantitative experimental results or standard performance metrics; instead, it relies on qualitative evidence to validate the frequency-based hypothesis. The authors provide concrete examples from historical linguistics, specifically analyzing the evolution of Latin to Romance numerical forms to demonstrate that high-frequency tokens‚Äîsuch as the shift from Latin *unus* and *octo* to their Romance derivatives‚Äîundergo specific simplification and regularization processes driven by usage. These results serve to justify the "stochastic" nature of LLMs, demonstrating that a model based on frequency aligns with the empirically observable history and biological processing of human language.

The significance of this paper lies in its potential to shift the paradigm of AI evaluation from structuralist ideals to empiricist reality. By defending LLMs against arguments regarding grounding and deep structure, it offers a theoretical basis for accepting statistical models as legitimate models of language.
