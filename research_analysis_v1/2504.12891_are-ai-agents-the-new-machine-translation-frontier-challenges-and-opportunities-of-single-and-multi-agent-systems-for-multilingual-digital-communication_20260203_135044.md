### üìù Executive Summary

This research addresses the critical limitations of traditional monolithic Neural Machine Translation (NMT) systems in high-stakes professional environments. While static text-to-text mapping suffices for general communication, it fails to meet the rigorous standards of domain-specific fields like legal translation, where contextual awareness, terminological precision, and adherence to professional workflows are mandatory.

The paper confronts the gap between automated translation efficiency and the quality required for complex communication scenarios, investigating whether AI agent-based architectures can bridge this divide by mimicking professional human reasoning. The core innovation lies in transitioning from static NMT models to dynamic, LLM-driven AI agent workflows capable of autonomous reasoning and iterative refinement.

Technically, the framework distinguishes between Single-Agent Systems (optimized for straightforward tasks) and Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) designed for complexity. The authors implement a specific MAS architecture comprising four specialized roles: **Translation**, **Adequacy review**, **Fluency review**, and **Final editing**. These agents utilize advanced orchestration strategies‚Äî**Prompt Chaining**, **Routing**, and **Parallelization**‚Äîwhile integrating external tools like Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and Translation Memories.

The study validated the framework through a pilot study targeting legal machine translation. Empirical results demonstrated that the four-agent MAS workflow significantly outperformed traditional NMT and single-agent systems in handling complex domain-specific constraints. This paper establishes a foundational blueprint for integrating autonomous AI agents into professional translation landscapes, offering a viable path for industries requiring high-precision multilingual communication.

## Technical Details

The paper proposes shifting from monolithic Neural Machine Translation (NMT) to AI Agent-based workflows utilizing Large Language Models (LLMs).

### System Architecture
*   **Core Components:** Agents possess autonomy, access to external tools (RAG, Translation Memory, domain databases), and memory for feedback loops.
*   **Single-Agent Systems:** Designed for straightforward, lower-complexity tasks.
*   **Multi-Agent Systems (MAS):** Designed for complex workflows requiring high precision.

### Orchestration Strategies
*   **Prompt Chaining:** A sequential pipeline involving preprocessing, translation, and post-editing.
*   **Routing:** Dynamic allocation of tasks based on domain, language, or complexity.
*   **Parallelization:** Concurrent execution of independent subtasks (e.g., multi-language translation or quality assurance).
