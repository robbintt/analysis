## Executive Summary

This paper addresses the fundamental limitations of isolated Large Language Models (LLMs), which suffer from issues such as hallucinations, auto-regressive constraints that prevent "slow-thinking," and difficulties in scaling to complex, real-world tasks. As the field shifts from standalone models to **"collaboration-centric"** architectures, there is a growing need to understand how these models can effectively perceive, reason, and act collectively.

This survey is significant because it tackles the fragmentation in the current literature regarding Multi-Agent Systems (MASs), providing a structured approach to solving complex problems that exceed the cognitive capacity of single agents. The key innovation is the introduction of a comprehensive, extensible framework that characterizes LLM-based MASs across five distinct dimensions:

1.  **Actors**
2.  **Types** (Cooperation, Competition, Coopetition)
3.  **Structures** (Peer-to-peer, Centralized, Distributed, etc.)
4.  **Strategies** (Role-based, Rule-based, Model-based)
5.  **Coordination Protocols**

Technically, the paper defines the standard architecture of an LLM-based agent, comprising a **"Brain" or Orchestrator** (the LLM handling reasoning and planning), integration with external tools, learning capabilities, and communication languages. This framework contextualizes agents within Environments (simulated or physical) and Organizations (hierarchical or emergent), effectively demystifying the transition from monolithic models to collaborative multi-agent architectures.
