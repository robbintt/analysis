# Executive Summary

### Problem
Conventional circuit simulators like SPICE struggle with emerging devices featuring high nonlinearity, such as ferroelectric field-effect transistors (FeFETs) and memories. These devices require complex physical models (e.g., Landau-Khalatnikov equations) that are difficult to integrate into standard time-stepping numerical solvers. Furthermore, SPICE is designed for forward problems (analysis) rather than design optimization, making it ill-suited for inverse tasks that require calculating gradients for automated design.

### Innovation
NeuroSPICE addresses these limitations by reframing circuit simulation as an optimization problem using a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) framework. Instead of time-discretized finite difference methods, NeuroSPICE uses continuous, analytical time-domain functions to model circuit waveforms. This allows the system to use automatic differentiation (backpropagation) to calculate exact temporal derivatives, creating a data-free, differentiable solver that integrates physical modeling with gradient-based optimization.

### Results
The study validated NeuroSPICE using nonlinear ferroelectric circuits. While accurate (Relative L2 error $\approx 10^{-4}$), the method faces computational trade-offs:
*   **Voltage Divider:** NeuroSPICE (12.4s) vs. SPICE (0.05s).
*   **1T1C Memory Array:** NeuroSPICE (23.1s) vs. SPICE (0.25s).
Despite the speed penalty in single simulations, NeuroSPICE successfully solved inverse design problems, accurately identifying device parameters to match target waveformsâ€”a task impractical for standard simulators.

### Impact
NeuroSPICE represents a paradigm shift in Electronic Design Automation (EDA) by providing a fully differentiable circuit solver. Its primary significance is enabling gradient-based inverse design and automated optimization for emerging technologies, bridging the gap between physical modeling and design optimization to accelerate the development cycle for complex hardware.

# Key Findings

*   **Novel Solver Mechanism:** Unlike conventional SPICE, NeuroSPICE solves circuit Differential-Algebraic Equations (DAEs) by minimizing residuals via backpropagation.
*   **Waveform Modeling:** Models waveforms using analytical equations for exact temporal derivatives, avoiding finite-difference approximations.
*   **Performance Trade-off:** PINNs offer functional advantages in optimization and inverse problems despite not outperforming standard SPICE in raw speed or accuracy during the training phase.
*   **Optimization Capabilities:** Highly effective for creating surrogate models, performing design optimization, and solving inverse problems.
*   **Handling Nonlinearity:** Flexible and robust for emerging devices and highly nonlinear systems, specifically ferroelectric memories.

# Methodology

The researchers proposed **NeuroSPICE**, a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) framework. The methodology shifts away from traditional time-discretized numerical solving, treating circuit simulation instead as a problem of minimizing the residual of circuit Differential-Algebraic Equations (DAEs) using backpropagation.

The approach employs:
1.  **Analytical Equations:** Used within the time domain to represent device and circuit waveforms.
2.  **Exact Derivatives:** Ensuring the use of exact temporal derivatives rather than approximations.

---

# Technical Details

NeuroSPICE utilizes Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) to replace traditional time-discretized solvers.

*   **Core Mechanism:** Solves circuit DAEs by minimizing residuals through backpropagation.
*   **Derivative Calculation:** Models waveforms using analytical time-domain equations with exact temporal derivatives.
*   **Data Requirement:** The approach is **data-free**, relying on loss functions defined by physical equations without the need for labeled datasets.
*   **Target Applications:** Designed for highly nonlinear systems, multiphysics coupling, ferroelectric memories, photonic elements, and 3D thermal coupling.
