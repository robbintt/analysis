## Executive Summary

Current Large Language Model (LLM)-based social agents suffer from a lack of generalizability, often relying on bespoke, scenario-tailored implementations that fail to adapt to diverse contexts. Furthermore, existing approaches frequently lack a grounding in established social science theories, leading to behaviors that are inconsistent or psychologically implausible. This paper addresses the critical need for a unified, systematic design framework that bridges the gap between theoretical social science principles and practical LLM engineering, ensuring agents can maintain consistent, realistic behaviors across varying social environments without manual re-engineering.

The authors propose a theory-informed Generative Social Agent framework grounded in Social Cognitive Theory (SCT), utilizing a closed-loop architecture composed of three distinct but integrated modules. First, the **Motivation Module** employs Maslow’s Hierarchy and Affective Events Theory, using drive-reduction formulas for physiological needs ($N_i^t = \min\{N_i^{max}, N_i^{t-1} + Inc_i^t - \Delta N_i^{t-1}\}$) and LLM-based Chain-of-Thought for cognitive appraisal. Second, the **Action Planning Module** applies the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) to generate and evaluate options based on attitude, subjective norms, and perceived control, selecting actions via intention maximization ($a_t^* = \arg\max_{a_i \in A_t} I(a_i)$). Finally, the **Learning Module** leverages Social Learning Theory, enabling the agent to adapt dynamically based on action feedback and environmental traces, moving beyond static responses to evolving social interactions.

Evaluations across three distinct datasets—Beijing Mobility (Motivation), NYC Foursquare (Planning), and SafeGraph + CBG (Learning)—demonstrated the framework's superior fidelity. Agents built using this proposed framework achieved up to a 75% lower deviation from real-world behavioral data compared to classical generative baselines. Robustness was confirmed through ablation studies, which revealed that removing the motivation, planning, or learning modules resulted in a 1.5 to 3.2 times increase in behavioral errors. Qualitatively, the system exhibited high flexibility, successfully reproducing complex human behavior patterns and maintaining coherence in dynamic social settings.

This research significantly advances the field of generative agents by providing a rigorous, generalizable blueprint for simulating human social behavior. By formally integrating psychological theories into the LLM workflow, the study moves the industry away from ad-hoc prompt engineering toward a standardized, scientifically grounded design process. The ability of these agents to learn and adapt over time paves the way for more sophisticated applications in social simulation, human-computer interaction, and behavioral modeling, where contextual appropriateness and psychological realism are paramount.

## Methodology

The researchers proposed a theory-informed framework grounded in **Social Cognition Theory** to guide the systematic design of LLM-based social agents. The methodology centers on integrating three distinct but joint modules within the agent's workflow:

1.  **Motivation:** Enables the agent to reason about its specific goals.
2.  **Action Planning:** Facilitates the generation of coherent actions based on those goals.
3.  **Learning:** Allows the agent to adapt its behavior dynamically over time.

This architecture moves away from scenario-tailored implementations toward a general design process that leverages the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs).
