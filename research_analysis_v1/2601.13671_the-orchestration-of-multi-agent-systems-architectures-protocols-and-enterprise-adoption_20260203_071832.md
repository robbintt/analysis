## Executive Summary

The proliferation of multi-agent systems (MAS) in enterprise environments has outpaced the development of standardized architectural frameworks, leading to challenges in system coherence, interoperability, and governance. As organizations attempt to deploy complex AI ecosystems, they face fragmentation in how agents plan, enforce policies, and manage state. Without a unified orchestration layer, maintaining system reliability and observability across distributed agents becomes unmanageable. This paper addresses the critical need for a cohesive structural blueprint that integrates functional modules with standardized communication protocols to enable scalable, enterprise-grade MAS deployments.

The authors introduce a **Unified Architectural Framework** that synthesizes four core functional modules: Planning, Policy Enforcement, State Management, and Quality Operations. Technically, the innovation relies on the specification of two distinct protocols to facilitate interoperability: the **Model Context Protocol (MCP)**, which provides standardized access to external tools and data, and the **Agent2Agent protocol**, which manages peer-to-peer coordination. This architecture ensures system coherence by tightly coupling orchestration logic with governance frameworks and observability mechanisms, moving away from ad-hoc implementations toward a formalized, interoperable design standard.

This research employs a conceptual formalization and architectural synthesis approach rather than empirical experimentation; consequently, the paper does not present quantitative metrics, performance benchmarks, or comparative analysis data. The primary output is a comprehensive technical blueprint that validates the integration of the proposed modules and protocols through logical consolidation of existing design principles. The "result" is a fully defined, implementation-ready design guide that translates theoretical multi-agent concepts into practical enterprise architecture, successfully filling the gap between agent capabilities and operational requirements.

The significance of this work lies in its potential to standardize the engineering and adoption of multi-agent systems in the enterprise sector. By establishing clear protocols for agent communication (Agent2Agent) and context management (MCP), the paper provides a foundational common language for developers and architects. This framework lowers the barrier to entry for implementing complex AI workflows, ensuring that governance, security, and operational quality are embedded into the system architecture from the outset rather than added as afterthoughts.
