# Fusing Sequence Motifs and Pan-Genomic Features: Antimicrobial Resistance Prediction using an Explainable Lightweight 1D CNN-XGBoost Ensemble

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.23552
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23552
- Reference count: 33
- Primary result: A hybrid ensemble framework combining 1D CNN sequence learning with XGBoost feature-based learning achieved highest MCC of 0.926 for Ciprofloxacin and Macro F1-score of 0.691 for Gentamicin on E. coli AMR prediction.

## Executive Summary
This study addresses the challenge of predicting antimicrobial resistance (AMR) from genomic data by developing AMR-EnsembleNet, a hybrid framework that combines a lightweight 1D CNN for learning sequence motifs from SNP data with XGBoost for capturing pan-genomic feature interactions. The approach overcomes limitations of treating genomes as unordered data while avoiding the computational complexity of transformer models. Evaluated on a benchmark E. coli dataset with 809 isolates across four antibiotics with varying class imbalances, the ensemble consistently outperformed individual models, achieving superior performance particularly for the challenging Gentamicin prediction and maintaining high MCC for Ciprofloxacin.

## Method Summary
The AMR-EnsembleNet framework fuses sequence-aware learning through a lightweight 1D CNN that captures SNP patterns and motifs with XGBoost that models complex feature interactions from pan-genomic data. This ensemble approach addresses the dual challenge of maintaining biological relevance through sequence pattern recognition while leveraging comprehensive genomic features. The model was trained and evaluated on a benchmark E. coli dataset containing 809 isolates and four antibiotics with different resistance mechanisms and class imbalances, using multiple performance metrics including Matthews Correlation Coefficient and Macro F1-score to ensure robust evaluation across imbalanced classes.

## Key Results
- Achieved highest Matthews Correlation Coefficient of 0.926 for Ciprofloxacin prediction
- Achieved highest Macro F1-score of 0.691 for challenging Gentamicin prediction
- Outperformed individual CNN and XGBoost models with improved recall and balanced precision across all antibiotics

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The ensemble architecture works by combining complementary learning approaches: the 1D CNN extracts biologically meaningful sequence motifs and local patterns from SNP data that capture the positional and contextual information often lost when treating genomes as unordered sets, while XGBoost leverages pan-genomic features to model complex interactions and global patterns. This fusion addresses the fundamental limitation of traditional approaches that either ignore sequence order or require excessive computational resources. The lightweight CNN architecture maintains efficiency while preserving sequence awareness, and the ensembling strategy leverages the strengths of both models to achieve robust performance across different antibiotics with varying resistance mechanisms and class imbalances.

## Foundational Learning
- **Sequence motif learning**: Essential for capturing biologically relevant patterns in genomic data that indicate resistance mechanisms; quick check: verify motifs align with known AMR gene regions
- **Pan-genomic feature engineering**: Critical for incorporating comprehensive genomic information beyond individual SNPs; quick check: ensure feature selection captures known resistance-associated genes
- **Class imbalance handling**: Necessary given the varying resistance prevalence across antibiotics; quick check: validate performance metrics are balanced across classes
- **Ensemble learning principles**: Enables combining complementary model strengths for robust predictions; quick check: compare ensemble performance against individual models
- **SHAP interpretability**: Required for validating biological relevance of learned patterns; quick check: confirm identified features align with known AMR mechanisms
- **1D CNN architecture**: Balances sequence awareness with computational efficiency; quick check: verify model captures local sequence patterns effectively

## Architecture Onboarding
- **Component map**: SNP data -> 1D CNN (sequence motif learning) -> XGBoost (feature interactions) -> Ensemble prediction -> SHAP analysis (interpretability)
- **Critical path**: Raw genomic data undergoes feature extraction and SNP identification, feeds into both 1D CNN and XGBoost models in parallel, ensemble combines predictions, final output validated through SHAP analysis for biological relevance
- **Design tradeoffs**: Lightweight CNN sacrifices some pattern complexity for computational efficiency versus deeper models; ensembling adds complexity but improves robustness versus single-model approaches
- **Failure signatures**: Poor performance on unseen species suggests limited generalization; failure to identify known AMR genes in SHAP analysis indicates model misalignment with biology; performance degradation on highly imbalanced classes reveals sensitivity to class distribution
- **First experiments**: 1) Test ensemble performance on multi-species dataset to evaluate generalization, 2) Conduct ablation study removing SHAP analysis to quantify interpretability value, 3) Compare against transformer-based approach on same dataset to measure efficiency-accuracy tradeoff

## Limitations
- Results based on single E. coli dataset may not generalize to other bacterial species or resistance mechanisms
- Biological validation through SHAP analysis shows correlation but not causation between learned motifs and resistance phenotypes
- Lightweight CNN architecture may miss complex sequence patterns that deeper models could capture

## Confidence
- Performance claims: High - Ensemble consistently outperformed individual models across multiple metrics on benchmark dataset
- Biological relevance: Medium - SHAP analysis identified SNPs in known AMR genes but causal validation was not performed
- Generalization potential: Low - Single-species evaluation limits confidence in broader applicability
