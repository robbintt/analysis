# From Wearables to Warnings: Predicting Pain Spikes in Patients with Opioid Use Disorder

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.19577
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.19577
- Reference count: 19
- Machine learning models achieved >0.7 accuracy in predicting pain spikes up to 5 days in advance

## Executive Summary
This study developed machine learning models to predict pain spikes in patients with chronic pain and opioid use disorder using wearable device data. The models demonstrated high accuracy (>0.7) in forecasting morning and evening pain spikes up to five days in advance. SHAP analysis revealed that step counts, sleep duration, and light activity levels were the most predictive features. The research highlights the potential of wearable-based AI monitoring for early detection of pain spikes, which could help prevent opioid relapse and improve medication adherence in this vulnerable population.

## Method Summary
The researchers collected wearable device data from patients with chronic pain and opioid use disorder, focusing on metrics such as step counts, sleep duration, and light activity levels. They developed machine learning models to predict pain spikes, testing their performance for both morning and evening pain episodes. The models were evaluated using standard classification metrics, and SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) analysis was employed to identify the most influential predictive features. Additionally, the study compared the performance of traditional machine learning models with large language models in providing actionable insights for pain spike prediction.

## Key Results
- Machine learning models achieved >0.7 accuracy in predicting pain spikes up to 5 days in advance
- SHAP analysis identified step counts, sleep duration, and light activity levels as key predictive features
- Large language models showed limited performance in providing actionable insights for pain spike prediction

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The predictive power of wearable data for pain spikes stems from the physiological and behavioral patterns that precede pain episodes in patients with chronic pain and OUD. Changes in activity levels, sleep patterns, and daily routines serve as early indicators of impending pain spikes, allowing for timely interventions.

## Limitations
- Sample size and demographic representation were not fully specified, potentially limiting generalizability
- The study focused on specific wearable metrics without exploring other potentially relevant physiological signals
- Comparison between ML models and LLMs was limited to a specific set of prompts

## Confidence
- ML model accuracy (>0.7) for pain spike prediction: High
- SHAP analysis identifying key predictive features: Medium
- LLM performance limitations: Medium
- Clinical utility for preventing opioid relapse: Low (based on predictive modeling alone)
