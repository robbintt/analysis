# How to model Human Actions distribution with Event Sequence Data

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.05856
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.05856
- Reference count: 21
- This paper demonstrates that explicit distribution forecasting consistently outperforms complex autoregressive methods for human action sequence prediction across multiple domains.

## Executive Summary
This paper challenges the conventional autoregressive paradigm for modeling human action sequences by investigating whether explicitly forecasting future event distributions can outperform order-preserving methods. The authors focus on domains where the precise temporal order of events matters less than the set of outcomes themselves, such as retail, finance, and healthcare. Through comprehensive experiments across six diverse domains, they demonstrate that a simple explicit distribution forecasting objective consistently surpasses complex implicit baselines, particularly in order-invariant scenarios.

The work introduces a KL-based metric to quantify temporal drift and analyzes local order invariance, providing both theoretical insights and practical guidance for building more accurate and robust forecasting systems. The authors further show that mode collapse in predicted categories is primarily driven by distributional imbalance rather than model architecture limitations.

## Method Summary
The authors propose a framework that shifts away from traditional autoregressive modeling toward explicit distribution forecasting for human action sequences. They investigate order-invariant modeling approaches that predict the distribution of future events without preserving temporal order, challenging the assumption that order preservation is always necessary. The method employs a KL-based metric to quantify temporal drift and includes a systematic analysis of when local order invariance holds. The core approach uses a simple explicit distribution forecasting objective that directly predicts the probability distribution of future events, contrasting with implicit methods that generate sequences token-by-token.

## Key Results
- Explicit distribution forecasting consistently outperforms complex autoregressive methods across most domains studied
- Mode collapse in predicted categories is primarily driven by distributional imbalance rather than model architecture limitations
- The KL-based temporal drift metric effectively quantifies when order-preserving approaches provide diminishing returns
- Simple explicit objectives achieve superior performance compared to sophisticated implicit baselines

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The explicit distribution forecasting approach works by directly modeling the probability distribution of future events rather than generating sequences sequentially. This bypasses the compounding error problem inherent in autoregressive models and eliminates the need to preserve temporal order when it's not predictive. By focusing on the set of outcomes rather than their sequence, the method can capture long-term distributional patterns more effectively and avoid the computational complexity of maintaining order-dependent states throughout generation.

**Critical Path**: The core innovation lies in replacing autoregressive sequence generation with direct distribution prediction, connected to the KL-based temporal drift analysis that guides when this simplification is valid.

**Design Tradeoffs**: Simplicity and computational efficiency versus potential loss of temporal nuance; explicit distribution modeling trades detailed sequence information for robustness to order-invariant scenarios and reduced computational overhead.

**Failure Signatures**: Mode collapse occurs primarily under distributional imbalance; performance degradation indicates scenarios where temporal order actually matters; high KL divergence suggests order-preserving approaches may be necessary.

## Limitations
- Focus exclusively on order-invariant scenarios, leaving open questions about performance in domains where temporal order remains critical
- Limited exploration of hybrid approaches that could leverage both explicit distribution modeling and order-aware components
- Reliance on synthetic and proprietary datasets that may not capture all edge cases present in production systems
- Minimal analysis of computational efficiency and scalability for industrial applications

## Confidence
- Superiority of explicit distribution forecasting: **Medium** confidence (consistent gains but varying margins across domains)
- Mode collapse mechanism analysis: **High** confidence (clear correlation with distributional imbalance, supported by quantitative and qualitative evidence)
- KL-based temporal drift metric utility: **Medium** confidence (useful diagnostic tool but requires further investigation of sensitivity)
