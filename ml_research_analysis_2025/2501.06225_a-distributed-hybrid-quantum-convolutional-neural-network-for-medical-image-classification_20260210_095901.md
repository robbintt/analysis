# A Distributed Hybrid Quantum Convolutional Neural Network for Medical Image Classification

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2501.06225
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.06225
- Reference count: 15
- Primary result: Distributed 8-qubit QCNN implemented with 5 qubits achieves 91.14% accuracy on HAM10000 dataset

## Executive Summary
This paper proposes a distributed hybrid quantum-classical approach for medical image classification that addresses the limitations of current quantum hardware while leveraging quantum computing advantages. The method combines classical feature extraction using MobileNetV2 with a distributed quantum convolutional neural network, where quantum circuits are split into smaller components to reduce qubit requirements. The approach demonstrates strong performance across multiple medical image datasets while using significantly fewer parameters than comparable models.

## Method Summary
The proposed distributed hybrid QCNN consists of two main components: a classical MobileNetV2 network for initial feature extraction and a distributed quantum convolutional neural network. The quantum component is implemented using a circuit splitting strategy that divides an 8-qubit quantum circuit into 4-qubit and 5-qubit components, allowing the model to run on quantum hardware with fewer qubits. This distributed approach enables the execution of larger quantum circuits while maintaining the quantum advantage in feature learning. The model was evaluated on three medical image datasets (HAM10000, ISIC2017, and MedMNIST) and demonstrated competitive performance with significantly reduced parameter requirements.

## Key Results
- Achieved 91.14% accuracy on HAM10000 dataset, 94.54% on ISIC2017, and 89.58% on MedMNIST
- Used only 2.16 million parameters, significantly fewer than comparable models
- Quantum circuit splitting provided 2-3% performance improvement according to ablation studies
- Successfully implemented an 8-qubit QCNN using only 5 qubits through distributed architecture

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The distributed hybrid approach works by leveraging classical networks for efficient initial feature extraction while using quantum circuits for enhanced feature learning. Quantum circuits can capture complex patterns and correlations that classical networks struggle with, particularly in medical imaging where features are intricate and subtle. The circuit splitting strategy allows larger quantum circuits to be executed on smaller quantum hardware by distributing the computation across multiple quantum processing units, effectively overcoming current hardware limitations while maintaining quantum advantages.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:**
Classical MobileNetV2 -> Classical-Quantum Interface -> Distributed Quantum Circuit (4-qubit + 5-qubit) -> Classification Output

**Critical Path:**
Image input → MobileNetV2 feature extraction → Quantum feature enhancement → Classification layer → Output prediction

**Design Tradeoffs:**
- Reduced quantum resource requirements vs. potential communication overhead
- Simpler quantum circuits vs. potential loss of quantum advantage
- Classical feature extraction vs. quantum-only feature learning capability

**Failure Signatures:**
- Poor quantum-classical interface synchronization leading to degraded performance
- Circuit splitting introducing errors or losing quantum coherence
- Classical feature extraction insufficient for complex medical patterns

## Limitations
- Performance claims based on simulated quantum environments rather than physical hardware
- Limited experimental scope to three medical image datasets of modest size
- Modest 2-3% accuracy improvements from quantum circuit splitting may not justify complexity
- Lack of quantitative comparison between MobileNetV2 and alternative classical feature extractors

## Confidence
- Quantum circuit splitting effectively reduces qubit requirements while maintaining accuracy: Medium
- Distributed hybrid QCNN outperforms classical-only approaches: Medium
- Model generalizes well across different medical imaging domains: Low
