# Assessing the use of Diffusion models for motion artifact correction in brain MRI

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2502.01418
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.01418
- Reference count: 0
- Diffusion models achieve competitive performance with supervised U-Nets for motion artifact correction, but effectiveness varies significantly across anatomical planes and shows hallucination risks in diagnostic contexts.

## Executive Summary
This study critically evaluates diffusion models (specifically DDPM) for correcting motion artifacts in 2D brain MRI scans. The authors compare the unsupervised diffusion model approach against supervised U-Net architectures trained on synthetic motion-affected images from the MR-ART benchmark dataset across sagittal, coronal, and transverse anatomical planes. While diffusion models show promise with competitive performance metrics, particularly in the transverse view, they exhibit significant vulnerabilities including hallucinations that could be problematic in clinical diagnostic settings. The study demonstrates that diffusion models hold potential for MRI motion artifact correction but require careful consideration of specific use cases and anatomical contexts.

## Method Summary
The study employs a diffusion model-based approach (DDPM) for motion artifact correction in brain MRI, comparing it against U-Net architectures trained on synthetic motion-affected images. The DDPM approach operates unsupervised, requiring no paired training data, while U-Nets use synthetic data generation techniques. Evaluation is conducted on the MR-ART benchmark dataset across three anatomical planes (sagittal, coronal, and transverse) using quantitative metrics including NMSE, PSNR, and SSIM. The diffusion model's effectiveness is assessed through its ability to denoise motion-corrupted images without requiring ground truth paired data for training.

## Key Results
- Diffusion models achieved best performance in transverse view with 0.047±0.053 NMSE and 24.9±2.4 PSNR
- U-Nets excelled in SSIM metrics but showed plane-dependent performance variations
- Sagittal view revealed significant hallucination artifacts in diffusion models (0.666±0.180 SSIM, 0.132±0.182 NMSE)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The diffusion model approach works by learning the reverse diffusion process to denoise motion-corrupted MRI images without requiring paired training data. Unlike supervised methods that need ground truth images for training, diffusion models can operate on unpaired data by learning to reverse the noising process that creates the motion artifacts. This unsupervised nature allows the model to handle diverse motion patterns without being constrained to specific synthetic artifact types. The model effectively learns the underlying image distribution and can generate clean versions of motion-corrupted images by iteratively denoising through the reverse diffusion process.

## Architecture Onboarding

### Component Map
DDPM (denoising diffusion probabilistic model) -> U-Net backbone -> Reverse diffusion process -> Clean image output

### Critical Path
The critical path involves the reverse diffusion process where the model iteratively denoises the corrupted image through multiple steps, using the U-Net architecture as the backbone for predicting noise at each step. This process transforms the motion-corrupted input into a clean output through learned denoising transitions.

### Design Tradeoffs
The unsupervised nature of diffusion models eliminates the need for paired training data but sacrifices control over specific artifact correction. Supervised U-Nets offer more targeted correction for known artifact types but require extensive paired data generation. Diffusion models show better generalization across diverse motion patterns but introduce hallucination risks. Computational efficiency favors U-Nets during inference, while diffusion models require multiple iterative steps.

### Failure Signatures
Hallucinations in sagittal view indicate the model's tendency to generate non-existent anatomical features when correction is challenging. Performance degradation in specific anatomical planes suggests sensitivity to anatomical orientation and tissue contrast patterns. The unsupervised approach may struggle with extreme artifact types not well-represented in the training distribution.

## Limitations
- The U-Net performance evaluation relies on synthetic data generation that may not capture real-world motion artifact characteristics
- Quantitative metrics (NMSE, PSNR, SSIM) may not fully capture clinical relevance or diagnostic impact of residual artifacts
- Dataset composition and motion artifact severity distribution across anatomical planes are not fully characterized

## Confidence
- Diffusion model effectiveness in transverse plane: Medium (strong performance metrics but limited real-world validation)
- Diffusion model performance in sagittal plane: Low (significant hallucination artifacts observed)
- Comparative methodology validity: Medium (relies on synthetic data for U-Net comparison)
