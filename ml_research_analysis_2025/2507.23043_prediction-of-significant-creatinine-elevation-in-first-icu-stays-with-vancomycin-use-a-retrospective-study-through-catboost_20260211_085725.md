# Prediction of Significant Creatinine Elevation in First ICU Stays with Vancomycin Use: A retrospective study through Catboost

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2507.23043
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.23043
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: CatBoost achieved AUROC 0.818 for predicting vancomycin-associated creatinine elevation in ICU patients

## Executive Summary
This retrospective study developed a machine learning model to predict vancomycin-associated creatinine elevation (a form of acute kidney injury) in ICU patients using routine clinical data. The CatBoost algorithm demonstrated strong predictive performance with an AUROC of 0.818, sensitivity of 0.800, and specificity of 0.681. The model identified key risk factors including phosphate, total bilirubin, magnesium, Charlson comorbidity index, and APSIII score, enabling early identification of high-risk patients before clinically apparent kidney dysfunction occurs.

## Method Summary
The study utilized MIMIC-IV v2.2 to identify 10,288 patients (first ICU stay, age 18-80, received IV vancomycin, no active cancer). A temporal snapshot approach captured the latest clinical values before vancomycin initiation. Feature selection employed a two-stage process using SelectKBest and Random Forest to identify 15 key variables from chartevents, labevents, and admission data. The CatBoost model was trained using 5-fold cross-validation with SMOTE applied to address class imbalance. Performance was evaluated using AUROC as the primary metric, with sensitivity fixed at 0.800 and specificity as a secondary outcome.

## Key Results
- CatBoost achieved AUROC of 0.818, sensitivity of 0.800, and specificity of 0.681
- Phosphate, total bilirubin, magnesium, Charlson comorbidity index, and APSIII score were identified as top predictors
- Bayesian analysis estimated mean risk at 60.5% (95% credible interval: 16.8-89.4%) for high-risk patients
- SHAP analysis confirmed phosphate as a major risk factor, while ALE plots revealed dose-response patterns

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The model effectively captures the complex interactions between patient characteristics and vancomycin-induced nephrotoxicity by leveraging gradient boosting algorithms that can handle non-linear relationships and feature interactions. The temporal snapshot approach ensures that only pre-treatment information is used, preventing data leakage while still capturing baseline risk factors that influence subsequent kidney injury.

## Limitations
- Single-center retrospective design limits external validity and generalizability to other healthcare settings
- Temporal snapshot approach may miss dynamic risk patterns during vancomycin therapy that could improve prediction accuracy
- 70/30 split without explicit temporal ordering could introduce information leakage between training and validation sets
