# WallStreetFeds: Client-Specific Tokens as Investment Vehicles in Federated Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2506.20518
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.20518
- Authors: Arno Geimer; Beltran Fiz Pontiveros; Radu State
- Reference count: 38
- Primary result: Introduces decentralized federated learning framework using client-specific tokens to enable third-party investment and improve incentive mechanisms

## Executive Summary
This paper proposes a decentralized federated learning framework where clients create client-specific tokens that represent proportional claims on future reward payouts. These tokens can be traded on automated market makers, allowing participants to raise capital by selling future reward shares before earning them. The system uses smart contracts to automate reward distribution based on contribution assessments, while the constant mean market maker provides liquidity and transparent pricing. This approach aims to address limitations of closed incentive schemes by enabling external investment and speculation on data value, potentially increasing participation and aligning interests between participants and investors.

## Method Summary
The framework introduces a decentralized architecture where clients create tokens upon joining a federation, which represent claims on future rewards. These tokens are traded on an automated market maker (CMMM) that provides liquidity and price discovery. Smart contracts (Orchestrator and Scenario) manage federation setup, client registration, and reward distribution based on contribution assessments. The Federator runs federated learning, computes contributions, and deposits rewards to the Scenario contract, which distributes them to token holders proportionally. This creates a market-based incentive system where token prices theoretically reflect contribution quality and expected rewards.

## Key Results
- Token prices can reflect contribution quality and market demand through automated market maker mechanisms
- Participants can raise capital by selling future reward shares, reducing entry barriers
- Incentive mechanisms align participant and investor interests while mitigating upfront costs
- Framework enables external investment flow and supports speculation on data value

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1: Tokenized Reward Claims Enable Capital Access
Claim: Client-specific tokens allow participants to monetize future expected rewards before earning them, reducing entry barriers. When a client joins a federation, they create tokens tied to their identity representing proportional claims on future reward payouts. The Scenario smart contract distributes rewards to token holders proportionally each round. Clients can sell tokens immediately on the AMM to raise capital for computational resources. Core assumption: Investors believe token prices reflect credible expectations of future contribution quality and reward amounts.

### Mechanism 2: Constant Mean Market Maker Provides Liquidity and Price Discovery
Claim: The CMMM enables continuous token trading without requiring bilateral matching of buyers and sellers. The AMM holds pools of all client tokens plus a baseline currency (numeraire). The trading function f(x₁,...,xₙ) = ∏ᵢ xᵢ^ωᵢ maintains constant geometric mean. When demand for a token increases, its price rises algorithmically as the pool rebalances. This creates transparent, demand-driven pricing. Core assumption: Sufficient liquidity providers deposit initial token/currency pools; low-liquidity tokens experience high slippage.

### Mechanism 3: Decentralized Smart Contract Layer Enforces Transparent Payouts
Claim: Smart contracts automate reward distribution based on published contribution data, enabling trustless participation. The Orchestrator creates Scenarios (federation-specific contracts). Each round, the Federator sends contribution assessments and rewards to the Scenario contract, which then distributes to token holders based on ownership records. All transactions are on-chain and public. Core assumption: The Federator's contribution assessment method is accepted as fair by all parties; smart contracts execute without bugs or manipulation.

## Foundational Learning

- Concept: **Federated Learning (cross-silo vs. cross-device)**
  - Why needed here: The framework targets cross-silo federations (few participants with large datasets) where incentive alignment is critical; cross-device has different constraints.
  - Quick check question: Can you explain why cross-silo settings prioritize privacy and incentives over communication costs?

- Concept: **Contribution Assessment Methods (Shapley values, accuracy-based)**
  - Why needed here: Reward distribution depends on assessing each client's contribution quality; Shapley values are computationally expensive but more fair for non-IID data.
  - Quick check question: Why might Shapley values produce more volatile reward distributions than equal payouts?

- Concept: **Automated Market Makers and Trading Functions**
  - Why needed here: Understanding how AMMs price assets via bonding curves is essential for evaluating token price dynamics.
  - Quick check question: In a constant product AMM (x × y = k), what happens to token Y's price when more token X is deposited?

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  Orchestrator (smart contract) -> Scenario (smart contract) -> AMM (CMMM) -> Federator (off-chain server) <- Clients

- Critical path:
  1. Federator registers federation with Orchestrator → Scenario created
  2. Client contacts Scenario → receives token allocation
  3. Client lists tokens on AMM (optional) → raises capital
  4. FL training rounds proceed → Federator sends contribution data + rewards to Scenario
  5. Scenario distributes rewards to all token holders proportionally

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Equal token creation vs. weighted: Paper assumes equal initial tokens; alternative could pre-weight by reputation
  - Fixed vs. variable rewards: Fixed total reward makes token valuation predictable but depreciates over rounds; variable rewards introduce speculation but complicate valuation
  - Contribution method choice: Shapley values are fair but computationally infeasible at scale; simpler heuristics may be gamed

- Failure signatures:
  - Low liquidity: Tokens cannot be sold; clients cannot raise capital
  - Contribution disputes: Clients contest assessments; system loses credibility
  - Regulatory intervention: Token issuance classified as securities; legal barriers halt deployment
  - Collusion: Clients + Federator manipulate contributions to inflate specific token prices

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Token price tracking simulation: Implement minimal Scenario with 5-10 simulated clients, fixed total reward over 50 rounds; track token prices on CMMM as rewards are distributed. Verify price decay matches theoretical expectations.
  2. Contribution method impact: Run same federation with equal rewards vs. Shapley-based rewards; compare token price volatility and client retention rates.
  3. Capital raising stress test: Simulate clients selling 50-80% of tokens at round 0; measure whether raised capital correlates with improved contribution in subsequent rounds.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: Does the introduction of tradable client-specific tokens lead to market manipulation or speculative bubbles that decouple token prices from actual client contribution quality?
- Basis in paper: The Conclusion states the need for "a more detailed analysis of the economic viability," while the Abstract notes the system "supports speculation on data value."
- Why unresolved: The paper demonstrates that prices can reflect quality but does not model adversarial economic behaviors that might distort incentives.
- What evidence would resolve it: Game-theoretic simulations or empirical data from testnet deployment showing correlation between token volatility and model utility under adversarial trading conditions.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How do existing financial regulations classify these client-specific tokens, and does this classification impede the framework's decentralization goals?
- Basis in paper: The Conclusion calls for an "in-depth exploration of potential regulatory considerations surrounding the issuance and trading of these client-specific tokens."
- Why unresolved: The framework mimics stock dividends by sharing rewards but operates on decentralized blockchain, creating ambiguity regarding legal compliance.
- What evidence would resolve it: Comparative legal analysis determining if tokens constitute "investment contracts" or utility tokens in major jurisdictions.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How does the choice of Automated Market Maker (AMM) algorithm influence the liquidity and price accuracy for client tokens, particularly in federations with high variance in contribution rewards?
- Basis in paper: The Conclusion proposes implementing the framework to "explore the effect of different pricing strategies."
- Why unresolved: The paper utilizes a constant mean market maker but acknowledges that rewards fluctuate considerably with non-linear pricing methods, which might cause liquidity issues or slippage.
- What evidence would resolve it: Quantitative analysis comparing different AMM bonding curves on price stability and slippage using historical FL reward data.

## Limitations
- Token valuation uncertainty: No empirical evidence that token prices reliably reflect contribution quality
- Regulatory risk: Client-specific tokens may qualify as securities under existing financial regulations
- Liquidity constraints: Many client tokens may have minimal trading activity, leading to high slippage and meaningless price signals

## Confidence

**High Confidence:**
- Smart contracts can automate reward distribution based on contribution assessments
- Constant Mean Market Makers can provide algorithmic price discovery for tokenized assets
- Client-specific tokens can theoretically represent proportional claims on future rewards

**Medium Confidence:**
- Third-party investment via token trading would reduce participant entry barriers
- Token prices could provide market-based signals about contribution quality
- Decentralized incentive mechanisms improve upon closed federation models

**Low Confidence:**
- Token prices will reliably reflect contribution quality in practice
- The system will achieve sufficient liquidity for meaningful price discovery
- Regulatory frameworks will accommodate this token-based incentive model

## Next Checks
1. **Price-Quality Correlation Simulation:** Build comprehensive simulation framework with 50+ clients, varying data quality distributions, and different contribution assessment methods. Track token price movements against ground-truth contribution metrics across 100+ federation runs to establish whether price discovery reliably signals quality.

2. **Liquidity Stress Testing:** Model CMMM pools with different initial liquidity levels and trading volumes. Measure how price slippage, bid-ask spreads, and price discovery effectiveness vary with liquidity. Identify minimum liquidity thresholds below which the token investment function breaks down.

3. **Manipulation Resistance Analysis:** Design attack scenarios where Federator and clients collude to inflate specific token prices through contribution manipulation. Test whether the system's price discovery mechanism can detect and resist such manipulation, and quantify economic damage from successful attacks.
