# A Study of Anatomical Priors for Deep Learning-Based Segmentation of Pheochromocytoma in Abdominal CT

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2507.15193
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.15193
- Reference count: 35
- Primary result: Tumor + Kidney + Aorta (TKA) strategy achieved highest PCC segmentation accuracy with DSC improvement (p=0.0097) and NSD improvement (p=0.0110) compared to Tumor + Body baseline

## Executive Summary
This study develops a deep learning framework for accurate 3D segmentation of pheochromocytoma tumors from abdominal CT scans. Using nnU-Net architecture, the authors systematically evaluate eleven annotation strategies incorporating anatomical priors from adjacent organs. The Tumor + Kidney + Aorta (TKA) strategy demonstrates superior performance, significantly outperforming previous approaches with strong tumor burden quantification (R²=0.968) and consistent performance across genetic subtypes.

## Method Summary
The study employs 3D nnU-Net with various anatomical priors for PCC segmentation. Eleven annotation strategies were tested, combining tumor masks with different organ combinations (kidney, aorta, liver, spleen, etc.) from TotalSegmentator. Models were trained using composite loss (Cross-Entropy + Dice Loss) and evaluated on 172 patients using DSC, NSD, and F1 metrics. The TKA strategy (tumor + kidney + aorta) showed optimal performance.

## Key Results
- TKA strategy achieved highest segmentation accuracy with DSC improvement (p=0.0097) and NSD improvement (p=0.0110)
- 25.84% higher F1 score at IoU=0.5 compared to Tumor + Body baseline
- Strong tumor burden quantification (R²=0.968) and consistent performance across genetic subtypes
- Incorporating large-volume organs (liver, spleen) degraded performance due to class imbalance

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1: Anatomical Spatial Prior
Joint training with Kidney and Aorta constrains prediction search space, reducing false positives in distant regions. The model learns PCC tumors are statistically only probable near these anchor structures, suppressing predictions in incorrect locations like liver or subcutaneous tissue.

### Mechanism 2: Class Imbalance Effects
Adding large-volume organs (liver, spleen) degrades performance because their massive volume dominates optimization gradients. The loss function prioritizes learning large organ boundaries at the expense of subtle tumor features, starving the tumor class of learning signal.

### Mechanism 3: Boundary Delineation
Defining adjacent "negative" classes improves boundary alignment by forcing hard decisions at tissue interfaces. Mutual exclusion pressure between tumor-kidney boundaries forces the model to learn discriminative features, sharpening boundary alignment.

## Foundational Learning

- **Multi-class Segmentation vs. Binary**: Understanding how Softmax (C classes) differs from Sigmoid (1 class) in the final layer is critical since the intervention treats segmentation as Tumor/Kidney/Aorta/Background.
- **The "Receptive Field" in 3D CNNs**: The model must "see" kidney to identify adjacent tumor. The patch size (80×160×160) must cover typical kidney-to-tumor distance.
- **Class Imbalance & Dice Loss**: Understanding why standard Cross-Entropy fails small objects and how Dice Loss mitigates this is essential, especially given explicit blame of class imbalance for liver/spleen failures.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map**: CT Volume → Resampling/Intensity Clipping/Normalization → 3D nnU-Net → Multi-class Segmentation Head → Extract Tumor channel
- **Critical path**: The Label Merging Strategy - the neural network is standard nnU-Net, but the construction of ground truth (merging TotalSegmentator outputs with tumor masks) is the "secret sauce."
- **Design tradeoffs**: Context vs. Confusion - adding priors helps locate tumor but adds complexity; TKA is local optimum, adding more organs creates noise; Automated vs. Manual priors - TotalSegmentator is fast but introduces noisy labels.
- **Failure signatures**: Under-segmentation of invasive tumors (model hesitates to predict tumor where it touches kidney); High F1, Low DSC (model detects presence but struggles with precise boundaries).
- **First 3 experiments**: 1) Baseline: Train Tumor-Only (T) labels; 2) Context Ablation: Compare TB vs TKA on small validation split; 3) Generalization: Test TKA on different genetic subtypes (SDHx vs Kinase).

## Limitations
- Reliance on automated anatomical priors from TotalSegmentator introduces potential label noise
- Single institution validation (172 patients) raises generalizability concerns across different CT scanners/protocols
- Exclusion pressure mechanism may fail for invasive tumors that infiltrate adjacent organs
- No evaluation of computational efficiency or inference time for clinical deployment

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: TKA strategy's superior performance metrics (DSC, NSD, F1 score) with statistical significance (p < 0.05) across multiple evaluation metrics
- **Medium Confidence**: Mechanistic explanation for class imbalance effects when including large organs is plausible but lacks direct empirical validation
- **Low Confidence**: Generalization claims across genetic subtypes based on limited sample sizes per subtype require broader validation

## Next Checks
1. **Multi-institutional Validation**: Test TKA model on external datasets from different hospitals with varying CT protocols to assess robustness and generalizability
2. **Invasive Tumor Analysis**: Create subset of cases with histologically confirmed tumor invasion into adjacent organs to evaluate performance on boundary cases where exclusion pressure may fail
3. **Ablation of Prior Quality**: Compare model performance using manual vs automated anatomical priors to quantify impact of label noise from TotalSegmentator on segmentation accuracy
