# Noise-Resilient Quantum Aggregation on NISQ for Federated ADAS Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2512.13196
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.13196
- Authors: Chethana Prasad Kabgere; Sudarshan T S B
- Reference count: 22
- Primary result: Achieves 86.1% accuracy and 0.84 F1-score in federated ADAS learning via noise-resilient quantum aggregation on NISQ devices

## Executive Summary
This paper presents Noise-Resilient Quantum Federated Learning (NR-QFL), a hybrid quantum-classical framework designed for secure, low-latency federated learning in autonomous driving systems. The approach leverages variational quantum circuits on near-term quantum devices to perform model aggregation with enhanced noise resilience through angle-encoded parameters and adaptive gate reparameterization. Quantum entropy-based client selection and multi-server coordination improve fairness and security in distributed automotive environments. Experiments using Qiskit Aer simulation demonstrate superior performance compared to classical FedAvg and baseline quantum approaches while maintaining compatibility with existing vehicular edge-cloud architectures.

## Method Summary
NR-QFL implements a hybrid quantum-classical federated learning pipeline where edge vehicles upload locally trained model parameters to edge servers. These parameters are encoded as quantum state rotations using angle encoding, then aggregated through parameterized quantum circuits with depth-optimized structures. The framework employs quantum entropy measures to select federated clients based on contribution quality and implements adaptive gate reparameterization to maintain performance under noise. Multi-server coordination distributes the quantum computation load while preserving security through entanglement-based verification. The entire system operates within NISQ constraints using shallow circuits (typically <10 depth) and integrates seamlessly with classical ResNet-18/CIFAR-10 workloads adapted for automotive perception tasks.

## Key Results
- Achieves 86.1% accuracy and 0.84 F1-score, surpassing classical FedAvg (79.2%, 0.76) and baseline QFL (84.5%, 0.81)
- Demonstrates only 8% higher communication cost compared to classical approaches despite quantum operations
- CAM analysis reveals superior feature attention under noise conditions compared to classical baselines
- Validates theoretical noise-resilience bounds through simulation on realistic quantum noise models

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework exploits quantum superposition and entanglement properties to represent and aggregate high-dimensional model parameters more efficiently than classical averaging. Angle encoding maps continuous parameter spaces into quantum state amplitudes, while parameterized circuits perform non-linear transformations that enhance noise robustness. The adaptive reparameterization dynamically adjusts quantum gate parameters based on measured noise characteristics, maintaining aggregation fidelity. Quantum entropy metrics provide principled measures of client contribution quality and system uncertainty, enabling intelligent client selection and fault detection in federated settings.

## Foundational Learning

**Quantum Federated Learning**: Hybrid approach combining quantum computing with classical federated learning protocols to enhance model aggregation and privacy preservation in distributed systems. Needed because classical aggregation methods struggle with noise and heterogeneity in federated settings. Quick check: Verify quantum circuit can represent classical model parameter distributions through angle encoding.

**Angle Encoding**: Quantum technique mapping continuous parameters to rotation angles on qubits, enabling representation of high-dimensional data in quantum states. Needed because classical model parameters must be translated into quantum representations for NISQ device processing. Quick check: Confirm rotation precision limitations don't cause information loss for typical neural network weight ranges.

**Variational Quantum Circuits**: Parameterized quantum circuits optimized through classical learning loops, suitable for NISQ devices with limited coherence times. Needed because deep quantum circuits would exceed hardware capabilities while shallow circuits provide sufficient expressivity for aggregation tasks. Quick check: Validate circuit depth <10 maintains acceptable fidelity on target NISQ hardware.

**Quantum Entropy**: Information-theoretic measure using quantum state probabilities to quantify uncertainty and information content. Needed because classical entropy metrics don't capture quantum correlations essential for federated client evaluation. Quick check: Compare quantum vs classical entropy measures for client contribution ranking accuracy.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Vehicle Edge Devices -> Classical NN Training -> Parameter Encoding -> Quantum Aggregation Circuit -> Classical Post-processing -> Model Distribution

**Critical Path**: Client parameter encoding → Quantum state preparation → Variational circuit execution → Classical measurement processing → Model parameter extraction → Federated model update

**Design Tradeoffs**: Shallow circuits favor NISQ feasibility over theoretical expressiveness; angle encoding provides simple implementation but may limit information capacity; quantum entropy selection improves quality but adds quantum state preparation overhead.

**Failure Signatures**: Excessive decoherence causing parameter drift; barren plateaus from poor parameter initialization; quantum state preparation errors from insufficient rotation precision; classical-quantum interface bottlenecks from measurement latency.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

**Open Question 1**: How does NR-QFL performance differ on physical NISQ hardware compared to noise simulations? The study relies entirely on Qiskit Aer simulations using CPTP noise models, while explicitly stating that "automotive qualification remains a future milestone." Simulated noise channels may not capture the full complexity of hardware-specific crosstalk, calibration drift, and SPAM errors found in real quantum processors. Empirical convergence results and accuracy metrics from deploying the NR-QFL protocol on physical quantum backends would resolve this uncertainty.

**Open Question 2**: Can the proposed NISQ co-processor maintain coherence under automotive thermal and vibration constraints? The Conclusion identifies "thermal-aware hardware integration" as a necessary future direction for deploying the framework. The paper notes NISQ devices require cryogenic temperatures (10–20mK), which presents significant engineering challenges for integration into standard vehicular environments. A hardware prototype demonstrating stable qubit coherence and gate fidelity within a thermally managed, vehicular-grade enclosure would provide resolution.

**Open Question 3**: Does angle encoding introduce information bottlenecks when scaling to higher-dimensional ADAS models? The paper maps complex ResNet-18 parameters to quantum states using very low qubit counts (≤6). Aggregating high-dimensional classical weights into a low-dimensional quantum Hilbert space may result in irreversible information loss or "barren plateaus" not visible in the specific ResNet-18/CIFAR-10 test case. Ablation studies testing aggregation fidelity as the ratio of model parameters to available qubits increases would clarify this limitation.

**Open Question 4**: What latency reductions are achievable via pulse-level co-design compared to standard circuit execution? The Conclusion suggests "quantum–classical co-design at the pulse level" as a future avenue to advance the framework. Current implementation uses high-level APIs (Qiskit Runtime), which may incur overheads that could be eliminated by direct pulse control, critical for real-time ADAS. Comparative benchmarks measuring aggregation latency between OpenQASM-based execution and optimized pulse schedules would provide empirical evidence.

## Limitations
- Experimental validation relies entirely on Qiskit Aer simulator outputs rather than real NISQ hardware measurements
- Framework assumes angle-encoding can represent arbitrary neural network weights despite practical limits on rotation precision and circuit depth
- Quantum entropy-based client selection mechanism requires substantial quantum state preparation overhead that may offset communication efficiency gains
- Multi-server coordination approach lacks detailed security analysis against sophisticated adversarial attacks beyond noise injection

## Confidence
High: The hybrid quantum-classical architecture design and integration with existing federated learning protocols are theoretically sound and well-scoped.

Medium: Performance improvements over classical FedAvg and baseline QFL are demonstrated in simulation, but real-world quantum hardware constraints could significantly impact these gains.

Low: Claims about noise-resilience bounds and quantum entropy-based fairness mechanisms require empirical validation on actual NISQ hardware with comprehensive noise characterization.
