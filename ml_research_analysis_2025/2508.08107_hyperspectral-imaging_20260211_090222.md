# Hyperspectral Imaging

## Executive Summary
This primer provides a thorough overview of hyperspectral imaging (HSI), a sensing modality that captures detailed spatial and spectral information across hundreds of contiguous bands. The paper systematically covers the physical principles, sensor architectures, and complete data pipeline from acquisition through analysis. It addresses essential preprocessing steps, analytical techniques, and diverse applications spanning Earth observation, precision agriculture, biomedicine, industrial inspection, cultural heritage, and security. The authors also identify key challenges and emerging solutions while emphasizing best practices for reproducibility and data sharing.

## Method Summary
The primer synthesizes current knowledge across the HSI field by organizing content around the complete imaging workflow. It begins with fundamental physical principles governing light-matter interactions and sensor design considerations. The methodology section details preprocessing requirements including radiometric and atmospheric corrections, followed by analytical approaches such as dimensionality reduction, classification, and spectral unmixing. The paper integrates traditional signal processing methods with modern AI-driven approaches, examining their respective strengths and limitations. Case studies across multiple application domains illustrate practical implementation challenges and opportunities.

## Key Results
- HSI captures both spatial and spectral information across hundreds of contiguous bands, enabling detailed material characterization
- Modern HSI systems face trade-offs between spatial, spectral, and temporal resolution requiring careful sensor design
- AI-driven methods including self-supervised learning and cross-modal fusion show promise for addressing HSI's high-dimensional complexity
- Future HSI systems will likely integrate joint hardware-software design with foundation models for scalable, real-time analysis

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
HSI works by capturing detailed spectral signatures at each spatial location, enabling material identification through their unique reflectance patterns across wavelengths. The physical principle relies on light-matter interactions where different materials absorb, reflect, or transmit specific wavelengths. Modern computational approaches enhance this by extracting discriminative features from high-dimensional data while addressing challenges like spectral variability and mixed pixels. The integration of AI methods helps manage the curse of dimensionality and enables automated feature extraction that complements traditional physics-based approaches.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map:
Light source/illumination -> Sensor acquisition -> Pre-processing (radiometric correction, atmospheric correction) -> Dimensionality reduction -> Analysis (classification, unmixing) -> Applications

Critical Path:
The critical path for HSI analysis flows from sensor acquisition through preprocessing to analysis. Raw data must undergo radiometric and atmospheric correction before meaningful analysis can occur. Dimensionality reduction typically precedes classification or unmixing to manage computational complexity. The quality of preprocessing directly impacts downstream analysis accuracy.

Design Tradeoffs:
Spatial vs spectral resolution vs temporal resolution represents the fundamental tradeoff in HSI system design. Higher spectral resolution requires narrower bandwidths but reduces signal-to-noise ratio. Spatial resolution improvements often come at the cost of spectral detail. Computational approaches can partially compensate for hardware limitations but introduce their own constraints around processing time and model complexity.

Failure Signatures:
Common failure modes include poor atmospheric correction leading to inaccurate surface reflectance, inadequate dimensionality reduction causing overfitting in classification, and spectral unmixing errors when endmembers are not well-characterized. AI models may fail due to insufficient training data or domain shift between training and deployment conditions. Hardware failures typically manifest as calibration drift or sensor noise that propagates through the entire pipeline.

First Experiments:
1. Acquire test HSI data over known reference materials to validate sensor performance and calibration
2. Apply atmospheric correction to raw data and compare corrected spectra against ground truth measurements
3. Test dimensionality reduction techniques on a standard dataset to evaluate information preservation and computational efficiency
