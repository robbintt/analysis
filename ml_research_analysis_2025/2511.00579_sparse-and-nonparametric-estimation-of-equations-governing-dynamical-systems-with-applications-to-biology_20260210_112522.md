# Sparse and nonparametric estimation of equations governing dynamical systems with applications to biology

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.00579
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.00579
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: KB-Sindy achieves 99.4% prediction fit on complex stacked Lorenz systems while capturing nonlinearities without requiring prior functional form knowledge

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces KB-Sindy, a novel framework that extends the Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (Sindy) algorithm by incorporating nonparametric kernel-based estimation. The method addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional Sindy approaches by modeling system dynamics as a sum of sparse parametric components (using basis functions) and nonparametric components (estimated via kernels). This combination enables accurate inference of governing equations from data even when the nonlinearities cannot be captured parsimoniously using standard monomial bases. The framework is particularly valuable for biological systems where complex interactions, feedback mechanisms, and stochastic dynamics are common but the exact functional forms are unknown.

## Method Summary
KB-Sindy combines sparse parametric estimation with nonparametric kernel-based methods to infer model equations from data. The approach models system dynamics as a sum of two components: a sparse parametric part (described by basis functions) and a nonparametric part (estimated using a kernel that encodes smoothness information). The nonparametric component is implemented using Gaussian processes, where the kernel function encodes prior knowledge about the smoothness of the underlying dynamics. The method employs sparse estimation techniques, including the Lasso algorithm, to select relevant basis functions while avoiding overfitting. This hybrid approach allows KB-Sindy to handle complex nonlinearities, multiple inputs, feedback mechanisms, and stochastic dynamics without requiring prior knowledge of their functional form.

## Key Results
- KB-Sindy achieved prediction fits of 99.4% for complex stacked Lorenz systems
- The method maintained high accuracy even with significant measurement noise
- Successful reconstruction of both parametric coefficients and nonlinear transformations in diverse biological examples including gene regulation and calcium oscillations

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The KB-Sindy framework works by decomposing complex dynamical systems into sparse parametric components that capture known or hypothesized relationships and nonparametric components that flexibly model unknown nonlinearities. The kernel-based estimation acts as a universal approximator, capable of representing any smooth function without requiring explicit parameterization. This decomposition allows the method to leverage the interpretability and parsimony of sparse models while maintaining the flexibility needed for complex biological systems. The Gaussian process framework provides uncertainty quantification and naturally handles measurement noise, while the sparse estimation ensures that only the most relevant terms are included in the final model.

## Architecture Onboarding
Component map: Data -> Basis Function Generation -> Sparse Parametric Estimation -> Nonparametric Kernel Estimation -> Combined Model Output
Critical path: The core workflow involves (1) generating an augmented dictionary of basis functions from the data, (2) applying sparse estimation to identify relevant parametric terms, (3) using kernel methods to estimate the nonparametric residual, and (4) combining these components into a final model
Design tradeoffs: The method balances model complexity against generalization by using sparse selection for parametric terms while allowing flexible nonparametric components; this creates tension between interpretability and expressive power
Failure signatures: Poor performance may indicate (1) insufficient basis functions to capture the parametric component, (2) inappropriate kernel choice for the underlying dynamics, or (3) violation of smoothness assumptions in the nonparametric component

## Limitations
- The method requires careful selection of basis functions and kernel parameters, which can significantly impact performance
- The convergence analysis assumes the number of samples grows faster than the dictionary size, which may not hold for biological datasets with limited measurements
- The combined KB-Sindy approach lacks rigorous convergence guarantees for the joint sparse-nonparametric formulation

## Confidence
Theoretical foundations of sparse parametric estimation (High) are well-established through connections to the Lasso framework, while nonparametric kernel-based estimation (Medium) relies on standard Gaussian process assumptions that may not fully capture complex biological dynamics. The combined KB-Sindy approach (Medium) shows strong empirical performance but lacks rigorous convergence guarantees for the joint sparse-nonparametric formulation.
