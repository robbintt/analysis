# VessShape: Few-shot 2D blood vessel segmentation by leveraging shape priors from synthetic images

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.27646
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.27646
- Reference count: 0
- Primary result: Achieves strong few-shot segmentation performance with only 4-10 samples by pre-training on synthetic data with shape bias

## Executive Summary
VessShape addresses the challenge of blood vessel segmentation in medical imaging by generating large-scale synthetic datasets designed to instill a shape bias in segmentation models. Traditional CNN approaches tend to learn texture-based features, limiting their performance across different imaging modalities. VessShape overcomes this by creating procedurally generated tubular geometries with diverse textures, encouraging models to learn shape cues rather than textures. The approach demonstrates that pre-training on synthetic data enables effective few-shot learning, requiring only 4-10 samples for fine-tuning on real-world datasets.

## Method Summary
The VessShape methodology generates synthetic 2D images containing procedurally created tubular geometries combined with varied foreground and background textures. This design specifically targets inducing a shape bias in segmentation models, as opposed to the texture bias typically learned by CNNs. The synthetic dataset is used for pre-training segmentation models, which are then fine-tuned on small numbers of real samples from target domains. The approach leverages the geometric consistency of blood vessels across imaging modalities while providing the diversity needed for generalization.

## Key Results
- Model pre-trained on VessShape achieves strong few-shot segmentation with only 4-10 real samples needed for fine-tuning
- Demonstrates notable zero-shot capabilities, effectively segmenting vessels in unseen domains without target-specific training
- Works across different imaging domains, showing improved generalization compared to traditional approaches

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The approach works by breaking the texture bias that typically limits CNN performance across domains. By training on synthetic data where the underlying geometry (tubular vessel shapes) remains consistent while textures vary widely, the model learns to rely on shape cues rather than texture patterns. This shape bias transfers effectively to real data across different imaging modalities because blood vessels maintain similar geometric properties regardless of how they're imaged. The pre-training establishes robust shape priors that can be quickly adapted to specific domains with minimal real data.

## Foundational Learning
- **Shape vs Texture Bias**: CNNs typically learn texture-based features which limits cross-domain generalization; understanding this bias is crucial for designing effective synthetic data
- **Few-shot Learning**: The ability to learn from very limited labeled data through pre-training; key for medical applications where annotations are scarce
- **Synthetic Data Generation**: Creating realistic training data programmatically; essential for overcoming data scarcity in specialized domains
- **Domain Adaptation**: Transferring knowledge from one domain to another; critical for medical imaging where multiple modalities exist
- **Procedural Geometry Generation**: Creating diverse yet consistent geometric structures; necessary for building effective shape priors
- **Transfer Learning**: Leveraging pre-trained models for new tasks; fundamental to the few-shot approach

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Synthetic Data Generator -> Pre-training Module -> Fine-tuning Module -> Evaluation

Critical Path: The synthetic data generation pipeline directly determines the quality of shape priors learned during pre-training, which then enables effective few-shot adaptation to real domains.

Design Tradeoffs: The balance between geometric consistency (maintaining recognizable vessel shapes) and texture diversity (ensuring shape-based learning) is critical. Too much texture variation might confuse the model, while too little reduces generalization benefits.

Failure Signatures: Poor performance on pathological cases, failure to generalize to significantly different vessel geometries, or over-reliance on texture cues despite synthetic training.

First Experiments:
1. Test synthetic data generation with varying levels of texture diversity to identify optimal parameters
2. Evaluate shape bias strength by comparing performance on synthetic vs real data across different texture combinations
3. Measure zero-shot capabilities on completely unseen imaging modalities to establish generalization limits

## Limitations
- Uncertainty about whether synthetic data captures all possible vessel geometries and pathological variations in clinical practice
- Shape bias may not generalize to rare or complex vessel configurations not represented in synthetic dataset
- Computational efficiency of synthetic data generation pipeline not thoroughly discussed

## Confidence

**High Confidence**: The core methodology of using synthetic data to induce shape bias is well-established and the implementation details are sound.

**Medium Confidence**: The few-shot learning results are convincing but may benefit from additional validation across more diverse imaging modalities and pathological cases.

**Medium Confidence**: The zero-shot capabilities are demonstrated but the scope of conditions under which this truly works remains to be fully characterized.

## Next Checks

1. Test the model on pathological cases including aneurysms, stenoses, and abnormal vessel configurations not represented in the synthetic dataset to assess generalization limits.

2. Evaluate performance degradation as the number of training samples decreases below the stated 4-10 sample range to establish the practical minimum.

3. Conduct a comprehensive computational efficiency analysis comparing synthetic data generation time versus traditional data annotation, including storage and preprocessing requirements.
