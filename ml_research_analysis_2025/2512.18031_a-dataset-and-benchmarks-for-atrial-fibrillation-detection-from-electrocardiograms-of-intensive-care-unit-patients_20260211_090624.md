# A Dataset and Benchmarks for Atrial Fibrillation Detection from Electrocardiograms of Intensive Care Unit Patients

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2512.18031
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.18031
- Reference count: 38
- Compared AI approaches for AF detection in ICU ECGs, with ECG foundation models achieving best F1 score of 0.89

## Executive Summary
This study presents a comprehensive comparison of AI approaches for detecting atrial fibrillation (AF) in intensive care unit (ICU) electrocardiograms (ECGs). The research team evaluated feature-based classifiers, deep learning models (both 1D and 2D), and ECG foundation models using two datasets: an institutional ICU dataset and the 2021 PhysioNet Challenge data. The ICU dataset contains nearly 600 10-second four-lead ECG recordings from unique patients and is made publicly available for further research. The study demonstrates that ECG foundation models, particularly when using transfer learning, achieve superior performance with an F1 score of 0.89 on ICU test data, highlighting their potential for real-time patient monitoring in clinical settings.

## Method Summary
The study employed a multi-faceted approach to compare various AI methods for AF detection from ECG signals. The researchers developed and tested several classification strategies including traditional feature-based approaches using handcrafted ECG features, deep learning models operating on 1D ECG signals and 2D spectrogram representations, and ECG foundation models pre-trained on large-scale ECG datasets. The models were trained and evaluated on two distinct datasets: a newly published institutional ICU dataset with approximately 600 labeled 10-second four-lead ECG recordings, and the publicly available 2021 PhysioNet Challenge dataset. Transfer learning was specifically investigated for the foundation models to assess their ability to adapt to the ICU-specific data distribution. Model performance was evaluated using standard metrics including accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and F1 score across different training configurations.

## Key Results
- ECG foundation models achieved the highest performance with F1 score of 0.89 on ICU test set through transfer learning
- The study published a labeled ICU dataset with nearly 600 10-second four-lead ECG recordings from unique patients
- Deep learning approaches outperformed traditional feature-based classifiers across all evaluation metrics
- Transfer learning significantly improved foundation model performance on ICU-specific data compared to training from scratch

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The superior performance of ECG foundation models stems from their pre-training on massive ECG datasets, allowing them to learn general ECG signal representations that capture subtle patterns indicative of atrial fibrillation. These models leverage transfer learning to adapt these learned representations to the specific characteristics of ICU ECG data, which often contains noise, artifacts, and patient-specific variations not present in general ECG databases. The multi-lead input (four-lead configuration) provides complementary spatial information about cardiac electrical activity, enabling more robust detection of the irregular rhythms characteristic of AF. Deep learning approaches, particularly those using 2D spectrograms, can capture temporal-frequency patterns in ECG signals that may be missed by traditional feature-based methods relying on hand-crafted metrics.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: ECG signal → Preprocessing → Feature extraction / Deep learning model → Classification → Performance metrics

**Critical Path**: Raw ECG data → Noise filtering and normalization → Model-specific processing (either handcrafted features or learned representations) → Binary classification (AF vs normal) → Evaluation metrics calculation

**Design Tradeoffs**: The study balances model complexity against interpretability, with deep learning models offering superior performance but less transparency compared to feature-based approaches. The choice of 10-second segments prioritizes real-time monitoring capability over capturing longer-term rhythm patterns. Transfer learning provides computational efficiency and improved performance but requires access to large pre-trained models and may introduce domain adaptation challenges.

**Failure Signatures**: Models may fail on ECG segments with significant noise or artifacts common in ICU settings, potentially leading to false positives or negatives. Intermittent AF patterns occurring outside the 10-second window would be missed entirely. Patient-specific variations in ECG morphology that differ from training data distributions could reduce model accuracy. Feature-based approaches may miss subtle AF patterns that require complex temporal analysis beyond handcrafted metrics.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
The paper does not explicitly identify specific open questions, though the limitations section suggests areas for future research including multi-institutional validation and evaluation of longer monitoring periods.

## Limitations
- The ICU dataset contains only 600 labeled recordings from a single institution, potentially limiting generalizability to diverse patient populations
- The study focuses exclusively on 10-second ECG segments, which may miss intermittent AF patterns requiring longer monitoring periods
- Computational efficiency and real-time implementation constraints for clinical deployment were not evaluated
- Model performance was not validated on multi-institutional datasets with varying ECG acquisition protocols

## Confidence

**High confidence**: The empirical performance benchmarks for different AI approaches on the provided datasets are reliable and reproducible, as they are based on standard evaluation metrics and publicly available PhysioNet data.
