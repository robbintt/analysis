# Towards Federated Clustering: A Client-wise Private Graph Aggregation Framework

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.10915
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.10915
- Authors: Guanxiong He; Jie Wang; Liaoyuan Tang; Zheng Wang; Rong Wang; Feiping Nie
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Achieves up to 10% improvement in NMI over federated baselines while maintaining strong privacy guarantees

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces SPP-FGC, a novel federated clustering framework that addresses the critical challenge of balancing privacy preservation with clustering performance in distributed environments. The framework operates on a client-server architecture where clients construct private structural graphs that capture data relationships without exposing raw data or embeddings. The server then aggregates these private graphs into a unified global graph for clustering analysis. The approach offers both a one-shot mode for efficiency and an iterative feature learning mode (SPP-FGC+) for complex data scenarios, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance while maintaining strong privacy guarantees.

## Method Summary
SPP-FGC resolves the privacy-performance trade-off in federated clustering by using local structural graphs as a privacy-preserving medium for knowledge sharing. Each client builds a private graph representing data relationships within their local dataset, which is then transmitted to a central server for aggregation. The server constructs a global graph from these private graphs and performs clustering on this unified representation. The framework includes two operational modes: a one-shot mode that aggregates graphs once for efficient clustering, and SPP-FGC+ that incorporates iterative feature learning for enhanced performance on complex datasets. This approach avoids the privacy risks associated with sharing raw data or model embeddings while enabling effective knowledge aggregation across distributed clients.

## Key Results
- Achieves up to 10% improvement in Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) over federated baselines
- Maintains strong privacy guarantees through private graph aggregation without exposing raw data
- Demonstrates robustness to data heterogeneity across different clients
- Shows scalability to large-scale federated environments with thousands of participants

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by transforming raw data into structural graphs at the client level, which capture relational information while preserving privacy. These graphs serve as a compressed, privacy-preserving representation that retains essential clustering information. When aggregated at the server level, they form a comprehensive global graph that enables effective clustering without requiring access to individual data points. The iterative feature learning in SPP-FGC+ further enhances this by allowing the model to refine representations based on the aggregated graph structure, leading to improved clustering performance on complex datasets.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Clients (build private graphs) -> Server (aggregate graphs) -> Clustering Algorithm (perform analysis)

**Critical Path:** Local data preprocessing → Private graph construction → Graph transmission → Global graph aggregation → Clustering execution

**Design Tradeoffs:** The framework trades computational overhead of graph construction and transmission against privacy preservation and clustering performance. One-shot mode prioritizes efficiency while SPP-FGC+ sacrifices speed for accuracy on complex datasets.

**Failure Signatures:** Poor clustering results may indicate insufficient graph connectivity, imbalanced graph contributions from clients, or inadequate graph construction methods. Privacy breaches could occur if graph construction reveals sensitive patterns in the data.

**3 First Experiments to Run:**
1. Validate baseline clustering performance on a single client without federation to establish reference metrics
2. Test graph aggregation with synthetic data where ground truth clusters are known to verify the aggregation process
3. Evaluate privacy leakage by attempting to reconstruct original data from transmitted private graphs

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

The paper acknowledges several open questions regarding the practical deployment of federated clustering frameworks. These include the need for more extensive evaluation on real-world federated datasets beyond controlled environments, the challenge of handling massive numbers of clients in truly large-scale federated systems, and the development of more efficient graph construction and aggregation methods that reduce computational overhead. Additionally, the paper notes the importance of exploring how the framework can be extended to support supervised or semi-supervised learning scenarios beyond the current unsupervised clustering focus.

## Limitations

- The framework focuses exclusively on unsupervised clustering tasks and lacks support for supervised or semi-supervised learning scenarios
- Evaluation primarily relies on synthetic or controlled datasets rather than real-world federated environments with network heterogeneity and device failures
- Computational overhead of maintaining and transmitting private structural graphs may become prohibitive in resource-constrained federated settings with thousands of participants

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The theoretical framework for private graph aggregation and the basic clustering methodology are sound and well-explained
- **Medium Confidence**: The claimed 10% NMI improvement over baselines requires validation on more diverse, real-world federated datasets
- **Low Confidence**: The scalability claims to large-scale federated environments lack rigorous stress testing with hundreds of clients and heterogeneous network conditions
