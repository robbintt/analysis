# FrameShield: Adversarially Robust Video Anomaly Detection

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.21532
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.21532
- Authors: Mojtaba Nafez; Mobina Poulaei; Nikan Vasei; Bardia Soltani Moakhar; Mohammad Sabokpour; MohammadHossein Rohban
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Achieves 71.0% average AUROC improvement in adversarial robustness across five benchmarks

## Executive Summary
FrameShield addresses the critical vulnerability of weakly supervised video anomaly detection (WSVAD) models to adversarial attacks. The key insight is that existing methods using video-level labels and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) produce weak adversarial perturbations during training, leaving models exposed during inference. FrameShield introduces a two-phase approach that first generates pseudo-labels for anomaly videos using PromptMIL, then employs adversarial training with these pseudo-labels and synthetically generated anomalies created through Spatiotemporal Region Distortion (SRD).

The method demonstrates substantial improvements in adversarial robustness while maintaining competitive performance on standard setups. By applying severe augmentations to localized regions in normal videos while preserving temporal consistency, SRD provides precise frame-level annotations that traditional MIL-based approaches lack. This dual approach of pseudo-label generation and synthetic anomaly creation addresses the fundamental weakness of WSVAD models in handling adversarial examples.

## Method Summary
FrameShield implements a two-phase training pipeline to enhance the robustness of WSVAD models. The first phase trains a standard PromptMIL model to generate pseudo-labels for anomaly videos. The second phase employs adversarial training using these pseudo-labels combined with anomalies synthesized through Spatiotemporal Region Distortion (SRD). SRD applies severe augmentations to localized regions in normal videos while maintaining temporal consistency, creating precise frame-level annotations. This approach overcomes the weakness of traditional MIL-based methods that rely on video-level labels and produce weak adversarial perturbations during training.

## Key Results
- Achieves 71.0% average AUROC improvement in adversarial robustness across UCSD Ped2, ShanghaiTech, TAD, UCF Crime, and MSAD benchmarks
- Outperforms state-of-the-art methods in defending against adversarial attacks while maintaining competitive standard performance
- Demonstrates effectiveness across diverse anomaly detection scenarios and datasets

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
FrameShield works by addressing the fundamental weakness in WSVAD models: their reliance on video-level labels and MIL-based losses produces weak adversarial perturbations during training. By generating precise frame-level pseudo-labels through PromptMIL and combining them with synthetically generated anomalies via SRD, the model receives stronger supervision signals during adversarial training. This dual approach ensures that the model learns to distinguish anomalies more effectively at the frame level, rather than relying on weak video-level supervision that fails to capture fine-grained temporal patterns.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Normal Videos -> SRD Generator -> Synthetic Anomalies + Pseudo-labels -> Adversarial Training -> Robust WSVAD Model

**Critical Path:** The most critical path is the adversarial training phase where synthetic anomalies from SRD and pseudo-labels from PromptMIL converge. This path determines the model's ability to learn robust representations that can withstand adversarial attacks.

**Design Tradeoffs:** The two-phase approach introduces computational overhead but provides superior robustness compared to single-phase methods. The reliance on synthetic anomalies through SRD may not perfectly match real-world anomalies, but this tradeoff is justified by the significant robustness gains.

**Failure Signatures:** The model may fail when pseudo-labels generated by PromptMIL are inaccurate, or when SRD-generated anomalies don't adequately represent the diversity of real-world anomalies. Additionally, the computational cost of the two-phase pipeline may limit deployment in resource-constrained environments.

## Limitations
- The two-phase training pipeline may be computationally expensive compared to single-phase approaches
- The approach assumes pseudo-labels from PromptMIL are sufficiently accurate across all datasets and anomaly types
- SRD's effectiveness in creating realistic anomalies for diverse real-world scenarios remains unverified
