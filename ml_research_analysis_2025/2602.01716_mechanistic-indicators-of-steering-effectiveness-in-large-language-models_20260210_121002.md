# Mechanistic Indicators of Steering Effectiveness in Large Language Models

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2602.01716
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.01716
- **Reference count:** 17
- **Primary result:** Internal model signals (NBF and KL divergence) can predict activation-based steering quality with R²=0.47-0.54

## Executive Summary
This paper investigates whether internal signals from large language models can predict the effectiveness of activation-based steering techniques. The authors propose two information-theoretic measures - Normalized Branching Factor (NBF) derived from entropy and KL divergence between steered and targeted concept distributions - as mechanistic indicators of steering success. They validate these indicators through correlation with human-evaluated steering quality scores across multiple model sizes and steering techniques.

## Method Summary
The study develops two mechanistic indicators: NBF (Normalized Branching Factor) calculated from entropy across token predictions, and KL divergence between steered and target concept distributions. These indicators are evaluated across 13 models ranging from 125M to 7B parameters using both additive steering and a novel rotational steering baseline. Steering effectiveness is measured through LLM-based annotations validated via inter-annotator agreement (ICC=0.78), then correlated with internal signal metrics to assess predictive power.

## Key Results
- Internal signals predict steering quality scores with R²=0.47-0.54 across different model sizes
- Effective steering shows structured entropy patterns and coherent KL divergence dynamics
- Rotational steering baseline outperforms additive steering, highlighting the importance of steering technique selection
- NBF and KL divergence metrics show consistent correlations across 13 model sizes from 125M to 7B parameters

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The proposed indicators work by quantifying how steering interventions affect the internal representation dynamics of LLMs. NBF captures the information-theoretic complexity of token prediction distributions, which correlates with how well steering aligns model behavior with desired concepts. KL divergence measures the statistical distance between steered and target distributions, providing a direct metric of steering alignment. These measures capture the mechanistic effects of steering at both layer-wise and token-level granularity, enabling prediction of steering effectiveness without requiring external evaluation.

**Design Tradeoffs:**

- Choice between additive vs rotational steering techniques based on preservation of internal dynamics
- LLM-based vs human evaluation of steering quality balancing scalability with reliability

**Failure Signatures:**
- High entropy without corresponding KL divergence may indicate ineffective steering
- Mismatched layer-wise patterns suggest steering instability
- Low ICC values indicate unreliable quality assessments

## Limitations
- LLM-based annotations may introduce subjectivity despite reasonable ICC=0.78 agreement
- Primary validation conducted on smaller models (7B parameters), raising scalability concerns
- Binary classification of steering success may oversimplify nuanced effectiveness measures

## Confidence

**High confidence:** Correlation between NBF and steering quality scores (R²=0.54, p<0.001)
**Medium confidence:** Generalizability across steering techniques and model architectures
**Medium confidence:** Practical utility for real-world steering applications
