# Using Kolmogorov-Smirnov Distance for Measuring Distribution Shift in Machine Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.15996
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.15996
- Authors: Ozan K. Tonguz; Federico Taschin
- Reference count: 26
- Primary result: KS distance predicts DRL performance degradation in traffic signal control

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the critical challenge of measuring distribution shift in machine learning, specifically for traffic signal control using reinforcement learning. The authors propose using the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) distance as a statistical measure to quantify the discrepancy between training and test traffic distributions, defined as the normalized distribution of vehicles across 8 NEMA phases at intersections. Through comprehensive experiments, they demonstrate that even small KS distances (e.g., 0.02) can lead to significant performance degradation in DRL agents, with a 50% increase in extended travel time. The method provides a valuable statistical tool for monitoring distribution shift in real-time and predicting performance degradation in AI-based traffic systems.

## Method Summary
The paper employs the Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance as a statistical metric to measure distribution shift between training and test data distributions. The method calculates the maximum absolute difference between the cumulative distribution functions of vehicle distributions across 8 NEMA phases at intersections. This KS distance is then correlated with DRL agent performance metrics including normalized throughput, extended travel time, and queue length. The approach involves computing the distribution of vehicles across different phases during training, then comparing this with the distribution observed during testing to quantify the shift. The normalized KS distance provides a scale-invariant measure that can be used to predict performance degradation without requiring access to ground truth labels.

## Key Results
- Small KS distances (0.02) can cause 50% increase in extended travel time
- 3.7% loss in normalized throughput observed with KS distance increase of 0.02
- 42% increase in travel time for the same KS distance change
- Linear relationship between KS distance and performance degradation demonstrated

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance effectively captures distributional differences by measuring the maximum vertical distance between cumulative distribution functions. In traffic signal control, this translates to quantifying how much the traffic pattern distribution has shifted from the training scenario. Since DRL agents learn policies based on the statistical properties of training data distributions, any shift in these distributions directly impacts the agent's ability to make optimal decisions. The KS metric provides a non-parametric, distribution-free measure that doesn't assume any specific distribution shape, making it particularly suitable for the complex, multimodal distributions typically found in traffic data.

## Foundational Learning
- Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test: A non-parametric statistical test that compares two cumulative distribution functions. Why needed: Provides the theoretical foundation for measuring distribution differences without assuming specific distribution shapes. Quick check: Verify that the test statistic correctly identifies known distribution differences in controlled experiments.
- Reinforcement Learning in Traffic Control: DRL agents learn optimal traffic signal timing policies through interaction with traffic environments. Why needed: Understanding how DRL agents respond to environmental changes is crucial for interpreting the impact of distribution shift. Quick check: Confirm that performance degradation correlates with distribution changes across different DRL algorithms.
- NEMA Phasing: Standardized traffic signal phase configurations (8 phases) used to organize traffic movements. Why needed: Provides the framework for defining traffic distributions that can be measured and compared. Quick check: Ensure phase-based distribution captures the essential characteristics of traffic flow patterns.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Traffic Data -> Phase Distribution Extraction -> KS Distance Calculation -> Performance Prediction -> DRL Agent Monitoring

Critical Path: The core workflow involves extracting vehicle distributions across NEMA phases from raw traffic data, computing the KS distance between training and test distributions, and using this distance to predict DRL performance degradation. The critical path requires accurate phase distribution extraction and reliable KS distance computation as foundational steps.

Design Tradeoffs: The method prioritizes simplicity and interpretability over capturing all possible traffic dynamics. By focusing solely on phase-based distributions, it sacrifices some granularity (e.g., vehicle types, turning movements) for computational efficiency and ease of real-time monitoring. This tradeoff enables scalable deployment but may miss subtle distribution shifts that don't manifest in phase counts.

Failure Signatures: The method may fail when distribution shifts occur in ways that don't significantly affect phase distributions (e.g., changes in vehicle mix without changing phase occupancy). It also assumes that the training distribution adequately represents the space of possible traffic patterns, which may not hold for highly dynamic or seasonal traffic conditions.

## Limitations
- Experimental scope limited to single intersection with 8 NEMA phases, raising scalability concerns
- Assumes vehicle distribution across phases fully captures traffic dynamics, potentially missing important factors
- Linear relationship between KS distance and performance degradation may not generalize to all DRL applications
