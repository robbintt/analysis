# Defining Energy Indicators for Impact Identification on Aerospace Composites: A Physics-Informed Machine Learning Perspective

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.01592
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.01592
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Physics-informed feature extraction reduces prediction errors by 3x compared to conventional approaches

## Executive Summary
This study addresses the challenge of impact energy identification in aerospace composites, where internal damage from low-velocity impacts is difficult to detect. The core method involves embedding domain knowledge into machine learning through physics-motivated feature extraction from time, frequency, and time-frequency domains, followed by statistical evaluation, targeted feature selection, and noise robustness testing. The optimized feature set is then used to train a fully connected neural network. Results show that this approach reduces prediction errors by a factor of three compared to conventional time-series techniques and purely data-driven models, while maintaining interpretability and robustness under sparse, unbalanced, and noisy data conditions typical of structural health monitoring applications.

## Method Summary
The methodology combines physics-informed feature extraction with machine learning for impact energy identification. The process involves extracting features from time, frequency, and time-frequency domains based on structural dynamics principles, then statistically evaluating their relevance and robustness to noise. A neural network is trained using the optimized feature set, achieving significant improvements in prediction accuracy over traditional time-series approaches while maintaining interpretability and performance under challenging data conditions.

## Key Results
- Prediction errors reduced by factor of three compared to conventional time-series techniques
- Physics-informed approach maintains accuracy under sparse, unbalanced, and noisy data conditions
- Fully connected neural network trained on optimized features outperforms purely data-driven models

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The physics-informed approach works by incorporating domain knowledge into the feature extraction process, ensuring that selected features are mechanistically linked to impact-induced damage mechanisms. This targeted extraction from multiple domains (time, frequency, time-frequency) captures the full spectrum of structural response signatures, while statistical evaluation and noise testing ensure robustness. The approach bridges the gap between purely physics-based models that may be too complex and purely data-driven models that lack interpretability and struggle with limited data.

## Foundational Learning
- Feature extraction from multiple domains: Why needed - captures different aspects of structural response; Quick check - verify time, frequency, and time-frequency features are all included
- Statistical evaluation of feature relevance: Why needed - eliminates redundant or non-informative features; Quick check - correlation matrix shows minimal redundancy among selected features
- Noise robustness testing: Why needed - ensures performance under real-world conditions; Quick check - performance metrics stable across varying noise levels
- Physics-motivated feature selection: Why needed - maintains interpretability while improving accuracy; Quick check - selected features can be mapped to known damage mechanisms
- Neural network training on optimized features: Why needed - leverages machine learning for prediction while avoiding feature engineering complexity; Quick check - prediction accuracy exceeds baseline methods

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Impact data → Time/Frequency/Time-Frequency feature extraction → Statistical evaluation → Noise testing → Feature selection → Neural network training → Energy prediction

Critical path: Feature extraction → Statistical evaluation → Neural network training

Design tradeoffs: Physics-informed features provide interpretability but require domain expertise; purely data-driven approaches need more data but require less domain knowledge

Failure signatures: Poor feature selection leads to overfitting; inadequate noise testing results in poor real-world performance; insufficient statistical evaluation creates redundant features

First experiments:
1. Test feature extraction on synthetic impact data with known damage patterns
2. Compare statistical evaluation methods (correlation, mutual information, PCA) for feature selection
3. Validate noise robustness using controlled noise injection at multiple levels

## Limitations
- Results derived from single experimental setup with 4-16 J impact energy range
- Limited testing across different composite layups and impact scenarios
- No validation under varying environmental conditions or cumulative damage effects

## Confidence

High confidence: The methodology of combining physics-informed feature extraction with statistical evaluation is sound and well-documented

Medium confidence: The reported performance improvements are likely valid for the specific test conditions and materials studied

Low confidence: Generalizability to different composite configurations, impact scenarios, and real-world structural health monitoring conditions

## Next Checks

1. Test the feature selection and prediction accuracy across multiple composite layup configurations (0/90, +45/-45, quasi-isotropic) to assess transferability

2. Evaluate performance under realistic operational conditions including temperature variations, moisture exposure, and cumulative damage effects

3. Validate the approach using data from different impactor geometries and velocities beyond the 4-16 J range to test robustness across impact scenarios
