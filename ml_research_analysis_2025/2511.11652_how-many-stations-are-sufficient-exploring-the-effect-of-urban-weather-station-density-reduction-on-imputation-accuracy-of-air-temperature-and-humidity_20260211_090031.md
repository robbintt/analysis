# How many stations are sufficient? Exploring the effect of urban weather station density reduction on imputation accuracy of air temperature and humidity

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.11652
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.11652
- Authors: Marvin Plein; Carsten F. Dormann; Andreas Christen
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Reducing from 42 to 4 stations increased prediction RMSEs by only 20% for temperature and 16% for humidity

## Executive Summary
This study investigated optimal station density for urban weather networks by systematically reducing a 42-station network in Freiburg, Germany. Using a step-wise removal algorithm combined with machine learning models, the authors evaluated how network thinning affects temperature and humidity prediction accuracy across the city. They found that substantial reductions are possible while maintaining acceptable accuracy levels, with stations at urban-rural edges proving most valuable for city-wide predictions.

The research demonstrates that cost-effective network management is feasible without sacrificing reliable urban climate monitoring. The approach provides a framework for optimizing existing weather station networks, potentially enabling resource reallocation while maintaining data quality for urban climate studies and applications.

## Method Summary
The authors developed a systematic approach to evaluate weather station network sufficiency by starting with a dense 42-station urban network in Freiburg, Germany. They implemented a step-wise station removal algorithm that sequentially eliminated stations while training machine learning models to predict temperature and humidity across all locations. The models were trained on the reduced station subsets and evaluated against the full network data. Various reduction scenarios were tested, from minimal thinning to extreme cases with only 4 stations remaining. The performance was measured using RMSE metrics, and comparisons were made against simpler interpolation methods and numerical weather prediction models to contextualize the results.

## Key Results
- Network reduction from 42 to 4 stations increased temperature prediction RMSE by only 20%
- Humidity prediction RMSE increased by 16% under the same reduction scenario
- Stations at urban-rural edges were most valuable for city-wide temperature and humidity predictions
- Thinned networks outperformed simpler interpolation methods (IDW) and numerical weather prediction models (WRF)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The effectiveness of network reduction stems from the spatial correlation structure of urban climate variables. Temperature and humidity exhibit strong spatial autocorrelation within cities, meaning nearby stations provide redundant information. The machine learning models can effectively interpolate between remaining stations when the network maintains adequate spatial coverage, particularly at transition zones between urban and rural areas where gradients are steepest.

## Limitations
- Results may not generalize to cities with different urban morphologies, topographies, or building densities
- Study focused only on temperature and humidity, not other meteorological variables
- Machine learning models were trained and validated on the same urban dataset, potentially leading to overfitting

## Confidence
- Network reduction feasibility (High): Consistent RMSE increases across multiple reduction scenarios provide strong evidence
- Station importance patterns (Medium): Patterns emerged but may shift under different urban configurations
- Model superiority (Medium): Limited comparison to specific alternatives rather than exhaustive evaluation
