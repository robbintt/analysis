## Executive Summary
LIR is a new workshop on late interaction and multi-vector retrieval, to be held at ECIR 2026. It aims to bring together researchers and practitioners to discuss challenges and future directions for multi-vector retrieval methods, which represent both queries and documents as bags of token-level vectors and compute relevance using the maxsim operator. These methods, pioneered by ColBERT, have shown strong performance especially in out-of-domain settings, and are gaining adoption in industry for LLM retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipelines. However, they face significant challenges around efficiency, usability, and integration into real-world systems. The workshop will focus on early research, applied results, and negative findings, with a format emphasizing discussion over traditional presentations. It seeks to foster collaboration between academic and industry communities to advance the field.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Late-interaction and multi-vector retrieval methods work by representing both queries and documents as bags of token-level vectors, then computing relevance using the maxsim operator. This approach allows for more granular matching between query and document tokens, capturing fine-grained semantic relationships that traditional single-vector methods might miss. The flexibility of this representation enables better handling of out-of-domain queries and complex semantic matching scenarios, which is particularly valuable for retrieval-augmented generation pipelines where precise retrieval directly impacts the quality of generated responses.

## Architecture Onboarding
- **Component map**: Query encoder -> Document encoder -> Vector database -> Maxsim computation -> Ranking module
- **Critical path**: Encoding phase (query + document) → Vector storage → Retrieval phase (maxsim computation) → Result ranking
- **Design tradeoffs**: Granular matching vs. computational cost; early vs. late interaction; storage efficiency vs. retrieval accuracy
- **Failure signatures**: Degraded performance on short queries; memory constraints with large document collections; precision-recall tradeoffs at scale
