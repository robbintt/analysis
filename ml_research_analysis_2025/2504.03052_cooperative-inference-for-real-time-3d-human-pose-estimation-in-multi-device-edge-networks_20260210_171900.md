# Cooperative Inference for Real-Time 3D Human Pose Estimation in Multi-Device Edge Networks

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2504.03052
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.03052
- **Authors:** Hyun-Ho Choi; Kangsoo Kim; Ki-Ho Lee; Kisong Lee
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Novel cooperative inference method for multi-device edge networks that improves 3D human pose estimation accuracy while meeting real-time constraints through optimal dual confidence thresholds

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of achieving accurate and real-time 3D human pose estimation in resource-constrained and dynamic environments, where high computational complexity limits performance. The authors propose a novel cooperative inference method for multi-device edge networks, where lightweight inference models on end devices filter ambiguous images using dual confidence thresholds, and only the filtered images are offloaded to an edge server with a more powerful model for re-evaluation. This approach improves estimation accuracy under computational and communication constraints. The study formulates a joint optimization problem to derive optimal confidence thresholds and transmission times for each device, minimizing mean per-joint position error (MPJPE) while satisfying required end-to-end delay constraints. Through extensive experiments, the authors demonstrate that the proposed method significantly reduces MPJPE compared to device- and server-centric approaches, while consistently meeting delay requirements across various mobile edge computing environments.

## Method Summary
The authors propose a cooperative inference framework for multi-device edge networks that optimizes 3D human pose estimation accuracy under real-time constraints. The method employs dual confidence thresholds on lightweight models deployed on end devices to filter ambiguous images before offloading to an edge server. A joint optimization problem is formulated to determine optimal confidence thresholds and transmission times that minimize MPJPE while satisfying end-to-end delay constraints. The optimization is solved using Lagrange multiplier and bisection methods, yielding near-optimal solutions with low computational complexity. The approach is validated through extensive simulations in a Unity environment with various mobile edge computing scenarios.

## Key Results
- The proposed method reduces MPJPE by up to 40% compared to device-centric and server-centric approaches while meeting required end-to-end delay constraints
- Joint optimization of confidence thresholds and transmission times yields near-optimal performance with low computational complexity
- The method consistently meets delay requirements (within 4% of optimal) across various mobile edge computing environments with different numbers of devices and servers
- Experimental results demonstrate significant improvement in estimation accuracy under both computational and communication constraints

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method leverages the complementary strengths of lightweight and heavyweight models in a cooperative framework. By using dual confidence thresholds, devices can filter out both high-confidence (good) and low-confidence (poor) estimations, offloading only the ambiguous cases that benefit most from server-side re-evaluation. This selective offloading strategy reduces computational load on devices while ensuring critical cases receive enhanced processing. The joint optimization problem effectively balances the tradeoff between accuracy and delay by considering both inference times and transmission delays across the entire edge network, rather than optimizing components independently.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
### Open Question 1
- Question: How does the proposed cooperative inference method perform when deployed in physical real-world environments compared to the simulated Unity environment?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors state, "Future research will verify the performance of the proposed method in real-world environments," noting the current reliance on a virtual setup.
- Why unresolved: The study utilizes a virtual environment to ensure exact ground-truth data for 3D joint coordinates, which avoids the noise, lighting variations, and calibration issues present in physical camera networks.
- What evidence would resolve it: Experimental validation using physical cameras and servers, demonstrating that the MPJPE and delay constraints are maintained under real-world channel conditions and image noise.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can the joint optimization strategy be extended to support multi-person 3D pose estimation without violating real-time delay constraints?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The conclusion explicitly identifies "multi-person scenarios" as a necessary extension to validate the practical applicability of the proposed approach.
- Why unresolved: The current framework is tested on a single humanoid avatar; multi-person scenarios introduce additional computational overhead for identity association and increased data traffic, potentially breaking the end-to-end delay budget.
- What evidence would resolve it: A modified algorithm that handles multiple sets of 2D coordinates and 3D reconstructions, experimentally shown to satisfy the end-to-end delay requirement ($D_{req}$) while maintaining low MPJPE for multiple subjects.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How does heterogeneity in device processing capabilities affect the convergence and optimal solution of the joint optimization problem?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper explicitly assumes "all devices are homogeneous with the same processing speed" to simplify the end-to-end delay analysis.
- Why unresolved: Real-world edge networks often consist of heterogeneous devices with varying computational power ($v_d$) and model complexities ($N_d$), which would alter the Lagrangian function and the optimal time allocation ($\tau_i$).
- What evidence would resolve it: A modified theoretical analysis and simulation where $T_{inf}^d$ varies per device, demonstrating that the proposed algorithm still converges to thresholds that satisfy delay constraints.

## Limitations
- The method assumes reliable network connectivity and may not perform optimally under highly variable network conditions
- The current framework is tested on single-person scenarios and may not scale effectively to multi-person pose estimation
- The optimization assumes homogeneous devices, which may not reflect real-world heterogeneous edge computing environments
- The approach relies on accurate confidence scoring from lightweight models, which may be unreliable in challenging visual conditions
