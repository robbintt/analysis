# A Priest, a Rabbi, and an Atheist Walk Into an Error Bar: Religious Meditations on Uncertainty Visualization

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.08213
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.08213
- Reference count: 37
- One-line primary result: The paper critiques modern uncertainty visualization as rooted in a narrow statistical metaphysics and advocates for incorporating diverse religious and cultural epistemologies of uncertainty

## Executive Summary
This paper challenges the conventional assumptions underlying uncertainty visualization, particularly error bars, arguing that current practices reflect a recently developed statistical metaphysics that may not align with diverse audience perspectives. Through exploration of Abrahamic religious traditions, the authors examine alternative approaches to doubt, evidence, and decision-making that could inform more inclusive visualization design. The work reframes uncertainty visualization as a culturally and philosophically situated practice rather than a purely technical endeavor.

## Method Summary
The paper employs a conceptual analysis approach, drawing on examples from Abrahamic religions to explore alternative epistemologies of uncertainty. Rather than empirical studies, the authors use illustrative examples from religious texts and traditions to construct their argument about epistemic diversity in visualization design.

## Key Results
- Current uncertainty visualization practices are rooted in a narrow, recently developed statistical metaphysics
- Different religious traditions offer alternative approaches to uncertainty, evidence, and decision-making
- Visualization design should account for epistemic diversity rather than defaulting to conventional statistical conventions

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The paper works by challenging the implicit metaphysical assumptions underlying visualization design, revealing how technical choices encode specific epistemological positions. By drawing parallels between religious and statistical approaches to uncertainty, it demonstrates how visualization conventions reflect particular cultural and philosophical commitments rather than universal principles.

## Limitations
- Arguments rely heavily on illustrative examples rather than systematic cross-cultural studies
- Connection between philosophical principles and concrete design recommendations remains speculative
- Limited discussion of how different visualization contexts might interact with epistemological differences
- Focuses primarily on Abrahamic traditions, leaving questions about other religious and cultural perspectives
