# Battling the Non-stationarity in Time Series Forecasting via Test-time Adaptation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2501.04970
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.04970
- Authors: HyunGi Kim; Siwon Kim; Jisoo Mok; Sungroh Yoon
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: Introduces TAFAS, a test-time adaptation framework that reduces forecasting errors by up to 45% on Chronos foundation model

## Executive Summary
This study addresses the critical challenge of non-stationarity in time series forecasting by introducing TAFAS (Test-time Adaptation Framework for Time Series). The framework tackles the distribution shift problem that occurs when models trained on historical data face real-world data with evolving patterns. TAFAS achieves this through a novel combination of periodicity-aware adaptation scheduling and a model-agnostic gated calibration module, demonstrating consistent performance improvements across seven benchmark datasets and six state-of-the-art TSF architectures.

The research specifically focuses on the test-time adaptation scenario where ground truth data becomes partially available during inference, enabling proactive model adaptation. This approach is particularly valuable for long-term forecasting tasks where distribution shifts accumulate over time. The framework maintains compatibility with existing normalization-based pre-training approaches while effectively handling both local and global distribution shifts that challenge conventional forecasting methods.

## Method Summary
TAFAS introduces a two-component test-time adaptation framework for time series forecasting. The first component, periodicity-aware adaptation scheduling (PAAS), determines the optimal length of partially-observed ground truth data to use for model adaptation, leveraging periodic patterns in the data to trigger proactive adaptation. The second component, the gated calibration module (GCM), handles distribution shifts through temporal calibration and gating mechanisms that can operate on both local and global shifts. The framework is designed to be model-agnostic, allowing it to work with various state-of-the-art TSF architectures while maintaining compatibility with existing normalization-based pre-training approaches.

## Key Results
- TAFAS consistently reduces forecasting errors across seven benchmark datasets and six state-of-the-art TSF architectures
- Achieves up to 45% improvement in test MSE on Chronos foundation model
- Demonstrates particular effectiveness in long-term forecasting scenarios where distribution shifts accumulate over time
- Maintains compatibility with existing normalization-based pre-training approaches

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework's effectiveness stems from its ability to leverage partially-observed ground truth data during inference for proactive adaptation. By detecting periodic patterns through PAAS, the system identifies optimal moments for adaptation, while the GCM component calibrates the model's predictions through temporal adjustments and gating mechanisms. This dual approach addresses both the timing of adaptation and the method of calibration, allowing the model to respond dynamically to evolving data distributions without requiring complete retraining.

## Foundational Learning
**Time Series Non-stationarity**: Real-world time series data often exhibits changing statistical properties over time, requiring models to adapt to evolving patterns. This is needed because static models trained on historical data degrade in performance when faced with distribution shifts. Quick check: Verify stationarity tests (e.g., Augmented Dickey-Fuller) show non-stationary behavior in benchmark datasets.

**Test-time Adaptation**: The process of adapting models during inference using available ground truth data, as opposed to traditional training-time adaptation. This is crucial for scenarios where models must maintain performance on streaming data. Quick check: Confirm that partially-observed ground truth is available during inference in the experimental setup.

**Distribution Shifts**: Changes in the input data distribution between training and testing phases, which can significantly degrade model performance. Understanding this concept is essential for developing robust forecasting models. Quick check: Analyze feature distributions across time to identify shifts in the datasets used.

**Periodic Pattern Detection**: The ability to identify recurring patterns in time series data, which can be leveraged for scheduling model adaptations. This is fundamental to the PAAS component of TAFAS. Quick check: Apply Fourier analysis or autocorrelation to verify periodic patterns exist in the datasets.

**Gating Mechanisms in Neural Networks**: Techniques that learn when to apply specific transformations or calibrations, allowing models to selectively respond to different types of distribution shifts. This is central to the GCM component. Quick check: Examine the learned gate values to understand when and how the model adapts.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Input Time Series -> PAAS (Periodicity Detection) -> GCM (Gated Calibration) -> Forecast Output

Critical Path: The framework processes incoming time series data through PAAS to determine adaptation timing, then applies GCM calibration using partially-observed ground truth to generate forecasts. The PAAS component identifies periodic patterns and determines when to trigger adaptation, while GCM performs the actual calibration through temporal adjustments and gating mechanisms.

Design Tradeoffs: The framework balances adaptation frequency (controlled by PAAS) against computational overhead, while the GCM must balance calibration strength against maintaining learned patterns. The model-agnostic design sacrifices potential architecture-specific optimizations for broader applicability across different TSF models.

Failure Signatures: Performance degradation may occur when periodic patterns are weak or absent, when ground truth availability is limited or irregular, or when distribution shifts are too rapid for the adaptation scheduling to capture. The framework may also struggle with multivariate time series where inter-series dependencies are complex.

## Limitations
- Evaluation is limited to univariate time series forecasting tasks, leaving multivariate scenarios unexplored
- Generalizability to real-world industrial applications with more complex data patterns remains uncertain
- Assumes detectable periodic patterns through PAAS, which may not hold for all time series data types
- Computational overhead introduced by GCM during inference could impact deployment efficiency in resource-constrained environments
