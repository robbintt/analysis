# XXLTraffic: Expanding and Extremely Long Traffic forecasting beyond test adaptation

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2406.12693
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.12693
- **Reference count**: 40
- **Primary result**: Introduces XXLTraffic, the largest and longest-spanning public traffic dataset spanning over 23 years of sensor data from Los Angeles (PeMS) and New South Wales (TfNSW).

## Executive Summary
XXLTraffic is a groundbreaking traffic forecasting dataset that addresses the challenge of long-term traffic pattern evolution by providing over 23 years of sensor data from two major metropolitan areas. The dataset includes both hourly and daily aggregated data with specialized gap-based subsets (1, 1.5, and 2 year intervals) to support extremely long forecasting scenarios beyond traditional test adaptation approaches. The authors benchmarked multiple state-of-the-art models including Transformer, Mamba, and Graph Neural Network architectures, revealing significant performance degradation when predicting over long gaps. Results demonstrate that models struggle with domain shifts over time, with MICN surprisingly outperforming others in gap settings due to its data-centric approach.

## Method Summary
The XXLTraffic dataset was constructed by aggregating traffic sensor data from PeMS (Los Angeles) and TfNSW (New South Wales) spanning over two decades. The data was processed into hourly and daily resolutions with three specialized gap-based subsets (1, 1.5, and 2 year intervals) to enable evaluation of extremely long-range forecasting beyond test adaptation. Multiple state-of-the-art models were benchmarked including Transformer-based architectures, Mamba variants, and Graph Neural Networks. The evaluation focused on root mean square error (RMSE) metrics across different forecasting horizons and gap intervals to assess model performance under temporal distribution shifts.

## Key Results
- Models show significant performance degradation when forecasting over long temporal gaps, with RMSE increasing substantially as gap intervals extend
- MICN model surprisingly outperforms other architectures in gap settings despite general performance decline trends
- Traditional test adaptation approaches fail to adequately address the challenges of extremely long-range traffic forecasting
- Performance differences between hourly and daily aggregated data reveal distinct challenges in temporal resolution handling

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The dataset's effectiveness stems from its unprecedented temporal coverage spanning over two decades, capturing real-world traffic pattern evolution including infrastructure changes, population growth, and temporal distribution shifts. The gap-based subsets (1, 1.5, and 2 year intervals) specifically target the challenge of domain shift over time, forcing models to learn robust representations that generalize beyond immediate temporal contexts. The combination of hourly and daily aggregations provides flexibility in addressing different forecasting needs while maintaining consistency in underlying traffic patterns. The inclusion of two geographically distinct metropolitan areas (Los Angeles and NSW) ensures that learned patterns are not overly specific to a single urban environment.

**Design tradeoffs**
The dataset prioritizes temporal coverage over spatial granularity, potentially missing local pattern variations. Hourly vs daily aggregation creates different computational and modeling challenges. The focus on RMSE metrics may overlook other important forecasting qualities like uncertainty quantification.

**Failure signatures**
Performance degradation over longer gaps indicates models struggle with temporal domain shifts. Unexpected MICN performance suggests potential overfitting to specific data characteristics or effective handling of long-term patterns. Differences between hourly and daily performance may reveal temporal resolution sensitivity.

## Limitations
- The extensive 23-year timeframe introduces uncertainty about sensor infrastructure changes that may affect data consistency and model generalization
- MICN's unexpected superior performance in gap settings lacks clear mechanistic explanation and may be dataset-specific
- The evaluation framework may not adequately capture model performance on unpredictable traffic disruptions over extended timeframes

## Confidence

**High confidence**: Dataset construction methodology and basic benchmarking framework are sound and reproducible

**Medium confidence**: General trend of performance degradation for long-range forecasting is well-established, though specific model comparisons require further validation

**Low confidence**: MICN model's superior performance in gap settings and the interpretation of its success mechanism
