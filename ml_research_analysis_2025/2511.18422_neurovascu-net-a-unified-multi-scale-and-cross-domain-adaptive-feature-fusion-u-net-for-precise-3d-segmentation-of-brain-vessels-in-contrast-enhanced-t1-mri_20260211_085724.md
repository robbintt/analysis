# NeuroVascU-Net: A Unified Multi-Scale and Cross-Domain Adaptive Feature Fusion U-Net for Precise 3D Segmentation of Brain Vessels in Contrast-Enhanced T1 MRI

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.18422
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.18422
- Authors: Mohammad Jafari Vayeghan; Niloufar Delfan; Mehdi Tale Masouleh; Mansour Parvaresh Rizi; Behzad Moshiri
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Achieved Dice Similarity Coefficient of 0.8609 for 3D brain vessel segmentation from contrast-enhanced T1 MRI

## Executive Summary
NeuroVascU-Net is a deep learning architecture designed for precise 3D segmentation of cerebrovascular structures from T1-weighted contrast-enhanced MRI in neuro-oncology patients. The method combines two specialized modules - Multi-Scale Contextual Feature Fusion (MSC2F) and Cross-Domain Adaptive Feature Fusion (CDA2F) - to capture both local and global vascular information while integrating domain-specific features. Trained on 137 patients with expert annotations, the model demonstrates high accuracy in segmenting both major and fine vascular structures while maintaining computational efficiency with only 12.4 million parameters.

## Method Summary
The NeuroVascU-Net architecture extends the traditional U-Net framework with two specialized modules: MSC2F captures contextual information at multiple scales to improve the detection of both major and fine vascular structures, while CDA2F adaptively integrates features from different domains to enhance segmentation accuracy. The model processes 3D MRI volumes using a multi-level encoder-decoder structure with skip connections, implementing attention mechanisms to focus on vessel-specific features. The training dataset consisted of 137 patients with expert-annotated vessel segmentations, and the model was evaluated using standard segmentation metrics including Dice Similarity Coefficient and precision.

## Key Results
- Achieved Dice Similarity Coefficient of 0.8609 for vessel segmentation
- Obtained precision of 0.8841 for detecting vascular structures
- Demonstrated computational efficiency with only 12.4 million parameters
- Successfully segmented both major and fine vascular structures in neuro-oncology patients

## Foundational Learning
- **3D Convolutional Neural Networks**: Used for volumetric feature extraction from MRI data; needed because vascular structures are inherently 3D and require spatial context across all dimensions
- **Multi-scale feature fusion**: Combines features from different receptive fields; needed to capture both large vessel pathways and fine peripheral vasculature
- **Attention mechanisms**: Focuses model on vessel-specific features; needed to suppress non-vascular tissue and enhance segmentation accuracy
- **Cross-domain feature adaptation**: Integrates information from different feature spaces; needed to handle the heterogeneous appearance of vessels in contrast-enhanced MRI
- **U-Net architecture**: Provides symmetric encoder-decoder structure with skip connections; needed for precise localization and reconstruction of segmented vessels
- **Dice coefficient optimization**: Loss function tailored for segmentation tasks; needed to handle class imbalance between vessel and non-vessel regions

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:**
Input MRI -> Encoder (MSC2F) -> Bottleneck (CDA2F) -> Decoder -> Output Segmentation

**Critical Path:**
Contrast-enhanced T1 MRI volumes → Multi-scale feature extraction → Cross-domain adaptive fusion → Upsampling and reconstruction → Vessel segmentation

**Design Tradeoffs:**
- Parameter efficiency vs. transformer-based models (12.4M vs higher parameter counts)
- Single-domain training vs. multi-institutional generalization
- Computational speed vs. segmentation accuracy
- Fixed architecture vs. adaptive feature fusion capabilities

**Failure Signatures:**
- Incomplete segmentation of fine vessels due to partial volume effects
- Over-segmentation in regions with similar intensity to vessels
- Sensitivity to contrast agent distribution variations
- Performance degradation with different scanner protocols

**Three First Experiments:**
1. Evaluate model performance on a held-out test set with varying vessel sizes
2. Compare MSC2F and CDA2F module contributions through ablation studies
3. Test inference speed on different hardware configurations

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: Can NeuroVascU-Net maintain high segmentation accuracy when applied to multi-institutional datasets with diverse scanner protocols and patient demographics?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors state the model was validated on a single-center dataset and suggest future studies should evaluate performance across multi-institutional datasets to ensure broader applicability.
- Why unresolved: Validation on data from a single hospital (Rasoul Akram Hospital) introduces potential biases related to specific MRI protocols and scanner characteristics that may not generalize globally.
- What evidence would resolve it: Successful validation on external datasets from different clinical sites showing comparable Dice coefficients and precision metrics.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can the integration of super-resolution techniques or novel loss functions improve the segmentation of sub-voxel scale vessels obscured by partial volume effects?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The discussion identifies the segmentation of sub-voxel scale vessels as an "unresolved challenge" due to MRI partial volume effects, suggesting super-resolution as a potential solution.
- Why unresolved: The current resolution limits the model's ability to detect vessels smaller than the voxel size, a constraint not fully addressed by the current architectural design.
- What evidence would resolve it: Modified implementations utilizing super-resolution that demonstrate statistically significant improvements in detecting fine, low-signal vascular structures.

### Open Question 3
- Question: Can model pruning or quantization effectively reduce the high inference time to enable near real-time application in intraoperative settings?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper notes that while the model is efficient in parameters, "further optimization techniques such as model pruning or quantization could be explored" for near real-time performance.
- Why unresolved: The reported inference time (approx. 64 minutes per volume) is prohibitively long for real-time surgical feedback, necessitating significant speed optimizations.
- What evidence would resolve it: A pruned or quantized version of the model that performs inference in seconds rather than minutes without a substantial drop in Dice score.

## Limitations
- Performance evaluated on single-center dataset, limiting generalizability to other institutions and scanner types
- Model performance on non-contrast-enhanced T1 MRI or other imaging modalities remains untested
- Clinical utility in actual neurosurgical planning workflows not demonstrated through prospective validation
