# Translating the Grievance Dictionary: a psychometric evaluation of Dutch, German, and Italian versions

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2505.07495
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.07495
- **Reference count**: 0
- **Primary result**: Dutch and German translations of the Grievance Dictionary show psychometric properties comparable to the original English version, while the Italian version requires further refinement

## Executive Summary
This study evaluates the psychometric properties of translated versions of the Grievance Dictionary into Dutch, German, and Italian. The original English dictionary measures psychological constructs related to grievance, violence, and radical action through linguistic patterns. Using automated translation followed by human annotation, the research team assessed reliability and validity across three European languages. While Dutch and German versions demonstrated reliability scores comparable to the English original, the Italian translation showed concerningly low reliability for several key categories. The study provides important insights into cross-cultural adaptation of psycholinguistic tools and identifies areas requiring further development.

## Method Summary
The research employed an automated translation approach followed by human annotation to translate the Grievance Dictionary from English into Dutch, German, and Italian. Translation was performed using Google Translate's API, then refined by native-speaking annotators who verified category assignments and made linguistic adjustments. Psychometric evaluation included computing Cronbach's alpha for reliability assessment and Gwet's AC1 for inter-rater agreement. The translated dictionaries were validated against LIWC (Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count) to establish convergent validity. Testing was conducted on a corpus of manifestos to evaluate practical application across languages.

## Key Results
- Dutch and German translations showed reliability comparable to the original English version (Dutch violence: α=0.43; German violence: α=0.24)
- Italian version exhibited critically low reliability for several categories (frustration: α=0.01; loneliness: α=0.00)
- Inter-rater agreement was high across all languages (Gwet's AC1: 0.63–1.00)
- LIWC correlations were generally small but psychologically meaningful
- Dutch and German versions are deemed suitable for research applications

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The translation approach leverages automated tools for efficiency while incorporating human expertise to address linguistic and cultural nuances. The combination of automated translation and human annotation allows for rapid adaptation of the dictionary across multiple languages while maintaining semantic accuracy. The psychometric evaluation framework, including reliability assessment and convergent validity testing with LIWC, provides robust validation of the translated tools' measurement properties.

## Limitations
- Automated translation followed by human annotation introduces potential variability in translation quality
- Italian version shows critically low reliability for several categories (α<0.10), suggesting fundamental issues with translation approach
- Testing limited to manifesto corpus, restricting generalizability to other text types
- Moderate LIWC correlations (small magnitude) raise questions about unique value added by translated versions

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: Methodological approach is sound and clearly described; inter-rater agreement was strong (Gwet's AC1: 0.63-1.00)
- **Medium confidence**: Reliability findings for Dutch and German translations; correlation patterns with LIWC
- **Low confidence**: Reliability estimates for Italian version; generalizability to other text types beyond manifestos
