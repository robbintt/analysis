# AI on the Pulse: Real-Time Health Anomaly Detection with Wearable and Ambient Intelligence

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2508.03436
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.03436
- **Authors**: Davide Gabrielli; Bardh Prenkaj; Paola Velardi; Stefano Faralli
- **Reference count**: 34
- **Primary result**: Real-time anomaly detection system fusing wearable and ambient sensor data with ~22% F1 score improvement over state-of-the-art methods

## Executive Summary
AI on the Pulse introduces a real-time anomaly detection system that continuously monitors patients using a fusion of wearable and ambient sensor data. The system employs UniTS, a universal time-series model, to autonomously learn individual baselines and detect deviations without requiring labeled data. Fine-tuned UniTS incorporates contextual features and selective masking for improved anomaly detection. Deployed in @HOME, the system continuously monitors 6 patients, achieving 93.75% true positive accuracy in detecting health anomalies. LLM-generated explanations enhance interpretability for clinicians by translating anomaly scores into clinically meaningful insights.

## Method Summary
The system combines wearable devices and ambient sensors to collect physiological and environmental data from patients. UniTS, a universal time-series model, processes this multimodal data to learn individual baselines and detect anomalies. The model is fine-tuned with contextual features and selective masking mechanisms to improve detection accuracy. LLM integration provides natural language explanations for detected anomalies, enhancing clinical interpretability. The system operates in real-time, continuously monitoring patients without requiring labeled training data, making it suitable for personalized health monitoring.

## Key Results
- Achieves ~22% improvement in F1 score compared to 12 state-of-the-art methods across four datasets
- Detects anomalies with 93.75% true positive accuracy in @HOME deployment with 6 patients
- Demonstrates robust performance on both medical-grade devices and consumer wearables
- Provides clinically meaningful explanations through LLM integration

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The system's effectiveness stems from its universal time-series modeling approach that learns individual patient baselines without labeled data. UniTS processes multimodal sensor inputs, capturing complex temporal patterns in physiological signals. Selective masking allows the model to focus on relevant features during anomaly detection, while contextual information improves accuracy. The real-time processing capability enables continuous monitoring, and the LLM component translates technical anomaly scores into interpretable clinical insights, bridging the gap between machine detection and human decision-making.

## Architecture Onboarding

### Component Map
Data Collection -> UniTS Processing -> Anomaly Detection -> LLM Explanation -> Clinical Interface

### Critical Path
The critical path flows from real-time data collection through UniTS processing to anomaly detection, with LLM explanations serving as the final output for clinical decision-making. Any delay or failure in data collection or UniTS processing directly impacts the system's ability to provide timely alerts.

### Design Tradeoffs
The system prioritizes real-time processing over batch analysis, accepting potential computational overhead for immediate detection. Using consumer wearables alongside medical devices trades some accuracy for broader accessibility. The absence of labeled training data simplifies deployment but may limit detection of rare anomalies.

### Failure Signatures
Common failure modes include sensor data dropout, model drift due to changing patient conditions, and LLM hallucination in explanation generation. Data quality issues from consumer devices can introduce false positives, while extreme physiological variations may exceed learned baseline thresholds.

### 3 First Experiments
1. Test UniTS performance with synthetic data containing known anomalies
2. Validate anomaly detection accuracy across different sensor combinations
3. Evaluate LLM explanation quality through clinician feedback sessions

## Limitations
- Evaluation limited to four datasets, restricting generalizability across diverse patient populations
- Potential challenges with data drift and concept drift in long-term deployments not explicitly addressed
- Focus on neurodegenerative conditions raises questions about efficacy for other disease classes
- Accuracy variations between medical-grade and consumer wearable devices may affect reliability

## Confidence

**High Confidence**: Technical architecture of UniTS and selective masking mechanisms; practical feasibility demonstrated in @HOME deployment
**Medium Confidence**: LLM-generated explanation benefits; ~22% F1 score improvement claims
**Low Confidence**: Long-term performance metrics; clinical impact on patient outcomes; privacy implications of continuous monitoring

## Next Checks
1. Conduct longitudinal study (minimum 6 months) to assess model performance degradation, data drift effects, and false alarm rate stability across diverse patient populations
2. Perform randomized controlled trial comparing clinical decision-making outcomes with and without LLM-generated explanations to validate interpretability benefits
3. Test system performance on additional disease classes beyond neurodegenerative conditions, including acute conditions and general wellness monitoring scenarios
