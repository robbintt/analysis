# Uncertainty Quantification for Visual Object Pose Estimation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.21666
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.21666
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: SLUE generates statistically rigorous pose uncertainty bounds using minimum volume ellipsoids via S-lemma relaxation

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of quantifying uncertainty in monocular object pose estimation by introducing SLUE (S-Lemma Uncertainty Ellipsoid Estimation). The method computes statistically rigorous pose uncertainty bounds by propagating high-probability keypoint uncertainty bounds through non-convex pose constraints to obtain a joint ellipsoidal uncertainty bound containing the true pose. Using a relaxation of the minimum volume bounding ellipsoid problem based on the generalized S-lemma, SLUE can be extended to a sum-of-squares hierarchy guaranteed to converge to the optimal bound. Experiments demonstrate that SLUE produces significantly tighter translation bounds (multiple orders of magnitude smaller than baselines) and competitive orientation bounds while being substantially faster than prior approaches.

## Method Summary
SLUE generates ellipsoidal uncertainty bounds around 6D pose estimates from monocular RGB images with statistical coverage guarantees. The method requires 3D CAD models with annotated keypoints, 2D keypoint detections from a heatmap CNN, and keypoint uncertainty bounds via conformal prediction. The core algorithm forms a non-convex pose uncertainty constraint set from backprojection and chirality constraints, then solves a convex Semidefinite Program using a generalized S-lemma relaxation to obtain the minimum-volume bounding ellipsoid. The joint ellipsoid is projected to separate translation and orientation bounds. The approach supports a sum-of-squares relaxation hierarchy that trades computational cost for bound tightness.

## Key Results
- Translation bound volumes are multiple orders of magnitude smaller than baseline spherical approximations
- Empirical coverage meets or exceeds the specified confidence level (1-α) across three real-world datasets
- SLUE-1 (first-order relaxation) runs in milliseconds, significantly faster than previous approaches
- SOS hierarchy converges to the minimum volume bound, with order 2 providing noticeable improvement over order 1

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
Distribution-free 2D keypoint bounds induce a high-probability feasible set of 6D poses. If the pixel error of detected keypoints lies within an infinity-norm bound with probability 1-α, back-projecting these bounds through the camera model creates a non-convex "Pose Uncertainty Constraint Set" in 3D. The true pose must lie within this set, assuming the front-end detector produces keypoint bounds that cover the ground truth with the specified probability.

### Mechanism 2
Optimizing for the full ellipsoid shape via S-Lemma relaxation yields tighter bounds than fixed-shape approximations. Instead of assuming a spherical uncertainty region, SLUE formulates a convex SDP using a generalized S-Lemma to solve for the matrix H of the minimum volume ellipsoid. This allows the bound to adapt to the geometry of the uncertainty, such as elongation along the optical axis due to scale ambiguity.

### Mechanism 3
A sum-of-squares relaxation hierarchy enables a tunable trade-off between computational cost and bound tightness. The algorithm allows increasing the "relaxation order," where higher orders increase the complexity of the dual polynomials in the SDP, shrinking the ellipsoid volume toward the theoretical minimum. Finite convergence occurs at low orders, empirically observed.

## Foundational Learning

- **Conformal Prediction**: Generates input 2D keypoint bounds that hold with user-specified probability without assuming specific noise distribution. Quick check: How does the calibration set size affect the statistical tightness of the bounds?

- **Semidefinite Programming (SDP) & Log-Det Objective**: Solves the convex relaxation of the minimum volume ellipsoid problem. The log det(H) objective is the standard convex surrogate for minimizing volume. Quick check: Why is maximizing log det(H) preferred over minimizing Tr(H) for volume minimization?

- **Rotation Parameterization (Quaternions vs. Rotation Matrices)**: The paper uses quaternion formulation for computational speed (4 variables) versus rotation matrices (9 variables) in optimization. Quick check: How does the quaternion "double cover" property affect the constraint set, and how does the paper handle it?

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map**: Front-End (Keypoint Detector + Conformal Calibration) → 2D Pixel Bounds (y_i, r_i) → Constraint Builder (Forms quadratic constraints A_i, Q_j) → SLUE Core (Solves SDP for ellipsoid matrix H) → Projector (Marginalizes H to translation H_t and axis-angle H_θ bounds)

- **Critical path**: The SDP solver (SLUE Core) is the main computational bottleneck, scaling with relaxation order

- **Design tradeoffs**: Use κ=1 (fast, conservative) for real-time tracking; use κ=2 (slower, tighter) for planning. The paper prefers ℓ_∞ keypoint bounds (square boxes) over ℓ_2 (circles) for better numerical stability and speed, though both are supported

- **Failure signatures**: Scale ambiguity can cause bounds to explode along the optical axis when objects are far or few keypoints are visible. Calibration drift occurs if test data diverges from calibration data, violating exchangeability and causing coverage to drop

- **First 3 experiments**: 1) Implement κ=1 SLUE on synthetic pinhole camera setup to verify ellipsoid covers ground truth when noise is exactly at the bound. 2) Compare bound volumes of Spherical (Baseline) vs. Ellipsoidal (SLUE) on LM-O dataset to quantify tightness gain. 3) Measure runtime gap between κ=1 (ms) and κ=2 (sub-second) on target hardware to select operational mode

## Limitations
- Distribution shift sensitivity: Conformal calibration requires test distribution to match calibration; synthetic data may not perfectly represent real conditions
- High-order computational cost: SLUE-2 provides tighter bounds but SDP runtime increases from milliseconds to seconds
- Scale ambiguity: Bounds become overly conservative along optical axis when objects are far or few keypoints are visible

## Confidence

- **High**: The core claim that SLUE-1 and SLUE-2 produce valid ellipsoidal bounds with guaranteed coverage (given valid keypoint bounds) is well-supported by theoretical framework and experimental validation
- **Medium**: The "multiple orders of magnitude" improvement in translation bound volume over Spherical (Baseline) is supported but requires careful interpretation against a very conservative baseline
- **Low**: The convergence guarantees of the SOS hierarchy to the theoretical minimum are asymptotic; practical benefits of higher orders versus computational cost are not fully characterized
