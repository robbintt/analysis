# SMAB: MAB based word Sensitivity Estimation Framework and its Applications in Adversarial Text Generation

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2502.07101
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.07101
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** SMAB uses a two-level bandit approach to efficiently estimate word-level sensitivities, improving adversarial attack success rates by 15.58% and 12.00% over baseline methods.

## Executive Summary
SMAB introduces a multi-armed bandit framework to efficiently estimate word-level sensitivities in text classification tasks. By replacing the exponential complexity of traditional sensitivity calculations with a two-level bandit approach—outer arms for words and inner arms for sentences—SMAB provides both local and global sensitivity scores. The framework demonstrates improved performance in adversarial attacks while offering a scalable, label-free method for understanding model behavior across multiple languages.

## Method Summary
The paper proposes a two-level Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) framework for estimating word sensitivities in text classification. The outer MAB layer selects words to evaluate, while the inner MAB layer selects specific sentences containing those words. This approach reduces computational complexity from exponential to linear while maintaining accuracy in sensitivity estimation. The framework operates without requiring labeled data, making it practical for real-world applications.

## Key Results
- SMAB captures high- and low-sensitive words accurately across multiple languages
- Sensitivity scores correlate with model accuracy, providing insights into model behavior
- Integration with adversarial attack methods improves success rates by 15.58% (perturbation-based) and 12.00% (paraphrase-based)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The two-level bandit approach efficiently navigates the search space by treating words as outer arms and sentences as inner arms. This hierarchical structure allows the algorithm to focus computational resources on the most informative word-sentence pairs, rather than exhaustively testing all combinations. The MAB framework naturally balances exploration and exploitation, gradually converging on accurate sensitivity estimates while minimizing unnecessary computations.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Sentence corpus -> Outer MAB (words) -> Inner MAB (sentences) -> Sensitivity scores -> Model analysis/attack

**Critical Path**: The algorithm selects words via outer MAB, then for each word, samples sentences via inner MAB, computes sensitivity, and aggregates results to produce final sensitivity scores.

**Design Tradeoffs**: Linear complexity vs. potential approximation errors; label-free operation vs. possible reduced precision compared to supervised methods.

**Failure Signatures**: Poor sensitivity estimates when word distributions are highly skewed; degraded performance on extremely long sentences due to bandit convergence limitations.

**First Experiments**: 1) Compare SMAB sensitivity estimates with ground truth on synthetic data; 2) Measure wall-clock time versus original exponential method; 3) Test sensitivity correlation with model accuracy across different model architectures.
