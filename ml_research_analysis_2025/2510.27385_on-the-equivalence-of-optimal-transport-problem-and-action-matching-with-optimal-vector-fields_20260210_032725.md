# On the Equivalence of Optimal Transport Problem and Action Matching with Optimal Vector Fields

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2510.27385
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.27385
- **Authors**: Nikita Kornilov; Alexander Korotin
- **Reference count**: 5
- **Key outcome**: Action Matching with optimal vector fields is equivalent to dual Optimal Transport loss

## Executive Summary
This paper establishes an equivalence between the Optimal Transport (OT) problem and Action Matching (AM) when both methods consider optimal vector fields. The authors demonstrate that when AM is restricted to optimal vector fields (which are typical for dynamic OT solutions), its loss function becomes equivalent to the dual OT loss up to constants, regardless of the initial sequence of distributions. This theoretical result reveals that optimal vector fields serve as a robust bridge between different generative modeling approaches for solving OT problems.

## Method Summary
The authors analyze the relationship between Action Matching and Optimal Transport by examining their formulations when restricted to optimal vector fields. They show that both methods, when using these specific vector fields, converge to the same solution: the Brenier potential that defines the optimal transport map. The analysis involves comparing the loss functions of both approaches and demonstrating their equivalence through mathematical derivation.

## Key Results
- Action Matching with optimal vector fields has loss equivalent to dual OT loss up to constants
- Both methods converge to the Brenier potential when using optimal vector fields
- Optimal vector fields serve as a bridge between different generative modeling approaches for OT

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The equivalence emerges because both OT and AM, when constrained to optimal vector fields, are solving for the same underlying geometric structure. Optimal vector fields represent the most efficient paths for transforming one distribution into another, which is exactly what OT seeks to find. When AM uses these same paths, it naturally aligns with OT's objective function.

## Foundational Learning
- **Optimal Transport (OT)**: Mathematical framework for comparing probability distributions; needed to understand the baseline problem being solved
- **Brenier Potential**: Convex function whose gradient gives the optimal transport map; quick check: verify convexity properties
- **Action Matching**: Generative modeling approach that learns to transform distributions; needed to understand the alternative method being compared
- **Optimal Vector Fields**: Flow fields that represent most efficient transformations between distributions; quick check: verify they satisfy Euler-Lagrange equations
- **Dual OT Formulation**: Alternative representation of OT problem in terms of convex potentials; needed to establish the equivalence relationship
- **Dynamic OT Solutions**: Time-dependent formulations of optimal transport; needed to understand the context of optimal vector fields

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Initial Distributions -> Optimal Vector Fields -> Loss Function (AM/OT) -> Brenier Potential

**Critical Path**: The analysis traces from initial probability distributions through the computation of optimal vector fields to the final convergence on the Brenier potential. The critical insight is that this path is identical for both AM and OT when using optimal vector fields.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The equivalence suggests that one could choose between AM and OT based on computational considerations rather than theoretical differences. AM might offer more flexibility in implementation while maintaining theoretical guarantees equivalent to OT.

**Failure Signatures**: The equivalence breaks down if: (1) the Brenier potential doesn't exist or isn't unique, (2) non-optimal vector fields are used, or (3) regularized OT formulations are employed without proper analysis.

## Limitations
- Analysis primarily focuses on static optimal transport problems
- Assumes existence and uniqueness of the Brenier potential, which may not hold for all cost functions
- Does not explore practical computational complexity differences between the approaches
- Limited analysis of regularized OT formulations (e.g., entropic regularization)

## Confidence

**High Confidence**: The mathematical derivation showing equivalence of the loss functions when using optimal vector fields

**Medium Confidence**: The claim that this equivalence holds regardless of the initial sequence of distributions

**Medium Confidence**: The assertion that both methods converge to the Brenier potential
