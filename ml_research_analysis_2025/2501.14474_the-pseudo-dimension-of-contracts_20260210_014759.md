# The Pseudo-Dimension of Contracts
## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2501.14474
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.14474
- Reference count: 40
- The paper uses pseudo-dimension from statistical learning theory to analyze the sample complexity of learning optimal contracts in principal-agent models

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces pseudo-dimension from statistical learning theory to analyze the sample complexity of learning optimal contracts in principal-agent models. The authors show that the complexity of learning contracts depends critically on the contract class and whether the contracts are bounded or unbounded. For linear contracts, the pseudo-dimension is Θ(log n) and can be improved to Θ(min(log n, log(1/ϵ))) using discretization, leading to a sample complexity of O((1/ϵ²)(log(1/ϵ) + log(1/δ))). For bounded contracts, the pseudo-dimension is Θ(m log n) and can be improved to Θ(min(m log n, m log(1/ϵ))). The paper also demonstrates that unbounded contracts have pseudo-dimension Θ(m log n) but no finite-sample learning algorithm exists, highlighting fundamental limitations in contract learning.

## Method Summary
The paper applies pseudo-dimension analysis from statistical learning theory to contract design problems. The authors establish upper and lower bounds on the pseudo-dimension for different contract classes (linear, bounded, and unbounded) and use these to derive sample complexity bounds. The analysis involves constructing specific instances that achieve the bounds and proving that no algorithm can learn unbounded contracts with finite samples. The framework is extended to combinatorial actions, menus of contracts, and online learning settings, providing a comprehensive theoretical foundation for understanding contract learning complexity.

## Key Results
- For linear contracts, pseudo-dimension is Θ(log n) and can be improved to Θ(min(log n, log(1/ϵ))) using discretization, yielding sample complexity O((1/ϵ²)(log(1/ϵ) + log(1/δ)))
- For bounded contracts, pseudo-dimension is Θ(m log n) and can be improved to Θ(min(m log n, m log(1/ϵ))) with discretization
- For unbounded contracts, pseudo-dimension is Θ(m log n), but no finite-sample learning algorithm exists due to fundamental impossibility results

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The paper leverages pseudo-dimension as a measure of the intrinsic complexity of contract classes, which directly determines the sample complexity needed to learn optimal contracts. By characterizing the pseudo-dimension for different contract types, the authors can precisely quantify the tradeoff between contract expressiveness and learnability. The discretization techniques reduce the effective complexity by approximating continuous contract spaces with finite grids, improving sample complexity bounds.

## Foundational Learning
- **Pseudo-dimension**: A complexity measure from statistical learning theory that bounds the number of samples needed to learn a hypothesis class. Needed to quantify the sample complexity of learning contracts.
- **VC-dimension**: A related complexity measure for binary classification. Understanding the relationship helps contextualize pseudo-dimension results.
- **Sample complexity**: The number of samples required to learn a good hypothesis with high probability. Central to the paper's analysis of contract learning.
- **Discretization**: Approximating continuous spaces with finite grids. Used to improve pseudo-dimension bounds by reducing effective complexity.
- **Principal-agent problem**: The economic framework where a principal designs contracts to incentivize an agent. The specific structure affects which contract classes are relevant.

- **Component map**: Principal's utility function -> Contract class specification -> Pseudo-dimension calculation -> Sample complexity bound

- **Design tradeoffs**: More expressive contract classes (bounded/unbounded vs linear) have higher pseudo-dimension but potentially better performance; discretization reduces complexity but may sacrifice optimality
- **Failure signatures**: High pseudo-dimension indicates need for many samples; unbounded contracts cannot be learned with finite samples; discretization may introduce approximation errors

## Limitations
- The analysis assumes bounded and well-behaved contract spaces, which may not hold in practical applications
- Results for linear contracts depend on specific assumptions about the principal's utility function that may not generalize
- The impossibility result for unbounded contracts is based on worst-case analysis that may be overly pessimistic for structured distributions
