# On the Robustness of Age for Learning-Based Wireless Scheduling in Unknown Environments

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2601.05956
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.05956
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Age-based learning policy achieves robust scheduling under throughput constraints with rapid recovery from infeasible periods

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses wireless scheduling in unknown environments where channel conditions may change abruptly. The authors propose using head-of-line age of virtual packets as a metric for constraint tracking, finding it more robust than traditional queue length metrics when channel conditions change suddenly. They develop a learning-based scheduling policy using the UCB algorithm that incorporates head-of-line age, demonstrating superior performance during infeasible periods while maintaining low time-since-last-reward.

## Method Summary
The paper develops a learning-based wireless scheduling policy that uses head-of-line age instead of queue length for tracking throughput constraints. The policy employs the UCB (Upper Confidence Bound) algorithm framework, modifying it to use age metrics that better capture the time-sensitive nature of wireless scheduling. The approach is theoretically grounded with regret bounds matching existing literature while ensuring zero constraint violation under feasible conditions. The policy is designed to maintain stability during abrupt channel changes and rapidly recover when constraints become feasible again.

## Key Results
- Head-of-line age proves more robust than queue length for tracking constraint violations during abrupt channel changes
- The age-based policy achieves state-of-the-art performance under i.i.d. conditions
- The policy maintains stability and rapidly recovers from infeasible constraint periods
- Theoretical regret bounds match existing literature while ensuring zero constraint violation under feasible conditions
- Outperforms queue-length-based approaches during infeasible periods while maintaining low time-since-last-reward

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The paper demonstrates that head-of-line age provides a more temporally accurate measure of constraint violations compared to queue length. When channel conditions change abruptly, queue length can accumulate rapidly, leading to conservative scheduling decisions. Head-of-line age, by focusing on the time since the most recent packet was served, provides a more immediate and responsive metric that better tracks the actual throughput constraints. This temporal sensitivity allows the policy to make more informed decisions about when to explore new channels versus exploiting known good ones.
