# Structuring Reasoning for Complex Rules Beyond Flat Representations

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2510.05134
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.05134
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** DAT framework improves complex rule reasoning accuracy from 34.11% to 62.49% on Qwen-2.5-7B, outperforming Chain-of-Thought baselines

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of complex rule-based reasoning in large language models (LLMs), where models often treat interdependent rules as unstructured text, leading to reasoning divergence and error propagation. To overcome these limitations, the authors propose the Dynamic Adjudication Template (DAT), a structured reasoning framework inspired by human expert cognition. DAT guides the model through three methodical stages: qualitative analysis, evidence gathering, and adjudication. The framework also includes an automated pipeline for dynamic template generation, filtering, and selection. Empirical results show that DAT consistently outperforms Chain-of-Thought (CoT) baselines, enabling smaller models to match or exceed the performance of significantly larger LLMs.

## Method Summary
The Dynamic Adjudication Template (DAT) framework implements a three-stage reasoning process for complex rule systems: (1) Qualitative Analysis to generate an initial holistic judgment, (2) Evidence Gathering to systematically identify and verify relevant rules using structured templates, and (3) Adjudication to synthesize evidence and make final decisions. The method employs an automated pipeline that generates a template library using seed templates, structured continuation, and style transfer, then filters and selects optimal templates via a dual-scoring selector (global and local performance metrics). The framework is trained on the EVADE dataset using specific LoRA parameters for the selector and demonstrates significant improvements over flat Chain-of-Thought approaches on rule-intensive reasoning tasks.

## Key Results
- DAT improved accuracy from 34.11% to 62.49% on Qwen-2.5-7B for complex rule reasoning
- Small models (Qwen-2.5-7B) outperformed large CoT-based models (Qwen-2.5-72B-Instruct) on multiple tasks
- DAT showed consistent superiority over Chain-of-Thought baselines across different model scales and reasoning tasks
- The framework successfully handled a four-level hierarchical rule system with priority-based override in e-commerce content moderation

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
DAT works by structuring the reasoning process into distinct phases that mirror human expert cognition. The Qualitative Analysis stage provides an initial judgment that guides evidence gathering, while the Evidence Gathering phase systematically collects and verifies relevant rules using structured templates with placeholders. The Adjudication stage then synthesizes all collected evidence to make final decisions, preventing early errors from propagating unchecked. The dynamic template generation and selection pipeline ensures templates remain task-appropriate and effective, while the three-stage approach maintains reasoning coherence across complex rule interdependencies.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** EVADE Dataset → Template Generation → Template Library → Selector Training (DPO) → Inference Pipeline → DAT Execution (Qualitative → Evidence → Adjudication)

**Critical Path:** The most important execution path is the three-stage DAT reasoning: Initial Judgment → Evidence Gathering (with template completion) → Final Adjudication synthesis. This path must maintain logical coherence and complete evidence collection.

**Design Tradeoffs:** Structured templates vs. flexibility (rigid structure prevents drift but may miss novel patterns), model size dependency (larger models benefit more from complex templates), and template diversity vs. selection efficiency (more templates provide better coverage but increase computational cost).

**Failure Signatures:** 
- Early stage errors propagating unchecked
- Incomplete evidence gathering (unfilled placeholders)
- Template selector collapse to single template
- Model ignoring structured instructions and falling back to CoT

**First Experiments:**
1. Run DAT with a single template on a simple rule case to verify three-stage pipeline functionality
2. Test template selector on validation set to ensure diverse template selection
3. Compare intermediate outputs (Initial Judgment vs. Final Adjudication) to measure correction effectiveness

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

**Open Question 1:** How can the Dynamic Adjudication Template (DAT) framework be adapted to improve the reasoning capabilities of smaller Vision-Language Models (VLMs) that currently struggle with complex textual instructions? The paper acknowledges that smaller VLMs showed reduced effectiveness due to "limited capacity in following detailed instructions," explicitly identifying the tailoring of templates for vision-language tasks as a future direction.

**Open Question 2:** Does the performance advantage of DAT over Chain-of-Thought (CoT) generalize to high-stakes rule-intensive domains outside of e-commerce, such as legal or financial compliance? The paper lists "legal advisory services" and "financial risk analysis" as target domains, yet all empirical validation is conducted exclusively on the e-commerce specific EVADE dataset.

**Open Question 3:** To what extent does the "Initial Judgment" generated in the Qualitative Analysis phase introduce cognitive anchoring bias that prevents the correction of errors during the final Adjudication phase? The methodology establishes an "Initial Judgment" prior to evidence gathering, but does not analyze failure cases where the final output may have been skewed by an early, incorrect holistic assessment.

## Limitations
- Performance highly dependent on model capacity (smaller VLMs ignore complex templates)
- Template generation parameters underspecified (v, k, θ not disclosed)
- Limited to single domain (e-commerce content moderation)
- No statistical significance testing or variance reporting across runs

## Confidence

**High confidence** in the core contribution: Dynamic Adjudication Template represents a novel, structured approach to complex rule reasoning that addresses a well-documented limitation of flat Chain-of-Thought methods.

**Medium confidence** in empirical results: While the reported accuracy improvements are substantial and the methodology appears sound, the lack of statistical testing, limited dataset scope, and missing parameter details prevent full validation of the claimed performance gains.

**Low confidence** in generalizability: The framework's dependence on model capacity and the absence of testing on diverse rule-intensive domains limit confidence in broad applicability claims.

## Next Checks

1. **Parameter sensitivity analysis**: Systematically vary template generation parameters (v, k) and performance threshold θ to determine their impact on final accuracy and identify minimum viable configurations.

2. **Cross-domain robustness test**: Apply DAT to at least two additional rule-intensive datasets from different domains (e.g., medical diagnosis, legal reasoning) to validate generalizability beyond e-commerce content moderation.

3. **Statistical significance verification**: Conduct multiple runs with different random seeds and perform statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to confirm that observed improvements are not due to chance or dataset-specific artifacts.
