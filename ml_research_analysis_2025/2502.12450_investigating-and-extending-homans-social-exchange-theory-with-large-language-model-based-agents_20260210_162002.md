# Investigating and Extending Homans' Social Exchange Theory with Large Language Model based Agents

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2502.12450
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.12450
- Authors: Lei Wang; Zheqing Zhang; Xu Chen
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: LLM-based agents successfully validate Homans' six propositions and extend the theory with three new corollaries about stability, reciprocity, and resilience

## Executive Summary
This study introduces a novel framework using LLM-based agents to investigate and extend Homans' Social Exchange Theory. By constructing a virtual society where agents engage in resource exchange games, the researchers validate all six propositions of the theory, demonstrating consistency between agent and human behaviors. The study further extends the theory by exploring how cognitive styles and social value orientations influence exchange dynamics, leading to three new corollaries: the Stability Corollary, the Reciprocity Corollary, and the Resilience Corollary. These findings highlight the potential of LLM agents as a powerful tool for studying complex social theories, offering insights into the mechanisms of social exchange systems that would be difficult to achieve through traditional methods.

## Method Summary
The researchers developed a framework called EXCHANGE that employs LLM-based agents to simulate social exchanges within a virtual society. Agents are endowed with different cognitive styles (intuitive or deliberative) and social value orientations (prosocial or proself). They participate in resource exchange games where they can either engage in cooperative exchanges or refuse, leading to various social dynamics. The framework systematically tests Homans' six propositions by varying exchange costs, rewards, and social networks. Agents make decisions based on their internal states, past experiences, and the actions of other agents, mirroring human decision-making processes in social exchanges. The study uses both quantitative metrics (such as exchange rates and network structures) and qualitative analyses to assess the validity of the theory and the emergence of new patterns.

## Key Results
- All six propositions of Homans' Social Exchange Theory were validated through LLM agent behaviors in resource exchange games.
- Three new corollaries were proposed: the Stability Corollary (high-cost exchanges are more stable), the Reciprocity Corollary (prosocial agents show stronger reciprocity), and the Resilience Corollary (exchange networks recover faster from disruptions).
- Cognitive styles and social value orientations significantly influence exchange dynamics, with intuitive-prosocial agents showing the highest stability in exchange networks.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework leverages LLM agents' ability to simulate complex decision-making processes that mirror human social cognition. By encoding cognitive styles and social value orientations into the agents' prompt templates, the researchers create distinct behavioral profiles that interact within a controlled virtual environment. The agents' decisions are influenced by their internal states (satisfaction, profit, power), past experiences (frequency of exchanges), and social network structures, replicating the key factors identified in Homans' theory. The iterative nature of the exchange games allows for the emergence of stable patterns and social norms over time, providing a dynamic testing ground for theoretical propositions that would be difficult to observe in traditional human-subject experiments.

## Foundational Learning
- Homans' Social Exchange Theory: A sociological framework explaining how individuals engage in social interactions based on rewards, costs, and reciprocity. **Why needed**: Provides the theoretical foundation for testing and extending social exchange dynamics.
- Resource Exchange Games: Experimental setups where participants engage in cooperative or competitive interactions to gain rewards. **Why needed**: Serve as the controlled environment for testing social exchange theories.
- Cognitive Styles: Individual differences in information processing, either intuitive (fast, automatic) or deliberative (slow, analytical). **Why needed**: Influence decision-making processes in social exchanges.
- Social Value Orientations: Individual preferences in social interactions, either prosocial (benefiting others) or proself (benefiting oneself). **Why needed**: Determine the nature of reciprocity and cooperation in exchanges.
- LLM-based Agents: Artificial entities powered by large language models that can simulate human-like decision-making. **Why needed**: Provide a scalable and controllable platform for testing social theories.

## Architecture Onboarding
**Component Map**: EXCHANGE Framework -> LLM Agents -> Virtual Society -> Resource Exchange Games -> Validation Metrics

**Critical Path**: Agent Configuration → Game Initialization → Iterative Exchange Decisions → Network Analysis → Theory Validation

**Design Tradeoffs**: The study uses LLM agents to balance ecological validity with experimental control. While agents can simulate complex social behaviors, they may not fully capture the nuances of human cognition. The virtual society allows for rapid iteration and large-scale testing but may oversimplify real-world social complexities.

**Failure Signatures**: Inconsistent validation of Homans' propositions, lack of emergence of expected social patterns, or unrealistic agent behaviors that diverge significantly from human data.

**First Experiments**:
1. Validate Homans' six propositions with a small set of agents to establish baseline behaviors.
2. Introduce varying costs and rewards to test the impact on exchange rates and network structures.
3. Simulate disruptions in the exchange network to observe recovery patterns and test the Resilience Corollary.

## Limitations
- The use of LLM-based agents as proxies for human social behavior introduces uncertainty about ecological validity.
- Potential biases in LLM training data may influence agent behavior and affect the generalizability of findings.
- The study does not explore how different LLM architectures or training approaches might affect the results.

## Confidence
- Validation of Homans' six propositions through LLM agents: **High confidence**
- Extension of the theory with three new corollaries: **Medium confidence**
- LLM agents as a viable tool for studying social theories: **Medium confidence**

## Next Checks
1. Conduct human-subject experiments replicating the resource exchange games to directly compare human and LLM agent behaviors, particularly focusing on the newly proposed corollaries about stability, reciprocity, and resilience.
2. Test the framework with multiple LLM architectures (e.g., GPT, Claude, Llama) to assess the robustness of findings across different models and identify any model-specific behavioral patterns.
3. Implement longitudinal studies where agents interact over extended periods to examine how social exchange dynamics evolve and whether the Stability Corollary holds over time.
