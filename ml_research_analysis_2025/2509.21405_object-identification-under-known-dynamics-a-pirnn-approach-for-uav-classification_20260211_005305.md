# Object Identification Under Known Dynamics: A PIRNN Approach for UAV Classification

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.21405
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21405
- Authors: Nyi Nyi Aung; Neil Muralles; Adrian Stein
- Reference count: 27
- Primary result: 96% classification accuracy for UAV type identification from motion trajectories using Physics-Informed Residual Neural Networks

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the problem of object identification under known dynamics in unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) applications, specifically classifying quadcopters, fixed-wing aircraft, and helicopters based on their motion trajectories. The authors propose a Physics-Informed Residual Neural Network (PIRNN) that combines a ResNet-style architecture with a hybrid loss function incorporating both data loss and physics regularization based on temporal smoothness of UAV states. The model uses a softmax-weighted confidence estimation for multi-class classification, training on simulated trajectories of each UAV type. Results demonstrate high classification accuracy (96% overall) with reduced training time (55 minutes for 100 epochs), even when tested on trajectories corrupted with up to 10-15% Gaussian noise.

## Method Summary
The method trains a physics-informed residual neural network to predict UAV state derivatives from trajectory data. The architecture uses weighted skip connections across 5 hidden layers with 128 neurons each, combining multiple intermediate features for the output. Training employs a hybrid loss function that balances data fidelity (MSE of predicted vs actual state derivatives) with physics regularization (temporal smoothness penalty). Classification is performed by running three forward passes per trajectory (one per UAV type), computing per-class losses, and applying softmax to inverse losses to obtain confidence scores. The model is trained on 3000 simulated trajectories (1000 per class) with 80/10/10 train/validation/test split.

## Key Results
- Achieved 96% overall classification accuracy across three UAV types
- Maintained >75% accuracy with trajectories corrupted by 10-15% Gaussian noise
- Reduced training time to 55 minutes for 100 epochs compared to standard PINNs
- Successfully distinguished fixed-wing aircraft from rotorcraft while handling overlap between quadcopters and helicopters

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
Physics regularization via temporal smoothness improves generalization and robustness to noise in trajectory-based classification. The hybrid loss function `L_hybrid = λ_data * L_data + λ_phys * L_phys` combines mean squared error between predicted and ground-truth derivatives with a penalty on abrupt state changes across time steps (`L_phys = 1/(T-1) * Σ||ŷ_{t+1} - ŷ_t||²`). This soft constraint encodes the shared physical prior that UAV trajectories evolve smoothly, without requiring explicit PDEs for each vehicle class. Core assumption: UAV state derivatives change gradually over time; abrupt jumps indicate non-physical predictions or noise. Evidence anchors: [abstract] states "physics-informed learning for state mapping and state-derivative prediction"; [Section II.B] explains temporal consistency enforcement; [Table VI] shows classification accuracy remains above 75% with up to 10-15% Gaussian noise corruption.

### Mechanism 2
ResNet-style skip connections with weighted aggregation enable stable training and richer feature extraction for small datasets. Skip connections (e.g., `h2 = tanh(·) + h1`, `h5 = tanh(·) + 0.6·h4 + 0.4·h1`) create multiple gradient pathways, mitigating vanishing gradients and allowing intermediate features to contribute directly to the output. The output layer combines weighted contributions from h1, h3, and h5 (`0.5·h5 + 0.3·h3 + 0.2·h1`). Core assumption: Shallow and deep features both contain useful information for derivative prediction; residual connections do not introduce harmful interference. Evidence anchors: [Section II.A] states "residual connections with independently learned weights enhances representation learning and mitigates the risk of overfitting"; [Table I] provides specific weighted skip connection formulas; [Figure 4a] shows training and validation losses plateau early, indicating stable convergence.

### Mechanism 3
Multi-class classification via inverse physics inference—comparing predicted derivatives against ground truth for each candidate dynamics model—enables type identification without explicit class embeddings in the forward pass. The trained PIRNN predicts state derivatives conditioned on a one-hot class vector: `ŷ^{(i)}_t = f_θ(x_t, c_i)`. For classification, the model runs three forward passes (one per UAV type), computes per-class loss `L^{(i)}_class = 1/T * Σ||ŷ^{(i)}_t - y_t||²`, then applies softmax to negated losses: `p^{(i)} = exp(-γ L^{(i)}_class) / Σ_j exp(-γ L^{(j)}_class)`. The class with highest confidence is selected. Core assumption: Each UAV type has sufficiently distinct dynamics that its trajectory will produce lower prediction loss under the correct model. Evidence anchors: [Section II.C] provides full softmax-weighted confidence estimation equations (3-6); [Table IV] shows sample confidence percentages with clear separation for fixed-wing but closer margins for quadcopter/helicopter; [Figure 5] demonstrates PCA and t-SNE visualizations showing fixed-wing trajectories are well-separated while quadcopter and helicopter clusters overlap.

  - Why needed here: The architecture uses weighted skip connections rather than standard identity shortcuts. Understanding gradient flow through residuals explains why small networks can train efficiently.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Input(15-dim: 12-state + 3-class) → Hidden1(128, tanh) → Hidden2(128, tanh + skip) → Hidden3(128, tanh + skip) → Hidden4(128, tanh + skip) → Hidden5(128, tanh + skip) → Output(weighted sum of h1, h3, h5) → Predicted derivatives

- Critical path: 1) Data preparation: Generate simulated trajectories (1000 per class, 10 seconds each) using dynamics models in Section II.D 2) Training: Minimize `L_hybrid` with scheduled learning rate (Figure 4b: high → medium → low across epochs) 3) Inference: For each test trajectory, run 3 forward passes, compute per-class losses, apply softmax with γ=10, return argmax

- Design tradeoffs: `tanh` vs. ReLU: tanh provides bounded outputs and smooth derivatives, beneficial for physics predictions but slower to train than ReLU; Weighted skips vs. identity skips: Learned weights (`0.6·h4 + 0.4·h1`) provide flexibility but introduce additional parameters; may overfit on very small datasets; λ_phys = 0.2: Balances data fidelity vs. physics constraint; higher values enforce smoother predictions but may underfit abrupt maneuvers

- Failure signatures: Quadcopter/helicopter confusion (51/49 confidence splits): Indicates dynamics overlap; may require additional state features (e.g., motor RPM, control inputs) or longer trajectories; Accuracy collapse at >15% noise: Physics regularization insufficient to overcome signal corruption; consider preprocessing (Kalman filtering) or noise-aware training; Loss plateau early with high validation error: Possible underfitting—try increasing λ_phys or network capacity

- First 3 experiments: 1) Reproduce baseline: Train on 80/10/10 split with reported hyperparameters (Table III); verify ~96% accuracy and 55-minute training time on comparable hardware 2) Ablate physics loss: Set λ_phys = 0 and compare accuracy and noise robustness; quantify contribution of temporal smoothness constraint 3) Noise robustness sweep: Test on trajectories with σ_x ∈ {1%, 3%, 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%} and plot accuracy vs. noise level; identify degradation threshold for your deployment environment

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

- Can PIRNN-based UAV classification maintain 96% accuracy when deployed on real-world trajectory data from high-resolution, high-speed camera systems? All experiments used simulated trajectories from known dynamic models; real-world data introduces unmodeled sensor noise, occlusions, and tracking errors not present in simulation.

- Can systematic hyperparameter optimization (e.g., Bayesian optimization, grid search) significantly improve PIRNN classification accuracy beyond the current 96% achieved with trial-and-error tuning? The conclusion acknowledges "hyper-parameters in this study were selected through trial and error," and notes "significant potential for further improvement through systematic hyperparameter optimization."

- Can incorporating class-specific PDEs rather than generic smoothness regularization improve differentiation between dynamically similar UAV types (quadcopter vs. helicopter)? The authors acknowledge that "quadcopters and helicopters share similar flight characteristics in hovering, climbing, and descending scenarios," resulting in near-50/50 confidence scores. The paper deliberately avoided class-specific PDEs to reduce complexity, using only temporal smoothness (L_phys) as a physics constraint.

## Limitations

- The classification method relies on simulated trajectories using generic UAV dynamics parameters without validation on real-world flight data, leaving open questions about performance degradation from sensor noise and unmodeled dynamics.
- The physics regularization assumes smooth temporal evolution, which may not hold for aggressive maneuvers or control discontinuities, potentially limiting applicability to all flight regimes.
- The overlapping dynamics between quadcopters and helicopters represent a fundamental limitation that may require additional sensor modalities or trajectory features to resolve.

## Confidence

- High Confidence: Physics-informed hybrid loss structure and training methodology are clearly specified and reproducible; the 96% accuracy claim on simulated data is directly supported by Table IV results.
- Medium Confidence: Noise robustness claims (75% accuracy at 10-15% corruption) are supported by experimental data but may not generalize to real-world sensor noise characteristics; the specific physical parameters for UAV dynamics are cited but not explicitly provided.
- Low Confidence: Claims about computational efficiency and reduced training time relative to standard PINNs lack direct comparison metrics; the method's generalization to unseen vehicle variants or real-world deployment conditions is not validated.

## Next Checks

1. **Real Data Validation**: Test the trained PIRNN on real UAV flight trajectory datasets (e.g., from public repositories or collected with onboard sensors) to quantify performance gap between simulated and real-world conditions.

2. **Ablation of Physics Loss**: Systematically vary λ_phys from 0 to 0.5 and measure impacts on both classification accuracy and noise robustness to isolate the contribution of temporal smoothness regularization.

3. **Class Confusion Analysis**: Generate and analyze trajectories specifically designed to maximize quadcopter/helicopter similarity (e.g., hovering with aggressive yaw) and evaluate whether additional state features or trajectory segments improve discrimination.
