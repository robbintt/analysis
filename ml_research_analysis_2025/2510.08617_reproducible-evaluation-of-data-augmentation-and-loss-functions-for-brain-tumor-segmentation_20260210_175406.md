# Reproducible Evaluation of Data Augmentation and Loss Functions for Brain Tumor Segmentation

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2510.08617
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.08617
- **Reference count**: 21
- **Primary result**: U-Net with focal loss achieved 90% precision on brain tumor MRI segmentation

## Executive Summary
This study presents a reproducible evaluation of U-Net segmentation performance on brain tumor MRI using focal loss and basic data augmentation strategies. The research systematically investigates the impact of focal loss parameter tuning and three data augmentation techniques (horizontal flip, rotation, and scaling) on segmentation performance. All code and results are made publicly available, establishing a transparent baseline for future research in brain tumor segmentation.

The experimental framework focuses on a single publicly available MRI dataset, measuring performance through Dice coefficient and IoU metrics. The findings demonstrate that horizontal flip augmentation provides the most significant performance improvement, while scaling shows minimal impact. The U-Net with focal loss achieves precision comparable to state-of-the-art results, though this claim lacks direct benchmark comparisons.

## Method Summary
The study employs a U-Net architecture trained on a publicly available brain tumor MRI dataset, with experiments designed to evaluate two main components: focal loss function performance and data augmentation effectiveness. Focal loss parameter tuning was conducted within a limited range, while three augmentation techniques were systematically applied: horizontal flip, rotation, and scaling. Performance metrics included Dice coefficient, IoU, and precision scores. The experimental design emphasizes reproducibility by making all code and results publicly accessible, though it remains limited to a single dataset and basic augmentation strategies without exploring more sophisticated techniques.

## Key Results
- U-Net with focal loss achieved 90% precision on brain tumor MRI segmentation
- Horizontal flip augmentation emerged as the most effective technique, significantly improving Dice coefficient and IoU scores
- Scaling augmentation showed minimal impact on segmentation performance

## Limitations
- Results focus on a single MRI dataset without validation across diverse imaging protocols or scanner types
- Limited focal loss parameter exploration may miss optimal configurations
- Exclusion of sophisticated augmentation techniques (elastic deformations, noise injection, GAN-based methods) represents a significant gap

## Confidence
- High: Reproducibility aspects and relative ranking of augmentation techniques
- Medium: Focal loss performance claims given limited parameter exploration
- Low: Claims about achieving "state-of-the-art" performance without direct benchmark comparisons
