# Gather-Scatter Mamba: Accelerating Propagation with Efficient State Space Model

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.00862
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.00862
- Authors: Hyun-kyu Ko; Youbin Kim; Jihyeon Park; Dongheok Park; Gyeongjin Kang; Wonjun Cho; Hyung Yi; Eunbyung Park
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: GSM achieves state-of-the-art PSNR 38.25 on Vimeo-90K-T while maintaining competitive results on REDS4 and Vid4 datasets

## Executive Summary
GSM combines shifted window self-attention for spatial context aggregation with Mamba-based selective scanning for efficient temporal propagation in video super-resolution. The key innovation is an alignment-aware gather-scatter mechanism that warps features toward a center anchor frame before Mamba processing and redistributes residuals back to all frames afterward. This approach addresses the limitations of existing methods that struggle with spatial misalignment and inefficient feature utilization in long-range temporal modeling.

## Method Summary
GSM is a hybrid VSR framework that integrates spatial refinement through Shifted Window Self-Attention (SWSA) with temporal propagation using a novel Gather-Scatter Mamba (GSM) mechanism. The method processes 5-frame temporal windows, aligning all frames to a center anchor through optical flow-based warping before applying Mamba's selective scanning. Residuals are then redistributed to all frames via inverse warping. The model is trained in two stages: first on REDS for 600k iterations, then on Vimeo-90K for 300k iterations, achieving state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks while maintaining computational efficiency.

## Key Results
- Achieves state-of-the-art PSNR 38.25 and SSIM 0.9534 on Vimeo-90K-T dataset
- Maintains competitive performance on REDS4 and Vid4 datasets
- Demonstrates superior efficiency with 10.5M parameters and 1.52T FLOPs
- Shows faster runtime compared to existing approaches

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Center-anchored alignment reduces occlusion artifacts and warping errors compared to forward-anchored propagation.
- Mechanism: By placing the anchor frame at the center of the temporal window, neighboring frames are warped across shorter temporal distances, reducing accumulated motion estimation errors and allowing more frames to contribute valid information.
- Core assumption: Optical flow estimation accuracy degrades with temporal distance; occlusion regions increase when aligning from distant frames.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "...alignment-aware gather-scatter mechanism that warps features toward a center anchor frame..."; [section 3.4] "Center-anchored propagation mitigates this issue by symmetrically leveraging nearby past and future frames..."
- Break condition: If optical flow quality is extremely poor, shorter paths may not provide sufficient benefit.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Explicit feature alignment before temporal-first Mamba scanning enables effective long-range dependency modeling.
- Mechanism: Mamba processes video as flattened 1D sequences. Without alignment, spatially adjacent features across frames become temporally distant in the sequence. The gather phase warps all features to a common reference frame first, preserving spatial correspondence during 1D flattening.
- Core assumption: Mamba's selective scan relies on sequential token adjacency to build effective hidden states; spatial misalignment disrupts this.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "...Mamba alone struggles to capture fine-grained spatial dependencies due to its causal nature..."; [section 3.4] "Mamba's 1D sequential scanning is highly sensitive to spatial misalignment..."
- Break condition: If alignment fails, Mamba still receives misaligned inputs.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Residual scattering to all frames in the temporal window improves feature utilization and temporal consistency.
- Mechanism: After Mamba processing produces residuals at the anchor frame, inverse warping redistributes these residuals back to each supporting frame's original position, allowing all frames to benefit from aggregated temporal information.
- Core assumption: Features computed for supporting frames contain useful information worth preserving.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "...redistributes residuals back to all frames afterward..."; [section 3.4] "residual reuse (scatter) efficiently recycles valuable intermediate computations..."
- Break condition: If inverse warping introduces artifacts, scattered residuals may degrade supporting frames.

## Foundational Learning

- Concept: **State Space Models and Selective Scan**
  - Why needed here: GSM replaces recurrent propagation with Mamba's selective SSM. Understanding how input-dependent state transitions enable content-aware information routing is essential for debugging temporal propagation failures.
  - Quick check question: Can you explain why Mamba's input-dependent timestep Δ allows the model to selectively "remember" or "forget" information across frames?

- Concept: **Optical Flow and Backward Warping**
  - Why needed here: The gather-scatter mechanism depends on flow-based alignment. Without understanding how flow fields map pixels between frames and how bilinear/implicit resampling works, you cannot diagnose alignment artifacts or occlusion handling failures.
  - Quick check question: Given flow from frame i to frame k, which operation correctly warps feature f_k to align with f_i: forward warping using flow directly, or backward warping using inverse sampling?

- Concept: **Shifted Window Self-Attention**
  - Why needed here: GSM uses SWSA for spatial refinement before temporal propagation. Understanding window partitioning and shifting is necessary to modify spatial receptive fields or debug boundary artifacts.
  - Quick check question: Why does shifting windows between successive layers enable cross-window information flow, and what would happen if all layers used identical (non-shifted) window positions?

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Input -> SWSA blocks -> Window Propagation Module (WPM) -> Bidirectional Pass -> Output
- Critical path: Flow estimation quality → Gather alignment accuracy → Mamba temporal modeling → Scatter redistribution fidelity. Errors compound; flow is the bottleneck.
- Design tradeoffs:
  - Window size (K=5): Larger windows capture more temporal context but increase alignment difficulty and memory.
  - Center vs. forward anchor: Center provides bidirectional context but requires both past and future frames available at inference.
  - Implicit vs. bilinear alignment: Implicit (from IART) is more accurate but slower; bilinear is faster but lower quality.
  - SWSA before vs. after propagation: Current design refines locally first; swapping order would change error accumulation patterns.
- Failure signatures:
  - Occlusion artifacts at boundaries: Scatter phase struggles at disoccluded regions where inverse flow is undefined.
  - Temporal flickering: If scatter updates are too aggressive, per-frame quality improves but inter-frame consistency degrades.
  - Drift on long sequences: Despite bidirectional passes, error accumulation may still occur.
  - Mamba ignores long-range context: If alignment quality is poor, temporal-first scanning degrades.
- First 3 experiments:
  1. Ablate anchor position: Compare forward-anchored vs. center-anchored propagation on a short validation clip.
  2. Disable scatter: Run with scatter off (anchor-only update) to quantify residual redistribution benefit.
  3. Flow quality stress test: Add synthetic noise to flow estimates before gather; measure PSNR sensitivity to alignment degradation.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
- Question: How does GSM's performance scale with larger temporal window sizes (K > 5), given Mamba's theoretical linear complexity with sequence length?
- Question: How robust is the gather-scatter mechanism to optical flow estimation errors in severe occlusion or large displacement scenarios?
- Question: Can the shifted window self-attention (SWSA) component be replaced with spatial Mamba variants to achieve a fully unified Mamba-based architecture?
- Question: Is the bidirectional (forward + backward) pass strictly necessary given that center-anchored windows already provide symmetric access to past and future frames?

## Limitations
- Evaluation scope is constrained to specific datasets with relatively controlled motion patterns, raising questions about robustness to real-world video scenarios.
- The optical flow component (SpyNet) remains a potential bottleneck, with flow estimation errors directly propagating through the gather-scatter pipeline.
- The center-anchored alignment benefit lacks ablation studies comparing it against alternative temporal weighting schemes or learned alignment strategies.
- The implicit bilinear alignment variant was not benchmarked, leaving efficiency-quality tradeoffs unclear.

## Confidence
**High Confidence:** GSM's architectural design combining SWSA with Mamba-based temporal propagation is technically sound and well-implemented.
**Medium Confidence:** The reported PSNR/SSIM improvements over baselines are credible but should be interpreted cautiously without statistical significance testing across multiple runs.
**Low Confidence:** The paper's claims about GSM's superiority in handling long-range dependencies and complex temporal relationships are not fully substantiated.

## Next Checks
1. Systematically degrade optical flow estimates and measure GSM's PSNR degradation curve to quantify the alignment bottleneck and reveal robustness margin to flow errors.
2. Replace the fixed center-anchored propagation with a learned temporal attention mechanism to determine if fixed center anchoring is optimal or if GSM underutilizes its parameter budget.
3. Evaluate GSM on unconstrained video datasets with natural camera motion, occlusion, and lighting variation to assess practical deployment readiness.
