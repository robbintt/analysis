# Generative AI Meets Wireless Sensing: Towards Wireless Foundation Model

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2509.15258
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.15258
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Comprehensive survey on integrating generative AI into wireless sensing systems

## Executive Summary
This survey paper explores the convergence of generative AI and wireless sensing, presenting a framework for developing wireless foundation models. The authors categorize approaches into two main modes: using generative AI as a plugin to augment existing models or as a solver to directly address sensing tasks. The paper examines applications across human activity recognition, localization, and environmental reconstruction, highlighting the potential of generative models to address challenges like data scarcity and model generalization in wireless sensing systems.

## Method Summary
The paper surveys various generative AI approaches for wireless sensing, focusing on GANs, VAEs, and diffusion models. The methodology involves analyzing existing research to identify two primary integration modes: (1) GenAI as a Plugin for data augmentation and domain adaptation, and (2) GenAI as a Solver for direct task solving. The survey examines implementation details, performance metrics, and architectural choices across different wireless sensing applications, with particular emphasis on WiFi CSI and radar data processing.

## Key Results
- Generative AI can effectively augment wireless sensing systems through data augmentation and domain adaptation
- Diffusion models offer stable training and robust performance for wireless sensing tasks
- The proposed framework outlines a path toward unified wireless foundation models using self-supervised pretraining and cross-modal generation

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Generative AI models can learn the complex distribution of wireless signals and generate realistic synthetic samples that capture the underlying physics of signal propagation. By conditioning on relevant factors like activity labels or environmental features, these models can produce targeted augmentations that improve downstream task performance. The ability to generate data across different domains and modalities enables better generalization and adaptation to new sensing scenarios.

## Foundational Learning
1. **Wireless Channel State Information (CSI)** - The fine-grained measurement of wireless signal propagation between transmitter and receiver; needed to understand the raw data format and preprocessing requirements; quick check: verify CSI amplitude and phase extraction works correctly on test dataset.
2. **Conditional Generative Modeling** - Training generative models to produce outputs conditioned on specific inputs or labels; needed to create targeted data augmentations for wireless sensing tasks; quick check: confirm generated samples match conditional distribution statistics.
3. **Domain Adaptation in Wireless Systems** - Techniques for adapting models trained on one wireless environment to perform well in different environments; needed to address the generalization challenges in real-world deployments; quick check: measure performance degradation when testing on unseen environments.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Raw CSI Data -> Preprocessing -> Generative Model (GAN/Diffusion) -> Synthetic Data -> Task-Specific Classifier -> Final Prediction

**Critical Path:** Data preprocessing → Generative model training → Synthetic data validation → Task model training → Performance evaluation

**Design Tradeoffs:** GANs offer faster generation but suffer from mode collapse and training instability; Diffusion models provide more stable training and diverse outputs but require more computation and inference time; VAEs offer efficient inference but may produce less sharp outputs.

**Failure Signatures:** If augmented models perform worse than baseline, synthetic data likely introduces distribution shift or physically impossible signal patterns; if inference is too slow, the generative model is too complex for real-time deployment; if training diverges, the GAN architecture needs stabilization techniques.
