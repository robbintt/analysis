# BridgeDrive: Diffusion Bridge Policy for Closed-Loop Trajectory Planning in Autonomous Driving

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.23589
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.23589
- Reference count: 20
- This paper introduces BridgeDrive, a novel diffusion bridge policy for closed-loop trajectory planning in autonomous driving.

## Executive Summary
BridgeDrive is a diffusion bridge policy for autonomous driving trajectory planning that translates coarse anchor trajectories into fine-grained, context-aware plans while maintaining theoretical consistency of the diffusion process. The method addresses the challenge of guiding diffusion models effectively in reactive, dynamic environments by using a principled diffusion framework that ensures symmetry between forward and denoising processes. BridgeDrive is compatible with efficient ODE solvers, enabling real-time deployment. Experiments on the Bench2Drive benchmark show that BridgeDrive achieves state-of-the-art performance, improving the success rate by 7.72% over prior arts.

## Method Summary
BridgeDrive leverages anchor-guided diffusion models to translate coarse anchor trajectories into fine-grained, context-aware trajectory plans. The method uses a principled diffusion framework that ensures symmetry between forward and denoising processes, compatible with efficient ODE solvers for real-time deployment. The core innovation is formulating planning as a diffusion bridge between anchor and trajectory endpoints rather than noise-to-data, using geometric path waypoints (equal-spaced coordinates) instead of temporal ones for improved generalization.

## Key Results
- BridgeDrive achieves state-of-the-art performance on Bench2Drive benchmark
- Improves success rate by 7.72% over prior arts
- Geometric waypoints improve success rate by 5.46% to 15.09% over temporal waypoints
- Optimal anchor count is 60, balancing diversity and classifier accuracy

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1: Symmetric Diffusion Bridge for Anchor-to-Trajectory Translation
The diffusion bridge formulation provides theoretically consistent translation from coarse anchors to refined trajectories by maintaining forward-backward symmetry. The forward bridge SDE constructs a Gaussian transition kernel that interpolates between ground-truth trajectory and anchor. The reverse process uses a PF-ODE solver with score function approximation to denoise from anchor to refined trajectory. Critically, at t=0, coefficients satisfy a_0=c_0=0, b_0=1, ensuring the denoiser is trained to recover x_0 exactly.

### Mechanism 2: Anchor Prior for Constraining Multi-Modal Planning
Pre-computed anchor trajectories from K-means clustering of expert data provide strong priors that reduce planning complexity and enforce safety constraints. Anchors form a discrete set of atomic building blocks that planners use to construct solutions, which can dramatically reduce planning complexity. During inference, a classifier selects the most appropriate anchor given scene context, constraining the denoising process to a localized region of trajectory space around sensible behaviors.

### Mechanism 3: Geometric Path Waypoints for Improved Generalization
Separating path geometry (equal-spaced coordinates) from speed (scalar) improves generalization compared to temporal waypoints where speed is encoded in spacing. Trajectory representation x = (x_geo, v) where x_geo uses equal geometric spacing (e.g., every 1 meter). A separate speed scalar v is predicted. This decouples learning path patterns from speed adaptation, making geometric path patterns more consistent across speeds than temporal patterns.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:** Inputs (LiDAR, Camera, Target Point) → Perception Module (TransFuser++ backbone, frozen) → BEV segmentation, bounding boxes, fused features z → Anchor Classifier h_φ(z, Y) → Selected anchor x_T = y → Diffusion Bridge Denoiser x_θ(x_t, t, x_T, z) → Iterative denoising via ODE solver (20 steps) → Output: Geometric path waypoints + speed (x_geo, v)

- **Critical path:** The anchor classifier runs once per frame; the denoiser runs 20 ODE steps. The perception backbone is the computational bottleneck (~0.05s), not the diffusion process itself (applied to small 8×2 trajectory matrix).

- **Design tradeoffs:**
  - Anchor count (N_anchor=20-60): More anchors increase diversity but harm classifier accuracy. Current optimum at 60.
  - ODE steps (T=20): First-order DDIM sufficient; higher-order solvers don't justify cost for trajectory-scale data.
  - Geometric vs temporal waypoints: Trade off between generalization and field convention; geometric works better on Bench2Drive but not universally validated.
  - Frozen perception vs end-to-end: Perception backbone is pre-trained and frozen during diffusion training; trades joint optimization for training stability.

- **Failure signatures:**
  - Wrong anchor selection: If classifier selects inappropriate anchor, success rate degrades significantly (74.99% → 69.09% for 2nd-best anchor).
  - Out-of-distribution scenarios: Overtaking abort with oncoming traffic absent from training data causes collisions.
  - Comfortness degradation: Model prioritizes safety over comfort, showing "frequent or poorly timed braking."
  - Mode collapse without anchors: Full diffusion (no anchors) fails to adhere to target points for lane changes.

- **First 3 experiments:**

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: Can incorporating Vision-Language Action (VLA) priors or reinforcement learning post-training effectively resolve BridgeDrive's failure in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios?
- Basis in paper: The conclusion states the model "struggles to handle out-of-distribution scenarios" and suggests this "may be overcome by incorporating prior knowledge from VLA and post-training with reinforcement learning."
- Why unresolved: The current model relies on imitation learning from data with privileged expert information, lacking diverse failure recovery data.
- What evidence would resolve it: Demonstrated success rate improvements on specific OOD scenarios after VLA integration or RL fine-tuning.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Is it possible to distill BridgeDrive into a single-step model to maximize inference speed without significantly sacrificing generation quality?
- Basis in paper: The authors note that "further acceleration can be achieved by distilling our model into a one-step model," citing Xie et al. (2024).
- Why unresolved: The current method relies on multi-step ODE solvers (e.g., 20 steps), which are computationally heavier than a direct regression.
- What evidence would resolve it: A comparison of driving scores and latency between the current diffusion bridge and a distilled one-step variant.

### Open Question 3
- Question: Can BridgeDrive maintain state-of-the-art performance when relying solely on camera inputs without LiDAR data?
- Basis in paper: The authors list as a limitation that "BridgeDrive also requires lidar input," unlike some competitors, and state future work will investigate its capability "in the absence of lidar."
- Why unresolved: The current architecture depends on fused LiDAR and camera features; the effectiveness of the diffusion bridge policy on sparse visual features alone is untested.
- What evidence would resolve it: Bench2Drive evaluation results for a camera-only variant of BridgeDrive compared to LiDAR-enabled baselines.

## Limitations
- Performance gains over DiffusionDrive stem partly from methodological differences and partly from architectural choices, making attribution difficult without controlled ablation studies.
- The anchor-based diffusion bridge assumes expert driving behaviors are adequately captured by ~20-60 discrete anchors, which may not hold for highly diverse or rare scenarios.
- Performance is sensitive to anchor selection quality, with significant degradation when using suboptimal anchors.

## Confidence
- **High confidence:** The symmetric diffusion bridge formulation improves training stability and consistency (supported by ablation and theoretical justification).
- **Medium confidence:** Anchor guidance effectively constrains planning and improves success rate (empirical results are strong but sensitive to anchor selection quality).
- **Low confidence:** Geometric waypoints universally outperform temporal ones (evidence is dataset-specific and not yet field-consensus).
