# Flower Across Time and Media: Sentiment Analysis of Tang Song Poetry and Visual Correspondence

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2505.04785
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.04785
- Reference count: 17
- Primary result: BERT-based sentiment analysis tracks emotional shifts in Tang-Song poetry, correlating peony joy with Tang exuberance and plum sadness with Song literati introspection.

## Executive Summary
This study employs a fine-tuned BERT model to analyze emotional valences in Tang and Song dynasty poetry, focusing on peony and plum blossom imagery. By quantifying sentiment across 100 randomly selected poems, the research identifies measurable emotional transitions: Tang-era peony poetry shows joy and anger reflecting courtly exuberance and decline, while Song-era plum poetry shifts to sadness and fear mirroring literati introspection and political instability. The study then correlates these textual sentiment shifts with visual culture in contemporaneous paintings, demonstrating that poetic sentiment both reflects and anticipates aesthetic trends in material culture. Key metrics include a strong negative correlation between peony joy and plum sadness (r = -0.82, p < 0.01) and a positive correlation between plum fear and Jin dynasty invasions (r = 0.76).

## Method Summary
The study fine-tunes a Chinese BERT model on a 7-class emotion taxonomy to analyze Tang and Song poetry corpora (Quan Tangshi and Quan Songci). After filtering for peony and plum blossom poems, 50 poems from each era are randomly sampled. The BERT model generates probability distributions over seven emotions for each poem, producing sentiment scores that are aggregated by era and motif. These scores are then correlated with historical events and used to construct an argument about bidirectional influence between textual and visual culture, with qualitative validation drawn from art historical analysis of contemporaneous paintings.

## Key Results
- Strong negative correlation between peony joy (Tang) and plum sadness (Song): r = -0.82, p < 0.01
- Positive correlation between plum fear and Jin dynasty invasions: r = 0.76
- Emotional shifts in poetry parallel aesthetic transitions in paintings from bold, colorful peony imagery to monochrome, minimalist plum depictions

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: A fine-tuned BERT model can capture and quantify nuanced emotional valences in classical Chinese poetry, transforming qualitative literary expression into a time-series dataset.
- Mechanism: The study employs a transformer-based architecture (BERT) which uses contextual embeddings to represent poetic verses. A fine-tuning process adapts the model to the specific domain of classical Chinese, mapping its high-dimensional output onto a seven-category emotion taxonomy (e.g., joy, sadness, fear). This allows for the scoring of each poem on multiple emotional axes, providing a numerical representation of sentiment that can be tracked over time.
- Core assumption: The chosen seven-category emotion taxonomy is a valid and sufficiently granular framework for mapping the complex, metaphorical affective range of Tang-Song poetry. The mechanism also relies on the assumption that the model's training data allows it to correctly interpret archaic lexicon and rhetorical devices without significant semantic drift.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] Mentions "BERT-based sentiment analysis to track emotional shifts... identifies measurable emotional transitions."
  - [section] Section III, "BERT Emotion Taxonomy and Cultural Relevance," details the use of the seven categories and the fine-tuning rationale.
  - [corpus] A neighbor paper, "Capabilities and Evaluation Biases of Large Language Models in Classical Chinese Poetry Generation," suggests this is an active area of research but also highlights potential evaluation challenges.
- Break condition: The mechanism's reliability would be compromised if the model's fine-tuning data was insufficient, leading to systematic misclassification of classical metaphors (e.g., interpreting a lament disguised as praise as literal joy). It would also fail if the seven emotion categories prove too reductive for the source material.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Quantified sentiment shifts in poetry show a strong statistical correlation with dynastic transitions and political instability, suggesting poetry acts as a cultural barometer.
- Mechanism: The mechanism treats the generated sentiment scores as quantitative variables. It calculates Pearson correlation coefficients between these scores and categorical variables (dynastic era, floral motif) or external historical data (e.g., frequency of Jin dynasty invasions). The strong negative correlation (r = -0.82) between "peony joy" (Tang) and "plum sadness" (Song) and the positive correlation (r = 0.76) between "plum fear" and invasions are used as evidence of a link between artistic sentiment and socio-political reality.
- Core assumption: The choice of floral motif (peony vs. plum) is not random but is a deliberate, culturally-loaded carrier of specific symbolic and emotional weight. The mechanism further assumes a direct link where political instability is causally or reflectively related to an increase in poetic expressions of negative emotions like fear and sadness.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] States the key metrics: "strong negative correlation between peony joy and plum sadness (r = -0.82, p < 0.01) and a positive correlation between plum fear and Jin dynasty invasions (r = 0.76)."
  - [section] Section IV, "ANALYTICAL PROCESS AND FINDINGS IN SENTIMENTAL STUDY," presents these statistics and links them to historical events.
  - [corpus] The corpus provides no direct evidence to validate the specific historical claims about the Jin invasions or Tang-Song transitions.
- Break condition: This mechanism breaks if the sample of 100 poems is too small, making the correlation coefficients spurious. It would also fail if the correlation is misattributed—for instance, if the rise in "plum fear" is due to a change in literary fashion rather than an increase in actual fear caused by invasions.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Emotional trajectories identified in poetry have parallel "visual correlatives" in contemporaneous paintings, demonstrating a bidirectional influence between textual and visual culture.
- Mechanism: This is an intermedial correspondence mechanism. The study links its quantitative sentiment findings to qualitative observations from art history. It argues that the shift from Tang "joy" to Song "sadness" in poetry is mirrored by a visual shift from bold, colorful, symmetrical peony paintings to monochrome, minimalist, asymmetric plum paintings. The shared emotional tone is presented as the connecting tissue between the two distinct media.
- Core assumption: Aesthetic shifts in different media (poetry, painting) are driven by a shared underlying cultural sentiment, and artists in both domains were responding to similar socio-political and intellectual currents. It assumes that visual style (e.g., ink monochrome) can be reliably interpreted as an expression of a specific emotion (e.g., sadness, solitude).
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] Claims the findings "demonstrate that poetic sentiment both reflects and anticipates aesthetic trends in material culture."
  - [section] Section V, "VISUAL CORRELATIVES IN PAINTINGS," draws explicit parallels, such as linking the "shift toward ink monochrome" to "the rise in sadness (0.58) and fear (0.31)" in the poetic data.
  - [corpus] The corpus provides no papers on art history or cross-modal analysis to support or refute this specific claim.
- Break condition: The mechanism would break if the visual changes were driven by unrelated factors, such as the availability of new materials (e.g., a specific type of ink or paper) or technical innovations, rather than a shared emotional trajectory. It is also vulnerable if the qualitative interpretation of the paintings is subjective and not widely accepted by art historians.

## Architecture Onboarding

### Component map:
Data Ingestion & Sampling Module (Quan Tangshi, Quan Songci) -> NLP Core (BERT-based) -> Quantification Engine -> Correlation & Analysis Layer -> Cross-Modal Validation Interface

### Critical path:
1. **Data Curation:** The process is initiated by defining the corpus and motifs. Errors or bias in this sampling will affect all downstream results.
2. **Model Fine-Tuning & Inference:** The BERT model is adapted to the classical Chinese domain. This is a critical technical step; poor performance here invalidates the sentiment scores.
3. **Sentiment Aggregation & Correlation:** Generates the study's key quantitative metrics (r = -0.82, r = 0.76).
4. **Cross-Modal Synthesis:** The final, most interpretive step where statistical findings are linked to art history to form the core conclusion.

### Design tradeoffs:
- **Sample Size vs. Canonical Bias:** The choice of a small, random sample (100 poems) avoids over-reliance on famous poets but may reduce the statistical power and generalizability of the findings.
- **Computational vs. Expert Analysis:** The architecture blends quantitative NLP with qualitative art history. This allows for scalable pattern detection but introduces subjectivity in the final cross-modal interpretation.
- **Emotion Taxonomy:** Using 7 categories provides more nuance than a binary (positive/negative) scale but increases the complexity of the classification task and the potential for ambiguity between categories (e.g., sadness vs. fear).

### Failure signatures:
- **Model Drift on Metaphor:** The fine-tuned model may consistently misclassify a specific rhetorical device, such as irony, leading to systematically skewed sentiment data.
- **Spurious Correlation:** A high correlation coefficient (e.g., r > 0.9) between an emotion and an unrelated historical variable would suggest a confounding factor or data leakage.
- **Over-Interpretation:** The qualitative argument may become untethered from the quantitative data, with art historical claims being only loosely supported by the sentiment analysis.

### First 3 experiments:
1. **Motif Ablation Test:** Re-run the entire analysis on a control set of poems featuring a neutral motif (e.g., "mountain"). If the same emotional trends appear, they may be a property of the era, not the floral symbol.
2. **Method Triangulation:** Run a lexicon-based sentiment analysis on the same corpus in parallel with the BERT model. Comparing results would highlight which findings are robust to the choice of NLP method.
3. **Sample Size Scaling:** Incrementally increase the poem sample size (e.g., from 100 to 1000) and re-calculate the key correlations. This tests the stability of the statistical claims and checks if the initial sample was sufficient.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
- Can the sentiment analysis framework be generalized to other botanical motifs (e.g., orchids, bamboo) or historical periods beyond the Tang-Song transition?
- Does the observed emotional shift persist when controlling for the specific floral motif by analyzing plum blossoms in Tang poetry and peonies in Song poetry?
- Do the high correlation coefficients (e.g., $r = -0.82$) remain stable when expanding the sample size from 100 poems to the full literary canon?

## Limitations
- The sample size of 100 poems may not capture the full variance of the Quan Tangshi and Quan Songci collections, risking statistical anomalies or spurious correlations.
- The cross-modal argument linking textual sentiment to visual style depends on subjective interpretation of paintings, which lacks validation from art historians.
- The study's conclusions about bidirectional influence between poetry and painting may conflate correlation with causation, as visual changes could be driven by technical or material factors rather than shared cultural sentiment.
