# To MT or not to MT: An eye-tracking study on the reception by Dutch readers of different translation and creativity levels

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2504.19850
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.19850
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Creative units significantly increase cognitive load across all translation modalities, with the highest effect in human translation and lowest in machine translation.

## Executive Summary
This eye-tracking study examines how machine translation, post-editing, human translation, and original source text affect Dutch readers' cognitive load, particularly focusing on creative units in a fictional text. Using eye-tracking metrics and retrospective think-aloud interviews, the research finds that units of creative potential significantly increase cognitive load across all modalities, with the strongest effect in human translation. While translation errors were expected to increase cognitive load, no significant effect was observed, possibly due to skim reading behavior in machine translation. The qualitative interviews revealed that readers enjoyed creative units and related them to personal experiences, suggesting a link between cognitive load and engagement.

## Method Summary
The study used eye-tracking to measure cognitive load while eight native Dutch speakers read "2BR02B," a 2548-word short story translated into four modalities: machine translation, post-editing, human translation, and original source text. The text was annotated for Units of Creative Potential (UCPs) and errors. Participants were randomly assigned to conditions (two per modality), and eye movements were recorded using an EyeLink Portable Duo eye tracker at 2000Hz. Two datasets were created: word-level metrics and segment-level normalized metrics. Data analysis combined generalized additive mixed effect models for TFD with non-parametric tests for other metrics, triangulated with retrospective think-aloud interviews.

## Key Results
- Units of creative potential significantly increase cognitive load across all translation modalities
- Human translation shows the strongest creative effect, while machine translation shows the lowest
- No significant effect of translation errors on cognitive load was observed, potentially due to skim reading behavior
- Readers reported enjoying creative units and relating them to personal experiences

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The study's methodology effectively captures the relationship between translation creativity and reader cognitive load by combining physiological measurements (eye-tracking) with subjective experiences (RTA interviews). This dual approach allows researchers to identify both immediate cognitive responses and reflective interpretations, revealing that while creative units demand more processing effort, they also enhance reader engagement and enjoyment. The eye-tracking metrics serve as objective proxies for mental effort, while the qualitative data explains the underlying reader experiences that quantitative measures alone cannot capture.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
- **Do readers adopt skim-reading strategies in error-dense machine translation texts, thereby artificially lowering the cognitive load measurements that would otherwise be elevated by errors?** The authors hypothesize that increased skim reading in MT nullified the expected cognitive load increase from errors, as suggested by RTA interviews where readers "gave up trying to understand the text."
- **Is paragraph-level analysis more effective than document-level analysis for detecting the effects of translation modality and creativity on cognitive load?** The study used document-level approach which may have introduced too much variance; future research should focus on paragraph-level analysis with selected UCPs.
- **How does the translation modality of Large Language Models (LLMs) impact cognitive load and enjoyment compared to standard NMT and human translation in literary texts?** The authors' next experiment will include LLMs alongside existing modalities to explore the relationship between creativity and reader experience.

## Limitations
- Small sample size (N=8) creates substantial uncertainty in generalizability and limits confidence in effect sizes
- Single source text (2BR02B) may not represent broader literary translation contexts
- Analysis combines all error types into a single binary variable, potentially masking differential effects of various error categories

## Confidence
- **High Confidence**: Creative units increase cognitive load across all modalities; human translation shows strongest creative effect
- **Medium Confidence**: Machine translation shows lowest creative effect; readers engage differently with creative units
- **Low Confidence**: Lack of significant error effects on cognitive load is tentative, potentially explained by skim reading behavior
