# Integrating Probabilistic Trees and Causal Networks for Clinical and Epidemiological Data

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2501.15973
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.15973
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Key outcome:** PCF integrates Causal Bayesian Networks and Probability Trees to bridge prediction and causal reasoning in healthcare, validated on MIMIC-IV, Framingham Heart Study, and Diabetes datasets with comparable predictive performance to traditional ML while enabling causal inference and hypothetical intervention simulation

## Executive Summary
This study introduces the Probabilistic Causal Fusion (PCF) framework, which integrates Causal Bayesian Networks (CBNs) and ensembles of Probability Trees (PTrees) to bridge the gap between prediction and causal reasoning in healthcare. PCF leverages causal relationships from CBNs to structure PTrees, enabling quantification of factor impacts and simulation of hypothetical interventions. Validated on three real-world datasets (MIMIC-IV, Framingham Heart Study, and Diabetes), PCF demonstrated predictive performance comparable to traditional ML models while providing additional causal reasoning capabilities. To enhance interpretability, PCF incorporates sensitivity analysis and SHAP, quantifying the influence of causal parameters and highlighting feature importance in predictive modeling.

## Method Summary
The PCF framework combines Causal Bayesian Networks and Probability Tree ensembles to integrate causal reasoning with predictive modeling. The approach uses CBNs to capture causal relationships between variables, which then guide the structure and training of PTrees. This integration allows the model to make predictions while simultaneously providing insights into causal mechanisms and the impact of potential interventions. The framework incorporates SHAP values for interpretability and sensitivity analysis to assess the influence of causal parameters on predictions.

## Key Results
- Demonstrated predictive performance comparable to traditional ML models across three real-world healthcare datasets
- Enabled causal reasoning capabilities through integration of Causal Bayesian Networks with Probability Tree ensembles
- Incorporated SHAP and sensitivity analysis for enhanced interpretability and quantification of causal parameter influence

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by leveraging the complementary strengths of Causal Bayesian Networks and Probability Trees. CBNs provide a structured representation of causal relationships that guide the learning process of PTrees, ensuring that the predictive model respects known causal structures. This integration allows for both accurate prediction and meaningful causal inference, addressing the traditional trade-off between predictive accuracy and interpretability in healthcare applications.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:**
CBN Structure Definition -> PTrees Ensemble Training -> SHAP Integration -> Sensitivity Analysis

**Critical Path:**
1. Define Causal Bayesian Network structure based on domain knowledge
2. Train Probability Tree ensemble using CBN structure as guidance
3. Generate predictions and calculate SHAP values
4. Perform sensitivity analysis on causal parameters

**Design Tradeoffs:**
- Complexity vs interpretability: More complex CBN structures may improve causal reasoning but reduce interpretability
- Prediction accuracy vs causal inference: Balancing model complexity to maintain both predictive and causal capabilities
- Computational cost vs ensemble size: Larger ensembles improve performance but increase training time
- Domain knowledge vs data-driven learning: Tradeoff between expert-defined CBN structures and purely data-driven approaches

**Failure Signatures:**
- Poor predictive performance may indicate mismatch between CBN structure and data patterns
- Inconsistent SHAP values could suggest issues with ensemble training or causal structure
- High sensitivity to parameter changes may indicate unstable causal relationships
- Computational bottlenecks may arise from large ensemble sizes or complex CBN structures

**First Experiments:**
1. Validate predictive performance on held-out test data compared to baseline models
2. Test SHAP value consistency across different ensemble members
3. Conduct sensitivity analysis by perturbing causal parameters and measuring prediction changes
