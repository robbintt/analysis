# Kriging and Gaussian Process Interpolation for Georeferenced Data Augmentation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2501.07183
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.07183
- Reference count: 3
- One-line primary result: GP-based methods, particularly GP-COMB, significantly improve regression performance while requiring fewer additional data points for geo-referenced data augmentation

## Executive Summary
This study evaluates interpolation techniques for augmenting geo-referenced datasets to improve predictive models for Commelina benghalensis L. presence in sugarcane plots in La Réunion. The researchers compare Gaussian processes (GPs) with various kernels and kriging with different variograms as data augmentation strategies. The study demonstrates that interpolation-based augmentation can effectively enhance predictive performance, particularly for datasets with limited initial samples.

## Method Summary
The researchers implemented two main interpolation approaches: Gaussian processes with RBF, Matern, and combined kernels, and kriging with spherical, exponential, Gaussian, and Matérn variograms. They generated augmented datasets by applying these interpolators to an initial dataset of 85 georeferenced samples, creating 20 different augmented versions for each method. The augmented data was then used to train various regression algorithms (SVR, RFR, XGBR, DTR, KNR, BR) and evaluated using spatial cross-validation with RMSE and R² metrics. The study focused on predicting invasive weed presence in agricultural settings, providing a practical application for the interpolation techniques.

## Key Results
- GP-based methods, particularly GP-COMB, significantly improved regression performance while requiring fewer additional data points
- Kriging showed slightly lower performance but provided more homogeneous spatial coverage than GP methods
- Data augmentation via interpolation effectively enhanced predictive models for limited geo-referenced datasets

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Data augmentation through interpolation works by creating synthetic samples that respect the underlying spatial structure of the data. By leveraging the covariance patterns captured by Gaussian processes or variograms in kriging, the augmented points maintain realistic spatial relationships while filling gaps in the sampling distribution. This expanded dataset provides regression models with more training examples that better represent the true spatial variability, leading to improved generalization performance.

## Foundational Learning
1. **Gaussian Process Kernels** - Different kernel functions (RBF, Matern, combined) encode different assumptions about spatial correlation structure; needed to capture various types of spatial dependencies; quick check: examine kernel parameter values to understand correlation length scales
2. **Variogram Models** - Spherical, exponential, Gaussian, and Matérn variograms model spatial autocorrelation at different ranges and smoothness levels; needed to quantify spatial dependence for kriging; quick check: plot experimental variograms to assess model fit
3. **Spatial Cross-Validation** - Leave-one-location-out or block cross-validation prevents spatial autocorrelation bias in model evaluation; needed to ensure unbiased performance estimates; quick check: verify test points are spatially separated from training points

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Initial Data -> Interpolation (GP/Kriging) -> Augmented Dataset -> Regression Models -> Performance Evaluation

**Critical Path:** The core workflow follows: collect initial georeferenced samples → apply interpolation to generate synthetic points → combine with original data → train regression models → evaluate using spatial cross-validation

**Design Tradeoffs:** GP methods offer flexibility through kernel combinations and can capture complex spatial patterns but may be computationally intensive; kriging provides more homogeneous spatial coverage and interpretable parameters but may be less flexible for non-stationary processes

**Failure Signatures:** Poor interpolation performance may manifest as unrealistic synthetic points (e.g., negative weed presence probabilities), over-smooth predictions that miss local variations, or computational failures with large datasets; check residual patterns and synthetic point distributions

## Limitations

- Performance comparisons between kriging and Gaussian processes do not explore computational efficiency or scalability to larger datasets
- The study does not address potential overfitting risks associated with interpolation-based augmentation
