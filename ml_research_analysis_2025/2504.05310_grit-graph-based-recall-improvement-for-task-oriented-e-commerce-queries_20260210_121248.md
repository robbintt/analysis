# GRIT: Graph-based Recall Improvement for Task-oriented E-commerce Queries

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2504.05310
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.05310
- Authors: Hrishikesh Kulkarni; Surya Kallumadi; Sean MacAvaney; Nazli Goharian; Ophir Frieder
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Graph-based method improves recall in e-commerce product search by up to 6.3%

## Executive Summary
GRIT addresses a critical challenge in e-commerce search systems: improving recall for task-oriented queries in the first stage of multi-stage retrieval pipelines. When relevant products are missed in initial retrieval, they cannot be recovered in later stages, leading to suboptimal search outcomes. The paper proposes a graph-based approach that builds product-product similarity networks using user click or annotation data, then prioritizes more relevant neighbors over less relevant initial candidates. This method significantly improves recall while maintaining relevance quality, addressing a fundamental limitation in current e-commerce search architectures.

## Method Summary
GRIT constructs a product-product similarity graph using user interaction data, where nodes represent products and edges represent similarity based on co-clicks or annotations. The method identifies and prioritizes more relevant product neighbors from this graph over less relevant candidates returned by initial retrieval methods. This graph-based re-ranking approach is designed to enhance the first-stage retrieval results, ensuring a more comprehensive and relevant product set that improves overall system recall. The approach is particularly effective for task-oriented queries where users have specific goals requiring multiple product considerations.

## Key Results
- Achieves up to 6.3% recall improvement for task-oriented e-commerce queries
- Effectively improves recall while maintaining relevance quality in first-stage retrieval
- Demonstrates robustness across different parameters and works with various first-stage retrieval methods

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
GRIT leverages user interaction data to build meaningful product relationships that traditional retrieval methods might miss. By constructing a graph where products are connected based on actual user behavior (clicks or annotations), the system captures latent semantic relationships between products that aren't evident from text-based features alone. The graph structure allows the method to identify products that are contextually relevant to query intents, even when they don't share direct keyword matches with the query. This approach is particularly powerful for task-oriented queries where users need to consider multiple related products for a specific goal, as the graph can surface products that complete or complement each other based on observed user behavior patterns.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: User Query -> First-stage Retrieval -> GRIT Graph Processing -> Enhanced Product Set -> Downstream Ranking

**Critical Path**: The most critical components are the product-product similarity graph construction and the neighbor prioritization algorithm. The graph must accurately capture product relationships, and the prioritization must effectively distinguish between relevant and less relevant neighbors. Failures in either component directly impact the recall improvement capability.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The method trades computational complexity in graph construction and processing for improved recall. Building and maintaining the product similarity graph requires significant storage and processing resources, but this investment pays off in improved first-stage retrieval quality. The approach also requires access to user interaction data, which may not be available in all e-commerce contexts.

**Failure Signatures**: The method may fail when user interaction data is sparse or biased, leading to inaccurate product similarity graphs. It may also underperform when queries are highly specific or novel, with insufficient graph connections to surface relevant products. Additionally, the method assumes that user click patterns are good indicators of product relevance, which may not hold in all contexts.

## Limitations
- Heavy reliance on user click or annotation data, which may introduce biases and be limited by data availability
- Lack of detailed statistical validation for the 6.3% recall improvement claim
- Limited empirical evidence for robustness claims across different parameters and retrieval methods

## Confidence

**Graph-based neighbor prioritization effectiveness**: Medium Confidence
- The conceptual approach is sound and experimental results show improvements
- However, lack of detailed statistical analysis and limited evaluation across diverse retrieval baselines reduces generalizability confidence

**Robustness across parameters**: Low Confidence
- Claims of robustness are asserted but not thoroughly demonstrated with comprehensive sensitivity analyses
- Based on narrow parameter exploration rather than extensive testing

**Generalizability to different retrieval methods**: Low Confidence
- Effectiveness with various first-stage retrieval methods is claimed but not empirically validated across diverse baselines
