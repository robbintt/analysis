# Riemannian Flow Matching for Disentangled Graph Domain Adaptation

## Executive Summary
This paper tackles two fundamental challenges in graph domain adaptation: structural degeneration where hierarchical and semantic representations become entangled in Euclidean space, and optimization instability from adversarial minimax training dynamics. The authors propose DisRFM, a geometry-aware framework that embeds graphs in Riemannian manifolds using polar coordinates to disentangle structure (radial) from semantics (angular). By replacing adversarial alignment with stable Riemannian flow matching, the method learns a vector field to transport source features toward target features along geodesic paths while maintaining both topological preservation and semantic discrimination.

The framework enforces radial Wasserstein alignment for topological preservation and angular clustering for semantic discrimination, ensuring stable convergence through regression-based transport rather than adversarial optimization. Theoretical analysis proves asymptotic stability and derives a tighter generalization bound compared to existing approaches. Extensive experiments demonstrate DisRFM consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods across multiple datasets with both structure-based and feature-based domain shifts, achieving significant improvements in classification accuracy while maintaining stable training dynamics.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by leveraging Riemannian geometry to naturally separate hierarchical structure from semantic content. By embedding graphs in manifolds with polar coordinates, structural information becomes radial (distance from origin) while semantic information becomes angular (direction). This separation prevents the entanglement that occurs in Euclidean space. The flow matching approach replaces unstable adversarial training with stable regression-based transport, learning a vector field that smoothly transports source features along geodesic paths to match target distributions. The combination of radial Wasserstein alignment for topology and angular clustering for semantics ensures both structural preservation and semantic discrimination are maintained throughout the adaptation process.

## Foundational Learning
- **Riemannian manifolds**: Curved spaces where distances and angles are measured differently than Euclidean space; needed to naturally separate structure and semantics in graph embeddings
  - Quick check: Verify that geodesic distances on the manifold preserve graph structural relationships
- **Polar coordinates**: Coordinate system where points are represented by distance from origin and angle; enables disentanglement of radial (structural) and angular (semantic) information
  - Quick check: Confirm that structural variations correspond to radial changes while semantic variations correspond to angular changes
- **Wasserstein alignment**: Optimal transport framework for aligning probability distributions; used here for radial structural alignment
  - Quick check: Measure alignment quality using Wasserstein distance between source and target radial distributions
- **Flow matching**: Learning a vector field to transport one distribution to another; replaces adversarial training with stable regression
  - Quick check: Verify that the learned vector field provides smooth, stable transport without oscillations
- **Geodesic paths**: Shortest paths on curved manifolds; provide natural transport trajectories in Riemannian space
  - Quick check: Ensure transport follows geodesics rather than arbitrary paths in the manifold
- **Adversarial minimax dynamics**: Training framework where generator and discriminator compete; known to cause optimization instability
  - Quick check: Compare training stability metrics between flow matching and adversarial approaches

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map:
Graphs -> Polar coordinate embedding -> Radial Wasserstein alignment + Angular clustering -> Riemannian flow matching -> Adapted source features

Critical path:
Input graphs → Manifold embedding (polar coordinates) → Structure-semantics disentanglement → Flow matching transport → Adapted features for target domain

Design tradeoffs:
- Riemannian geometry provides natural disentanglement but increases computational complexity
- Flow matching ensures stability but may be slower than adversarial methods
- Polar coordinates enable clear separation but require careful manifold selection

Failure signatures:
- If angular clustering fails, semantic discrimination will degrade
- If radial alignment is poor, structural preservation will be compromised
- If flow matching doesn't converge, adaptation will fail entirely
