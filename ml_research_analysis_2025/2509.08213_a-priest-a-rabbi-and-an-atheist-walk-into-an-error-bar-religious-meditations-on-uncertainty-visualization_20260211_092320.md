# A Priest, a Rabbi, and an Atheist Walk Into an Error Bar: Religious Meditations on Uncertainty Visualization

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.08213
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.08213
- Authors: Michael Correll; Lane Harrison
- Reference count: 37
- Primary result: Philosophical critique of uncertainty visualization practices arguing they rely on unexamined statistical rituals and narrow epistemic assumptions

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a provocative philosophical critique of uncertainty visualization practices, particularly error bars, arguing that they rest on unexamined statistical rituals and narrow Western epistemic assumptions. Through illustrative vignettes from various religious traditions—including Jewish, Christian, Islamic, and Buddhist perspectives—the authors demonstrate how different epistemic frameworks conceptualize uncertainty, doubt, and knowledge differently from mainstream statistical practice. The paper challenges the visualization community to reconsider their metaphysical foundations and expand their design approaches to accommodate diverse ways of understanding uncertainty.

The authors argue that current visualization practices often present a false dichotomy between certainty and uncertainty, failing to acknowledge the richness of alternative frameworks for reasoning about the unknown. By drawing parallels between religious approaches to doubt and uncertainty visualization, they highlight the need for more inclusive, culturally-aware design practices that recognize multiple valid ways of interpreting and communicating uncertain information.

## Method Summary
This paper employs a philosophical and literary approach rather than empirical methodology. The authors use illustrative vignettes from religious texts and traditions to critique assumptions in uncertainty visualization practice. They draw on primary sources from Maimonides, Talmudic passages, The Cloud of Unknowing, Islamic hadiths, and Biblical passages, alongside 37 citations from uncertainty visualization and statistics literature. The method involves qualitative analysis of religious epistemic frameworks and their potential implications for visualization design, positioning the work as a provocation rather than a prescriptive guide.

## Key Results
- Current uncertainty visualization practices rely on unexamined statistical rituals that may not align with diverse epistemic frameworks
- Religious traditions offer alternative perspectives on knowledge by negation, the role of chance and discernment, and varying standards of evidence
- The paper challenges visualization community to expand conceptual foundations and consider culturally diverse approaches to uncertainty communication

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The paper works by reframing uncertainty visualization as a fundamentally philosophical and cultural practice rather than purely technical one. By exposing the metaphysical assumptions underlying statistical visualization techniques, it opens space for alternative approaches that might better serve diverse user populations. The mechanism involves using religious traditions as illustrative examples of alternative epistemic frameworks, demonstrating that there are multiple valid ways to reason about uncertainty beyond the dominant statistical paradigm.

   - Why needed: Understanding these differences is crucial for designing visualizations that serve diverse global audiences
   - Quick check: Can you identify three different approaches to uncertainty from different cultural traditions?

2. **Knowledge by negation**: The concept that understanding what is definitively unknown or disproven can be as important as positive estimates
   - Why needed: Current visualizations focus almost exclusively on positive knowledge, missing opportunities to communicate negative knowledge
   - Quick check: How might a visualization represent "what we know is definitely not true" versus "what we estimate might be true"?

3. **Reference class problem**: The challenge that probability estimates depend heavily on how reference classes are defined and understood
   - Why needed: Without explicit reference classes, users may misinterpret probability estimates based on incorrect assumptions
   - Quick check: Can you identify the implicit reference class in a typical error bar visualization?

   - Basis: [explicit] The authors explicitly state this as an important philosophical consideration regarding the focus on positive knowledge
   - Why unresolved: Current practice focuses almost exclusively on positive estimates with error bounds
   - Evidence needed: Novel visualization encodings for negative knowledge, evaluated through user studies

2. What design approaches can make implicit priors and assumptions visible and legible to visualization users?
   - Basis: [explicit] The paper cites Mercator's Biblically-informed cartography and references work on eliciting predictions
   - Why unresolved: Current visualizations typically present sanitized outputs without revealing analyst's prior beliefs
   - Evidence needed: Design studies exploring different encodings for prior knowledge

3. How do different cultural or religious epistemic frameworks affect interpretation of and preference for uncertainty visualizations?
   - Basis: [explicit] The authors explicitly call for designing for diverse global communities with different ways of reasoning
   - Why unresolved: The field lacks systematic understanding of cultural influences on uncertainty interpretation
   - Evidence needed: Cross-cultural user studies measuring interpretation accuracy across populations

## Limitations
- Presents philosophical critique rather than empirical research, limiting generalizability
- Uses religious vignettes as metaphors which may be seen as tenuous analogies rather than rigorous frameworks
- Does not provide concrete alternative visualization designs or empirical validation
- Relies heavily on qualitative interpretation without systematic methodology for translating insights into design principles

## Confidence
- High confidence: The critique of current error bar practices and documented misinterpretation rates
- Medium confidence: The accuracy of religious text interpretations and their relevance to epistemic frameworks
- Low confidence: The practical applicability of proposed alternative approaches without empirical validation
