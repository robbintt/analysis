## Executive Summary
This paper proposes brain-inspired planning methods to improve generalization in reinforcement learning by leveraging spatial abstraction and hierarchical decomposition inspired by cognitive neuroscience. The approach aims to enable systematic generalization behaviors observed in humans by incorporating principles of spatial reasoning and hierarchical decision-making into RL algorithms. The research explores how brain-like planning mechanisms can address fundamental challenges in transferring learned policies to novel situations.

## Method Summary
The proposed methodology integrates spatial abstraction techniques and hierarchical planning structures inspired by human cognitive processes into reinforcement learning frameworks. The approach involves decomposing complex decision problems into multiple levels of abstraction, where higher-level planning guides lower-level execution through learned spatial representations. This hierarchical decomposition is designed to capture the compositional structure of tasks and enable more flexible transfer of learned behaviors across different environments and task variations.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The proposed brain-inspired planning approach works by leveraging hierarchical spatial representations that mirror human cognitive processing of complex environments. By decomposing decision-making into multiple abstraction levels, the method enables agents to capture compositional task structures and transfer learned behaviors more effectively. The spatial abstraction component allows for more efficient representation of environmental relationships, while hierarchical decomposition provides a framework for systematic generalization across task variations.

## Architecture Onboarding
Component map: Sensor Input -> Spatial Abstraction Layer -> Hierarchical Planner -> Policy Execution Layer -> Environment

Critical path: The agent receives environmental observations, processes them through spatial abstraction to extract relevant features, uses hierarchical planning to generate high-level goals and sub-goals, and executes low-level actions through the policy layer.

Design tradeoffs: The hierarchical approach increases computational complexity but potentially improves generalization and sample efficiency. Spatial abstraction requires additional representational capacity but enables more robust transfer.

Failure signatures: Poor spatial abstraction may lead to inability to recognize task-relevant features; inadequate hierarchical decomposition could result in suboptimal planning at higher levels.
