# Towards Personalized Quantum Federated Learning for Anomaly Detection

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.07471
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.07471
- Authors: Ratun Rahman; Sina Shaham; Dinh C. Nguyen
- Reference count: 40
- Introduces PQFL framework for quantum federated anomaly detection with 24.2% AUROC improvement

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a personalized quantum federated learning (PQFL) framework for anomaly detection in distributed quantum networks. The authors address the challenge of training effective models when quantum clients have heterogeneous hardware, circuit designs, and data encoding methods, which leads to inconsistent model performance. PQFL enhances local training with parameterized quantum circuits and classical optimizers, while incorporating a quantum-centric personalization strategy that adapts each client's model to its specific hardware and data representation. This balances local customization with global coordination.

The framework demonstrates significant improvements in anomaly detection accuracy, reducing false errors by up to 23%, and achieving gains of 24.2% in AUROC and 20.5% in AUPR compared to state-of-the-art methods. The approach highlights its effectiveness and scalability in practical quantum federated settings, though experimental validation appears limited to simulated environments.

## Method Summary
PQFL combines parameterized quantum circuits with classical optimizers for local training on heterogeneous quantum devices. Each client performs local optimization using their specific quantum hardware and data encoding, then participates in a federated averaging process with personalized model adaptation. The framework incorporates a quantum-centric personalization strategy that accounts for hardware variability, circuit design differences, and data representation methods. This allows each client to maintain a personalized model while contributing to a global federated model. The algorithm alternates between local quantum training, classical post-processing, and coordinated model updates across the federated network.

## Key Results
- Reduces false errors by up to 23% compared to baseline methods
- Achieves 24.2% improvement in AUROC for anomaly detection
- Achieves 20.5% improvement in AUPR over state-of-the-art approaches

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
PQFL works by addressing the fundamental challenge of heterogeneity in quantum federated learning. Traditional federated learning assumes homogeneous model architectures and data distributions, but quantum devices vary significantly in qubit counts, error rates, circuit depths, and encoding schemes. By incorporating personalized model adaptation at each client, PQFL allows quantum devices to optimize their local models based on their specific hardware constraints and data characteristics. The framework uses parameterized quantum circuits that can be tailored to each device's capabilities while maintaining compatibility with the federated aggregation protocol. This hybrid approach of quantum-local and classical-coordinated learning enables effective anomaly detection across diverse quantum infrastructures.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Data Encoding -> Local PQC Training -> Classical Post-processing -> Federated Aggregation -> Global Model Update

Critical Path: Each client encodes local data using device-specific methods → runs parameterized quantum circuit optimization → performs classical post-processing → sends model updates to server → server aggregates weighted updates → broadcasts updated global model → clients apply personalization layer

Design Tradeoffs: Prioritizes hardware adaptability over model uniformity, accepts higher communication overhead for better accuracy, balances quantum circuit depth against error rates, trades off global convergence speed for local optimization quality.

Failure Signatures: Performance degradation when circuit depth exceeds coherence times, accuracy collapse when personalization weight becomes too high, communication bottlenecks with large model sizes, aggregation instability with highly heterogeneous client capabilities.
