# Pre to Post-Treatment Glioblastoma MRI Prediction using a Latent Diffusion Model

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.17851
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.17851
- Authors: Alexandre G. Leclercq; Sébastien Bougleux; Noémie N. Moreau; Alexis Desmonts; Romain Hérault; Aurélien Corroyer-Dulmont
- Reference count: 19
- Primary result: Latent Diffusion Model successfully predicts post-treatment glioblastoma MRI from pre-treatment scans with PSNR 24.88±1.68 and SSIM 0.830±0.043

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) approach for predicting post-treatment MRI scans of glioblastoma patients using only pre-treatment imaging data. The model treats this as a slice-to-slice translation task, incorporating tumor localization and survival information through conditioning mechanisms. Trained on 140 GBM patients with paired pre- and post-treatment T1-Gd MRI scans, the system demonstrates feasibility in generating realistic post-treatment images with modest quantitative improvements when incorporating survival class information.

The study bridges radiomics and generative modeling by enabling prediction of tumor evolution from a single pre-treatment scan, potentially informing treatment planning and patient prognosis. While quantitative metrics show PSNR of 24.88±1.68 and SSIM of 0.830±0.043 for the best-performing model, qualitative assessment by a medical physicist yielded realism scores between 6.0-7.0 out of 10, indicating room for improvement in clinical applicability.

## Method Summary
The approach uses a Latent Diffusion Model architecture to predict post-treatment MRI from pre-treatment scans, incorporating tumor segmentation masks and survival information through conditioning. The model is trained on 140 GBM patients with paired pre- and post-treatment T1-Gd MRI, using manual tumor segmentations and survival outcomes. Image conditioning is achieved through concatenation of pre-treatment MRI and tumor masks in the channel dimension, while survival information is incorporated via class-conditional guidance using a 2-class survival classifier.

The training process involves denoising diffusion probabilistic modeling in the latent space, with classifier-free guidance enabling flexible control over the influence of conditioning information. The model generates post-treatment slices conditioned on input slices, though the study acknowledges the limitation of working in 2D rather than full 3D context. Evaluation combines quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) with qualitative assessment by a medical physicist to assess image realism.

## Key Results
- Latent Diffusion Model successfully predicts post-treatment glioblastoma MRI from pre-treatment scans
- Class conditioning with 2-class survival classifier improves PSNR to 24.88±1.68 and SSIM to 0.830±0.043
- Qualitative realism scores range from 6.0-7.0 out of 10 according to medical physicist assessment
- Model demonstrates feasibility but with limited clinical impact given current performance levels

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The model leverages diffusion probabilistic modeling in latent space to capture the complex relationship between pre-treatment imaging features and post-treatment tumor evolution. By incorporating both spatial tumor information and survival outcomes through conditioning, the system learns to generate realistic post-treatment images that reflect expected tumor response patterns. The classifier-free guidance mechanism allows flexible control over how strongly conditioning information influences the generation process, enabling trade-offs between fidelity to conditioning and diversity of outputs.

The latent space representation reduces computational complexity while preserving essential image features, making the diffusion process more tractable for medical imaging applications. The slice-to-slice approach, while limited compared to full 3D modeling, allows for efficient training and inference while still capturing important tumor evolution patterns across the imaging volume.

## Foundational Learning
- **Latent Diffusion Models**: Denoising diffusion probabilistic models operating in compressed latent space rather than pixel space, reducing computational cost while maintaining generation quality
- **Classifier-Free Guidance**: Training technique where the model learns both conditional and unconditional generation, enabling flexible control over conditioning influence during inference
- **Conditioning Mechanisms**: Methods for incorporating additional information (tumor masks, survival outcomes) into the generation process through channel concatenation and classifier guidance
- **Slice-to-Slice Translation**: Frame-by-frame prediction approach that processes 2D slices independently rather than full 3D volumes
- **Diffusion Probabilistic Modeling**: Iterative denoising process that gradually transforms random noise into structured output by reversing a noising process
- **Image Quality Metrics**: PSNR and SSIM as quantitative measures of generation fidelity, though limited in capturing clinically relevant differences

## Architecture Onboarding
Component map: Pre-treatment MRI -> Tumor Mask + Survival Class -> Latent Diffusion Model -> Post-treatment MRI

Critical path: Input preprocessing (resampling, normalization) -> Conditioning concatenation -> Latent diffusion denoising steps -> Image reconstruction -> Quality evaluation

Design tradeoffs: 2D slice processing vs 3D volumetric context (efficiency vs completeness), latent space compression vs pixel-level fidelity, classifier-free guidance vs explicit conditioning (flexibility vs control)

Failure signatures: Mode collapse in generated tumors, unrealistic contrast enhancement patterns, failure to capture expected tumor growth/shrinkage patterns, artifacts at slice boundaries

First experiments:
1. Test conditioning ablation by removing survival class information to quantify its impact on generation quality
2. Compare 2D slice generation vs 3D volume generation using the same model architecture
3. Evaluate different latent space dimensions to optimize the compression-reconstruction trade-off

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
The paper identifies several key limitations and areas for future investigation. The relatively small dataset of 140 patients may limit generalizability, and the slice-to-slice approach misses important 3D contextual information. The evaluation metrics (PSNR, SSIM) may not fully capture clinically relevant differences in tumor evolution prediction. The study calls for multi-rater clinical assessment with multiple neuroradiologists to validate realism scores and better assess clinical utility. Additionally, testing 3D-aware model architectures is identified as a priority to determine if volumetric context improves prediction accuracy.

## Limitations
- Small dataset of 140 patients may limit generalizability and model robustness
- Slice-to-slice translation approach misses important 3D volumetric context
- Evaluation metrics may not capture clinically relevant differences in tumor evolution prediction
- Single medical physicist assessment provides limited clinical validation scope
- Limited exploration of different conditioning strategies and their relative importance

## Confidence
- High confidence in technical implementation of LDM framework and basic feasibility
- Medium confidence in quantitative metrics showing modest improvements with class conditioning
- Low confidence in clinical utility and impact of predictions given limited evaluation scope

## Next Checks
1. Evaluate model performance on an independent, external dataset to assess generalizability
2. Conduct multi-rater clinical assessment with multiple neuroradiologists to validate realism scores
3. Test 3D-aware model architectures to determine if volumetric context improves prediction accuracy
