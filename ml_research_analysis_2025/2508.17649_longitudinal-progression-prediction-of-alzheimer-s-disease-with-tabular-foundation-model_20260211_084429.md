# Longitudinal Progression Prediction of Alzheimer's Disease with Tabular Foundation Model

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2508.17649
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.17649
- Reference count: 3
- Primary result: L2C-TabPFN achieves state-of-the-art accuracy for predicting ventricular volume in Alzheimer's disease progression

## Executive Summary
This work introduces L2C-TabPFN, a method that combines longitudinal-to-cross-sectional transformation with a pre-trained Tabular Foundation Model to predict Alzheimer's disease progression. The approach converts sequential patient records into fixed-length feature vectors, enabling robust forecasting of diagnostic status, cognitive scores, and ventricular volume using the TADPOLE dataset. While competitive on diagnostic and cognitive tasks, the model demonstrates state-of-the-art performance specifically for ventricular volume prediction.

## Method Summary
L2C-TabPFN transforms longitudinal patient data into fixed-length feature vectors through a longitudinal-to-cross-sectional (L2C) transformation, which is then processed by a pre-trained Tabular Foundation Model (TabPFN). This architecture enables the model to capture complex temporal patterns in patient records while maintaining the computational efficiency of cross-sectional models. The approach leverages the pre-training of TabPFN on large tabular datasets to enhance generalization capabilities for Alzheimer's disease progression prediction tasks.

## Key Results
- Competitive performance on diagnostic status and cognitive score prediction tasks
- State-of-the-art accuracy for ventricular volume prediction
- SHAP analysis confirms identification of clinically relevant features
- Model demonstrates potential for advancing longitudinal prediction of imaging biomarkers

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by converting temporal patient data into static representations that preserve longitudinal information while enabling the use of powerful pre-trained tabular models. The L2C transformation effectively captures disease progression patterns across time, while TabPFN's foundation model architecture provides strong generalization from pre-training on diverse tabular datasets.

## Foundational Learning
- Longitudinal-to-cross-sectional transformation: Needed to convert time-series patient data into fixed-length vectors; quick check: verify temporal information preservation
- Tabular Foundation Models: Needed for strong generalization from pre-training; quick check: assess performance on unseen tabular patterns
- SHAP analysis: Needed to interpret model predictions and validate clinical relevance; quick check: confirm feature importance aligns with medical knowledge

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Longitudinal patient records -> L2C Transformation -> Fixed-length feature vectors -> TabPFN Encoder -> Prediction outputs

**Critical Path:** The transformation from longitudinal sequences to cross-sectional representations represents the most critical component, as it determines whether temporal disease progression patterns are adequately captured.

**Design Tradeoffs:** The method trades direct temporal modeling (like RNNs or transformers) for the efficiency and generalization benefits of pre-trained foundation models, potentially sacrificing some fine-grained temporal resolution.

**Failure Signatures:** Poor performance on tasks requiring precise temporal ordering or rapid disease progression patterns may indicate inadequate L2C transformation. Limited improvement over baseline methods could suggest insufficient pre-training relevance.

**First Experiments:**

2. Evaluate TabPFN performance on standard tabular benchmark datasets

## Limitations
- No independent validation on external datasets beyond TADPOLE, limiting generalizability
- Lacks uncertainty quantification for individual predictions, critical for clinical applications
- L2C transformation performance compared to alternative longitudinal modeling approaches not thoroughly explored

## Confidence
- High confidence: Competitive performance on diagnostic and cognitive tasks
- Medium confidence: State-of-the-art ventricular volume prediction accuracy
- Low confidence: Clinical utility and generalizability claims without external validation
