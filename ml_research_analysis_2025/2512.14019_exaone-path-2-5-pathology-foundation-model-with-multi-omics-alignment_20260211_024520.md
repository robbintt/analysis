# EXAONE Path 2.5: Pathology Foundation Model with Multi-Omics Alignment

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2512.14019
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14019
- Authors: Juseung Yun; Sunwoo Yu; Sumin Ha; Jonghyun Kim; Janghyeon Lee; Jongseong Jang; Soonyoung Lee
- Reference count: 40
- One-line primary result: State-of-the-art multimodal pathology foundation model achieving superior or on-par performance with SOTA on 80 Patho-Bench tasks while being highly parameter- and data-efficient

## Executive Summary
EXAONE Path 2.5 is a multimodal pathology foundation model that integrates whole-slide images with genomic, epigenetic, and transcriptomic data to capture cancer biology from genotype to phenotype. It uses a multimodal SigLIP loss for all-pairwise contrastive learning across five heterogeneous modalities, fragment-aware rotary positional encoding to preserve spatial tissue structure, and domain-specialized internal foundation models for stable, biologically meaningful embeddings. Evaluated on internal clinical benchmarks and Patho-Bench (80 tasks), it achieves superior or on-par performance with state-of-the-art models while being highly parameter- and data-efficient. The approach demonstrates strong generalization and improved biological coherence in learned representations.

## Method Summary
EXAONE Path 2.5 uses multimodal SigLIP loss with all-pairwise BCE contrastive learning across WSI, RNA-seq, SNP, CNV, and DNA methylation. The WSI encoder combines a frozen patch-level foundation model with F-RoPE aggregator using alternating RoPE-attention (with fragment-aware mask) and NoPE-attention blocks. RNA-seq uses a frozen internal foundation model, while SNP/CNV/methylation use linear+SwiGLU FFN encoders. All modalities are projected via dedicated Wi,j matrices and aligned through the multimodal SigLIP objective. The model was trained on 23,099 patients with at least two modalities available and evaluated on Patho-Bench (80 tasks) and internal clinical benchmarks.

## Key Results
- Achieves superior or on-par performance with state-of-the-art models on Patho-Bench (80 tasks) and internal clinical benchmarks
- Highly parameter- and data-efficient compared to baseline models
- Demonstrates strong generalization across organ systems and improved biological coherence in learned representations

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Multimodal SigLIP loss enables stable all-pairwise contrastive alignment across five heterogeneous biological modalities without positive competition.
- Mechanism: Standard softmax-based CLIP losses force row probabilities to sum to 1, causing multiple valid positives to compete. The sigmoid-based BCE formulation treats each modality pair (i, j) as an independent binary classification, with dedicated projection adapters (W_i,j) learning modality-pair-specific interaction spaces. This allows each biological layer (genomic → epigenetic → transcriptomic → phenotypic) to encode complementary signals without collapse toward easier modalities.
- Core assumption: The five modalities (WSI, RNA-seq, SNP, CNV, DNA methylation) encode correlated but partially orthogonal biological signals reflecting the genotype-to-phenotype cascade.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "multimodal SigLIP loss enabling all-pairwise contrastive learning across heterogeneous modalities"
  - [section]: Section 2.1 details the BCEWithLogits formulation and modality-pair-specific projections
  - [corpus]: MoRE (arXiv:2511.20382) addresses similar multi-omics representation challenges with frozen transformers; GROVER addresses spatial multi-omics fusion but uses different alignment strategies
- Break condition: If modalities share no biological signal (random pairing), independent BCE terms provide no learning gradient; if one modality has dramatically higher signal-to-noise, projections may still collapse.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Fragment-aware rotary positional encoding (F-RoPE) preserves spatial tissue topology while preventing spurious attention across physically disconnected regions.
- Mechanism: High-resolution WSIs are processed as patch sets. F-RoPE applies rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) to encode patch coordinates while using a fragment-aware attention mask that restricts attention to patches within the same tissue fragment. The aggregator interleaves RoPE-attention blocks (capturing local spatial geometry with fragment masking) and NoPE-attention blocks (fully position-agnostic for global slide-level evidence).
- Core assumption: Spatial organization of patches within connected tissue fragments encodes biologically meaningful patterns (tumor grade variation, stromal composition, immune infiltration) that reflect underlying molecular states.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "fragment-aware rotary positional encoding to preserve spatial tissue structure"
  - [section]: Section 2.2 describes the dual-attention scheme and fragment index-based masking
  - [corpus]: Limited direct corpus evidence for fragment-aware masking specifically; related WSI models (EXAONE Path 2.0, arXiv:2507.06639) use different aggregation strategies
- Break condition: If tissue segmentation produces incorrect fragment indices, spatial attention will be incorrectly constrained; if spatial patterns are not predictive of molecular states, RoPE adds noise.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Domain-specialized internal foundation models provide stable, biologically grounded embeddings that improve multimodal alignment robustness.
- Mechanism: The WSI encoder uses an internally pretrained patch-level foundation model (frozen) followed by the F-RoPE aggregator. The RNA-seq encoder uses an internally pretrained RNA foundation model (frozen). SNP, CNV, and methylation use simpler feed-forward encoders. These frozen specialized encoders produce embeddings that already capture domain-relevant features, reducing the burden on multimodal alignment to learn raw signal processing.
- Core assumption: Pretrained pathology and transcriptomic foundation models capture biologically relevant features that transfer to multimodal alignment; frozen encoders provide stable optimization targets.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "domain-specialized internal foundation models for both WSI and RNA-seq to provide biologically grounded embeddings"
  - [section]: Section 2.3 describes frozen WSI and RNA foundation models with projection heads
  - [corpus]: UNI, H-optimus-0, and other baselines in Table 4 demonstrate that patch-level foundation models provide strong features; corpus shows domain pre-training is established practice
- Break condition: If internal foundation models are undertrained or misaligned with target data distribution, their frozen embeddings constrain rather than help alignment.

## Foundational Learning

- Concept: Contrastive Learning (CLIP/SigLIP objectives)
  - Why needed here: The entire multimodal alignment depends on understanding how contrastive losses align embeddings across modalities, and why sigmoid-based losses handle multiple positives better than softmax.
  - Quick check question: Given a batch of 4 samples with 3 modalities each, how many positive and negative pairs does standard CLIP vs. SigLIP consider?

- Concept: Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE)
  - Why needed here: F-RoPE extends RoPE with fragment masking; understanding RoPE's rotation-based relative position encoding is prerequisite to understanding the dual-attention scheme.
  - Quick check question: Why does RoPE encode relative position through complex rotation rather than additive sinusoidal embeddings?

- Concept: Multi-omics Biological Layers
  - Why needed here: The model assumes a genotype-to-phenotype cascade; understanding what each modality measures (SNP/CNV = genomic alterations, methylation = epigenetic regulation, RNA-seq = transcription, WSI = morphology) clarifies why alignment is biologically meaningful.
  - Quick check question: Which modality would you expect to be most predictive of WSI morphological features: SNP mutations, DNA methylation, or RNA expression? Why?

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
WSI → [Frozen Patch Foundation] → patch embeddings → [F-RoPE Aggregator: RoPE+Mask | NoPE alternating] → slide embedding
RNA-seq → [Frozen RNA Foundation] → RNA embedding
SNP/CNV/Methylation → [Linear + SwiGLU FFN] → gene embeddings
All embeddings → [Modality-pair projection adapters W_i,j] → Multimodal SigLIP Loss (all-pairwise BCE)

- Critical path:
  1. Ensure tissue segmentation produces correct fragment indices for F-RoPE masking
  2. Verify frozen foundation models are loaded and producing expected embedding dimensions
  3. Validate modality availability masks (ValidPair) are correctly computed for each sample
  4. Confirm BCE loss is computed per-pair with proper class balancing

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Frozen vs. fine-tuned encoders: Freezing provides stability and efficiency but limits adaptation to new domains
  - All-pairwise vs. anchor-based contrastive: All-pairwise captures more signal but requires more memory per batch
  - RoPE+NoPE interleaving vs. pure RoPE: Interleaving balances spatial and global features but adds architectural complexity
  - Five modalities vs. fewer: More modalities capture richer biology but increase missing-modality challenges and alignment difficulty

- Failure signatures:
  - Embedding collapse: All modalities map to similar representations; check projection adapter gradients and loss scaling
  - Fragment attention leakage: Attention across disconnected regions; verify fragment index assignment and mask application
  - Missing modality bias: Model ignores modalities with higher missing rates; check ValidPair masking and class balancing
  - Overfitting to dominant modality: One modality drives all predictions; examine per-pair loss contributions

- First 3 experiments:
  1. **Ablate F-RoPE**: Replace with standard attention or remove positional encoding entirely; measure impact on organ-level clustering (t-SNE) and downstream task performance
  2. **Modality dropout analysis**: Train with each modality individually removed; identify which modalities contribute most to which task families (mutation prediction vs. morphological subtyping)
  3. **Projection adapter analysis**: Visualize learned W_i,j projections to verify modality-pair-specific spaces capture biologically meaningful relationships (e.g., RNA→WSI vs. SNP→WSI projections should differ)

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

- Open Question 1: What is the relative contribution of each architectural component (multimodal SigLIP loss, F-RoPE, internal foundation models) to overall performance gains?
  - Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper introduces three key components but provides no ablation study isolating their individual effects, making it unclear which design choices drive the observed improvements.
  - Why unresolved: Without systematic ablation experiments, the relative importance of each component cannot be determined.
  - What evidence would resolve it: Ablation experiments reporting performance when each component is removed or replaced individually on both internal benchmarks and Patho-Bench.

- Open Question 2: How robust is the model to missing modalities at inference time, and does performance degrade gracefully or catastrophically?
  - Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors mention designing for "real-world clinical variability and missing-modality patterns" and the loss function handles missing pairs via ValidPair, but no experiments quantify inference-time behavior when modalities are unavailable.
  - Why unresolved: Clinical deployment will inevitably encounter patients without complete multi-omics profiling; the model's behavior in these scenarios is uncharacterized.
  - What evidence would resolve it: Systematic evaluation on benchmark tasks with simulated missing modalities at varying rates, reporting performance degradation curves.

## Limitations
- The approach depends critically on frozen internal foundation models whose architectures and training details are not publicly available, making faithful reproduction challenging.
- The fragment-aware masking assumes reliable tissue segmentation for fragment index assignment, but errors in segmentation could propagate to the spatial encoding.
- The all-pairwise multimodal SigLIP approach requires careful handling of missing modalities and proper class balancing, with potential for modality collapse if not properly tuned.

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: The multimodal SigLIP formulation with independent BCE losses for each modality pair is theoretically sound and directly implementable from the paper's equations.
- **Medium confidence**: The fragment-aware RoPE masking and dual-attention scheme are clearly specified, but their effectiveness depends on tissue segmentation quality not discussed in detail.
- **Medium confidence**: The domain-specialized frozen encoders provide a reasonable architectural choice, but their actual performance impact depends on the quality and domain alignment of the internal foundation models.

## Next Checks
1. **Fragment attention integrity check**: Visualize attention weight matrices from F-RoPE blocks to confirm that attention is strictly constrained within fragment boundaries and verify that fragment index assignment correctly reflects tissue topology.
2. **Modality contribution analysis**: Systematically remove each modality in isolation during evaluation to quantify individual contributions to task performance, particularly distinguishing which modalities drive morphological vs. molecular prediction tasks.
3. **Projection adapter interpretability**: Apply dimensionality reduction to learned W_i,j projection matrices to visualize whether modality-pair-specific spaces capture biologically meaningful relationships (e.g., RNA→WSI projections should emphasize gene expression patterns linked to tissue morphology).
