# DLiPath: A Benchmark for the Comprehensive Assessment of Donor Liver Based on Histopathological Image Dataset

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2506.03185
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.03185
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: 636 whole-slide images from 304 donor liver patients annotated for six pathological features, enabling automated assessment with multiple-instance learning models achieving accuracy 0.69-0.98

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces DLiPath, a comprehensive benchmark dataset for donor liver assessment using histopathological whole-slide images. The dataset addresses the critical challenge of accurate and rapid intraoperative evaluation of donor liver biopsies, where manual assessment suffers from significant inter- and intra-observer variability. Containing 636 whole-slide images from 304 donor liver patients, DLiPath provides expert annotations across six key pathological features essential for donor liver assessment. The authors evaluate nine state-of-the-art multiple-instance learning models on this dataset, demonstrating that several models achieve high accuracy across different assessment tasks, with TransMIL performing best overall.

## Method Summary
The study presents a benchmark dataset and evaluation framework for donor liver assessment using histopathological whole-slide images. The DLiPath dataset consists of 636 whole-slide images from 304 donor liver patients, annotated by experts across six pathological features: cholestasis, portal tract fibrosis, portal inflammation, total steatosis, macrovesicular steatosis, and hepatocellular ballooning. The authors evaluate nine state-of-the-art multiple-instance learning models on this dataset, demonstrating their effectiveness for automated donor liver assessment. The evaluation includes classification accuracy and AUC metrics across different pathological indicators, with TransMIL achieving the highest overall performance.

## Key Results
- DLiPath contains 636 whole-slide images from 304 donor liver patients with expert annotations
- TransMIL achieves the best overall performance with accuracy ranging from 0.69 to 0.98 and AUC from 0.82 to 0.98
- Multiple-instance learning models demonstrate high accuracy across six different pathological assessment tasks
- Automated assessment shows potential to reduce pathologist workload and improve consistency in graft evaluation

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The dataset works because it provides standardized, expert-annotated whole-slide images that capture the complex spatial heterogeneity of liver histopathology. Multiple-instance learning is particularly suited for this task because each whole-slide image contains thousands of individual patches (instances), where the presence of any single patch indicating pathology can determine the overall assessment. The six pathological features selected represent clinically critical indicators that pathologists use to evaluate donor liver quality, ensuring the dataset addresses real clinical needs. By providing a standardized benchmark, the dataset enables systematic comparison of different MIL approaches and facilitates the development of more accurate automated assessment tools.

## Foundational Learning
- Multiple-instance learning: Why needed - Handles cases where labels are assigned to bags of instances rather than individual instances; Quick check - Can the model handle weak supervision where only bag-level labels are available
- Whole-slide image processing: Why needed - Enables analysis of entire histological specimens rather than small regions; Quick check - Does the method scale to images containing millions of pixels
- Expert annotation protocols: Why needed - Ensures consistent ground truth for training and evaluation; Quick check - What inter-observer agreement metrics were achieved
- Patch-based representation: Why needed - Allows efficient processing of large images by dividing into manageable units; Quick check - How were patch sizes selected relative to cellular features
- Clinical feature selection: Why needed - Focuses on pathological indicators most relevant to donor liver assessment; Quick check - Were the selected features validated against clinical outcomes
- MIL model evaluation: Why needed - Provides standardized metrics for comparing different approaches; Quick check - Do evaluation metrics align with clinical decision thresholds

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Whole-slide images -> Patch extraction -> MIL pooling -> Classification -> Clinical assessment

Critical path: Patch extraction (preprocessing) -> MIL model (feature learning and aggregation) -> Classification (pathology prediction) -> Clinical interpretation

Design tradeoffs: The dataset size vs. model complexity tradeoff is critical, as whole-slide images are computationally expensive to process but necessary for capturing complete pathological information. The choice of MIL pooling mechanism (e.g., attention-based vs. max-pooling) affects both accuracy and interpretability. Patch size selection involves balancing computational efficiency against the need to capture relevant cellular features. The number of pathological features to annotate involves tradeoffs between dataset comprehensiveness and annotation burden.

Failure signatures: Poor performance on specific features may indicate insufficient training examples for rare pathologies. High variance in model predictions across similar cases suggests the model is sensitive to image acquisition differences. Failure to generalize across different staining protocols or scanner types indicates lack of robustness to technical variation. Poor performance on subtle cases may indicate insufficient model capacity to capture fine-grained features.

Three first experiments:
1. Evaluate baseline MIL models (e.g., mi-Net, Attention MIL) on each individual pathological feature to establish performance baselines
2. Compare different patch extraction strategies (fixed vs. adaptive sizing) and their impact on model performance
3. Perform ablation study on the number of patches per slide to determine optimal trade-off between computational efficiency and accuracy

## Limitations
- Dataset size of 636 whole-slide images may be insufficient for robust generalization across diverse populations
- Annotation process details lack inter-observer agreement metrics and specific criteria for each pathological feature
- Evaluation focuses on classification accuracy without addressing critical clinical metrics like sensitivity for severe pathologies
- Clinical applicability for intraoperative assessment not validated in real clinical workflow
- No clinical implementation study to demonstrate actual reduction in pathologist workload
