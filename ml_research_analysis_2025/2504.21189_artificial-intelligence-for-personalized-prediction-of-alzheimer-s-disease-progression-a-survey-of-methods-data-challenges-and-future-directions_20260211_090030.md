# Artificial Intelligence for Personalized Prediction of Alzheimer's Disease Progression: A Survey of Methods, Data Challenges, and Future Directions

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2504.21189
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.21189
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Comprehensive survey of AI methodologies for personalized Alzheimer's disease progression prediction, emphasizing multimodal integration and future directions

## Executive Summary
This paper provides a comprehensive survey of artificial intelligence methodologies for personalized prediction of Alzheimer's disease (AD) progression. The review covers state-space models, deep learning approaches including RNNs, Graph Neural Networks, and AI-driven digital twins, with emphasis on their ability to capture temporal dynamics and integrate multimodal data. The authors highlight critical data challenges including high dimensionality, missing data, and class imbalance, while discussing AI-based mitigation strategies such as synthetic data generation using VAEs and GANs. The survey identifies key open challenges and future research directions, emphasizing the shift toward multimodal integration and personalized simulation for improved clinical decision-making in AD management.

## Method Summary
The survey systematically reviews AI methodologies for AD progression prediction through comprehensive literature analysis. The authors categorize approaches into state-space models for temporal dynamics, deep learning architectures (RNNs, transformers) for sequence modeling, Graph Neural Networks for relational data, and AI-driven digital twins for personalized simulation. They analyze how these methods handle multimodal data integration, temporal dependencies, and individual variability in AD progression. The review also examines data challenges including high dimensionality, missing values, and class imbalance, alongside AI-based mitigation strategies such as imputation techniques, synthetic data generation via VAEs and GANs, and transfer learning approaches.

## Key Results
- AI methodologies for AD progression prediction include state-space models, deep learning (RNNs), Graph Neural Networks, and AI-driven digital twins
- Data challenges include high dimensionality, missing data, and class imbalance, with AI-based mitigation strategies such as VAE and GAN synthetic data generation
- The field is shifting toward multimodal integration and personalized simulation for improved clinical decision-making in AD management

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The survey demonstrates that AI approaches work for AD progression prediction by leveraging their ability to capture complex temporal dynamics and handle heterogeneous data types. State-space models provide interpretable frameworks for tracking disease states over time, while deep learning architectures excel at learning nonlinear patterns in sequential biomarker data. Graph Neural Networks effectively model relationships between different biological systems and clinical measurements. The integration of multimodal data allows for comprehensive patient profiling, and synthetic data generation addresses critical limitations in real-world datasets. These mechanisms collectively enable more accurate and personalized predictions compared to traditional statistical approaches.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Data Preprocessing -> Feature Engineering -> Model Selection -> Training -> Validation -> Clinical Integration

**Critical Path**: Multimodal Data Integration -> Temporal Modeling -> Personalized Prediction -> Clinical Decision Support

**Design Tradeoffs**: 
- Interpretability vs. predictive accuracy (state-space models vs. deep learning)
- Computational complexity vs. real-time applicability (GNNs vs. simpler architectures)
- Data augmentation vs. model generalization (synthetic data generation)
- Personalized vs. population-level predictions (digital twins vs. standard models)

**Failure Signatures**:
- Overfitting on limited AD datasets
- Poor generalization across diverse patient populations
- Inability to handle missing or heterogeneous data
- Lack of clinical interpretability in complex models
- Failure to capture long-term temporal dependencies

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
