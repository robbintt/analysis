# Hybrid Phenology Modeling for Predicting Temperature Effects on Tree Dormancy

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2501.16848
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.16848
- **Reference count**: 14
- **Primary result**: Hybrid model outperforms traditional and ML baselines for cherry tree blooming prediction

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a hybrid phenology model that combines biophysical constraints with machine learning to predict tree dormancy release and blooming dates. The approach replaces a key component of traditional biophysical models with a learnable multilayer perceptron, enabling more flexible and data-driven modeling of temperature responses during dormancy. The model is evaluated on cherry tree phenology data from Japan, Switzerland, and South Korea, demonstrating superior performance compared to both traditional biophysical models and pure machine learning baselines.

## Method Summary
The hybrid model integrates biophysical constraints with machine learning by replacing a key component of traditional biophysical models with a learnable multilayer perceptron. This allows for flexible, data-driven modeling of temperature responses during dormancy. The approach combines the interpretability of biophysical models with the adaptability of machine learning, enabling accurate predictions of blooming dates across different locations and tree varieties without requiring site-specific recalibration.

## Key Results
- The hybrid model consistently outperforms both traditional biophysical models and pure machine learning baselines in predicting blooming dates
- The model generalizes well across different locations and tree varieties without requiring site-specific recalibration
- Learned temperature response functions show variation while maintaining strong predictive performance

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The hybrid approach works by leveraging the strengths of both biophysical and machine learning models. Traditional biophysical models provide interpretability and incorporate known physiological constraints, while machine learning components allow for data-driven learning of complex temperature-response relationships. By replacing a key component with a learnable multilayer perceptron, the model can capture nuanced patterns in temperature effects on dormancy that are difficult to express in purely analytical forms.
