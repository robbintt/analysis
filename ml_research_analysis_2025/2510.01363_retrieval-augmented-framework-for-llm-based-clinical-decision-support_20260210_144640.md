# Retrieval-Augmented Framework for LLM-Based Clinical Decision Support

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.01363
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.01363
- Reference count: 40
- Achieved up to 90% accuracy and 89% F1 score for predicting opioid prescriptions at standard dosage

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a retrieval-augmented generation framework designed to support clinical prescribing decisions by analyzing heterogeneous electronic health record data. The system retrieves semantically similar historical cases and grounds LLM-generated treatment recommendations in precedent-based evidence. Evaluated on a real-world emergency department dataset of 68,969 encounters, the RAG-LLM framework demonstrates strong performance in predicting opioid prescriptions while maintaining clinical consistency with observed prescribing patterns.

## Method Summary
The proposed framework combines structured and unstructured clinical inputs to retrieve semantically similar historical cases from electronic health records. These retrieved cases are then used to ground LLM-generated treatment recommendations, creating a precedent-based evidence system. The approach leverages both structured EHR data and unstructured clinical notes to provide comprehensive context for decision support, with the retrieval component ensuring recommendations are anchored in similar real-world clinical scenarios.

## Key Results
- Achieved up to 90% accuracy and 89% F1 score for predicting opioid prescriptions at standard dosage
- Demonstrated 82% clinical consistency rate with observed prescribing patterns
- Successfully combined structured and unstructured clinical inputs for comprehensive analysis

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The retrieval-augmented approach works by grounding LLM recommendations in real-world precedent cases, reducing hallucinations and increasing clinical alignment. By retrieving semantically similar historical cases before generating recommendations, the system ensures that suggestions are based on observed clinical patterns rather than purely statistical generation. This mechanism provides transparency through traceable case references while maintaining the flexibility of LLM-based reasoning.

## Foundational Learning

- **Multi-modal EHR integration**: Why needed - to capture complete clinical context; Quick check - validate structured and unstructured data alignment
- **Clinical consistency validation**: Why needed - to ensure recommendations match real-world practice; Quick check - compare recommendations against actual prescribing patterns
- **Precedent-based grounding**: Why needed - to reduce LLM hallucinations; Quick check - track hallucination rates with and without retrieval

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**
EHR Data -> Retrieval Engine -> Case Database -> LLM Generator -> Clinical Consistency Validator

**Critical Path**
The critical path follows: EHR ingestion → semantic similarity search → case retrieval → LLM generation → consistency validation. Each step must complete successfully for the system to provide recommendations.

**Design Tradeoffs**
The system trades computational overhead from retrieval operations against improved clinical alignment and reduced hallucination risk. The choice to use precedent-based grounding prioritizes clinical safety over pure generation speed.

**Failure Signatures**
- High retrieval precision but low clinical consistency indicates retrieval relevance without clinical applicability
- Low retrieval precision suggests semantic matching issues in the retrieval engine
- High hallucination rates indicate insufficient precedent grounding or poor case selection

**3 First Experiments**
1. Measure retrieval precision by manually validating top-5 retrieved cases for clinical similarity
2. Compare LLM generation quality with and without retrieved cases for the same input
3. Test system performance on edge cases with limited similar precedents in the database

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
None

## Limitations
- Clinical generalizability limited to single emergency department dataset with specific prescribing patterns
- 82% clinical consistency rate lacks detailed methodological transparency about validation process
- Does not address potential biases in retrieved historical cases or handling of rare/novel clinical scenarios

## Confidence
High confidence in technical implementation and demonstrated performance metrics on specific opioid prescription task. Medium confidence in broader clinical applicability due to limited dataset diversity and lack of external validation. Low confidence in system's ability to handle complex polypharmacy scenarios or integrate into existing clinical workflows.

## Next Checks
1. External validation on multi-center datasets spanning different clinical departments and geographic regions to assess generalizability across varying prescribing patterns and clinical contexts
2. Prospective clinical trial comparing physician decision-making with and without RAG-LLM assistance, measuring both prescribing accuracy and physician acceptance of recommendations
3. Bias analysis examining how the system performs across demographic subgroups and whether retrieved cases introduce or perpetuate historical prescribing disparities
