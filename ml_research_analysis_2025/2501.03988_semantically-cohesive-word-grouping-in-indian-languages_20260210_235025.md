# Semantically Cohesive Word Grouping in Indian Languages

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2501.03988
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.03988
- Reference count: 7
- Primary result: Word grouping improves machine translation performance and semantic preservation in Indian languages

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces word grouping as a preprocessing technique for Indian languages to address syntactic variations and improve downstream NLP tasks. The method aims to make syntactic structures more uniform across Indian languages by grouping morphologically related words. The approach uses rule-based grouping specifically for Hindi, the least agglutinative among Indian languages, and demonstrates both intrinsic and extrinsic benefits through comprehensive evaluations.

## Method Summary
The word grouping approach employs rule-based methods to identify and group morphologically related words in Hindi text. These groups are then treated as single units during downstream processing tasks. For intrinsic evaluation, the method tests semantic role preservation through sentence perturbation by shuffling words and measuring semantic drift. For extrinsic evaluation, decomposed prompting is used in machine translation tasks where word groups are preserved instead of using fixed-length chunks. The system shows consistent improvements in both BLEU and chrF++ scores when word groups are maintained during processing.

## Key Results
- Word-grouped sentences preserve semantic roles better during shuffling compared to ungrouped sentences
- Machine translation using decomposed prompting with preserved word groups shows consistent improvements in BLEU and chrF++ scores
- Rule-based Hindi word grouping demonstrates effectiveness as a preprocessing step for Indian languages

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The word grouping mechanism works by treating morphologically related words as single semantic units, which helps maintain syntactic coherence during processing. By preserving these semantic relationships, the approach reduces information loss during tokenization and improves the model's ability to capture linguistic nuances specific to Indian languages.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Raw Text -> Rule-Based Word Grouping -> Word-Groups -> Downstream Tasks (MT, QA, etc.)

**Critical Path**: The critical path involves rule-based word grouping followed by downstream task processing. The grouping must be accurate to ensure semantic preservation and downstream performance improvements.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The rule-based approach offers language-specific precision but lacks generalization across all Indian languages. The method prioritizes accuracy for Hindi over scalability to more agglutinative languages.

**Failure Signatures**: Poor word grouping leads to semantic drift during perturbation, reduced translation quality in downstream tasks, and loss of morphological information critical for Indian languages.

**First Experiments**: 1) Apply grouping rules to sample Hindi sentences and verify correct grouping patterns 2) Test semantic preservation through controlled perturbation of grouped vs ungrouped sentences 3) Run baseline MT without grouping and with grouping to establish performance differences

## Limitations
- Rule-based approach may not generalize well to all Indian languages and requires language-specific adaptations
- Limited exploration of more agglutinative languages like Tamil, Telugu, or Kannada
- Intrinsic evaluation uses relatively small perturbation scale (10%) which may not capture all semantic drift scenarios
- Extrinsic evaluation relies on decomposed prompting, a specific strategy that may not generalize to other tasks

## Confidence
- Word grouping effectiveness: Medium
- Generalization to other Indian languages: Low
- Semantic preservation claims: Medium
- Downstream task improvements: Medium

## Next Checks
1. Test the word grouping approach on more agglutinative Indian languages (Tamil, Telugu, Kannada) and evaluate performance degradation or adaptation requirements
2. Conduct extrinsic evaluation on additional downstream tasks beyond machine translation, such as question answering and text classification, using the same word-grouped representations
3. Perform ablation studies varying perturbation scales (beyond 10%) and measuring semantic drift in word-grouped versus non-grouped sentences to establish robustness thresholds
