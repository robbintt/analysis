# Leveraging Large Language Models for Robot-Assisted Learning of Morphological Structures in Preschool Children with Language Vulnerabilities

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.22287
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.22287
- Reference count: 24
- Primary result: TalBot system shows promise in robot-assisted morphological learning for preschool children with language vulnerabilities through LLM-powered conversational gameplay

## Executive Summary
This study explores the use of large language models integrated with conversational robots to support morphological learning in preschool children with language vulnerabilities. The researchers developed TalBot, which plays the word game "Alias" with children, generating clues and managing turn-taking while targeting specific morphological structures. Tested with 13 preschool children and 7 educators, the system demonstrates potential to compensate for speech recognition limitations and deliver structured linguistic input, addressing the challenges educators face in producing morphological models in real-time.

## Method Summary
The researchers created TalBot by integrating an LLM with the Furhat conversational robot to play "Alias" with preschool children. The system generates morphological targets and clues while managing game turn-taking. The evaluation involved 13 children and 7 educators in a single preschool setting, primarily with immigrant children. The study focused on feasibility and acceptability rather than measuring learning outcomes, testing the system's ability to deliver structured linguistic models and compensate for speech recognition challenges in children's speech patterns.

## Key Results
- TalBot successfully integrates LLM with conversational robot for morphological structure delivery
- System shows promise in compensating for speech recognition limitations in children's speech
- Demonstrates feasibility of robot-assisted language learning for morphological targets
- Addresses educator challenges in real-time morphological model production

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanism leverages LLM's ability to generate contextually appropriate morphological structures and clues on demand, while the robot provides consistent delivery and turn management. This combination addresses the dual challenge of needing both linguistic expertise and real-time responsiveness that traditional interventions require from educators.

## Foundational Learning
- **Morphological structures**: Why needed - forms the basis of word formation and grammar; Quick check - can the system generate and deliver prefixes, suffixes, and root words correctly
- **Conversational AI**: Why needed - enables natural interaction and turn-taking; Quick check - does the robot maintain coherent dialogue flow
- **Speech recognition for children**: Why needed - children's speech patterns differ significantly from adults; Quick check - what is the recognition accuracy rate for preschool speech
- **LLM prompt engineering**: Why needed - ensures consistent generation of educational content; Quick check - do generated clues align with morphological learning objectives

## Architecture Onboarding
**Component Map**: Speech Recognition -> LLM Processing -> Robot Control -> Morphological Target Delivery
**Critical Path**: Child speaks -> Speech recognition transcribes -> LLM generates clue -> Robot delivers clue -> Child responds
**Design Tradeoffs**: Real-time processing vs. accuracy in morphological generation; Natural conversation flow vs. strict educational targeting; Speech recognition robustness vs. system complexity
**Failure Signatures**: Speech recognition errors causing incorrect morphological targeting; LLM generating off-topic or inappropriate clues; Robot timing issues disrupting game flow
**First 3 Experiments**:
1. Measure speech recognition accuracy for preschool children's speech patterns
2. Test LLM's ability to generate age-appropriate morphological clues consistently
3. Evaluate robot's turn-taking management in maintaining game engagement

None

## Limitations
- Small sample size (13 children, 7 educators) limits generalizability
- Single preschool setting with predominantly immigrant children restricts external validity
- Limited evidence of actual learning outcomes and long-term retention
- No comparative studies with human-led morphological instruction

## Confidence
- High confidence: Technical feasibility of LLM + robot integration for language tasks
- Medium confidence: System's potential to compensate for speech recognition limitations
- Low confidence: Claims about robot outperforming human intervention and demonstrating educational effectiveness

## Next Checks
1. Conduct randomized controlled trial comparing TalBot with traditional human-led morphological instruction, measuring both immediate and delayed learning outcomes
2. Expand testing across multiple preschool settings with diverse populations, including different types of language vulnerabilities and socioeconomic backgrounds
3. Implement longitudinal tracking to assess retention of morphological structures and generalization to novel language contexts over 6-12 month periods
