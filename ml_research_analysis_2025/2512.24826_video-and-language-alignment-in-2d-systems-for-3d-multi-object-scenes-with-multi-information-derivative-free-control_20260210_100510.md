# Video and Language Alignment in 2D Systems for 3D Multi-object Scenes with Multi-Information Derivative-Free Control

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2512.24826
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.24826
- Authors: Jason Armitage; Rico Sennnrich
- Reference count: 40
- This paper introduces MI-ZO, a method for controlling in-scene cameras to improve 2D vision-language model performance on 3D multi-object scenes, achieving up to 21% error reduction without requiring model pretraining.

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of enabling 2D vision-language models (VLMs) to process 3D scenes by controlling an in-scene camera to provide optimal viewpoints. The authors propose MI-ZO, a novel method that estimates multivariate mutual information with active regret minimization using zeroth-order optimization. This approach learns to minimize redundancy between visual and linguistic inputs to guide camera control without requiring access to model parameters or costly training. The method improves performance on three new benchmarks for cross-modal reasoning over 3D multi-object scenes, achieving up to 21% error reduction compared to standard control methods.

## Method Summary
The paper proposes MI-ZO (Multi-Information with Zeroth-Order optimization), which controls an in-scene camera to provide optimal viewpoints for 2D-trained VLMs operating on 3D multi-object scenes. The method uses derivative-free optimization to estimate multivariate mutual information between visual inputs and linguistic descriptions, actively minimizing regret through weighted mixture distributions over multiple entropy sources. The controller operates in two rounds: a measurement round with default viewpoints to collect VLM predictions, followed by a correction round where predicted camera actions are applied. The approach uses polynomial regression with least-squares to fit demonstration data and an interaction matrix with strong product updates to compute camera actions. The method achieves improved performance on three benchmarks (GeoProperties-3DS, FeatureID-3DS, PartialView-3DS) without requiring VLM pretraining or finetuning.

## Key Results
- MI-ZO improves VLM performance on 3D multi-object scenes with up to 21% error reduction compared to standard control methods
- The method achieves 23.3% accuracy gains on FeatureID-3DS benchmark for feature identification tasks
- Active regret minimization (MI-ar) variants show significant separation between correct and incorrect VLM predictions (0.69-0.83 scores) versus non-ar variants (0.34-0.38)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by estimating multivariate mutual information between visual inputs and linguistic descriptions to guide camera control decisions. By using zeroth-order optimization, MI-ZO avoids the need for gradient information, making it applicable to black-box VLMs. The active regret minimization component ensures that the optimization process only updates weights when doing so increases the running mean mutual information, preventing degradation from poor updates. The weighted mixture distribution over multiple entropy sources (color histograms, edge density) allows the system to capture different aspects of visual information that may be relevant for different scene types and tasks.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: VLM Prediction -> MI-ZO Controller -> Camera Control -> VLM Input

Critical Path: The critical path flows from initial VLM predictions through the MI-ZO controller's mutual information estimation, to camera action prediction, and back to VLM input. The controller uses two rounds: measurement (collecting baseline predictions) and correction (applying predicted actions). The zeroth-order optimization operates on the mutual information between VLM prediction correctness and visual entropy sources.

Design Tradeoffs: The method trades computational complexity during inference (two-round evaluation, polynomial regression) for improved accuracy without requiring expensive VLM pretraining. Using multiple entropy sources increases information capture but also computational overhead. The derivative-free approach avoids gradient computation but may require more samples for convergence compared to gradient-based methods.

Failure Signatures: Performance degradation occurs when the VLM returns incorrect predictions on all initial viewpoints (hard limit case), when component model covariance traces become too high (poor polynomial regression fit), or when mutual information estimates fail to separate correct from incorrect predictions (overlapping distributions). High PC Dispersion values indicate model instability during controller operation.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
- **Can the MI-ZO controller be modified to overcome the "hard limit" on accuracy improvements observed when the Vision-Language Model (VLM) returns incorrect predictions on all initial viewpoints?** The authors acknowledge this constraint as a fundamental limitation of operating solely at inference time but do not investigate if alternative sampling strategies or hierarchical controls could circumvent this total failure.

- **How does the controller's performance scale as the number of objects in the 3D scene increases beyond three, given the identified limitations in the VLM's reasoning capabilities?** The paper identifies the VLM as the bottleneck for dense scenes but does not determine if the MI-ZO controller can partially compensate for this via better feature isolation or if the system failure is absolute above three objects.

- **Is the observed "ceiling" to performance gains from adding more entropy sources (e.g., >6) a result of input data redundancy or a theoretical limit of the zeroth-order optimization process?** It is unclear if the optimization algorithm (MI-ZO) struggles to minimize regret effectively when the dimensionality of the source mixture increases, independent of the visual similarity of the inputs.

## Limitations
- The method faces a "hard limit" on improvements when the VLM returns incorrect predictions on all initial viewpoints, representing a fundamental constraint of inference-time optimization
- Performance degrades significantly on scenes with three or more objects due to VLM limitations in reasoning over high object counts
- There appears to be a ceiling to performance gains from adding additional entropy sources beyond a certain point, though the cause (data redundancy vs. algorithmic saturation) is unclear

## Confidence

High: The method's novelty in combining derivative-free control with active regret minimization for VLM optimization in 3D scenes is clearly articulated and appears to be a genuine contribution not found in existing literature.

Medium: The main claims about performance improvements are supported by experimental results on three benchmarks, but confidence is tempered by multiple underspecified implementation details that could significantly impact reproducibility.

Medium-High: The theoretical analysis provides guarantees on expressiveness of information estimates and regret minimization conditions, though some proofs and assumptions are not fully detailed in the main text.
