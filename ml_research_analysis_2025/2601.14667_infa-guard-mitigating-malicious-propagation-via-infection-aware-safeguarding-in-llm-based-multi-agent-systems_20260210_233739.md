# INFA-Guard: Mitigating Malicious Propagation via Infection-Aware Safeguarding in LLM-Based Multi-Agent Systems

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2601.14667
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.14667
- Authors: Yijin Zhou; Xiaoya Lu; Dongrui Liu; Junchi Yan; Jing Shao
- Reference count: 40
- Reduces Attack Success Rate by 33% on average while preserving agent topology

## Executive Summary
INFA-Guard addresses the security vulnerabilities in LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems where malicious agents can propagate harmful influence through inter-agent communication. The method explicitly identifies infected agents as a distinct threat category and employs infection-aware detection with topological constraints to accurately localize attack sources and infected ranges. During remediation, it replaces attack agents while rehabilitating infected ones, preserving topological integrity. Experiments demonstrate that INFA-Guard achieves state-of-the-art performance across various attack scenarios while maintaining cross-model robustness and superior topological generalization.

## Method Summary
The framework models the threat space as comprising three agent categories: benign, attack, and infected. It employs an infection-aware detection mechanism that uses topological constraints to identify malicious propagation patterns, distinguishing between the original attack agent and those subsequently infected through communication. For remediation, the system replaces attack agents with benign substitutes while rehabilitating infected agents by filtering out malicious context through a dedicated refinement LLM. The approach explicitly preserves the underlying communication topology during defense operations, avoiding the disruption that traditional quarantine methods would cause.

## Key Results
- Reduces Attack Success Rate by an average of 33% across various attack scenarios
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance in mitigating malicious propagation
- Demonstrates superior topological generalization and cross-model robustness

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
INFA-Guard works by explicitly modeling infected agents as a distinct threat category, allowing it to distinguish between the original attacker and agents that have been compromised through communication. This nuanced understanding enables more precise localization of attack sources and infected ranges using topological constraints that consider the communication structure. The remediation strategy is tailored to the agent type - replacing attack agents entirely while rehabilitating infected ones through context filtering, which preserves the communication topology and maintains system functionality while eliminating the threat.

- **Type-specific remediation strategies**: Why needed - Attack agents and infected agents require different handling approaches. Quick check - Can you explain why infected agents are rehabilitated rather than replaced?
- **Context filtering for rehabilitation**: Why needed - Infected agents retain some benign behavior that can be preserved while removing malicious influence. Quick check - How does the system determine which parts of an agent's context are malicious?

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: LLM-based MAS agents -> Communication channels -> INFA-Guard detection module -> Remediation module -> Cleaned agents

Critical path: Detection identifies infected agents using topological constraints → Attack agents are replaced → Infected agents undergo context refinement → System returns to benign state while preserving topology

Design tradeoffs: The framework trades some computational overhead for precision in threat localization and preservation of communication topology. This approach avoids the disruptive effects of quarantining entire subnetworks while maintaining system functionality.

Failure signatures: False positives in infection detection may lead to unnecessary agent replacement or refinement. False negatives allow malicious propagation to continue. The rehabilitation LLM could potentially be compromised by sophisticated attacks that persist through context filtering.

First experiments:
1. Test infection detection accuracy on a simple 3-agent chain topology with one attack agent
2. Validate the topological constraint mechanism by introducing communication patterns that mimic malicious propagation
3. Evaluate the effectiveness of context filtering by measuring post-remediation behavior similarity to original benign state

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
### Open Question 1
- Question: How can the framework be adapted for label-scarce domains to reduce reliance on synthesized training data?
- Basis in paper: The authors state in the Limitations section that the "reliance on synthesizing training data and ground-truth annotations presents potential bottlenecks" and suggest integrating pseudo-labeling.
- Why unresolved: The current supervised approach may not be feasible in specialized or new domains where attack labels are unavailable.
- What evidence would resolve it: Successful integration of semi-supervised or unsupervised anomaly detection techniques that maintain high ASR reduction without ground-truth infection labels.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Is it possible to develop endogenous security architectures that prevent agent compromise before communication begins?
- Basis in paper: The authors identify transitioning from "run-time defense" to "an endogenous security architecture that prevents compromise at the agent level" as a "critical frontier."
- Why unresolved: INFA-Guard currently requires observing at least one round of dialogue to detect threats, leaving the system vulnerable to zero-day attacks or immediate corruption.
- What evidence would resolve it: A mechanism that validates agent integrity or inoculates agents against persuasion prior to the first interaction turn.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How robust is the remediation function against adversarial attacks specifically designed to bypass the rehabilitation LLM?
- Basis in paper: The remediation strategy relies on an LLM (`LM(·)`) to remove malicious contexts from infected agents; however, the paper does not analyze if this filter is itself vulnerable to prompt injection.
- Why unresolved: If the rehabilitation LLM is susceptible to the same attacks that infected the agent, the "refining" step may fail to restore the benign state.
- What evidence would resolve it: Stress-testing the `RF(·)` remediation module against adversarial prompts designed to persist through LLM-based rewriting.

## Limitations
- The methodology assumes infected agents can be rehabilitated, which may not hold in all scenarios
- The topological constraint approach may struggle with highly dynamic agent topologies
- Evaluation primarily focuses on ASR reduction without extensive analysis of false positive rates or computational overhead

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The core contribution of explicitly modeling infected agents as a distinct threat category and the effectiveness of infection-aware detection with topological constraints in reducing attack success rates.
- **Medium Confidence**: The generalizability of the approach to highly dynamic topologies and the long-term effectiveness of agent rehabilitation strategies.
- **Medium Confidence**: The cross-model robustness claims, which would benefit from testing against a wider variety of LLM architectures.

## Next Checks
1. Conduct longitudinal studies to evaluate the long-term effectiveness of the rehabilitation strategy for infected agents, including whether rehabilitated agents maintain behavioral integrity over extended periods and repeated interactions.

2. Test the system under highly dynamic topological conditions where agent connections change rapidly, to assess the robustness of the topological constraint approach in more volatile environments.

3. Evaluate the computational overhead and scalability of the infection-aware detection mechanism in large-scale multi-agent systems with hundreds or thousands of agents, measuring both latency and resource utilization.
