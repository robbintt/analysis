# Augmenting The Weather: A Hybrid Counterfactual-SMOTE Algorithm for Improving Crop Growth Prediction When Climate Changes

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.11945
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.11945
- Reference count: 0
- Primary result: CFA-SMOTE reduces MAE in crop growth prediction under climate change, particularly in highly imbalanced scenarios

## Executive Summary
This study addresses the challenge of poor crop growth prediction under climate change, where out-of-distribution weather events degrade model performance. The authors frame this as a class-imbalance problem and introduce CFA-SMOTE, a hybrid data augmentation method combining counterfactual generation with SMOTE. The algorithm generates synthetic extreme-weather cases using historical counterfactual pairs and applies SMOTE to these instances. Experiments on Irish dairy grass growth prediction during the 2018 drought year across 12 datasets with varying imbalance ratios demonstrate CFA-SMOTE's effectiveness, particularly under extreme imbalance conditions.

## Method Summary
CFA-SMOTE is a hybrid data augmentation algorithm that addresses climate-disrupted crop growth prediction by combining counterfactual reasoning with SMOTE. The method first generates synthetic extreme-weather instances through an instance-based counterfactual approach using historical counterfactual pairs. These newly created instances are then subjected to SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) to further enhance the representation of rare extreme weather events in the training data. This dual approach aims to improve model robustness when faced with out-of-distribution weather patterns that occur under climate change conditions.

## Key Results
- CFA-SMOTE reduces Mean Absolute Error (MAE) compared to baseline and other augmentation methods
- Most pronounced improvements occur under extreme imbalance (MAE of 24.9 vs 38.6 kg/DM/ha for March 2018 at IR=163.5)
- CFA-SMOTE significantly outperforms SMOTE, B-SMOTE, G-SMOTE, and DiCE-SMOTE in most climate-disrupted months

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The hybrid approach works by addressing two key challenges simultaneously: generating realistic synthetic examples of extreme weather events that weren't present in historical data, and then using SMOTE to balance the representation of these rare events in the training set. The counterfactual component leverages historical pairs to create plausible extreme scenarios, while SMOTE ensures these synthetic examples are well-distributed and representative of the minority class.

- **SMOTE application**: Why needed - to balance representation of rare extreme weather events in training data; Quick check - confirm minority class oversampling maintains feature space relationships
- **Class imbalance framing**: Why needed - to transform climate prediction challenges into a well-studied ML problem domain; Quick check - validate imbalance ratios accurately reflect prediction difficulty

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map:
Historical data -> Counterfactual generator -> Synthetic extreme instances -> SMOTE application -> Balanced training dataset

Critical Path:
Counterfactual generation → SMOTE oversampling → Model training → Performance evaluation

Design Tradeoffs:
- Historical data dependency vs. generalization to unprecedented weather patterns
- Computational cost of counterfactual generation vs. performance gains
- Complexity of hybrid approach vs. simpler single-method alternatives

Failure Signatures:
- Poor counterfactual generation leading to unrealistic synthetic examples
- Overfitting to synthetic data rather than improving generalization
- Performance degradation when imbalance ratios exceed training scope

First Experiments:
1. Validate counterfactual generation produces plausible extreme weather scenarios
2. Test SMOTE-only baseline to isolate contribution of counterfactual component
3. Evaluate performance across varying imbalance ratios to identify optimal operating range

## Limitations
- Evaluated only on Irish dairy grass and specific geographic region, limiting generalizability
- Relies on historical counterfactual pairs that may not capture unprecedented future extreme weather patterns
- Does not address potential trade-offs in other performance metrics or model interpretability

## Confidence
High: Methodological soundness, experimental design, and comparative analysis

Low: Long-term effectiveness under truly unprecedented climate scenarios
