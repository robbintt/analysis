# DPWMixer: Dual-Path Wavelet Mixer for Long-Term Time Series Forecasting

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2512.02070
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.02070
- Reference count: 36
- Primary result: Achieves state-of-the-art LTSF performance with up to 10.7% MSE reduction vs. iTransformer

## Executive Summary
DPWMixer addresses long-term time series forecasting challenges through a novel dual-path architecture that combines lossless Haar wavelet decomposition with a trend-detail mixer. The method achieves orthogonal decomposition of time series into trend and detail coefficients without information loss, enabling separate modeling of global trends and local dynamics. By adaptively fusing multi-scale predictions with channel-aware weights, DPWMixer outperforms strong baselines like iTransformer while maintaining linear complexity O(L).

## Method Summary
DPWMixer introduces a dual-path architecture built on lossless Haar Wavelet Pyramid for orthogonal decomposition of time series into trend and detail coefficients. The dual-path trend mixer combines linear mapping for global trend anchoring with MLP-Mixer for local dynamics. Multi-scale predictions are adaptively fused via channel-aware weights. The method operates with linear complexity O(L) and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on eight public datasets, reducing MSE by up to 10.7% compared to strong baselines.

## Key Results
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance on eight public LTSF datasets
- Reduces MSE by up to 10.7% compared to iTransformer baseline
- Maintains linear computational complexity O(L)
- Ablation studies confirm importance of wavelet decomposition and dual-path design

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The dual-path architecture addresses fundamental challenges in long-term forecasting by separating trend and detail components through lossless wavelet decomposition. This orthogonal separation allows specialized modeling of global patterns versus local dynamics without interference. The adaptive fusion mechanism intelligently weights multi-scale predictions based on channel characteristics, while the linear complexity ensures scalability to long sequences.

## Foundational Learning
- **Haar Wavelet Decomposition**: Orthogonal signal decomposition technique needed for lossless separation of trend/detail components without spectral aliasing. Quick check: Verify perfect reconstruction property holds for decomposed coefficients.
- **Dual-Path Architecture**: Parallel processing streams for trend and detail components required to balance global trend modeling with local dynamics capture. Quick check: Ensure both paths maintain information integrity.
- **Adaptive Fusion Mechanism**: Channel-aware weighting of multi-scale predictions necessary for optimal combination of trend and detail forecasts. Quick check: Validate fusion weights reflect signal characteristics appropriately.
- **Linear Complexity Design**: O(L) architecture crucial for scalability to long time series sequences. Quick check: Confirm computational complexity scales linearly with sequence length.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Input -> Wavelet Pyramid -> Dual-Path Mixer -> Adaptive Fusion -> Output
**Critical Path:** Time series → Haar wavelet decomposition → Trend/Detail mixing → Multi-scale fusion → Forecast
**Design Tradeoffs:** The method trades increased model complexity (dual paths, fusion mechanism) for improved forecasting accuracy and preservation of high-frequency details that are typically lost in downsampling approaches.
**Failure Signatures:** Potential issues include stationarity assumption violations in trend component, suboptimal fusion weight assignments, and computational overhead for very long sequences despite O(L) complexity claim.
**First Experiments:** 1) Verify perfect reconstruction after wavelet decomposition, 2) Test dual-path mixing performance vs. single-path baseline, 3) Evaluate adaptive fusion weight stability across different time series characteristics

## Limitations
- Evaluation based on only eight public datasets may not capture full diversity of real-world forecasting challenges
- Claims of 10.7% MSE improvement lack statistical significance testing across multiple runs
- Assumes stationarity in trend component after wavelet decomposition, which may not hold for structural breaks or regime changes

## Confidence
- High confidence: The core dual-path architecture using wavelet decomposition for orthogonal trend/detail separation is technically sound and well-grounded in signal processing theory
- Medium confidence: The state-of-the-art performance claims, as they are based on a limited dataset evaluation without statistical significance testing
- Medium confidence: The efficiency claims regarding linear complexity, as practical implementation details and scaling behavior on very long sequences are not fully explored

## Next Checks
1. Conduct statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests or bootstrap confidence intervals) across multiple random seeds to verify that performance improvements are robust and not due to random initialization
2. Test DPWMixer on additional datasets with known structural breaks and non-stationary patterns to evaluate how well the stationarity assumption holds in practice
3. Benchmark computational efficiency on sequences significantly longer than those used in the paper (e.g., L > 10,000) to validate the O(L) complexity claim and identify any hidden bottlenecks in the adaptive fusion mechanism
