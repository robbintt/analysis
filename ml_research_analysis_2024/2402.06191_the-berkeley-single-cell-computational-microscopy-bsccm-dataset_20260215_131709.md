# The Berkeley Single Cell Computational Microscopy (BSCCM) Dataset

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2402.06191
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.06191
- Reference count: 40
- Dataset contains over 12 million images of 400,000 white blood cells with diverse illumination patterns and fluorescent protein measurements

## Executive Summary
This work presents a comprehensive dataset for single-cell computational microscopy containing over 12 million images of 400,000 white blood cells. The dataset includes images captured with various illumination patterns on an LED array microscope, along with fluorescent measurements of surface proteins. The resource is designed to enable benchmarking of computational microscopy techniques and support the development of new algorithms for biomedical applications. The standardized format and rich metadata facilitate reproducible research and comparisons across different approaches.

## Method Summary
The dataset was generated using an LED array microscope to capture white blood cells under various illumination patterns. Each cell was imaged multiple times with different lighting configurations, and fluorescent measurements were taken to identify surface proteins. The images were processed and organized into a standardized format with comprehensive metadata. The dataset includes over 12 million images spanning 400,000 individual cells, providing a large-scale resource for algorithm development and validation.

## Key Results
- Collection of over 12 million images of 400,000 white blood cells
- Images captured with diverse LED illumination patterns
- Fluorescent protein measurements provide ground truth labels
- Standardized format enables reproducible algorithm benchmarking

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The dataset's effectiveness stems from its combination of large scale, diverse illumination patterns, and ground truth protein labels. The LED array microscope provides controlled illumination variations that enable the development and testing of computational reconstruction algorithms. The fluorescent measurements serve as reliable ground truth for protein identification, while the large number of cells and imaging conditions provides statistical power for algorithm validation.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map**: LED array microscope -> Image acquisition -> Fluorescent measurement -> Data processing -> Standardized dataset

**Critical path**: Illumination pattern generation -> Image capture -> Cell segmentation -> Protein identification -> Metadata annotation

**Design tradeoffs**: The choice of white blood cells limits biological diversity but ensures clinical relevance; LED array provides illumination control but may introduce artifacts; fluorescent measurements provide ground truth but may not capture all proteins

**Failure signatures**: Poor image quality from LED pattern artifacts, incomplete cell segmentation, fluorescent labeling errors, metadata inconsistencies

**3 first experiments**:
1. Test basic cell segmentation algorithms on sample images
2. Verify fluorescent protein detection accuracy against known standards
3. Evaluate illumination pattern diversity through image reconstruction tests
