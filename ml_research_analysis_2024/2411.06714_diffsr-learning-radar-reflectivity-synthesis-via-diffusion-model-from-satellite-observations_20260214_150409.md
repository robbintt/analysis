# DiffSR: Learning Radar Reflectivity Synthesis via Diffusion Model from Satellite Observations

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2411.06714
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.06714
- Reference count: 32
- Primary result: DiffSR achieves state-of-the-art performance on CSI-50, SSIM, and LPIPS metrics for radar reflectivity synthesis

## Executive Summary
DiffSR presents a novel two-stage diffusion-based approach for generating high-quality radar reflectivity data from satellite observations. The method addresses the critical challenge of areas lacking ground-based radar coverage by leveraging satellite data to synthesize realistic radar imagery. The core innovation lies in combining a Vision Transformer for coarse estimation with a conditional diffusion model for refinement, effectively overcoming the over-smoothing issues that plague existing reconstruction methods.

The approach demonstrates significant improvements in capturing high-frequency details and high-value observation areas associated with convective weather systems. Through extensive experiments, DiffSR shows superior performance across multiple evaluation metrics, making it a promising solution for meteorological applications where radar data is sparse or unavailable.

## Method Summary
DiffSR employs a two-stage architecture to generate high-quality radar reflectivity from satellite observations. First, a Vision Transformer processes satellite data to produce a coarse radar estimation. Second, a conditional diffusion model refines this initial estimate by incorporating both the original satellite data and the coarse radar output as conditioning information. This two-stage approach effectively addresses the over-smoothing problem common in previous methods, enabling better capture of high-frequency details and high-value observation areas associated with convective weather. The diffusion model operates by gradually denoising the radar estimate while maintaining consistency with both the satellite observations and the coarse prediction.

## Key Results
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance on CSI-50 metric for radar reflectivity synthesis
- Shows superior SSIM and LPIPS scores compared to existing reconstruction methods
- Effectively captures high-frequency details and high-value observation areas associated with convective weather

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The two-stage diffusion architecture works by first establishing a reasonable spatial context through the Vision Transformer's coarse estimation, then progressively refining details through the diffusion model's denoising process. The Vision Transformer leverages its strong spatial modeling capabilities to create an initial radar estimate that respects the overall structure and distribution patterns from satellite observations. The subsequent diffusion model then operates as a powerful refinement tool, conditioned on both the satellite data and the coarse estimate, allowing it to add high-frequency details and correct systematic biases in the initial prediction. This conditional approach ensures that the refinement process remains grounded in the observed satellite features while enhancing the radar-specific characteristics that the coarse model may have oversimplified.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Satellite Images -> Vision Transformer -> Coarse Radar Estimate -> Conditional Diffusion Model -> Refined Radar Reflectivity

Critical Path: The critical computational path runs through both the Vision Transformer and the conditional diffusion model sequentially. The Vision Transformer processes the satellite input to create the coarse estimate, which is then fed along with the original satellite data into the diffusion model for refinement. Both stages must complete successfully for the final output.

Design Tradeoffs: The two-stage approach trades computational efficiency for quality - while running two models sequentially increases inference time compared to single-stage methods, it significantly improves the quality of generated radar reflectivity. The diffusion model's iterative denoising process provides superior detail capture but requires multiple forward passes, making it computationally intensive compared to direct regression approaches.

Failure Signatures: Model failures typically manifest as either over-smoothed outputs (if the diffusion model fails to properly denoise) or unrealistic patterns (if the conditioning from satellite data is not properly respected). Common issues include loss of fine-scale convective features, incorrect spatial correlations, or generation of physically implausible reflectivity patterns that don't align with the satellite observations.
