# Patterns of Persistence and Diffusibility across the World's Languages

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2401.01698
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.01698
- Reference count: 37
- Primary result: Phonological forms are more genealogically persistent than colexification patterns across 1,966 languages

## Executive Summary
This paper investigates the linguistic causes of cross-lingual similarity in colexification (multiple meanings sharing a word) and phonology across the world's languages. The authors construct large-scale language graphs incorporating semantic, genealogical, phonological, and geographical data for 1,966 languages to test hypotheses about persistence (stability over time) and diffusibility (spread through contact). Their analysis reveals that phonological forms show stronger genealogical persistence than colexification patterns, while both exhibit differential patterns based on concept type - abstract concepts and affectively-loaded meanings show distinct persistence/diffusibility profiles compared to concrete meanings.

## Method Summary
The authors constructed a large-scale language graph incorporating data from multiple sources: wordnets for semantic relations, PanLex for lexical forms across languages, PHOIBLE for phonological data, and Glottolog for genealogical and geographical information. They analyzed patterns across 1,966 languages using graph-based methods to measure both genealogical persistence (how stably features are inherited within language families) and contact-induced diffusibility (how features spread through geographical proximity). The analysis compared these patterns across different semantic domains and concept types, testing whether abstract versus concrete concepts and affectively-loaded versus non-affective meanings show differential persistence and diffusibility.

## Key Results
- Phonological forms demonstrate stronger genealogical persistence than colexification patterns
- Abstract concepts show higher persistence but lower diffusibility compared to concrete concepts
- Affectively-loaded meanings exhibit distinct patterns, being more persistent than non-affective concepts
- Differential persistence/diffusibility patterns are consistent across semantic domains

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The persistence of phonological forms likely works through the cognitive and communicative advantages of maintaining distinctive sound patterns that aid in word recognition and reduce ambiguity. For colexification patterns, the observed lower persistence may reflect greater flexibility in meaning extension and borrowing across languages. The differential patterns across concept types suggest that cognitive salience and emotional significance create stronger anchoring effects that resist both internal change and external borrowing.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Lexical databases -> Language graph construction -> Feature extraction -> Persistence/diffusibility analysis -> Hypothesis testing

Critical path: The analysis depends critically on accurate lexical data extraction, proper handling of missing data across languages, and appropriate statistical controls for shared ancestry when measuring contact effects.

Design tradeoffs: The study prioritizes breadth (1,966 languages) over depth, potentially sacrificing detailed analysis of individual language families for broader typological patterns. The reliance on existing lexical resources creates potential biases in language and concept coverage.

Failure signatures: Inconsistent results across different semantic domains might indicate problems with concept mapping across languages. High correlations between genealogical and contact measures could suggest inadequate controls for shared ancestry.

First experiments:
1. Replicate persistence analyses within major language families to test robustness
2. Compare results using alternative lexical databases to assess sensitivity to data sources
3. Analyze a subset of languages with detailed phonological documentation to validate broad patterns

## Limitations
- Reliance on existing lexical resources with potentially uneven coverage across language families
- Difficulty distinguishing true contact-induced change from parallel independent development
- Potential circularity in using genealogical trees to validate genealogical persistence patterns

## Confidence
High confidence in the general persistence pattern of phonological forms versus colexification
Medium confidence in differential persistence across concept types due to sampling biases
Low confidence in contact-induced diffusibility patterns given methodological challenges

## Next Checks
1. Replication using independent lexical databases to test robustness of persistence and diffusibility patterns
2. Analysis of the same patterns within individual language families to control for family-specific biases
3. Experimental manipulation studies to test whether proposed persistence mechanisms (e.g., phonological distinctiveness aiding retention) can be directly observed in language learning or change scenarios
