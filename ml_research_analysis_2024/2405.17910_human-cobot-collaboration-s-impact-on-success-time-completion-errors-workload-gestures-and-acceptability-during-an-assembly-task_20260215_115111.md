# Human-Cobot collaboration's impact on success, time completion, errors, workload, gestures and acceptability during an assembly task

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2405.17910
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.17910
- Reference count: 0
- Primary result: H/C collaboration decreases task complexity's impact on workload while increasing time and gestures, with higher success rates and acceptability

## Executive Summary
This study investigates how human-cobot collaboration affects assembly task performance compared to human-human collaboration. The research found that working with a cobot reduces the impact of task complexity on human workload while increasing time completion and gestures. Despite taking longer, H/C pairs achieved higher success rates. The cobot's adaptation to participants' dominant hands showed no significant effect on performance. Importantly, participants reported higher acceptability of the cobot, particularly regarding ease of use and pleasure. These findings suggest that cobots can effectively moderate cognitive load during complex tasks while maintaining high user acceptance.

## Method Summary
The study employed a between-subjects design with 120 participants randomly assigned to either human-human (H/H) or human-cobot (H/C) collaboration conditions. Participants completed simple and complex Duplo assembly tasks, with H/C condition using a YuMi cobot equipped with vision and decision-making capabilities. Researchers measured success rates, errors, time completion, gesture frequency, NASA-TLX workload scores, and UTAUT2-based acceptability metrics. Data analysis utilized MANOVA, ANOVAs, and mixed models to examine main effects and interaction patterns between collaboration type and task complexity.

## Key Results
- H/C collaboration reduced the impact of task complexity on workload compared to H/H
- H/C pairs showed higher success rates but required more time and gestures than H/H pairs
- Cobot adaptation to dominant hand showed no significant effect on performance outcomes
- Participants reported higher acceptability in H/C condition, especially for ease of use and pleasure

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Human-cobot collaboration reduces the impact of task complexity on human workload.
- Mechanism: The cobot provides structured assistance that absorbs complexity-related cognitive load, allowing humans to maintain lower workload levels even as task difficulty increases.
- Core assumption: The cobot's assistance is effectively calibrated to the human's needs and doesn't introduce additional cognitive burden.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "Working with a cobot decreases the effect of the task complexity on the human workload"
  - [section] "An interaction effect was found between task demand and workload [f (1)=61,134; p>0,000]"
  - [corpus] Weak - corpus papers focus on different aspects like trust dynamics or AR alignment, not workload moderation
- Break condition: If cobot assistance is poorly calibrated or introduces significant latency/friction, the workload moderation effect would disappear.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Cobot collaboration increases task completion time but improves success rates.
- Mechanism: The cobot enforces quality standards and error correction that slow completion but reduce mistakes, leading to higher success rates.
- Core assumption: Error correction by the cobot is more thorough than human-only collaboration.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "it increases the time completion and the number of gestures (while decreasing their frequency)"
  - [section] "The type of collaboration moderates the effect of the task demand in terms of time completion [ f (1)=158,250; p >0,000]"
  - [corpus] Weak - corpus papers don't directly address this tradeoff between time and success quality
- Break condition: If cobot error correction is too aggressive or frequent, it could paradoxically increase errors by disrupting human workflow.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Cobot adaptation to dominant hand has no significant effect on performance.
- Mechanism: The cobot's operational flexibility is sufficient that specific hand adaptation provides negligible marginal benefit.
- Core assumption: The cobot's general adaptability compensates for lack of specific hand-side configuration.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "The cobot's adaptation to the participant's dominant hand had no significant effect"
  - [section] "The effect of the cobot's adaptation to the type of dominant hand has no effect on the different independent variables [ f(5)= 1,229; p=0,311]"
  - [corpus] Weak - corpus papers focus on different adaptation scenarios like exoskeleton load redistribution
- Break condition: If task requirements become highly asymmetric or require specialized hand-side operations, this adaptation might become significant.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Task setup → participant collaboration (H/H or H/C) → measurement collection → data analysis
- Critical path: Participants complete assembly tasks → researchers record metrics (success, errors, time, gestures) → administer questionnaires → analyze data
- Design tradeoffs: Random assignment vs. within-subject design (chosen to avoid learning/fatigue effects), simple vs. complex task design, real cobot vs. simulated cobot
- Failure signatures: Bugs increasing time and gestures without affecting workload, novelty effects causing workload spikes in simple tasks, lack of left-handed participants affecting generalizability
- First 3 experiments:
  1. Replicate with left-handed participants to test hand adaptation claims
  2. Test with experienced cobot operators to measure situated acceptance
  3. Vary task types (not just complexity) to test broader collaboration effects

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How do the observed effects of cobot collaboration on success rate and errors translate to real-world industrial settings with more complex tasks?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The study found higher success rates but equivalent error counts in H/C collaboration compared to H/H, with cobots encouraging error correction. However, the study used simplified assembly tasks.
- Why unresolved: The tasks in the experiment were relatively simple compared to real industrial tasks. The impact of cobot collaboration on error correction and success rates in more complex, realistic industrial settings remains unknown.
- What evidence would resolve it: Studies testing cobot collaboration in real industrial environments with complex, varied tasks, measuring success rates, error counts, and error correction behaviors.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What is the long-term impact of cobot collaboration on operator workload and acceptance as they gain experience with the technology?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The study found mixed effects on workload (increased for simple tasks, decreased for complex tasks) and high initial acceptance scores. However, the study only tested immediate reactions.
- Why unresolved: The study only measured short-term effects. Long-term impacts of cobot collaboration on workload adaptation, skill development, and sustained acceptance by operators are unknown.
- What evidence would resolve it: Longitudinal studies following operators over extended periods as they work with cobots, measuring changes in workload, performance, and acceptance over time.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can cobot programming and interface design be optimized to minimize bugs and improve efficiency in human-cobot collaboration?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] Bugs in the cobot system were found to increase time completion and gestures. The study highlighted the need for better-performing cobots in future research.
- Why unresolved: The study identified bugs as a significant issue but did not explore solutions for improving cobot programming or interface design to prevent such issues.
- What evidence would resolve it: Research focused on developing more robust cobot programming techniques, user-friendly interfaces, and error-prevention mechanisms to enhance the efficiency and reliability of human-cobot collaboration.

## Limitations
- Findings are based on a single cobot model (YuMi) in a specific assembly context, limiting generalizability
- The participant pool had only 3% ambidextrous individuals, potentially masking handedness adaptation effects
- The complex task included math additions, which may have introduced cognitive load factors not present in real-world assembly scenarios

## Confidence

**High Confidence**: The core finding that H/C collaboration reduces the impact of task complexity on workload while increasing time and gestures is well-supported by MANOVA results and interaction effects

**Medium Confidence**: The acceptability findings (higher perceived ease of use and pleasure in H/C condition) are reliable but may be influenced by novelty effects

**Low Confidence**: The lack of significant effects from handedness adaptation requires further validation due to the limited sample of left-handed participants

## Next Checks
1. Replicate the study with a larger proportion of left-handed and ambidextrous participants (target 30-40%) to properly test the handedness adaptation hypothesis
2. Test the collaboration effects across multiple cobot models and assembly task types to assess generalizability beyond Duplo blocks
3. Conduct a longitudinal study to distinguish between novelty effects and sustained acceptability improvements over repeated H/C interactions
