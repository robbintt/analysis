# Semantic-Aware Spectrum Sharing in Internet of Vehicles Based on Deep Reinforcement Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.07213
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07213
- Authors: Zhiyu Shao; Qiong Wu; Pingyi Fan; Nan Cheng; Wen Chen; Jiangzhou Wang; Khaled B. Letaief
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: 15% increase in HSSE and 7% increase in SRS compared to traditional communication-based and other RL-based spectrum sharing methods

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel semantic-aware spectrum sharing algorithm (SSS) for high-speed mobile Internet of Vehicles (IoV) environments. The SSS leverages Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) with a Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) approach to maximize High-Speed Semantic Spectrum Efficiency (HSSE) and enhance the Success Rate of Semantic Information Transmission (SRS) between Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) and Vehicle-to-Infrastructure (V2I) communications. The algorithm dynamically adjusts channel allocation, transmission power, and semantic symbol length to optimize spectrum utilization.

## Method Summary
The proposed SSS algorithm employs SAC-based DRL to optimize spectrum sharing in IoV environments. The algorithm considers semantic information transmission metrics rather than traditional throughput-based metrics. It jointly optimizes channel allocation, transmission power control, and semantic symbol length to maximize HSSE and SRS. The SAC framework enables efficient exploration and exploitation in the high-dimensional action space of spectrum management decisions.

## Key Results
- Achieves 15% increase in High-Speed Semantic Spectrum Efficiency (HSSE) compared to traditional methods
- Improves Success Rate of Semantic Information Transmission (SRS) by 7% over baseline approaches
- Demonstrates superior performance compared to both traditional communication-based and other reinforcement learning-based spectrum sharing methods

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The algorithm's success stems from its ability to capture and optimize semantic information transmission rather than raw data throughput. By using SAC-based DRL, the system can dynamically adapt to changing channel conditions and vehicle mobility patterns while optimizing for meaningful information delivery. The joint optimization of channel allocation, power control, and semantic symbol length enables more efficient spectrum utilization compared to methods that optimize these parameters independently.

## Foundational Learning
1. **Semantic Communication**: Understanding that successful transmission depends on information meaning rather than bit accuracy
   - Why needed: Traditional metrics fail to capture the quality of semantic information delivery in IoV
   - Quick check: Verify that semantic metrics align with actual information utility for vehicular applications

2. **Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) Algorithm**: An off-policy DRL algorithm that maximizes both expected reward and entropy
   - Why needed: Handles the high-dimensional action space of spectrum management decisions
   - Quick check: Confirm SAC's stability and sample efficiency compared to other DRL algorithms

3. **High-Speed Channel Modeling**: Accurate representation of rapidly changing V2V and V2I channels
   - Why needed: Vehicle mobility creates fast-fading conditions requiring adaptive resource allocation
   - Quick check: Validate channel model parameters against real-world vehicular measurements

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Environment (Channel Conditions, Vehicle Mobility) -> DRL Agent (SAC) -> Action Space (Channel Allocation, Power Control, Symbol Length) -> Performance Metrics (HSSE, SRS)

**Critical Path**: Channel State Information → DRL State Representation → Policy Network → Action Selection → Spectrum Resource Allocation → Transmission → Performance Evaluation

**Design Tradeoffs**: 
- Accuracy vs. Complexity: Higher-dimensional state representations improve performance but increase computational overhead
- Exploration vs. Exploitation: SAC's entropy maximization balances learning new strategies with exploiting known good policies
- Real-time vs. Offline Processing: Algorithm must operate within vehicular communication latency constraints

**Failure Signatures**: 
- Degradation in HSSE/SRS when vehicle density exceeds training scenarios
- Instability in performance metrics during rapid topology changes
- Performance collapse when semantic channel state information becomes unreliable

**First 3 Experiments**:
1. Compare SSS performance against traditional optimization methods under varying vehicle densities
2. Evaluate algorithm robustness to channel estimation errors and mobility prediction inaccuracies
3. Test computational complexity and real-time performance in high-density vehicular scenarios

## Limitations
- Performance improvements lack statistical significance testing and context on baseline performance levels
- Scalability to large-scale IoV networks with hundreds of vehicles is not addressed
- Algorithm assumes reliable V2V and V2I channel state information, which may not hold in real-world high-mobility scenarios
