# Scalable Mechanism Design for Multi-Agent Path Finding

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2401.17044
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.17044
- Reference count: 26
- This paper introduces strategyproof mechanisms for Multi-Agent Path Finding (MAPF) that enable truthful reporting of costs and values by agents

## Executive Summary
This paper presents strategyproof mechanisms for Multi-Agent Path Finding (MAPF) that allow agents to truthfully report their costs and values for arriving at goals. The core innovation leverages the maximal-in-range (MIR) property to enable strategyproofness even when using suboptimal MAPF algorithms. Three mechanisms are introduced: Payment-CBS (PCBS), Exhaustive-PBS (EPBS), and Monte-Carlo Prioritized Planning (MCPP), each offering different trade-offs between optimality and scalability.

The MCPP mechanism demonstrates significant scalability improvements over the other approaches while maintaining strategyproofness. It achieves 100% success rate with thousands of agents on 3D maps, while the other mechanisms fail to scale beyond hundreds of agents. The paper shows how MIR can be used to design strategyproof MAPF mechanisms that scale to realistic problem sizes while improving welfare by up to 40% over baseline approaches.

## Method Summary
The paper introduces three strategyproof mechanisms for MAPF that leverage the maximal-in-range (MIR) property. Payment-CBS (PCBS) is optimal but computationally expensive, Exhaustive-PBS (EPBS) provides a suboptimal but more scalable alternative, and Monte-Carlo Prioritized Planning (MCPP) achieves optimal scalability through random sampling of priority orderings. The mechanisms allow agents to report their costs and values for reaching goals, with the central algorithm allocating paths and determining payments to ensure strategyproofness. The MCPP mechanism samples priority orderings and solves MAPF for each ordering, selecting the solution that maximizes welfare.

## Key Results
- MCPP achieves 100% success rate with thousands of agents on 3D maps, while PCBS and EPBS fail to scale beyond hundreds of agents
- MCPP improves welfare by up to 40% over a simple baseline mechanism
- The mechanisms maintain strategyproofness while enabling truthful reporting of costs and values
- MCPP provides a practical trade-off between solution quality and computational efficiency through random sampling

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanisms work by leveraging the maximal-in-range (MIR) property, which ensures that the solution space considered by the mechanism is restricted in a way that prevents agents from benefiting from misreporting their costs and values. By designing the mechanism to only consider solutions within this restricted range that maximize social welfare, agents are incentivized to report truthfully. The MCPP mechanism extends this by sampling priority orderings, solving MAPF for each ordering, and selecting the solution with maximum welfare, thus maintaining strategyproofness while achieving scalability.

## Foundational Learning
- Maximal-in-Range (MIR) property: A mechanism property ensuring that the selected solution maximizes social welfare within a restricted solution space. Why needed: Enables strategyproofness even with suboptimal algorithms. Quick check: Verify that the mechanism's solution space is indeed restricted to the MIR range.
- Strategyproofness: A mechanism property where agents maximize their utility by reporting truthfully. Why needed: Ensures agents report their true costs and values. Quick check: Confirm that no agent can benefit from misreporting.
- MAPF algorithms: Methods for finding collision-free paths for multiple agents. Why needed: Core computational component of the mechanisms. Quick check: Validate that the chosen MAPF algorithm produces valid solutions within the MIR range.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map:**
Agents -> Reporting interface -> Mechanism (PCBS/EPBS/MCPP) -> MAPF solver -> Solution and payments -> Agents

**Critical path:**
Agent reports costs/values → Mechanism selects priority ordering (MCPP) or exhaustive search (PCBS/EPBS) → MAPF solver finds collision-free paths → Mechanism calculates payments → Agents receive paths and payments

**Design tradeoffs:**
- Optimality vs scalability: PCBS is optimal but doesn't scale; MCPP scales optimally but may produce suboptimal solutions
- Computational efficiency vs solution quality: MCPP trades solution quality for significant scalability improvements
- Mechanism complexity vs incentive compatibility: More complex mechanisms may be harder to implement but provide better incentives

**Failure signatures:**
- Agents benefiting from misreporting costs/values (violates strategyproofness)
- Mechanism failing to find any solution (scalability issues)
- Payments not covering agents' costs (violates individual rationality)
- Suboptimal solutions in MCPP due to insufficient sampling

## Limitations
- Assumes agents will truthfully report costs and values, which may not hold in practice with complex mechanisms
- Doesn't address potential collusion between agents or sophisticated strategic manipulation
- Uses synthetic benchmark problems that may not fully capture real-world MAPF scenarios
- MCPP's reliance on random sampling may lead to suboptimal solutions in some cases
