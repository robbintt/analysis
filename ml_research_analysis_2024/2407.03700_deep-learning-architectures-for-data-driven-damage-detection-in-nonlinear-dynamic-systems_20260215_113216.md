# Deep learning architectures for data-driven damage detection in nonlinear dynamic systems

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2407.03700
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.03700
- Reference count: 16
- Deep learning approach for unsupervised damage detection in nonlinear dynamic systems using AE and GAN architectures

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a deep learning approach for unsupervised damage detection in nonlinear dynamic systems using two architectures: an autoencoder (AE) with 1D convolutional neural networks and a generative adversarial network (GAN). The AE reconstructs system responses, with larger reconstruction errors indicating damage, while the GAN discriminator output serves as a damage index, with values closer to 0 indicating damage. Both methods are tested on numerical examples including single-degree-of-freedom and two-degree-of-freedom systems with cubic nonlinearity, as well as a seismic isolator with superelastic hysteresis and negative stiffness. Experimental validation is performed on a magneto-elastic system. Results show that both architectures successfully detect damage without prior knowledge of the system or excitation, with AE demonstrating more consistent performance and GAN showing exceptional sensitivity in certain cases.

## Method Summary
The study implements two deep learning architectures for unsupervised damage detection: an autoencoder (AE) using 1D convolutional neural networks that reconstructs system responses, and a generative adversarial network (GAN) that learns to generate healthy system responses. The AE architecture uses convolutional layers to process time-series data, with reconstruction error serving as the damage indicator. The GAN consists of a generator and discriminator, where the discriminator's output serves as the damage index. Both architectures are trained only on healthy system data and tested on scenarios with simulated damage. The methods are evaluated on numerical examples of nonlinear dynamic systems and validated experimentally on a magneto-elastic system.

## Key Results
- AE demonstrates more consistent damage detection performance across different scenarios
- GAN shows exceptional sensitivity in detecting certain types of damage
- Both architectures successfully detect damage without prior knowledge of system parameters or excitation
- Experimental validation on magneto-elastic system confirms numerical findings
- AE architecture is simpler to configure and train compared to GAN

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The deep learning architectures work by learning the underlying patterns and dynamics of healthy nonlinear systems. The AE learns to compress and reconstruct healthy system responses, so when damage occurs, the reconstruction error increases due to the altered system dynamics. The GAN learns to generate realistic healthy system responses, and the discriminator learns to distinguish between real healthy data and generated data. When damage occurs, the discriminator's output shifts toward 0, indicating the system response no longer matches the healthy pattern. Both methods leverage the nonlinear mapping capabilities of neural networks to capture complex damage signatures without requiring explicit physical modeling or prior damage knowledge.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Raw sensor data -> 1D CNN layers -> Encoder -> Latent space -> Decoder -> 1D CNN layers -> Reconstructed output

Critical path: Data preprocessing -> Model training on healthy data -> Damage detection through reconstruction error analysis

Design tradeoffs: AE offers simpler implementation and more consistent performance, while GAN provides higher sensitivity but requires more complex training and tuning. AE is computationally less intensive and more stable, whereas GAN may capture finer damage signatures but at the cost of training stability and increased computational resources.

Failure signatures: High reconstruction error in undamaged conditions indicates overfitting; inconsistent discriminator outputs suggest training instability; failure to detect known damage points to insufficient model capacity or training data.

Three concrete first experiments:
1. Train AE on healthy data from SDOF system with cubic nonlinearity, test on damaged data with parameter variations
2. Train GAN on healthy data, evaluate discriminator output on both healthy and damaged system responses
3. Compare reconstruction error thresholds for damage detection across different damage severities in 2DOF system

## Limitations
- Computational complexity and training stability of GAN architectures may limit practical applicability
- Results primarily demonstrated on single and two-degree-of-freedom systems, scalability to larger structures unclear
- Performance under varying environmental conditions and measurement noise levels not extensively explored
