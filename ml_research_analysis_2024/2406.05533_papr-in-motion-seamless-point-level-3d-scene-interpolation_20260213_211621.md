# PAPR in Motion: Seamless Point-level 3D Scene Interpolation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.05533
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05533
- Authors: Shichong Peng; Yanshu Zhang; Ke Li
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: Introduces novel point-level 3D scene interpolation task, achieves significant improvements over Dynamic Gaussian on SI-FID, SI-CD, and SI-EMD metrics

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces PAPR in Motion, a method for point-level 3D scene interpolation that generates smooth transitions between two distinct 3D scene states without requiring intermediate supervision. The approach builds upon the Proximity Attention Point Rendering (PAPR) technique, leveraging its ability to handle large geometry changes through an attention mechanism that normalizes point contributions. The method introduces two key regularization components - local distance preservation loss and displacement averaging - to ensure temporal coherence during interpolation. Extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world scenes demonstrate significant quantitative improvements over state-of-the-art dynamic scene neural renderers.

## Method Summary
PAPR in Motion addresses the novel task of interpolating between two 3D scene states by fine-tuning a pre-trained PAPR model on the end state while maintaining geometry parameters as trainable variables. The method employs a proximity attention mechanism that normalizes point contributions based on relative distances, preventing gradient vanishing for distant points. During fine-tuning, two regularization techniques are applied: a local distance preservation loss that maintains distances between each point and its k-nearest neighbors from the start state, and a local displacement averaging step that encourages uniform motion of nearby points. For appearance, feature vectors and attention mechanism weights are fine-tuned and interpolated based on the interpolation progress. The approach generates intermediate states by tracking point positions during fine-tuning and applies temporal smoothing to ensure visual coherence.

## Key Results
- Achieves Scene Interpolation FID (SI-FID) of 1.25 on synthetic scenes, outperforming Dynamic Gaussian's 6.33
- Improves Scene Interpolation Chamfer distance (SI-CD) from 0.022 to 0.011 on real-world scenes
- Maintains temporal consistency while handling complex scene changes including rigid and non-rigid transformations

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: PAPR in Motion can deform a point cloud to match a significantly different shape while maintaining visual coherence after non-rigid deformations.
- Mechanism: The proximity attention mechanism in PAPR computes relative contribution weights of points based on their distances to the pixel, normalizing total contribution to always sum to 1. This prevents gradient vanishing for distant points and allows movement of points regardless of their initial positions.
- Core assumption: The attention mechanism's normalization ensures that distant points still have meaningful gradient signals, preventing the model from getting stuck in local optima when initial and target geometries differ substantially.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "PAPR in Motion... builds upon the recent Proximity Attention Point Rendering (PAPR) technique, which can deform a point cloud to match a significantly different shape and render a visually coherent scene even after non-rigid deformations."
  - [section 4.1] "Instead of solely considering the absolute distance of each point to the pixel, PAPR takes the relative distances of different points to the pixel into account. This is achieved through an attention mechanism, which normalizes the contributions of all points, ensuring their total contribution always sums to 1."
- Break condition: If the point cloud becomes too sparse or the attention mechanism fails to identify relevant points for interpolation, the method may lose coherence in rendering.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Local distance preservation loss encourages local rigidity by maintaining distances between each point and its k-nearest neighbors from the start state.
- Mechanism: During fine-tuning, the loss term penalizes changes in squared ℓ2 distances between points and their neighbors, calculated as Lrigid = (1/kN) Σi Σj∈NNk(i) |d0i,j - dti,j|, where d0i,j is the initial distance and dti,j is the current distance.
- Core assumption: Points that are close together in the initial state tend to move together in coherent motion, so preserving their relative distances maintains structural integrity.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 4.3] "We propose adding a regularization term, termed the local distance preservation loss, during finetuning, which encourages local rigidity by preserving distances between each point and its neighbours."
  - [section 4.3] "Specifically, we begin by finding the k-nearest neighbours NNk(i) for each point i based on their positions at the start state p0i, and keeping track of its squared ℓ2 distances d0i,j to each neighbouring point j in NNk(i)."
- Break condition: If k is set too high, the regularization may overly constrain movement and prevent parts from interpolating correctly, especially in scenes with intricate geometries.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Local displacement averaging step encourages uniform motion of nearby points to maintain cohesiveness of moving parts.
- Mechanism: The position of each point is adjusted based on the average displacement of its k-nearest neighbors from their initial positions, using the formula: p̂t_i = p0_i + (1/k) Σj∈NNk(i) (pt_j - p0_j).
- Core assumption: Nearby points should exhibit similar motion patterns to maintain the visual coherence of moving parts throughout the interpolation process.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 4.3] "To address this and further smooth out point motion, we introduce a local displacement averaging step. This step is designed to encourage uniform motion of nearby points and its neighbours, which is crucial for maintaining the cohesiveness of moving parts and ensuring temporal visual coherence."
  - [section 4.3] "In the local displacement averaging step, we modify the position of each point i by first recalculating its displacement from its initial position at the start state h_t^i = p_t^i - p0_i. Then we adjust it based on the average displacement of its k-nearest neighbours at the start state NNk(i) from their respective initial positions."
- Break condition: If applied too early or with inappropriate frequency, this averaging may slow down geometry adaptation and prevent the model from reaching the target geometry accurately.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Pre-trained PAPR model -> Fine-tuning with geometry parameters -> Regularization (distance preservation + displacement averaging) -> Appearance fine-tuning -> Interpolation for intermediate states -> Temporal smoothing -> Novel view synthesis

- Critical path: Start state PAPR model → Fine-tuning with geometry parameters → Regularization (distance preservation + displacement averaging) → Appearance fine-tuning → Interpolation for intermediate states → Temporal smoothing → Novel view synthesis

- Design tradeoffs: Using a sparse point cloud (30,000 points) for PAPR initialization vs. denser point clouds for baseline methods; balancing rigidity (higher k) vs. flexibility (lower k) in regularization; early vs. late application of displacement averaging

- Failure signatures: Points drifting away from object surfaces, loss of structural integrity in object parts, failure to preserve appearance details, inability to handle areas not visible in the initial state

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Test PAPR's ability to handle large geometry changes by initializing with random points and attempting to fit a simple synthetic scene with rigid motion
  2. Evaluate the impact of local distance preservation loss by comparing interpolations with and without this regularization on a scene with moderate geometry changes
  3. Assess the effect of displacement averaging frequency by varying the interval m and measuring the quality of intermediate renderings on a scene with non-rigid motion

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How can the proposed method be extended to handle scenarios where new objects enter the scene during the interpolation, which is currently a limitation?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper mentions that the model is pretrained on the start state and might have challenges rendering areas not visible in the initial state but present in the end state, such as when new objects enter the scene.
- Why unresolved: The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but does not provide a solution or approach to handle such scenarios.
- What evidence would resolve it: Developing a method that can handle new objects entering the scene during interpolation, possibly by incorporating object detection or segmentation techniques to identify and model new objects dynamically.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can the method be improved to better capture and replicate real-world motion, given that it does not assume any prior knowledge on the scene object, such as its semantics?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper states that since it does not assume any prior knowledge on the scene object, such as its semantics, the generated motion may not precisely replicate real-world motion.
- Why unresolved: The paper recognizes this as a limitation but does not explore potential approaches to incorporate semantic understanding or real-world motion priors.
- What evidence would resolve it: Incorporating semantic understanding or real-world motion priors into the model, potentially through the use of pre-trained models or additional training data that captures realistic motion patterns.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How does the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of nearest neighbors (k) and the interval size (m) for local displacement averaging, affect the interpolation quality, and what is the optimal range for these parameters?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper includes a sensitivity analysis on the effect of varying k and m, showing that higher values of k lead to increased rigidity and larger values of m may slow down the geometry adaptation process.
- Why unresolved: While the paper provides insights into the impact of these hyperparameters, it does not establish a definitive optimal range or provide a systematic approach to determine the best values for different scenes.
- What evidence would resolve it: Conducting a more extensive sensitivity analysis across a wider range of scenes and scenarios to identify the optimal hyperparameter values, potentially through the use of automated hyperparameter tuning techniques.

## Limitations
- Performance depends heavily on the quality of initial PAPR training on the start state
- Assumes end state geometry can be represented with similar number of points as start state
- May struggle with scenes involving topology changes or significant occlusions between states

## Confidence

- High: Proximity attention mechanism's ability to handle large geometry changes
- Medium: Effectiveness of local distance preservation loss
- Medium: Local displacement averaging technique
