# Quantitative causality, causality-guided scientific discovery, and causal machine learning

## Quick Facts

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the need for rigorous causality analysis in scientific research, particularly in climate science, by developing a quantitative formalism based on dynamical systems. The Liang-Kleeman information flow method quantifies causality between variables using the Frobenius-Perron operator, providing explicit closed-form formulas. This approach overcomes limitations of traditional statistical methods by offering physical interpretability and computational efficiency. Applications demonstrate significant scientific discoveries, such as identifying the anthropogenic cause of global warming, predicting El Niño Modoki up to 10 years in advance, and forecasting extreme droughts. The method has also been integrated into causal AI algorithms, enhancing interpretability and performance in machine learning tasks.

## Method Summary
The Liang-Kleeman information flow method quantifies causality between variables using the Frobenius-Perron operator to derive the rate of information transfer. For linear systems, this reduces to a correlation-based estimator: T_i→j = a_ij * σ_ij / σ_i². The principle of nil causality allows efficient causal graph reconstruction by testing if information flow is zero. Maximum likelihood estimation and statistical significance testing (via Fisher information matrix) are used to estimate and validate causal relations from data.

## Limitations

- The method's validity hinges on the stochastic dynamical systems framework and Frobenius-Perron operator formulation, which may not hold for all real-world systems.
- The linear estimator's reliance on constant stochastic perturbation amplitudes is a significant limitation for systems with time-varying noise.
- The principle of nil causality may not fully account for latent confounders or measurement noise, potentially leading to false negatives in causal discovery.
