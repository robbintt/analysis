# The Fairness-Quality Trade-off in Clustering

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2408.10002
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.10002
- Authors: Rashida Hakim; Ana-Andreea Stoica; Christos H. Papadimitriou; Mihalis Yannakakis
- Reference count: 40
- This paper addresses the computation of Pareto fronts for fair clustering problems, where two objectives (clustering quality and fairness) must be optimized simultaneously.

## Executive Summary
This paper tackles the fundamental trade-off between clustering quality and fairness by computing Pareto fronts for fair clustering problems. Rather than optimizing one objective subject to constraints on the other, the authors develop algorithms to find all clusterings that are not dominated in both objectives. The approach is particularly relevant for pattern-based fairness objectives that depend only on attribute counts per cluster.

The authors present a dynamic programming algorithm that exactly computes the Pareto front for the assignment problem (fixed centers) in polynomial time, and provides approximation guarantees for the clustering problem (finding centers). A key theoretical contribution is a polynomial-time algorithm for computing the Pareto front when the fairness objective is the sum of imbalances between two groups.

## Method Summary
The core method uses dynamic programming to enumerate all possible clusterings for pattern-based fairness objectives. For the assignment problem with fixed centers, the algorithm exactly computes the Pareto front in O(knl(k-1)) time. For the clustering problem where centers need to be found, it provides a (2+α, 1)-approximation, where α is the approximation ratio of the underlying clustering algorithm. The approach is designed to work with pattern-based fairness objectives, which depend only on attribute counts per cluster rather than the specific identities of individuals.

## Key Results
- Exact Pareto front computation for assignment problem (fixed centers) in O(knl(k-1)) time
- (2+α, 1)-approximation for clustering problem (finding centers)
- Polynomial-time algorithm for sum-of-imbalance fairness objectives
- Experiments on three real-world datasets show meaningful trade-off curves between clustering cost and fairness

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The approach works by explicitly computing the Pareto front rather than treating fairness as a constraint. Dynamic programming enables efficient enumeration of all possible clusterings for pattern-based objectives. The key insight is that for pattern-based fairness (which depends only on attribute counts per cluster), the search space can be structured to enable polynomial-time computation of non-dominated solutions.

## Foundational Learning
- **Pareto optimality**: Why needed - to identify trade-offs between competing objectives; Quick check - verify no solution dominates another in both objectives
- **Dynamic programming**: Why needed - to efficiently explore the space of possible clusterings; Quick check - confirm overlapping subproblems and optimal substructure
- **Pattern-based fairness**: Why needed - enables polynomial-time computation by reducing complexity; Quick check - ensure fairness depends only on counts, not identities
- **Approximation algorithms**: Why needed - to handle cases where exact computation is intractable; Quick check - verify approximation ratios are maintained
- **Multi-objective optimization**: Why needed - to properly model the fairness-quality trade-off; Quick check - confirm both objectives are properly weighted

## Limitations

- Does not address high-dimensional data or complex fairness constraints
- Practical scalability for large datasets remains unclear
