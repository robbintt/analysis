# MealRec$^+$: A Meal Recommendation Dataset with Meal-Course Affiliation for Personalization and Healthiness

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2404.05386
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.05386
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Introduces MealRec+, a novel dataset enabling cooperative interaction learning for personalized and healthy meal recommendations

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the lack of publicly available meal recommendation datasets with meal-course affiliation, which hinders research on personalized and healthy meal recommendations. To solve this, the authors construct a new benchmark dataset called MealRec+, which includes user-meal interactions, user-course interactions, meal-course affiliations, and healthiness scores calculated using two widely recognized nutritional standards. The dataset is derived by simulating user dining sessions based on existing user-course interaction data and then mining additional interactions using collaborative filtering principles. Experiments with various baseline models demonstrate that cooperative interaction learning methods outperform separate interaction learning approaches for personalization. However, without explicit healthiness guidance, recommendations tend to be less healthy. The study also explores post-filtering methods to improve healthiness, showing that re-weighting can effectively balance personalization and healthiness. MealRec+ provides a valuable resource for advancing AI-driven, health-aware meal recommendation research.

## Method Summary
The authors construct MealRec+ by simulating user dining sessions to derive meal-course affiliations and user-meal interactions from existing user-course interaction data. They then apply collaborative filtering to mine additional interactions. Healthiness scores are calculated using FSA and WHO nutritional standards. The dataset is evaluated using various baseline models with separate and cooperative interaction learning methods, measuring personalization through Recall@K and NDCG@K, and healthiness through mean FSA/WHO scores and ranking exposure gaps.

## Key Results
- Cooperative interaction learning methods outperform separate interaction learning for personalization
- Healthiness of recommendations degrades without explicit healthiness guidance
- Re-weighting post-filtering methods effectively balance personalization and healthiness
- MealRec+ provides a valuable resource for advancing health-aware meal recommendation research

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How does the proposed simulation method for generating user-meal interactions compare in quality and representativeness to real-world data collection methods, and what are the potential biases introduced by this simulation approach?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper acknowledges that the simulation method is adopted due to constraints related to user health privacy and meal scenario characteristics, which hinder the collection of real-world data.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not provide a detailed evaluation of the simulation method's accuracy or potential biases compared to real-world data.
- What evidence would resolve it: A comparative study using real-world data (if available) to validate the simulation method's accuracy and identify potential biases would be needed.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What is the optimal balance between personalization and healthiness in meal recommendations, and how can this balance be achieved without compromising either aspect significantly?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper explores methods to improve the healthiness of recommendations but notes that hard filtering methods compromise personalization, while re-weighting methods offer a trade-off.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not provide a definitive answer on the optimal balance between personalization and healthiness, as it depends on individual user preferences and health goals.
- What evidence would resolve it: User studies or A/B testing with different balance strategies would be needed to determine the optimal trade-off for various user segments.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How do different nutritional standards (e.g., FSA, WHO) impact the healthiness evaluation of meals, and which standard is more suitable for meal recommendation systems?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper uses two nutritional standards (FSA and WHO) to calculate healthiness scores but does not compare their effectiveness in meal recommendation.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not provide a comparative analysis of the two standards' impact on recommendation outcomes or user satisfaction.
- What evidence would resolve it: A study comparing the performance and user acceptance of meal recommendations based on different nutritional standards would be needed.

## Limitations

- Healthiness scores are calculated based on standardized nutritional standards rather than user-specific health conditions or preferences
- Dataset construction relies on simulated dining sessions, which may not fully capture real-world user behavior patterns
- The dataset is derived from the Yummly dataset, which may have inherent biases from its original user base

## Confidence

- **High Confidence:** The dataset construction methodology and the experimental framework are clearly described and reproducible.
- **Medium Confidence:** The effectiveness of cooperative interaction learning is demonstrated, but the improvement over separate learning methods is incremental.
- **Low Confidence:** The healthiness evaluation metrics, while well-intentioned, may not fully capture real-world health outcomes due to their reliance on standardized scores rather than personalized health considerations.
