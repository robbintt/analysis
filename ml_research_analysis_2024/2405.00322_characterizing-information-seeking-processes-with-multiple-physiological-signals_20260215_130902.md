# Characterizing Information Seeking Processes with Multiple Physiological Signals

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2405.00322
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00322
- Authors: Kaixin Ji; Danula Hettiachchi; Flora D. Salim; Falk Scholer; Damiano Spina
- Reference count: 40
- One-line primary result: Physiological signals effectively characterize user behavior and emotional responses during information seeking processes

## Executive Summary
This study investigates how cognitive and affective states evolve during information-seeking processes using physiological signals. The researchers conducted a controlled lab study with 26 participants, collecting multimodal data including EEG, EDA, PPG, and pupillary responses. Four search stages were analyzed: Information Need realization, Query Formulation, Query Submission, and Relevance Judgment. Key findings include higher cognitive load at Information Need realization with increased alertness, greater attention requirements during Query Formulation, and more pronounced affective responses during Relevance Judgment, indicating increased interest and engagement.

## Method Summary
The study employed a controlled lab experiment with 26 participants using synchronized physiological sensors including EEG (Emotiv EPOC X), eye-tracker (Tobii Fusion), EDA/PPG (Empatica E4), and webcam. Participants completed 12 search tasks while physiological data was collected across four search stages. The data underwent preprocessing including filtering and artifact removal, then physiological indexes were computed (TAR for cognitive load, BAR/EDA/HRV for arousal, FAA for valence). Statistical analysis used Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Bonferroni correction to validate hypotheses about stage-specific physiological patterns.

## Key Results
- Higher cognitive load (TAR) and alertness at Information Need realization stage
- Greater attention requirements (RPD) during Query Formulation stage
- More pronounced affective responses (BAR, SCL, FAA) during Relevance Judgment indicating increased interest and engagement

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- **Claim:** Controlled lab conditions enable isolation of search-stage-specific physiological patterns without confounding variables from real-world noise.
- **Mechanism:** By tightly controlling stimulus presentation, timing, and sensor calibration, the experiment minimizes environmental and contextual variability that could obscure subtle physiological differences between search stages.
- **Core assumption:** Laboratory control sufficiently simulates real-world search behavior while allowing detection of low-amplitude physiological signals.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "Due to the complex nature of information activities and the sensitivity of physiological sensors [28], we conduct a highly controlled lab study to eliminate confounding variables as much as possible."
  - [section] "This study focuses on four search stages in a single iteration: the realization of Information Need (IN), Query Formulation (QF), Query Submission (QS), and Relevance Judgment (RJ)."
- **Break condition:** If real-world noise is critical to understanding search behavior (e.g., interruptions, multitasking), lab control could mask important patterns.

### Mechanism 2
- **Claim:** Physiological indexes derived from multimodal sensors capture distinct cognitive and affective states across search stages with sufficient sensitivity.
- **Mechanism:** Each index (TAR for cognitive load, BAR/EDA/HRV for arousal, FAA for valence) targets specific neural or peripheral activity patterns, and their combinations reveal stage-specific differences.
- **Core assumption:** The physiological indexes used are valid and reliable indicators of the constructs they measure, and multimodal integration provides complementary information.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "This study examines informational search with four stages: the realization of Information Need (IN), Query Formulation (QF), Query Submission (QS), and Relevance Judgment (RJ)."
  - [section] "Theta-Alpha Ratio (TAR) has been validated by Raufi and Longo [63] as an index level of cognitive load."
  - [corpus] "Found 25 related papers (using 8). Average neighbor FMR=0.472, average citations=0.0." *(Weak corpus support for this specific methodological approach)*
- **Break condition:** If indexes are not sensitive enough to detect stage differences, or if noise corrupts signal quality, conclusions about stage-specific states may be invalid.

### Mechanism 3
- **Claim:** Separating Query Formulation and Query Submission as distinct stages captures different cognitive demands and affective responses.
- **Mechanism:** QF involves problem-solving and term generation, while QS involves expression and motor coordination; these activities generate distinct physiological signatures detectable by the sensors.
- **Core assumption:** QF and QS are sufficiently distinct in cognitive and affective processes to produce measurable physiological differences.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "Although QF and QS are usually consecutive stages in real-world scenarios, the literature suggests that their underlying activities diverge (discussed later in Section 2), especially when considering the impact of interaction modalities [29]."
  - [section] "The high TAR and RPD at QS are potentially due to the simultaneous cognitive activities for recalling information, and forming and expressing the query."
- **Break condition:** If QF and QS are not sufficiently distinct, combining them could simplify analysis without loss of information.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:** 
  - Data collection: Eye-tracker (pupillary responses) -> wristband (EDA, PPG) -> EEG headset (brain activity)
  - Processing pipeline: Signal cleaning -> Index computation -> Statistical analysis
  - Analysis layer: Hypothesis testing with non-parametric methods
  - Output: Stage-specific physiological patterns

- **Critical path:** 
  - Data collection -> Signal preprocessing -> Index computation -> Statistical analysis -> Hypothesis validation
  - Bottleneck: Signal quality and preprocessing time

- **Design tradeoffs:**
  - Lab control vs. ecological validity
  - Sensor complexity vs. participant comfort
  - Granularity of stages vs. experimental complexity

- **Failure signatures:**
  - High data loss during preprocessing indicates sensor placement or environmental issues
  - Non-significant results across all hypotheses suggest either weak effects or methodological problems
  - Inconsistent index patterns across subjects indicate individual differences not accounted for

- **First 3 experiments:**
  1. Re-run analysis with different preprocessing parameters to assess robustness
  2. Test whether combining QF and QS stages affects results
  3. Apply machine learning to detect patterns across all physiological features simultaneously

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How do physiological signals differ between conversational information seeking with LLMs versus traditional search interfaces?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] "We believe our proposed methodology can inform the characterization of more complex processes, such as conversational information seeking with Large Language Model-based systems."
- Why unresolved: The current study only examined traditional search interfaces with predefined search stages. Conversational interactions involve different cognitive and affective dynamics.
- What evidence would resolve it: A controlled study comparing physiological signals during conversational search with LLMs versus traditional search, measuring the same indexes across comparable stages.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What are the temporal dynamics of physiological signals within each search stage, rather than aggregated values?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] "Although IN and RJ may share similarities, our findings of significant differences in HRV and RPD, and observations of higher FAA, further highlight the difference between these two stages." The paper compresses signals into single values.
- Why unresolved: The study aggregates physiological data within each stage, missing potential fluctuations and patterns during the process.
- What evidence would resolve it: Fine-grained temporal analysis of physiological signals within each stage, potentially revealing patterns of cognitive load and arousal changes during different phases of information processing.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How do individual differences in search expertise and domain knowledge affect physiological responses during information seeking?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] "Firstly, although we attempted to eliminate confounding variables, other factors, such as users' search skills and prior domain knowledge, may have influenced the results."
- Why unresolved: The study controlled for some variables but did not examine how individual differences might modulate physiological responses.
- What evidence would resolve it: A study explicitly measuring participants' search expertise and domain knowledge, then analyzing how these factors correlate with physiological signal patterns during information seeking.

## Limitations

- Controlled lab setting limits generalizability to real-world information seeking environments with multitasking and interruptions
- Small sample size (n=26) constrains statistical power and may not capture individual differences
- Focus on single iteration rather than examining patterns across multiple search sessions or complex multi-step tasks

## Confidence

High confidence: The laboratory control mechanism effectively isolates search-stage-specific physiological patterns
Medium confidence: The physiological indexes reliably capture distinct cognitive and affective states
Medium confidence: The separation of QF and QS stages captures meaningful differences

## Next Checks

1. Conduct an ecological validity test by replicating key measurements in a naturalistic setting with realistic distractions and compare physiological patterns to the lab results.
2. Increase sample size to 50+ participants and examine individual difference factors (search experience, domain expertise) as moderators of physiological responses.
3. Extend the methodology to conversational information seeking scenarios, comparing physiological patterns across single-query and multi-turn interactions.
