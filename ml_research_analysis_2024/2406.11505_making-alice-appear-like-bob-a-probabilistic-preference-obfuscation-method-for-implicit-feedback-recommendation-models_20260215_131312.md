# Making Alice Appear Like Bob: A Probabilistic Preference Obfuscation Method For Implicit Feedback Recommendation Models

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.11505
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.11505
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: SBO improves balanced accuracy by up to 9% for gender protection while maintaining recommendation quality

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces Stereotypicality-Based Obfuscation (SBO), a probabilistic method designed to protect user privacy in implicit feedback recommender systems by mitigating attribute inference attacks. SBO operates by identifying items with stereotypical preferences associated with sensitive attributes and probabilistically obfuscating them in user profiles. The method aims to reduce the correlation between user behavior patterns and sensitive attributes while maintaining recommendation accuracy. The approach is evaluated across three recommendation models (BPR-MF, LightGCN, MultVAE) using two datasets (MovieLens-1M and LFM-2B), demonstrating effective privacy protection with minimal impact on recommendation performance.

## Method Summary
SBO introduces a probabilistic obfuscation framework that protects user privacy by strategically modifying user-item interaction data. The method calculates stereotypicality scores for items based on their association with sensitive attributes, then applies probabilistic masking to these items in user profiles. The core innovation lies in using stereotypicality as a metric to identify items that are most revealing of sensitive attributes, rather than random obfuscation. The probabilistic nature of the approach allows for controlled privacy protection while maintaining the overall structure of user preferences. The method is model-agnostic and can be applied to various implicit feedback recommendation systems without requiring architectural modifications.

## Key Results
- Achieved up to 9% improvement in balanced accuracy for protecting gender attributes
- Maintained comparable recommendation accuracy (NDCG) across all tested models
- Demonstrated effectiveness across three different recommendation architectures (BPR-MF, LightGCN, MultVAE)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by exploiting the relationship between stereotypical item preferences and sensitive attributes. By identifying items that strongly correlate with specific attributes (like gender), SBO can strategically obfuscate these interactions to confuse attribute inference models. The probabilistic approach ensures that the obfuscation is not easily detectable while still providing meaningful privacy protection. This targeted obfuscation is more effective than random approaches because it specifically addresses the items that contribute most to attribute leakage.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: User Profiles -> Stereotypicality Calculator -> Obfuscation Layer -> Recommendation Model -> Protected Recommendations

**Critical Path**: The stereotypicality calculation identifies items for obfuscation, which are then probabilistically masked before being fed into the recommendation model. This ensures privacy protection occurs at the data input level rather than requiring changes to the recommendation algorithms themselves.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The method balances privacy protection against recommendation accuracy by controlling the obfuscation probability. Higher obfuscation provides better privacy but may degrade recommendations. The stereotypicality-based approach targets only the most revealing items rather than applying blanket obfuscation.

**Failure Signatures**: If the obfuscation probability is too low, attribute inference attacks may still succeed. If too high, recommendation quality may significantly degrade. Cultural differences in item preferences may affect stereotypicality calculations across different user populations.

## Limitations
- Evaluation primarily focused on gender attribute protection, limiting generalizability to other sensitive attributes
- Effectiveness of stereotypicality metrics across different cultural contexts remains unexplored
- Only tested on two datasets with three recommendation models, limiting understanding of broader applicability
