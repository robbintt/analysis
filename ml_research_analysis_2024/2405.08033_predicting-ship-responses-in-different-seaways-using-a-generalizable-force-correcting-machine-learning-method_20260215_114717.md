# Predicting Ship Responses in Different Seaways using a Generalizable Force Correcting Machine Learning Method

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2405.08033
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.08033
- Reference count: 16
- Key outcome: A hybrid ML method combining physics-based models with data-driven corrections achieves better generalizability across sea states than purely data-driven approaches

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a hybrid machine learning approach for predicting ship responses in irregular waves that combines a low-fidelity physics-based model with a data-driven force correction term. The method is designed to improve generalizability across different sea states while requiring smaller training datasets compared to purely data-driven approaches. The approach is tested on two case studies: a nonlinear Duffing equation and a Fast Displacement Ship (FDS) in head seas. Results show the hybrid method outperforms LSTM benchmarks in prediction accuracy and generalizability, particularly in extreme wave conditions and when trained on small datasets. The inclusion of linear physics terms in the low-fidelity model is crucial for performance, especially in lower sea states where linear behavior dominates.

## Method Summary
The method embeds a data-driven force correction term into a low-fidelity equation of motion that includes linear physics terms (linear restoring forces, linear damping, and linearized wave excitation). A neural network learns the residual force corrections needed to match high-fidelity simulation data. The approach is trained on one wave condition and tested on others that differ in significant wave height and/or peak period. Performance is evaluated using L2 error, Lâˆž error, and Jensen-Shannon divergence of response distributions, with particular attention to extreme values and generalizability across varying sea states.

## Key Results
- The hybrid method achieves better prediction accuracy and generalizability than LSTM models, especially in extreme wave conditions
- The approach requires smaller training datasets while maintaining superior performance
- Including linear physics terms in the low-fidelity model is crucial for improving performance in lower sea states
- The method successfully generalizes from training on one wave condition to predicting responses in different sea states

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
The hybrid method achieves better generalizability than the LSTM by retaining linear physics terms in the low-fidelity model. By embedding a data-driven force correction term into a low-fidelity model that includes linear physics (like linear restoring forces, linear damping, and linearized wave excitation), the hybrid model maintains correct dynamics at the linear limit while allowing the ML component to learn nonlinear corrections. This combination provides better performance in extreme wave conditions and smaller training datasets compared to purely data-driven models.

### Mechanism 2
The hybrid method requires a smaller training dataset than purely data-driven approaches while maintaining performance. The hybrid approach leverages existing physics-based knowledge to reduce the amount of data needed to learn the residual corrections. The ML component only needs to learn the differences between the low-fidelity and high-fidelity models, which is a smaller, more constrained problem than learning the entire system dynamics from scratch.

### Mechanism 3
The hybrid method provides more consistent performance across different wave conditions compared to purely data-driven approaches. By incorporating physics-based constraints, the hybrid model's predictions are bounded by the underlying physics model, preventing extreme prediction errors in conditions far from the training data. This leads to more stable performance across varying sea states.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Low-fidelity physics model -> Neural network force correction -> Integration scheme -> Performance evaluation
- Critical path: Generate low-fidelity and high-fidelity response data for training conditions -> Compute force corrections by comparing low- and high-fidelity forces -> Train neural network to predict force corrections from state and wave elevation -> Use trained model to predict responses in new wave conditions -> Evaluate prediction accuracy against high-fidelity reference data
- Design tradeoffs: Including more physics in the low-fidelity model improves generalizability but reduces the learning capacity of the ML component; larger stencil sizes improve accuracy for systems with memory effects but increase computational cost; smaller training datasets reduce data requirements but may limit accuracy
- Failure signatures: Poor performance in low sea states suggests insufficient physics in the low-fidelity model; large errors in extreme wave conditions suggest the ML component cannot learn appropriate corrections; inconsistent performance across wave conditions suggests overfitting to training data
- First 3 experiments: Implement the hybrid method for the Duffing equation with different low-fidelity forcing models to observe the effect of physics inclusion on generalizability; vary the training dataset size (measured in ZUCs) to determine the minimum dataset required for acceptable performance; compare the hybrid method's performance against a pure LSTM approach across a range of wave conditions to quantify the benefits of physics integration

## Limitations

- The training data is limited to a single moderate wave condition, which may not capture the full range of nonlinearities present in extreme seas
- The comparison with LSTM models uses a relatively simple LSTM architecture that may not represent state-of-the-art data-driven approaches
- The low-fidelity physics model is based on linear strip theory, which may not adequately represent all aspects of ship dynamics for the Fast Displacement Ship hull form

## Confidence

- High confidence: The hybrid method's superior performance over LSTM in extreme wave conditions and with small training datasets is well-supported by experimental results
- Medium confidence: The claim that including linear physics terms is crucial for lower sea states where linear dynamics dominate is supported but could benefit from more systematic analysis
- Medium confidence: The assertion that the hybrid method requires smaller training datasets than purely data-driven approaches is demonstrated but the minimum dataset size needed is not rigorously established
