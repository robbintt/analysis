# Integrating Reasoning Systems for Trustworthy AI, Proceedings of the 4th Workshop on Logic and Practice of Programming (LPOP)

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.19738
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.19738
- Authors: Anil Nerode; Yanhong A. Liu
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Advocates for Trustworthy AI through Rigorous Language Models combining rigorous problem specification languages, iterative development methods, and scalable reasoning engines

## Executive Summary
This position paper proposes Trustworthy AI through Rigorous Language Models (RLMs) that integrate rigorous problem specification languages with automated iterative development methods and efficient reasoning engines. The authors argue that trustworthy AI requires RRM-led approaches that contrast with LLM "fast thinking" by providing systematic, explainable reasoning ("slow thinking"). The framework aims to overcome the limitations of current AI systems by ensuring correctness through rigorous specifications while maintaining scalability through automated development processes.

## Method Summary
The paper synthesizes three core components for Trustworthy AI: a general rigorous language supporting uncertainty and logic, automated iterative specification development, and efficient reasoning exploiting all available algorithms. The approach emphasizes unified semantics for logic rules and seeks to automate error-prone manual specification development. The methodology contrasts Kahneman's System 1 thinking (LLMs) with System 2 thinking (RRMs), positioning rigorous reasoning as essential for trustworthy AI outcomes.

## Key Results
- Proposes three core RLM components: rigorous language, automated iterative development, and efficient reasoning
- Demonstrates simple unified semantics for logic rules across different reasoning paradigms
- Positions RRM-led methods as essential for trustworthy AI, contrasting with LLM approaches

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by combining the strengths of formal specification with automated development to reduce human error while maintaining rigor. The unified semantics approach allows different reasoning paradigms to interoperate, while the automated iterative process addresses the scalability challenges of traditional formal methods. The cognitive psychology analogy to System 1/2 thinking provides a conceptual framework for understanding the complementary roles of fast (LLM) and slow (RRM) reasoning in achieving trustworthy outcomes.

## Foundational Learning
- Rigorous Problem Specification Languages: Needed to ensure correctness and traceability in AI systems; quick check: can the language express domain constraints unambiguously
- Automated Iterative Development: Required to overcome the bottleneck of manual specification; quick check: does automation reduce specification time by >50% without introducing errors
- Unified Semantics for Logic Rules: Essential for combining different reasoning paradigms; quick check: can heterogeneous reasoning engines interoperate without semantic conflicts
- Cognitive Psychology Analogies: Provides conceptual framework for understanding AI reasoning approaches; quick check: does the System 1/2 analogy help practitioners choose appropriate tools

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Rigorous Language -> Automated Development -> Reasoning Engine

**Critical Path:** Specification (R) -> Development (A) -> Reasoning (E) -> Output (O)

**Design Tradeoffs:** The framework must balance rigor with usability - more expressive languages may be harder to automate, while more automated processes may sacrifice some rigor. The unified semantics approach trades potential optimization benefits of specialized systems for interoperability and composability.

**Failure Signatures:** 
- Specification bottlenecks indicate insufficient automation
- Reasoning errors suggest inadequate unified semantics
- Performance degradation indicates scalability issues with the integrated approach

**First 3 Experiments:**

3. Benchmark unified reasoning performance against specialized systems on mixed logical-probabilistic problems

## Limitations
- Unproven scalability of RRMs in real-world applications
- Limited empirical evidence for claimed trustworthiness benefits
- Practical challenges in combining incompatible reasoning paradigms

## Confidence
- Scalability claims: Low
- Theoretical framework soundness: High
- Empirical validation: Low
- Practical applicability: Medium

## Next Checks
