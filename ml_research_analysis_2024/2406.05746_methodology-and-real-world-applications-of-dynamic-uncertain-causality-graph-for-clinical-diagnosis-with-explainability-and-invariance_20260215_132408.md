# Methodology and Real-World Applications of Dynamic Uncertain Causality Graph for Clinical Diagnosis with Explainability and Invariance

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.05746
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05746
- Reference count: 34
- Key outcome: DUCG models covering 54 chief complaints and over 1,000 diseases were built through collaboration between clinical experts and DUCG technicians. These models were verified by third-party hospitals, achieving diagnostic precisions no less than 95% for all models and no less than 80% for every disease including uncommon ones. Over one million real-world diagnosis cases have been performed in China with only 17 incorrect diagnoses identified, all of which were traced and corrected. The diagnostic abilities of clinicians using DUCG frequently improved significantly.

## Executive Summary
The Dynamic Uncertain Causality Graph (DUCG) presents a causality-driven approach to clinical diagnosis that achieves high diagnostic precision while maintaining explainability and invariance across different application scenarios. The methodology involves constructing explicit causal graphs for diseases and symptoms, with expert collaboration ensuring accurate representation of medical knowledge. The system has been validated through third-party verification in Chinese hospitals and deployed in real-world settings with impressive performance metrics.

## Method Summary
DUCG models are constructed through collaboration between clinical experts and technicians, representing diseases and symptoms as nodes in causal graphs with uncertainty parameters. The system uses recursive algorithms to calculate disease probabilities given evidence, with modular construction allowing separate disease modules to be fused into complete models for each chief complaint. Third-party verification involves retrospective testing using real case records from independent hospitals, while real-world deployment includes integration with hospital systems and continuous feedback collection.

## Key Results
- Diagnostic precisions of all 46 DUCG models are no less than 95%, with every disease including uncommon ones achieving no less than 80% precision
- Over one million real-world diagnosis cases performed in China with only 17 incorrect diagnoses identified and corrected
- Diagnostic abilities of clinicians using DUCG improved significantly with frequent usage

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: DUCG's use of explicit causal graphs enables transparent and invariant diagnostic reasoning across different medical scenarios.
- Mechanism: Instead of relying on black-box deep learning, DUCG represents diseases and symptoms as nodes in a graph connected by causal relationships with uncertainty parameters. This explicit representation allows the model to propagate probabilities through the graph to calculate disease likelihood, maintaining interpretability and consistency across different clinical contexts.
- Core assumption: The causal relationships encoded in the graph accurately reflect real-world disease mechanisms and remain valid across different populations and clinical settings.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "DUCG is causality-driven, explainable, and invariant across different application scenarios"
  - [section] "Since the single-disease modules are constructed in a same way for common and uncommon diseases, there is no problem that diagnostic precision for uncommon disease is less than the common disease in principle."
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - related papers focus on graph-based approaches but don't directly validate DUCG's specific causal mechanism claims
- Break condition: If the encoded causal relationships are incorrect or if the underlying disease mechanisms change significantly between populations, the invariant property would fail.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: The modular construction approach allows DUCG to handle rare diseases effectively while maintaining high overall diagnostic accuracy.
- Mechanism: DUCG builds separate modules for individual diseases under each chief complaint, then fuses them into a complete model. This approach ensures that each disease, regardless of prevalence, has an explicit representation in the graph. The diagnostic algorithm evaluates all possible diseases simultaneously, giving equal consideration to rare conditions rather than being biased toward common ones.
- Core assumption: Expert knowledge about disease mechanisms is sufficient to construct accurate modules for rare diseases, even with limited case data.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "Since the single-disease modules are constructed in a same way for common and uncommon diseases, there is no problem that diagnostic precision for uncommon disease is less than the common disease in principle."
  - [section] "The verified diagnostic precisions were no less than 95%, in which the diagnostic precision for every disease including uncommon ones was no less than 80%."
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - related papers discuss handling rare conditions but don't provide direct validation of DUCG's approach
- Break condition: If expert knowledge is insufficient for rare diseases or if the fusion process creates conflicts between modules, diagnostic accuracy for rare conditions would degrade.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: The third-party verification methodology ensures that DUCG's performance claims are robust and generalizable.
- Mechanism: DUCG models undergo retrospective verification using real case records from independent, high-grade hospitals. The verification process tests each disease with a standardized number of cases (typically 10), ensuring that both common and rare diseases are evaluated. This methodology addresses potential biases in training data and validates the model's performance in real-world clinical settings.
- Core assumption: The selected third-party hospitals represent diverse clinical populations and their case records are representative of real-world diagnostic challenges.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "The so-called third-party means the independent hospitals who have nothing to do with the construction of the DUCG models and whose data are not used in the DUCG system."
  - [section] "To ensure the quality of the verification, the third-party hospital should be in the highest grade, i.e. grade IIIA in China."
  - [section] "The results are: the diagnostic precisions of all the 46 DUCG models are no less than 95%, in which the precision for every disease including uncommon one is no less than 80%."
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - related papers discuss verification but don't detail the specific third-party methodology used by DUCG
- Break condition: If the verification hospitals are not truly independent or their case records are not representative, the generalizability claims would be invalid.

## Limitations

- Performance claims based exclusively on Chinese hospitals and patient populations, limiting generalizability to other healthcare settings
- Limited transparency around verification datasets and case distributions, making it difficult to assess true coverage of disease presentations
- Insufficient detail on how DUCG handles rare diseases and updates causal relationships as medical knowledge evolves
