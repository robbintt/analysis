# Robust random graph matching in Gaussian models via vector approximate message passing

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2412.16457
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.16457
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: First efficient random graph matching algorithm robust to n^(1-o(1)) adversarial corruptions

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the problem of matching two correlated Gaussian Wigner matrices when both matrices are corrupted by adversarial perturbations. The authors propose a vector approximate message passing (vector AMP) algorithm that can recover the latent vertex correspondence despite a small fraction of adversarial corruptions. The algorithm combines spectral cleaning procedures with iterative refinement to achieve exact matching recovery with high probability under specific conditions on the correlation strength and perturbation fraction.

## Method Summary
The proposed vector AMP algorithm operates by first applying a spectral cleaning procedure to remove the influence of adversarial perturbations from the corrupted matrices. It then performs iterative random graph matching using vector AMP iterations that update signal matrix estimates and spectral components in parallel. The algorithm includes a rounding and refinement phase to obtain the final vertex correspondence. The key innovation is the combination of spectral cleaning with vector AMP iterations, allowing the algorithm to maintain robustness against adversarial perturbations while converging to the correct matching.

## Key Results
- Achieves exact matching recovery with high probability when correlation ρ is non-vanishing constant and ε = o(1/(log n)^20)
- First efficient algorithm robust to n^(1-o(1)) size adversarial corruptions in random graph matching
- Polynomial time complexity with explicit running time analysis

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: What is the optimal corruption scale for Gaussian matrix matching beyond the n/poly(log n) threshold?
- Basis in paper: Explicit - The paper mentions this as an open problem, noting that their algorithm works for n/poly(log n) size adversarial corruptions but it remains an open question whether algorithms can handle any constant fraction ǫ of adversarial perturbations.
- Why unresolved: The current algorithm's performance degrades significantly when the fraction of adversarial perturbations exceeds o(1/(log n)^20), and extending this to constant ǫ requires new theoretical insights.
- What evidence would resolve it: A polynomial-time algorithm that achieves exact matching recovery for any constant fraction ǫ of adversarial corruptions, along with a proof of its correctness and analysis of its running time.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can efficient algorithms achieve almost-exact matching recovery for sparse graphs with adversarial perturbations?
- Basis in paper: Explicit - The paper explicitly states this as an open problem, noting that their current design and analysis rely on dense matrices and cannot extend to the case where the average density of a graph q = n^(-c) for some c > 0.
- Why unresolved: The current algorithm's performance relies on the dense nature of the matrices, and extending it to sparse graphs requires addressing the challenge of adversarial perturbations corrupting all edges incident to one vertex.
- What evidence would resolve it: An efficient algorithm that achieves almost-exact matching recovery for sparse graphs with adversarial perturbations, along with a proof of its correctness and analysis of its running time.

### Open Question 3

- Why unresolved: The current algorithm is specifically designed for Gaussian Wigner matrices, and extending it to other graph models requires addressing the unique challenges posed by each model's structure and properties.
- What evidence would resolve it: Efficient algorithms that achieve robust graph matching for other important correlated random graph models, along with proofs of their correctness and analysis of their running time.

## Limitations

- Theoretical corruption bounds (ε = o(1/(log n)^20)) appear extremely conservative and may not reflect practical scenarios
- Algorithm assumes adversarial perturbations are confined to principal minors, which may not hold in real-world applications
- Computational complexity involves multiple spectral decompositions that could become prohibitive for very large graphs
