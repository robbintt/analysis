# Knowledge Transfer for Cross-Domain Reinforcement Learning: A Systematic Review

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2404.17687
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.17687
- **Reference count**: 40
- **Primary result**: Systematic analysis of 53 methods for transferring knowledge across different state/action space representations in RL, showing trend toward autonomous approaches with less human supervision

## Executive Summary
This systematic review examines methods for transferring knowledge between reinforcement learning domains where source and target tasks have different state and/or action space representations, such as robots with varying morphologies. The authors analyze 53 works, categorizing them based on knowledge source (expert demonstrations vs learned knowledge), source type (single vs multiple sources), and transfer approach (demonstrations, policies, parameters, reward shaping, etc.). The review reveals a clear trend toward more autonomous transfer methods that require less human supervision for inter-domain mapping, addressing the challenge of limited data in real-world applications.

## Method Summary
The authors conducted a comprehensive systematic review of cross-domain reinforcement learning transfer methods, organizing 53 papers according to where knowledge originates (expert demonstrations or learned knowledge), whether sources are single or multiple, and what type of knowledge is transferred (demonstrations, policies, parameters, reward shaping, etc.). The categorization scheme provides a structured framework for understanding the landscape of inter-domain transfer approaches, highlighting the evolution from supervised methods requiring explicit mapping to more autonomous techniques. The review synthesizes key challenges including negative transfer prevention, formalization of similarity assumptions, and practical deployment constraints.

## Key Results
- Cross-domain transfer methods are categorized by knowledge source (expert vs learned), source type (single vs multiple), and transfer approach (demonstrations, policies, parameters, reward shaping)
- Recent methods show clear trend toward autonomous approaches requiring less human supervision for inter-domain mapping
- Major challenges identified include avoiding negative transfer, formalizing similarity assumptions, and practical applicability in data-scarce real-world scenarios

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The effectiveness of cross-domain transfer methods stems from leveraging shared structure between related tasks while accommodating differences in state/action representations. By extracting relevant knowledge from source domains and adapting it to target environments through various mechanisms (policy transfer, reward shaping, parameter initialization), these methods reduce sample complexity and accelerate learning in new domains. The shift toward autonomous approaches addresses the practical limitation of requiring manual inter-domain mappings, making transfer more scalable and applicable to diverse real-world scenarios.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Expert Demonstrations -> Knowledge Extraction -> Transfer Mechanism -> Target Domain Adaptation -> Performance Evaluation

**Critical Path**: Knowledge Source Identification → Transfer Approach Selection → Mapping Strategy Implementation → Target Domain Fine-tuning → Evaluation

**Design Tradeoffs**: Supervised approaches offer more control but require human expertise vs autonomous approaches scale better but may be less precise; single-source methods are simpler but multiple sources provide richer knowledge; demonstration-based transfer is interpretable but limited by available demonstrations vs policy-based transfer is more flexible but requires learned policies

**Failure Signatures**: Negative transfer (performance worse than learning from scratch), mapping errors (inability to align source and target representations), reward mis-specification (incorrect reward shaping leading to suboptimal behavior), and computational overhead (transfer preparation costs exceeding learning benefits)

**First Experiments**: 1) Simple navigation task with different robot morphologies to test policy transfer, 2) Object manipulation task with varying gripper designs to evaluate demonstration transfer, 3) Multi-task environment with shared features to assess autonomous transfer methods

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
- How to formalize and measure domain similarity assumptions that underlie transfer approaches
- Methods for automatically detecting when transfer is likely to be beneficial vs harmful
- Techniques for handling large differences in state/action space dimensionality between domains
- Approaches for transfer in continuous domains where exact mappings are difficult to establish
- Integration of transfer methods with meta-learning frameworks for improved generalization

## Limitations
- Categorization framework may not capture all nuances of hybrid transfer approaches
- Assessment of method autonomy relies on author claims rather than empirical verification
- Practical applicability evaluation based on reported results rather than independent implementation
- Difficulty comparing methods due to heterogeneous experimental setups and lack of standardized benchmarks
- Potential bias toward published methods, missing unpublished or industry-specific approaches
