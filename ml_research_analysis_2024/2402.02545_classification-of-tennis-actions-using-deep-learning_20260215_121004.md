# Classification of Tennis Actions Using Deep Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2402.02545
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02545
- Reference count: 3
- Primary result: SlowFast model achieves 74% accuracy on tennis action classification

## Executive Summary
This paper explores the use of deep learning for classifying tennis actions in videos using the SlowFast architecture. The authors train three SlowFast models of varying sizes on the THETIS dataset, which contains videos of tennis players performing different shots without a ball. The best model achieves a generalization accuracy of 74%, demonstrating good performance for tennis action classification. Error analysis reveals challenges in distinguishing between similar shots due to limited visual cues. The authors conclude that high-quality datasets with real tennis match videos and additional annotations are needed to further advance tennis video analysis research.

## Method Summary
The authors train three SlowFast models (2x32, 4x16, 8x16) on the THETIS dataset containing 1980 videos of 12 tennis shot types. The models use dual temporal pathways: a slow pathway sampling frames every 16 timesteps for spatial semantics, and a fast pathway sampling 8x more frames for motion cues. The models are evaluated on their ability to classify tennis actions, with performance measured by generalization accuracy. Data augmentation, dropout, early stopping, and weight decay are employed to prevent overfitting on the small dataset.

## Key Results
- Best SlowFast model (4x16) achieves 74% generalization accuracy on THETIS dataset
- Model struggles to distinguish between similar shots like different types of serves
- RGB-only inputs are viable for tennis action recognition when ball is absent
- Small dataset size (1980 videos) limits model performance and generalization

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: SlowFast architecture improves tennis action classification by using dual temporal pathways—one slow with large temporal stride for spatial semantics, one fast with high frame rate for motion cues.
- Mechanism: The slow pathway samples frames every 16 timesteps to capture spatial identity, while the fast pathway samples 8x more frames to capture fine-grained temporal dynamics. Lateral connections fuse the two pathways, allowing spatial semantics to be enriched by motion cues.
- Core assumption: Spatial semantics remain constant over short temporal windows, so fewer frames suffice for spatial modeling, while motion requires higher sampling.
- Evidence anchors:

  - [section] "Slow pathway... large temporal stride... Fast pathway... high frame rate"

- Break condition: If spatial semantics vary quickly (e.g., rapid racket reorientation) the slow pathway under-samples, missing key discriminative cues.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Tennis actions in THETIS are distinguishable primarily through motion cues rather than object/ball presence, making RGB-only inputs viable.
- Mechanism: The dataset contains simulated strokes without a ball; motion trajectories and player pose are the main discriminative features. Deep CNNs can extract these from RGB frames.
- Core assumption: Without a ball, player movement and racquet trajectory patterns are sufficient to differentiate shot types.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "The authors trained three SlowFast models... best model achieved a generalization accuracy of 74%"
  - [section] "They perform the tennis shots using a racquet, but no ball... limited to the movements of the player"
  - [corpus] Weak. Corpus neighbors focus on ball tracking or spin; none address ball-absent action classification.
- Break condition: If motion patterns are too similar across shot types (e.g., serve vs. smash), classification accuracy degrades.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Data augmentation and dropout effectively mitigate overfitting given the small THETIS dataset (1980 videos).
- Mechanism: Augmentation increases effective sample size; dropout reduces co-adaptation of features; early stopping halts training when validation error rises.
- Core assumption: Synthetic variability from augmentation approximates real-world variability.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "three SlowFast models... trained and evaluated on the academic tennis dataset THETIS"
  - [section] "For the data set, dropout, data augmentation, early stopping, and weight decay were used to avoid over fitting."

- Break condition: If augmentation does not cover real-world pose or lighting variance, model fails on real match footage.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  Raw video -> resize to 256px shortest side -> center crop to 224×224 -> clip to 64 frames -> SlowFast model -> softmax probabilities -> argmax for class label

- Critical path:
  Raw video → preprocessing → SlowFast model → softmax probabilities → argmax for class label

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Sampling stride τ=16 in slow path reduces computation but may miss rapid pose changes.
  - Fast path high frame rate captures motion but adds memory overhead; channel reduction β=1/8 limits expressivity.
  - Single clip sampling for validation vs. multi-crop inference for testing trades speed for accuracy.

- Failure signatures:
  - Slow path underfits: validation accuracy plateaus early, training accuracy high.
  - Fast path underfits: training loss decreases slowly, high bias.
  - Lateral fusion ineffective: similar performance to single-stream baseline.
  - Class confusion (e.g., serve types): indicates missing discriminative cues in RGB-only input.

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Ablate fast pathway: train SlowFast with only slow stream, compare accuracy to full model.
  2. Vary temporal stride τ: try τ=8 and τ=32 to assess sensitivity to temporal sampling.
  3. Multi-crop inference: implement 3×2 crop evaluation, measure accuracy gain over single-clip inference.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: What is the optimal SlowFast architecture for tennis action classification?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper compares three SlowFast models of varying sizes (2x32, 4x16, 8x16) and finds that the 4x16 model performs best on the THETIS dataset. However, it is unclear if this is the optimal architecture or if further tuning could yield better results.
- Why unresolved: The paper only tests a limited set of SlowFast architectures. There could be other configurations that perform even better.
- What evidence would resolve it: Training and evaluating a wider range of SlowFast architectures on the THETIS dataset, potentially using techniques like neural architecture search, to find the best-performing model.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can the THETIS dataset be improved to better capture the nuances of tennis actions?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper identifies several limitations of the THETIS dataset, including the lack of ball trajectory, player position on the court, and realistic backgrounds. These factors make it difficult to distinguish between similar shots.
- Why unresolved: Improving the dataset would require collecting new video data with additional annotations, which is a significant undertaking.
- What evidence would resolve it: Creating an enhanced version of the THETIS dataset with ball tracking, court position annotations, and more realistic backgrounds, then evaluating the impact on model performance.

### Open Question 3
- Question: What other techniques could be used to improve tennis action classification beyond SlowFast?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] While the paper focuses on SlowFast, it mentions other approaches like CNN-RNN networks and two-stream models that have been used for action recognition. It's unclear if these or other techniques could outperform SlowFast for tennis.
- Why unresolved: The paper only explores one family of deep learning architectures. Other methods could potentially capture tennis-specific features better.
- What evidence would resolve it: Training and comparing a variety of deep learning architectures, including but not limited to CNN-RNN, two-stream models, and attention-based networks, on the THETIS dataset to determine the best approach.

## Limitations
- Dataset Realism: THETIS lacks real match footage and ball presence, limiting generalization to actual broadcast tennis videos.
- Architecture Reproducibility: Key implementation details of SlowFast models are not fully specified, hindering exact reproduction.
- Performance Ceiling: 74% accuracy suggests substantial room for improvement, especially in distinguishing visually similar shots.

## Confidence
- High: The use of SlowFast architecture for tennis action classification and the reported 74% accuracy on THETIS.
- Medium: The effectiveness of RGB-only inputs for tennis action recognition in the absence of a ball.
- Low: The generalizability of findings to real-world tennis match videos due to dataset limitations.
