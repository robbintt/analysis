# AI-Driven Approaches for Optimizing Power Consumption: A Comprehensive Survey

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.15732
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15732
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Survey of 17 studies applying AI techniques to power optimization across solar PV, thermal management, smart grids, and industrial automation, showing significant energy savings and performance improvements

## Executive Summary
This comprehensive survey examines 17 studies that apply artificial intelligence techniques to power optimization across diverse domains including solar photovoltaics, thermal energy management, smart grids, and industrial automation. The review identifies key AI methods such as fuzzy logic control, reinforcement learning, genetic algorithms, swarm intelligence, machine learning, neural networks, and predictive analytics. The findings demonstrate substantial performance improvements, with deep learning achieving 98.71% accuracy in PV fault diagnosis and fuzzy controllers reducing energy consumption by 25%. Reinforcement learning approaches have shown up to 80% energy savings in 5G base stations, while genetic algorithm-based models achieved 3.35% MAPE in energy forecasting.

## Method Summary
The survey systematically reviews and synthesizes findings from 17 studies applying AI techniques to power optimization across multiple domains. The methodology involves identifying and categorizing AI approaches used in each study, extracting performance metrics and outcomes, and analyzing patterns across different application areas. The review covers diverse AI techniques including fuzzy logic control, reinforcement learning, genetic algorithms, swarm intelligence, machine learning, and neural networks, with particular focus on their effectiveness in solar PV systems, thermal management, smart grids, and industrial automation applications.

## Key Results
- Deep learning achieved 98.71% accuracy in photovoltaic fault diagnosis
- Fuzzy controllers reduced energy consumption by 25% in thermal management systems
- Reinforcement learning saved up to 80% energy in 5G base stations
- Genetic algorithm-based models achieved 3.35% MAPE in energy forecasting

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The effectiveness of AI-driven approaches in power optimization stems from their ability to process complex, multidimensional data and identify optimal control strategies that would be difficult to derive through traditional methods. These techniques can learn patterns from historical data, adapt to changing conditions in real-time, and handle the non-linear relationships inherent in power systems. Machine learning models excel at predictive analytics for energy demand forecasting, while reinforcement learning algorithms can optimize control policies through continuous interaction with the environment. The adaptability and learning capabilities of these AI systems allow them to continuously improve performance as more data becomes available.

### Critical Path
The critical path involves data collection from sensors and monitoring systems, AI model processing and decision-making, control system implementation, and performance feedback loops. This path requires low-latency communication between components to enable real-time optimization and must maintain data quality throughout the cycle.

### Design Tradeoffs
Key tradeoffs include model complexity versus computational efficiency, accuracy versus real-time responsiveness, and implementation cost versus energy savings potential. More sophisticated models may achieve better optimization but require greater computational resources and may introduce latency that impacts system performance.

### Failure Signatures
Common failure modes include model drift due to changing operating conditions, data quality issues affecting AI predictions, computational bottlenecks limiting real-time optimization, and integration challenges with existing infrastructure. Systems may also experience performance degradation when operating outside trained parameter ranges.

## Limitations
- Review covers only 17 studies, potentially missing significant research in the field
- Performance metrics vary across studies, making direct comparisons challenging
- Computational overhead and long-term operational costs of AI models are not addressed
- Environmental impact of AI systems themselves (energy consumption during training) is not discussed
