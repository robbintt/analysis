# AutoSplat: Constrained Gaussian Splatting for Autonomous Driving Scene Reconstruction

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2407.02598
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.02598
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Introduces AutoSplat method that adapts 3D Gaussian Splatting to reconstruct autonomous driving scenes with dynamic foreground objects, achieving higher PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS scores on Pandaset and KITTI datasets

## Executive Summary
AutoSplat addresses the challenge of reconstructing autonomous driving scenes with dynamic foreground objects using 3D Gaussian Splatting. The method decomposes scenes into background and foreground components, applying geometric constraints to ensure multi-view consistency. Background regions (road, sky, and others) are separated and constrained geometrically, while foreground objects are initialized from 3D templates and optimized using reflected Gaussian consistency constraints to supervise unseen sides. The approach also employs residual spherical harmonics to model dynamic appearance variations.

The method demonstrates superior performance compared to state-of-the-art approaches in scene reconstruction and novel view synthesis on established autonomous driving datasets. By enabling realistic simulation of scenarios such as ego-vehicle lane changes and object trajectory modifications, AutoSplat provides a practical solution for autonomous driving scene reconstruction that can be used for training and validation purposes.

## Method Summary
AutoSplat adapts 3D Gaussian Splatting for autonomous driving scene reconstruction by addressing the specific challenges of sparse views and moving objects. The method employs a dual decomposition strategy: background decomposition into road, sky, and other regions with geometric constraints, and foreground decomposition using 3D templates initialized from prior knowledge. For background consistency, geometric constraints ensure multi-view alignment, while foreground objects utilize reflected Gaussian consistency to supervise unseen surfaces. Residual spherical harmonics capture dynamic appearance variations across frames. The approach integrates these components through an optimization framework that jointly refines Gaussian primitives while maintaining scene coherence.

## Key Results
- Outperforms state-of-the-art methods in scene reconstruction and novel view synthesis on Pandaset and KITTI datasets
- Achieves higher PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS scores compared to existing approaches
- Enables realistic simulation of autonomous driving scenarios including ego-vehicle lane changes and object trajectory modifications

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by decomposing complex autonomous driving scenes into manageable components with appropriate constraints. Background regions benefit from geometric constraints that leverage the regularity of road and sky structures, ensuring consistent reconstruction across views. Foreground objects, which are more challenging due to their dynamic nature and sparse observations, are initialized from 3D templates that provide prior shape information, then refined using reflected Gaussian consistency that supervises unseen surfaces through symmetry assumptions. The residual spherical harmonics component captures dynamic appearance changes that cannot be explained by geometry alone, allowing the system to handle varying lighting conditions and material properties. This hierarchical approach with component-specific constraints enables the system to handle the diverse challenges present in autonomous driving scenes while maintaining computational efficiency through Gaussian primitives.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Camera inputs -> Background Decomposition -> Geometric Constraints -> Gaussian Initialization -> Foreground Decomposition -> Template Initialization -> Reflected Consistency -> Residual Spherical Harmonics -> Joint Optimization -> Rendered Output

Critical Path: The core optimization loop that jointly refines Gaussian primitives while maintaining background consistency through geometric constraints and foreground consistency through reflected Gaussian consistency and spherical harmonics appearance modeling.

Design Tradeoffs: The method trades computational complexity for reconstruction accuracy by using a hierarchical decomposition approach. Background processing benefits from simpler geometric constraints but requires accurate road/sky detection. Foreground processing uses computationally expensive template-based initialization but achieves better results for dynamic objects. The spherical harmonics component adds computational overhead but significantly improves appearance modeling.

Failure Signatures: The method may struggle with irregular urban environments where road/sky separation is ambiguous, scenes with heavy occlusions that violate symmetry assumptions for reflected consistency, and objects that significantly deviate from available templates. Performance degradation may also occur in adverse weather conditions that affect both geometric and appearance modeling.
