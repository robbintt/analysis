# Network Interdiction Goes Neural
## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2405.16409
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.16409
- Authors: Lei Zhang; Zhiqian Chen; Chang-Tien Lu; Liang Zhao
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Novel MMILP-GNN outperforms SCIP in network interdiction with approximation ratios of 1.22 ± 0.06 for MFI20 and 0.82 ± 0.14 for SPI20

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the computational challenge of network interdiction problems through a novel neural approach. Network interdiction involves two players - a follower optimizing on a network and a leader modifying the network to hinder the follower - typically formulated as bi-level optimization problems. The authors propose MMILP-GNN, a multipartite graph neural network that learns to solve network interdiction problems represented as Mixed-Integer Linear Programming instances. The approach demonstrates superior performance compared to traditional exact solvers like SCIP, particularly when combined with a predict-and-search strategy that leverages neural predictions to accelerate the search process.

## Method Summary
The proposed approach reformulates network interdiction problems as MILP instances and uses MMILP-GNN to learn the mapping between graph representations and solution distributions. The multipartite graph neural network architecture is specifically designed to handle the complex structure of MILP problems, capturing relationships between variables, constraints, and the underlying network topology. The method employs an end-to-end prediction approach as well as a hybrid predict-and-search strategy that combines neural predictions with SCIP's search capabilities. This hybrid approach demonstrates significant computational advantages, particularly after 25-35 seconds of computation time, while maintaining competitive solution quality.

## Key Results
- Neural Interdiction consistently outperforms comparison models with approximation ratios of 1.22 ± 0.06 for MFI20 and 0.82 ± 0.14 for SPI20
- The predict-and-search strategy achieves faster solution times compared to SCIP alone, particularly after 25-35 seconds
- MMILP-GNN demonstrates superior solution quality and computational efficiency compared to traditional exact solvers
- The approach successfully handles both shortest path and maximum flow interdiction problem variants

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The effectiveness stems from MMILP-GNN's ability to capture the inherent structure of network interdiction problems through graph representation learning. By modeling the problem as a multipartite graph, the neural network can effectively learn the complex relationships between network topology, interdiction decisions, and optimal solutions. The predict-and-search strategy leverages the neural network's ability to provide high-quality initial solutions that guide the subsequent search process, reducing the computational burden of exhaustive exploration.
