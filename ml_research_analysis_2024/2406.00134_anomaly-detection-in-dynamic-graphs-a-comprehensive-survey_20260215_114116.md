# Anomaly Detection in Dynamic Graphs: A Comprehensive Survey

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.00134
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.00134
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: Comprehensive categorization of 53 recent anomaly detection methods in dynamic graphs across four methodological families

## Executive Summary
This survey provides a systematic overview of anomaly detection methods for dynamic graphs, organizing approaches into four major categories: traditional machine learning, matrix factorization, probabilistic, and deep learning techniques. The authors define formal mathematical frameworks for node, edge, and subgraph anomalies in both static and dynamic contexts, and review recent developments from 2016-2023. The survey identifies critical challenges including scalability, modeling temporal dynamics, multi-view and multi-task detection, graph theory foundations, adversarial attacks, fairness, and cost sensitivity.

## Method Summary
The survey categorizes dynamic graph anomaly detection (DGAD) methods into four major groups based on their core algorithms: traditional machine learning (tree-based, density-based, distance-based), matrix factorization/tensor decomposition, probabilistic approaches, and deep learning. The authors provide formal definitions for anomalies in both static and dynamic graphs, with discrete and continuous temporal representations. The survey reviews 53 recent papers, summarizes evaluation datasets and metrics (ROC-AUC, Precision, Recall, F1-score), and identifies key research challenges and future directions for the field.

## Key Results
- 53 recent papers (2016-2023) systematically categorized into four methodological families
- Formal mathematical definitions established for node, edge, and subgraph anomalies in dynamic graphs
- Identification of five key advantages of GNNs for dynamic graph anomaly detection: topological adaptation, multimodal integration, scalability, interpretability, and self-attention mechanisms
- Timeline analysis showing deep learning approaches dominating recent research (21/53 papers since 2020)

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: What specific graph theoretical foundations are needed to verify the reliability of dynamic graph anomaly detection algorithms, and how can these foundations improve model interpretability?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper states "Most existing anomaly detection algorithms for dynamic graphs are designed and evaluated through empirical experiments, lacking sufficient theoretical foundations to verify their reliability."
- Why unresolved: Current anomaly detection methods focus on empirical performance metrics without establishing rigorous mathematical guarantees about their effectiveness across different graph structures and evolving patterns.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development of theoretical bounds on detection accuracy, proofs of convergence for different graph structures, and mathematical characterizations of how graph properties affect anomaly detection performance.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can cost-sensitive anomaly detection be effectively implemented in dynamic graphs where the costs of false positives and false negatives vary significantly across different applications and contexts?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper discusses "Cost sensitivity in anomaly detection involves accounting for varying costs associated with misclassifying anomalies or normal instances."
- Why unresolved: Most current approaches treat all detection errors equally, without considering the real-world economic implications of different types of misclassifications in specific application domains.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development of frameworks that incorporate domain-specific cost matrices into the detection algorithms, along with empirical validation showing improved decision-making in high-stakes applications.

### Open Question 3
- Question: What are the most effective approaches for ensuring fairness in dynamic graph anomaly detection, particularly when historical data contains biases that could perpetuate discrimination against certain groups?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper highlights that "Addressing fairness in anomaly detection is crucial for ensuring equitable and effective models, especially in real-world applications."
- Why unresolved: While some static graph approaches have begun addressing fairness, dynamic graphs introduce additional complexity through evolving patterns and temporal dependencies that could amplify or mitigate biases over time.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development of fairness-aware algorithms that account for both structural and temporal aspects of dynamic graphs, validated through controlled experiments showing reduced bias across different demographic groups.
