# myAURA: Personalized health library for epilepsy management via knowledge graph sparsification and visualization

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2405.05229
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.05229
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: myAURA integrates heterogeneous health data into a multi-layer knowledge graph with metric backbone sparsification to enable personalized epilepsy self-management through visualization and recommendations

## Executive Summary
myAURA is a patient-centered application that addresses epilepsy self-management through the integration of diverse data sources into a unified knowledge graph. The system federates data from social media, electronic health records, biomedical databases, and patient communities, linking them via a human-centered biomedical dictionary. A novel network sparsification method using the metric backbone identifies the most important associations for inference and visualization, enabling personalized recommendations and drug interaction analysis. Focus group studies with patients and caregivers informed the user-centered design of the myAURA interface, which features tracking tools, medication reminders, and an emergency alert system.

## Method Summary
The myAURA system federates heterogeneous data resources (social media, EHR, biomedical databases, scientific literature) into a multi-layer knowledge graph where nodes are dictionary terms and edges represent co-occurrence proximities. The system employs metric backbone sparsification to remove redundant edges while preserving shortest paths, enabling efficient visualization and inference. The cartographic visualization interface allows patients to explore epilepsy-related information including drug-drug interactions, side effects, and relevant research. The approach is generalizable to other chronic conditions and represents a significant advancement in data-powered patient self-management through focus group-informed user-centered design.

## Key Results
- Federated multi-layer knowledge graph integrates data from social media, EHR, biomedical databases, and patient communities
- Metric backbone sparsification removes 85-95% of edges while preserving essential shortest-path relationships and community structure
- Focus group studies with patients and caregivers informed user-centered interface design featuring tracking tools, medication reminders, and emergency alerts

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Multi-layer knowledge graph construction enables integration of heterogeneous data sources for personalized epilepsy self-management.
- Mechanism: The system federates data from social media, EHR, biomedical databases, and patient communities into a multi-layer graph where nodes are dictionary terms and edges represent co-occurrence proximities. This unified representation allows querying across disparate sources through a single interface.
- Core assumption: Terms from different sources can be meaningfully linked via a shared biomedical dictionary without losing context-specific meaning.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "federation of an unprecedented collection of heterogeneous data resources... multi-layer knowledge graph linking all this heterogeneous data via the terms of a human-centered biomedical dictionary"
  - [section 2.3] "Given that the textual items of the federated data resources were tagged with dictionary terms, it is straightforward to build weighted graphs"
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - corpus neighbors mention graph transformers and knowledge graphs but not the specific multi-layer construction approach
- Break condition: Dictionary terms fail to capture domain-specific meanings across different data sources, leading to meaningless connections in the graph.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Metric backbone sparsification preserves essential shortest-path relationships while removing redundant edges for efficient visualization and inference.
- Mechanism: The system computes shortest paths on distance graphs derived from proximity graphs, then identifies the metric backbone subgraph that preserves all shortest paths. This removes 85-95% of edges while maintaining connectivity and community structure.
- Core assumption: Semi-metric edges (those that change under metric closure) are less important for inference than metric edges.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "employ a novel network sparsification methodology using the metric backbone of weighted graphs, which reveals the most important edges for inference, recommendation, and visualization"
  - [section 3.2] "Computing shortest paths of a distance graph... yields its metric closure DC(X)... There is a metric backbone subgraph Db(X)... invariant under the metric closure"
  - [section 3.3] "All layers of myAURA's KG have a small backbone (large amount of redundancy) as can be seen in Table 1"
- Break condition: The assumption that shortest paths capture all relevant information fails, meaning important edges are incorrectly removed during sparsification.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: User-centered design through focus groups produces an interface that meets actual patient needs for epilepsy self-management.
- Mechanism: Iterative focus group sessions with patients, caregivers, and stakeholders inform requirements gathering, mockup design, and interface refinement, ensuring the final product addresses real-world challenges in tracking, information sharing, and decision support.
- Core assumption: Patients and caregivers can articulate their needs effectively and provide actionable feedback on interface prototypes.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "we present our patient-centered design and pilot-testing of myAURA, including its user interface, based on focus groups and other stakeholder input"
  - [section 3.5] "Using these initial findings, we produced an initial interactive mockup prototype... Subsequently tested it with a second focus group"
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - corpus neighbors don't address the specific focus group methodology or patient-centered design process
- Break condition: Focus group participants cannot represent the broader patient population, leading to design decisions that don't generalize.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  - Data Ingestion Layer -> Dictionary Construction Layer -> Knowledge Graph Layer -> Metric Backbone Layer -> Visualization Layer -> User Interface Layer -> Recommendation Engine

- Critical path:
  1. Data ingestion and preprocessing
  2. Dictionary construction and refinement
  3. Knowledge graph construction
  4. Metric backbone extraction
  5. Visualization and interface development
  6. Focus group testing and iteration

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Precision vs. recall in dictionary term matching (medical accuracy vs. coverage)
  - Graph density vs. interpretability (complete vs. sparsified representations)
  - Patient privacy vs. data utility (anonymization vs. personalization capabilities)
  - Technical complexity vs. user accessibility (powerful features vs. ease of use)

- Failure signatures:
  - Dictionary terms missing or incorrectly mapped → irrelevant connections in KG
  - Incorrect distance calculations → wrong edges removed in metric backbone
  - Focus group bias → interface features that don't generalize to broader population
  - Data source access issues → incomplete KG coverage

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Build a small-scale knowledge graph from synthetic data to verify Jaccard similarity and metric backbone calculations
  2. Implement the visualization tool with a simplified KG to test map-based exploration
  3. Conduct a mini focus group with 3-4 participants to test the mockup interface and gather initial feedback

## Limitations

- The system's reliance on dictionary-based term matching across heterogeneous data sources may introduce semantic inconsistencies and lose context-specific meaning
- The metric backbone sparsification assumes shortest paths capture all relevant information, which may not hold for complex medical relationships
- Focus group methodology lacks detail on participant selection criteria and representativeness of broader patient population
- Clinical validation of the system's recommendations and impact on patient outcomes is not provided beyond design feedback

## Confidence

- **High confidence**: The technical methodology for knowledge graph construction and metric backbone sparsification is well-established and mathematically rigorous
- **Medium confidence**: The multi-layer graph construction approach and visualization methodology are reasonable but lack empirical validation of their effectiveness for epilepsy management
- **Low confidence**: Claims about the system's clinical utility and patient benefit are not supported by outcome measures beyond design feedback
