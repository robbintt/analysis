# Deep learning phase recovery: data-driven, physics-driven, or combining both?

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2404.01360
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.01360
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Proposes co-driven (CD) strategy combining data-driven and physics-driven approaches to balance high- and low-frequency information in phase recovery

## Executive Summary
This paper comprehensively compares two main deep learning strategies for phase recovery: data-driven (DD) with supervised learning and physics-driven (PD) with self-supervised learning. The authors analyze their performance in terms of time consumption, accuracy, generalization ability, ill-posedness adaptability, and prior capacity. They find that PD achieves higher accuracy with multiple inference cycles but favors high-frequency details, while DD performs single fast inference but emphasizes low-frequency background information. To address this limitation, they propose a co-driven (CD) strategy combining both approaches to balance high- and low-frequency information. Experimental tests on standard phase objects show that CD performs better than either DD or PD alone in balancing detailed and background information.

## Method Summary
The authors compare data-driven (DD) and physics-driven (PD) deep learning approaches for phase recovery, analyzing their performance characteristics including accuracy, speed, generalization, and ability to handle ill-posed problems. DD uses supervised learning with paired intensity and phase data for single fast inference, while PD employs self-supervised learning with iterative inference cycles that leverage physical constraints. The proposed co-driven (CD) strategy combines both approaches by leveraging DD's low-frequency background information and PD's high-frequency detail reconstruction, addressing the inherent trade-offs between the two methods through a carefully designed fusion framework.

## Key Results
- PD achieves higher accuracy than DD but requires multiple inference cycles
- DD performs single fast inference but emphasizes low-frequency background information
- CD strategy outperforms both DD and PD alone by balancing high- and low-frequency information

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The co-driven approach works by strategically combining the complementary strengths of data-driven and physics-driven methods. Data-driven learning excels at capturing low-frequency background information through supervised training on paired data, while physics-driven learning leverages iterative physical constraints to recover high-frequency details. By fusing these complementary frequency components, the CD strategy achieves better overall reconstruction quality than either approach alone. The physics-driven component provides superior accuracy through iterative refinement and physical constraint enforcement, while the data-driven component ensures computational efficiency and low-frequency consistency.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Raw intensity measurements -> DD network (low-frequency background) + PD network (high-frequency details) -> Fusion module -> Final phase reconstruction

**Critical Path**: Intensity input → Dual-branch processing (DD + PD) → Frequency-weighted fusion → Output phase reconstruction

**Design Tradeoffs**: The CD strategy balances the speed of DD (single inference) against the accuracy of PD (multiple cycles). The fusion mechanism must carefully weight the complementary frequency components while maintaining computational efficiency. Choosing the right fusion strategy and hyperparameters is critical for optimal performance.

**Failure Signatures**: 
- Poor fusion weighting leads to either DD-dominated (blurry high-frequency details) or PD-dominated (noisy low-frequency background) reconstructions
- Insufficient PD iterations result in incomplete high-frequency detail recovery
- Over-reliance on DD causes loss of fine structural details
