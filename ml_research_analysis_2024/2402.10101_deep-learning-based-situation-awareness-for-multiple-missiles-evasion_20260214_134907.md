# Deep Learning Based Situation Awareness for Multiple Missiles Evasion

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2402.10101
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.10101
- Authors: Edvards Scukins; Markus Klein; Lars Kroon; Petter Ögren
- Reference count: 18
- Pre-trained FNNs estimate miss distances for evasive maneuvers against multiple incoming missiles in BVR air combat

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a deep learning-based decision support tool for UAV operators facing multiple incoming missiles in Beyond Visual Range (BVR) air combat scenarios. The system uses pre-trained feed-forward neural networks to estimate miss distances for various predefined evasive maneuvers against each detected missile threat. By selecting the maneuver that maximizes the minimum miss distance across all threats, the system provides real-time situation awareness and recommends optimal evasive actions. The approach is evaluated in scenarios with three, four, and six incoming missiles, demonstrating its potential to enhance operator decision-making under high-stress combat conditions.

## Method Summary
The method involves training separate feed-forward neural networks (FNNs) for each predefined evasive maneuver using simulated data from single-missile encounters. For each missile threat, the system queries all FNNs to estimate miss distances, then selects the maneuver with the maximum minimum miss distance as the safest option. The system also incorporates uncertainty quantification through sensor error sampling and presents results via a circular visualization diagram showing risk levels in different directions relative to the aircraft's heading.

## Key Results
- Successfully demonstrated real-time situation awareness for multiple missile threats in three test scenarios
- Achieved effective risk assessment by selecting maneuvers based on maximum minimum miss distance
- Validated the approach in scenarios with 3, 4, and 6 incoming missiles using F16 aircraft models

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

- Mechanism: For each evasive policy πi, a separate FNN model fπi_FNN is trained offline using simulated data from single-missile encounters. During operation, the system queries all fπi_FNN models for each detected missile, takes the minimum miss distance per maneuver, and presents the results to the operator.
- Core assumption: The minimum miss distance across all missiles represents the effective risk for that maneuver, and this relationship holds regardless of missile coordination or formation behavior.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: State estimation -> FNN inference for each missile -> minimum distance aggregation per maneuver -> visualization update -> operator interface
- Critical path: State estimation -> FNN inference for each missile -> minimum distance aggregation per maneuver -> visualization update -> operator decision
- Design tradeoffs: Pre-training vs. online learning (pre-training enables real-time operation but may not adapt to novel missile types), individual FNNs vs. single multi-output network (simpler training vs. potential correlation learning)
- Failure signatures: System consistently predicts zero miss distance (indicates overconfidence), visualization shows uniform colors (indicates poor discrimination), inference latency exceeds real-time requirements
- First 3 experiments:
  1. Train and validate single FNN on synthetic data with known miss distance relationship to verify learning capability
  2. Implement circular visualization component and verify color mapping works correctly with dummy data
  3. Integrate uncertainty sampling module and verify confidence intervals are computed and displayed correctly

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How robust is the system to highly maneuverable adversary aircraft that can significantly alter their flight paths after missile launch?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper assumes estimates of missile type and guidance algorithms, and the evaluation scenarios use F16 aircraft. The system's performance with more agile adversaries is not explicitly tested.
- Why unresolved: The paper only evaluates scenarios with F16 aircraft. No analysis is provided for how the system would perform against more agile or unpredictable adversary aircraft that could execute evasive maneuvers themselves.
- What evidence would resolve it: Testing the system against high-fidelity simulations of more maneuverable aircraft types (e.g., Su-35, F-22) and comparing miss distance predictions and actual outcomes.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What is the impact of using pre-defined evasive maneuvers versus learning optimal evasive maneuvers in real-time?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper states "we let the set of evasive maneuvers be described by {πN , πNE, πE, πSE, πS, πSW, πW, πNW} = Π" and notes "the method can be used with any family of policies Π, deemed relevant by the user." The choice of using pre-defined maneuvers is presented as a design decision without comparison to alternative approaches.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not compare the performance of pre-defined maneuvers against approaches that could learn optimal maneuvers in real-time based on the specific threat configuration.
- What evidence would resolve it: A comparative analysis showing miss distances achieved using pre-defined maneuvers versus those learned in real-time for various threat scenarios.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How does the system perform when adversaries employ electronic warfare or deception tactics to obscure launch locations?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions "Providing decision support for more desperate measures, such as firing all remaining weapons and then losing the UA V, or relying on methods not captured by the model, such as electronic warfare or chaff/flare systems." This suggests the model doesn't account for such tactics, but doesn't test scenarios where adversaries actively use them.
- Why unresolved: The paper evaluates scenarios with perfect knowledge of launch locations and assumes estimates with bounded uncertainty. It doesn't test scenarios where adversaries actively use electronic warfare to create false launch signatures or obscure true launch locations.
- What evidence would resolve it: Testing the system against scenarios where adversaries use electronic warfare to create false launch signatures or where true launch locations are deliberately obscured, measuring how this affects miss distance predictions and recommended maneuvers.

## Limitations

- The approach assumes missiles use pure proportional navigation with increased altitude, but doesn't account for more sophisticated missile guidance systems or coordinated attacks
- The system's performance depends heavily on the quality of missile launch state estimates, which may be unreliable in real-world scenarios
- The visualization approach has not been validated with actual operators under stress conditions

## Confidence

- Mechanism 1 (min-distance aggregation): Medium - The approach is logically sound but relies on assumptions about missile independence that may not hold in coordinated attacks
- Mechanism 2 (uncertainty quantification): Low - The uncertainty sampling approach is described but not validated with real sensor data
- Mechanism 3 (circular visualization): Low - The visualization design is proposed but user testing is not reported
