## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel game-theoretic framework for analyzing Teacher-Student Curriculum Learning (TSCL) by interpreting units of experience as players in a cooperative game. The authors establish that TSCL dynamics approximate sequential coalition formation, demonstrating that every TSCL problem has an equivalent cooperative game representation. This theoretical foundation provides new insights into the mechanics of curriculum learning and suggests broader applicability across machine learning domains.

The framework is validated through experiments spanning supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and classical games, showing that estimating cooperative values of experiences can construct effective curricula even when traditional TSCL approaches struggle. This work bridges curriculum learning and cooperative game theory, offering a fresh perspective on how to design and analyze learning progression strategies.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by reinterpreting curriculum learning as a cooperative game where experiences are players and learning progression is equivalent to coalition formation. The teacher-student dynamic becomes a mechanism for sequential coalition building, where the value of each experience (player) is determined by its contribution to the overall learning outcome. This game-theoretic perspective allows for principled analysis of curriculum construction and provides theoretical guarantees about learning progression.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Experience units -> Cooperative game players -> Coalition formation -> Value estimation -> Curriculum construction -> Learning progression

Critical path: The key pathway is from experience representation through cooperative value estimation to curriculum construction, with the teacher policy selecting experiences based on their estimated cooperative values. The critical sequence is: (1) define experience units, (2) map to game players, (3) estimate cooperative values, (4) construct curriculum, (5) evaluate learning progression.

Design tradeoffs: The framework trades computational complexity for theoretical guarantees. Estimating cooperative values requires considering all possible coalitions, which can be computationally expensive, but provides principled curriculum construction. The bandit policy must balance exploration of new experience combinations with exploitation of known valuable curricula.

Failure signatures: Curriculum construction may fail if cooperative value estimates are inaccurate, leading to suboptimal learning sequences. The framework may struggle with continuous or high-dimensional experience spaces where coalition formation becomes intractable. Teacher policies that over-exploit may get stuck in local curriculum optima.

## Limitations
- The approximation quality of TSCL dynamics to sequential coalition formation requires further empirical validation across diverse problem domains
- Computational overhead for estimating cooperative values across large experience spaces remains unexplored
- The assumption that all experiences can be meaningfully represented as game players may not hold for complex or continuous learning scenarios
- Experimental results lack detailed statistical significance measures and confidence intervals

## Confidence
- High confidence: The mathematical equivalence between TSCL problems and cooperative games is well-established within the theoretical framework
- Medium confidence: The empirical demonstrations across supervised learning, reinforcement learning, and classical games show promising results but lack detailed statistical validation
- Medium confidence: The claim about broader applicability requires further investigation across diverse problem domains
