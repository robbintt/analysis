# An Intrinsic Approach to Scalar-Curvature Estimation for Point Clouds

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2308.02615
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.02615
- Reference count: 23
- One-line primary result: Method estimates scalar curvature of Riemannian manifolds directly from point clouds using only pairwise geodesic distances

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a novel approach to estimate scalar curvature directly from point clouds sampled from Riemannian manifolds, without requiring an embedding into Euclidean space. The method leverages the relationship between scalar curvature and the growth rate of geodesic ball volumes, using a maximum-likelihood estimator to fit a quadratic function and extract the scalar curvature coefficient. The authors prove stability under metric perturbations and convergence as the number of points increases, demonstrating accurate results on synthetic data from constant and variable-curvature manifolds.

## Method Summary
The method estimates scalar curvature by counting points within geodesic balls of varying radii and adjusting for local density to construct maximum-likelihood estimates of ball volumes. These volume ratios are then fitted to a quadratic function to extract the scalar curvature coefficient. The approach requires only pairwise geodesic distances, making it applicable to point clouds of any dimension and to graphs. Key steps include dimension estimation using the Levina-Bickel method, density estimation via kernel density estimation, and quadratic fitting of volume ratios. The method is designed to be stable with respect to noise and converge to the true scalar curvature as the number of points increases.

## Key Results
- Accurate recovery of scalar curvature values on synthetic data from constant-curvature manifolds (spheres, hyperbolic disk)
- Correct qualitative behavior on variable-curvature manifolds (torus) even with noise
- Applicability to point clouds of any dimension and to graphs, requiring only pairwise distance information
- Proven stability under metric perturbations and convergence as number of points increases

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Scalar curvature can be estimated from point clouds using only pairwise geodesic distances without requiring an embedding into Euclidean space.
- Mechanism: The method exploits the relationship between scalar curvature and the growth rate of geodesic ball volumes. Specifically, it uses the fact that as the radius of a geodesic ball increases, the volume ratio compared to a Euclidean ball reveals the scalar curvature coefficient through a quadratic fit.
- Core assumption: The point cloud is sampled from a compact Riemannian manifold with a probability density function that has full support.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "Our estimator depends only on the metric structure of the data and not on an embedding in Rn."
  - [section 1] "We estimate scalar curvature using only metric data (i.e., pairwise geodesic distances between points that are sampled from on a manifold)."
- Break condition: If the sampling density is not smooth or the manifold is not compact, the estimator may fail to converge or be unstable.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: The maximum-likelihood estimator for geodesic ball volumes provides a stable and consistent estimate of scalar curvature.
- Mechanism: By counting the number of points within geodesic balls of varying radii and adjusting for the local density, the method constructs a maximum-likelihood estimate of the ball volume. Fitting a quadratic function to these volume ratios extracts the scalar curvature coefficient.
- Core assumption: The probability density function ρ is continuous and the manifold is compact, ensuring that local density estimates are meaningful.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 3.1] "Our likelihood function for vol(BM(x, r)) is...because the random variable N(x, r) is a binomial random variable with N − 1 trials and success probability µρ(x, r) · vol(BM(x, r))."
  - [section 3.4] "Lemma 3.4. If X is a finite point cloud that is sampled from the pdf ρ : M → R+, then E[cvol[dX, ρ](x, r)] = vol(BM(x, r)) for all x ∈ X and r > 0."
- Break condition: If the number of points is too small or the radius range is not chosen appropriately, the maximum-likelihood estimate may have high variance.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: The estimator is stable with respect to perturbations in the metric data and converges to the true scalar curvature as the number of points increases.
- Mechanism: Stability is proven by showing that small errors in distance measurements lead to small errors in the curvature estimates. Convergence is established by demonstrating that as the number of points increases, the estimator approaches the true scalar curvature under certain asymptotic conditions.
- Core assumption: The distance errors δk and density estimation errors ηk approach zero as the number of points increases.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 4] "Theorem A (Theorem 4.1). Let M be a compact Riemannian manifold...Then for a suitable sequence of radius sequences and any sequence {xk} of points with xk ∈ Xk, we have | ˆS[dk](xk) − ˆS[d](xk)| → 0 in probability as k → ∞."
  - [section 5] "Theorem B (Theorem 5.3). Under the same hypotheses as the preceding theorem, for a suitable sequence of radius sequences, | ˆS[dk](xk) − S(xk)| → 0 in probability as k → ∞."
- Break condition: If the manifold has high variation in scalar curvature or if the hyperparameter sequences are not chosen correctly, the estimator may not converge or may be unstable.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Pairwise geodesic distances -> Dimension estimation -> Density estimation -> Ball volume estimation -> Quadratic fitting -> Scalar curvature estimates

- Critical path:
  1. Obtain or estimate geodesic distances between all point pairs
  2. Estimate manifold dimension
  3. Estimate pointwise densities using kernel density estimation
  4. For each point and radius, compute the maximum-likelihood ball volume
  5. Fit a quadratic function to the volume ratios to extract the scalar curvature coefficient
  6. Apply stability and convergence theorems to validate results

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Accuracy vs. computational complexity: Higher-dimensional manifolds require more points and computational resources.
  - Radius selection: Choosing appropriate minimum and maximum radii is crucial for accuracy but can be problem-dependent.
  - Density estimation method: Different kernels (Gaussian, biweight) may yield different results depending on the data.

- Failure signatures:
  - High variance in curvature estimates: May indicate insufficient points or poor radius selection.
  - Systematic bias: Could suggest issues with density estimation or geodesic distance approximation.
  - Non-convergence: May result from non-compact manifolds or discontinuous density functions.

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Test on a synthetic data set with known constant scalar curvature (e.g., unit sphere) to verify basic functionality.
  2. Apply the method to a point cloud sampled from a surface with non-constant scalar curvature (e.g., torus) to assess accuracy in varying regions.
  3. Introduce noise to the distance matrix and observe the stability of the estimator to validate the theoretical stability results.
