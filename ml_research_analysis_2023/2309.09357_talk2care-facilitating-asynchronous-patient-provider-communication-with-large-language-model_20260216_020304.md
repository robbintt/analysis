# Talk2Care: Facilitating Asynchronous Patient-Provider Communication with Large-Language-Model

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2309.09357
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.09357
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: Talk2Care is an LLM-powered voice assistant and dashboard system designed to facilitate asynchronous communication between older adults and healthcare providers.

## Executive Summary
Talk2Care is an LLM-powered voice assistant and dashboard system designed to facilitate asynchronous communication between older adults and healthcare providers. The system collects health information from older adults through natural conversation and summarizes it for providers, addressing challenges of limited availability and information loss in traditional methods. User studies with 10 older adults and 9 providers showed that Talk2Care was easy to use, helped older adults provide more health information, offered mental support, and saved providers' time and effort. The system effectively supported communication and information processing, demonstrating its potential for real-world deployment in home-based healthcare.

## Method Summary
The study conducted two interview studies with older adults (N=10) and healthcare providers (N=9) to understand needs and challenges in patient-provider communication. Based on these insights, Talk2Care was developed with a voice assistant interface for older adults and a dashboard for providers. The VA uses LLM-generated questions to collect detailed health information through natural conversation, while the dashboard provides automated summaries, highlights, and risk assessments. The system was evaluated through user studies with both groups to assess usability, effectiveness in enriching health information, and time savings for providers.

## Key Results
- Talk2Care was easy to use for both older adults and providers, with positive feedback on the conversational interface
- The system helped older adults provide more comprehensive health information compared to traditional methods
- Providers reported significant time savings and reduced mental load from automated summarization and risk assessment features

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The LLM-powered VA reduces information loss during patient-provider communication by collecting detailed symptom descriptions through natural conversation.
- Mechanism: Voice-based natural conversation allows older adults to express health concerns in a more conversational and comprehensive manner compared to text-based messaging or phone calls. The VA's iterative questioning ensures critical details are captured.
- Core assumption: Older adults can articulate health concerns more completely when engaged in a conversational format rather than writing brief messages.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "The system collects health information from older adults through natural conversation and summarizes it for providers, addressing challenges of limited availability and information loss in traditional methods."

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: The provider dashboard saves time and effort by summarizing and highlighting key information from patient-VA conversations.
- Mechanism: The dashboard uses LLM to automatically generate clinical notes summaries and highlight important quotes from raw conversation logs, allowing providers to quickly assess patient needs without reading entire transcripts.
- Core assumption: Providers can make effective clinical decisions based on summarized information without reviewing complete conversation transcripts.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "For health providers, we built an LLM-based dashboard to summarize and present important health information based on older adults' conversations with the VA."

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: AI-mediated communication reduces mental load for both older adults and providers during the communication process.
- Mechanism: The VA acts as an intermediary that handles routine questioning and information collection, allowing older adults to feel less judged and providers to avoid emotionally taxing conversations about basic health information.
- Core assumption: The presence of an AI mediator changes the psychological dynamics of patient-provider communication in a way that reduces stress for both parties.
- Evidence anchors: [section] "With LLM-powered VA playing the intermediate communication role, providers could reduce the mental load of talking with patients."

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Patient -> Voice Assistant (Alexa Skill + LLM question generation + loopback confirmation) -> Encrypted Database -> Provider Dashboard (LLM-powered summary generation + highlight extraction + risk prediction) -> Provider

- Critical path: 1. Older adult initiates conversation with VA about health concern 2. VA collects detailed information through multi-turn conversation using LLM-generated questions 3. Information stored in encrypted database 4. Provider receives notification and accesses dashboard 5. Dashboard generates summary, highlights, and risk assessment 6. Provider reviews information and takes appropriate action

- Design tradeoffs: Voice vs. text interface: Voice provides better accessibility for older adults but may have accuracy issues with speech recognition; Real-time vs. asynchronous: Asynchronous allows flexibility but may delay urgent care; LLM autonomy vs. human oversight: More autonomy saves time but increases risk of errors

- Failure signatures: Poor speech recognition leading to incorrect information capture; LLM generating inappropriate or misleading questions; Summary generation that misses critical health information; System delays causing providers to miss urgent cases

- First 3 experiments: 1. Test VA conversation flow with mock patients to validate question generation quality and information completeness 2. Evaluate dashboard summary accuracy by comparing LLM-generated summaries against human-annotated ground truth 3. Conduct usability testing with providers to assess whether dashboard saves time compared to reviewing raw conversation logs

## Limitations
- Small sample size (10 older adults, 9 providers) limits generalizability of findings
- Study conducted in single geographic region, potentially missing cultural and healthcare system variations
- Limited longitudinal evaluation of system impact on actual clinical outcomes and patient health
