# A Theory of Intelligences

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2308.12411
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.12411
- Reference count: 40

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a novel first-principles Theory of Intelligences (TIS) that provides a unified framework for understanding intelligence across diverse systems. The theory partitions intelligence into two fundamental processes: reducing information uncertainty through path optimization ("solving") and attaining goal accuracy through understanding ("understanding"). By developing compact mathematical expressions for intelligence and its components, the theory introduces an evolutionary intelligence index that quantifies selection potential for intelligence traits, demonstrating how intelligence drives both efficiency gains and exploration of new goal spaces.

## Method Summary
The paper develops a theoretical framework for intelligence by partitioning it into two fundamental processes: reducing information uncertainty and attaining goal accuracy. The authors propose mathematical expressions for intelligence and its components, including a novel formulation of goal difficulty that integrates complexity, environment, and agent ability. The framework is applied across physical, biological, and artificial systems, with examples ranging from crystal formation to human endeavors.

## Key Results
- Introduces a dual-process partition of intelligence into uncertainty reduction ("solving") and goal accuracy ("understanding")
- Develops an evolutionary intelligence index that quantifies selection potential for intelligence traits
- Demonstrates how intelligence drives both efficiency gains and exploration of new goal spaces

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  - Core system: The individual or entity solving the problem (controller, processor, memory)
  - Proxies: Social interactions, technology, culture, institutions
  - Environment: Contexts, settings, structures, and conditions interacting with the system
  - Goals: Objectives to be achieved, with associated difficulty and surprisal

- Critical path:
  1. Identify the goal and its complexity
  2. Assess the system's ability and available proxies
  3. Reduce information uncertainty through path optimization
  4. Ensure goal accuracy through understanding
  5. Evaluate intelligence using the evolutionary index

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Specialization vs. generality: Narrow intelligence excels in specific domains but lacks flexibility; general intelligence is flexible but may be less efficient in specialized tasks.
  - Efficiency vs. exploration: Efficient problem-solving may limit exploration of novel goals; exploration requires tolerance for uncertainty and inefficiency.
  - Individual vs. collective intelligence: Individual intelligence is limited by personal capacity; collective intelligence leverages proxies but introduces coordination challenges.

- Failure signatures:
  - Low uncertainty reduction with high goal accuracy: Indicates reliance on understanding without efficient problem-solving (e.g., educated guessing).
  - High uncertainty reduction with low goal accuracy: Indicates efficient problem-solving but lack of understanding (e.g., process errors or random guessing).
  - Both low uncertainty reduction and low goal accuracy: Indicates random or ineffective problem-solving.

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Compare the evolutionary index for systems with varying levels of solving efficiency and understanding accuracy to test its predictive power for selection.
  2. Evaluate the role of proxies in extending individual intelligence by comparing problem-solving performance with and without access to social or technological resources.
  3. Test the tradeoff between efficiency and exploration by measuring performance on novel vs. familiar goals across systems with different intelligence profiles.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How do microscopic processes necessary to understand macroscopic patterns in intelligences?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The author explicitly states "TIS is a macroscopic framework that omits explicit consideration of uncertainty and information entropy dynamics as well as the dynamics of system components, controller strategies and interactions with proxies and the environment" and asks "To what extent are microscopic processes necessary to understand macroscopic patterns in intelligences?"
- Why unresolved: The paper acknowledges that while TIS provides a macroscopic framework, it does not explicitly integrate the causal roles of more microscopic levels. The author suggests that incorporating these features is a daunting challenge and it is not clear how they will significantly change our understanding of intelligence.
- What evidence would resolve it: Empirical studies that demonstrate the necessity or lack thereof of microscopic processes in understanding macroscopic patterns of intelligence, possibly through computational models or experimental data.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can we identify a core set of phenotypic traits explaining the main effects and variances in intelligences?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The author poses the question "Can we identify a core set of phenotypic traits explaining the main effects and variances in intelligences?"
- Why unresolved: The paper discusses the partitioning of intelligence into solving and understanding, but does not delve into the specific phenotypic traits that underlie these processes. The complexity and multi-dimensional nature of intelligence make it challenging to identify a core set of traits.
- What evidence would resolve it: Genetic studies or phenotypic analyses that identify specific traits associated with intelligence, potentially through twin studies, genome-wide association studies (GWAS), or comprehensive phenotypic assessments.

### Open Question 3
- Question: What are minimally intelligent life systems?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The author asks "What are minimally intelligent life systems?"
- Why unresolved: The paper discusses intelligence in various contexts, from physical systems to biological and artificial systems, but does not define a clear threshold for what constitutes a minimally intelligent life system. This question touches on the fundamental nature of intelligence and its emergence in life forms.
- What evidence would resolve it: Studies that identify and characterize life systems with minimal intelligence, possibly through comparative analyses of different organisms or artificial life simulations.

## Limitations
- The theoretical framework is presented at a high level without detailed mathematical derivations or empirical validation protocols.
- The evolutionary intelligence index, while conceptually appealing, lacks concrete implementation details and validation across diverse systems.
- The paper assumes that intelligence traits are heritable and subject to selection, which may not hold for all forms of intelligence (e.g., artificial or collective intelligence).

## Confidence
- High confidence: The dual-process partition of intelligence into uncertainty reduction and goal accuracy is conceptually sound and aligns with established theories in information theory and cognitive science.
- Medium confidence: The evolutionary intelligence index as a quantitative measure of selection potential is promising but requires empirical validation and refinement.
- Low confidence: The application of the framework to explain phenomena ranging from crystal formation to human intelligence is speculative and needs rigorous testing.
