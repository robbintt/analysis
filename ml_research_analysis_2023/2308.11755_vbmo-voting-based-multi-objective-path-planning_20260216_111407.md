# VBMO: Voting-Based Multi-Objective Path Planning

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2308.11755
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.11755
- **Reference count:** 12
- **Primary result:** VBMO uses voting mechanisms to select non-dominated multi-objective plans without hand-tuned weights, outperforming weighted-sum approaches especially when objectives have different scales.

## Executive Summary
VBMO is a voting-based multi-objective path planning algorithm that efficiently generates optimal single-objective plans and uses voting mechanisms to select the best plan across multiple objectives. Unlike other approaches, VBMO does not require hand-tuned weights or consider multiple objectives at every search step. Instead, it leverages the efficiency of single-objective search and uses post-hoc evaluation and voting to select a non-dominated plan. VBMO incorporates three voting mechanisms: range, Borda, and combined approval. Extensive evaluation in diverse and complex environments demonstrates VBMO's ability to efficiently produce plans that satisfy multiple objectives. VBMO significantly outperforms a simple weighted sum approach in terms of both plan quality and computation time, particularly when the objectives have vastly different scales. VBMO is guaranteed to construct at least one plan on the Pareto frontier and always selects a non-dominated plan.

## Method Summary
VBMO performs independent A* searches for each objective on a graph where edges are labeled with multiple objective costs. After obtaining optimal single-objective plans, it evaluates each plan under all objectives, normalizes the scores to [0,1], and applies voting mechanisms (range, Borda, or combined approval) to select the final non-dominated plan. The approach guarantees at least one plan is on the Pareto frontier and avoids the scale bias problems of weighted-sum approaches.

## Key Results
- VBMO is guaranteed to construct at least one plan on the Pareto frontier and always selects a non-dominated plan
- VBMO significantly outperforms weighted-sum approaches in both plan quality and computation time, especially when objectives have vastly different scales
- VBMO incorporates three voting mechanisms (range, Borda, combined approval) that all guarantee selection of non-dominated plans

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: VBMO produces plans on the Pareto frontier without hand-tuned weights
- Mechanism: By running independent single-objective A* searches for each objective, then evaluating and voting among the resulting plans, VBMO guarantees at least one plan is optimal for its own objective and non-dominated overall
- Core assumption: Each objective can be minimized independently using standard A* and the resulting plans can be compared fairly after normalization
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "VBMO is guaranteed to construct at least one plan on the Pareto frontier and always selects a non-dominated plan"
  - [section] "VBMO uses a topologically identical graph but with a set of edge labels that represents different objectives. It constructs an optimal plan for each objective, evaluates each plan with every objective, and then selects the plan with voting"
  - [corpus] Weak - corpus neighbors focus on voting in task assignment rather than multi-objective path planning, no direct evidence for Pareto guarantee
- Break condition: If objectives are highly correlated or if A* cannot find an optimal path for an objective (e.g., inadmissible heuristic), the guarantee fails

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Voting mechanisms (range, Borda, CAV) always select a non-dominated plan
- Mechanism: Voting methods assign scores or points to each plan based on normalized costs across objectives; the plan with best aggregate score is selected. Theorem 1 proves range voting always picks non-dominated plans; similar proofs hold for Borda and CAV
- Core assumption: Dominance is transitive and the voting aggregation preserves the non-dominance property
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "Theorem 1 proves that, given a set of dominated and non-dominated plans, VBMO's range voting mechanism will always select a non-dominated plan because non-dominated plans score lower with respect to at least one objective and therefore have a lower total score"
  - [section] "Similar proofs can trivially show that Borda voting and combined approval voting also always select a non-dominated plan"
  - [corpus] No direct corpus evidence; voting mechanisms described but not proven in neighbors
- Break condition: If voting ties or if normalization masks meaningful differences, a dominated plan could be selected

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: VBMO avoids the bias of weighted-sum approaches toward objectives with larger magnitudes
- Mechanism: Normalization of plan scores to [0,1] before voting removes scale effects; each objective contributes equally to the final decision
- Core assumption: Normalization is meaningful across all objectives and does not distort the relative importance of objectives
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "Because the underlying graph has the same topological structure... VBMO sums βj's edge costs... To avoid any biases that would be introduced by the magnitude of an objective's values, all scores from any βj are normalized in [0,1]"
  - [section] "One challenge with using a weighted sum is to weight the objectives so that search is not biased toward any one objective because of the magnitude of its values. VBMO does not have this problem because it normalizes the scores before calculating the total scores"
  - [corpus] No corpus evidence; neighbors discuss voting but not normalization or scale bias
- Break condition: If objectives have vastly different scales or distributions, normalization may not fully eliminate bias

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:** Graph construction -> Edge labeling per objective -> A* search per objective -> Plan evaluation under all objectives -> Normalization -> Voting selection -> Output best plan
- **Critical path:** Single-objective A* -> Normalization -> Voting -> Selection (each step must complete for correct plan output)
- **Design tradeoffs:** Independence of objectives (simple but may miss compromises) vs. joint optimization (complex but potentially better compromise); speed vs. optimality (A* optimal but slower than greedy)
- **Failure signatures:** Non-optimal A* paths, incorrect normalization, voting ties, dominated plan selection, slow graph construction
- **First 3 experiments:**
  1. Run VBMO with 3 synthetic objectives (distance, uniform, random) on a small grid; verify output plan is optimal for at least one objective and non-dominated
  2. Compare VBMO's time and score against weighted-sum baseline on same environment; confirm VBMO is faster and scores better when objectives differ in scale
  3. Introduce highly correlated objectives; observe if VBMO's score degrades relative to weighted-sum, and analyze plan selection patterns

## Limitations

- Exact implementation details for safety edge cost calculation and normalization method are unspecified, potentially affecting reproducibility
- While Theorem 1 guarantees range voting selects non-dominated plans, proofs for Borda and CAV voting mechanisms are stated as "trivial" but not provided
- No empirical validation of VBMO's performance when objectives are highly correlated, which could impact plan diversity and quality

## Confidence

- **High confidence** in the core claim that VBMO produces at least one plan on the Pareto frontier (supported by explicit guarantee and mechanism)
- **Medium confidence** in the claim that all three voting mechanisms always select non-dominated plans (theoretical proofs provided for range voting; Borda and CAV proofs are referenced but not shown)
- **Medium confidence** in the claim that VBMO avoids scale bias (mechanism is sound but normalization details are unspecified)
