# FLOGA: A machine learning ready dataset, a benchmark and a novel deep learning model for burnt area mapping with Sentinel-2

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2311.03339
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.03339
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: Introduces FLOGA dataset and BAM-CD model achieving 94.03% F1-score and 89.24% IoU for burnt area mapping

## Executive Summary
This study introduces FLOGA, a comprehensive dataset for burnt area mapping containing Sentinel-2 and MODIS imagery from 326 wildfire events in Greece, along with expert-annotated ground truth labels. The authors conduct an extensive benchmark comparing traditional spectral indices, machine learning models (Random Forest, SVM, XGBoost, MLP), and various deep learning architectures on this dataset. They propose a novel deep learning model, BAM-CD, which achieves state-of-the-art performance with a mean F1-score of 94.03% and mean IoU of 89.24%, outperforming all other methods in accuracy and robustness.

## Method Summary
The study benchmarks multiple approaches for burnt area mapping using the FLOGA dataset. Methods compared include spectral indices (dSAVI, dNDVI, dEVI, etc.) with thresholding, traditional ML models (RF, SVM, XGBoost, MLP) using differenced spectral indices as features, and various deep learning architectures (FC-EF-Diff/Conc, SNUNet-CD, ADHR-CDNet, ChangeFormer) processing bitemporal Sentinel-2 imagery. The proposed BAM-CD model uses a dual-stream residual architecture with attention mechanisms, employing a Siamese encoder to process pre- and post-fire imagery in parallel before decoding with attention modules. The dataset is split 60%/20%/20% for training, validation, and testing while maintaining uniform geographic distribution.

## Key Results
- BAM-CD achieves state-of-the-art performance with 94.03% mean F1-score and 89.24% mean IoU
- Spectral indices alone produce inconsistent results due to land cover type variations
- Traditional ML models underperform compared to deep learning approaches when using differenced indices
- BAM-CD demonstrates superior precision in mapping burn scars while reducing false positives from similar surfaces like water bodies and shadows

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- **Claim:** BAM-CD achieves state-of-the-art performance through dual-stream residual architecture with attention mechanisms
- **Mechanism:** Uses Siamese encoder with shared weights to extract features from pre- and post-fire imagery separately, concatenates at each level, and applies decoder with attention modules to focus on salient changes
- **Core assumption:** Parallel processing of pre- and post-fire images captures temporal dynamics better than concatenation
- **Evidence anchors:** Abstract states BAM-CD achieves state-of-the-art results; section discusses double-stream framework for change detection
- **Break condition:** Performance advantage may diminish if parallel processing doesn't better capture temporal dynamics

### Mechanism 2
- **Claim:** Differenced spectral indices improve traditional ML model performance
- **Mechanism:** Differenced indices (dNBR, dNDVI) capture spectral change between pre- and post-fire imagery, making them more effective for identifying burnt areas than single-date indices
- **Core assumption:** Spectral change between pre- and post-fire imagery strongly indicates burnt areas
- **Evidence anchors:** Section focuses on differenced indices for change detection approach; notes global thresholds are difficult to determine
- **Break condition:** Performance may degrade if spectral change isn't reliable across certain land cover types or atmospheric conditions

### Mechanism 3
- **Claim:** BAM-CD's superior performance stems from generalization across diverse fire events and land cover types
- **Mechanism:** Trained on diverse FLOGA dataset covering 326 events across Greece with varying land cover types and conditions, enabling robust feature learning
- **Core assumption:** Diverse training data leads to better generalization in deep learning models
- **Evidence anchors:** Abstract notes FLOGA covers Mediterranean landscape; section describes uniform geographic distribution in train/val/test split
- **Break condition:** Performance may significantly degrade when applied to regions with underrepresented land cover types in training data

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:** Pre-fire imagery → Siamese encoder (ResNet-101) → Concatenation → Decoder with attention → Post-fire imagery → Siamese encoder (ResNet-101) → Concatenation → Decoder with attention → Output binary mask

- **Critical path:**
  1. Input pre- and post-fire Sentinel-2 imagery
  2. Extract features using Siamese encoder
  3. Concatenate feature maps at each level
  4. Pass concatenated features through decoder with attention
  5. Output binary mask of burnt areas

- **Design tradeoffs:** Siamese vs. pseudo-Siamese encoder training; ResNet-50 vs. ResNet-101 backbone; attention mechanisms in decoder; loss function selection (BCE vs. Focal vs. Dice)

- **Failure signatures:** High false positive rate on similar surfaces; inability to capture fine-grained details; poor generalization to unseen events; instability across training runs

- **First 3 experiments:**
  1. Train BAM-CD with Siamese encoder and Binary Cross-Entropy loss on FLOGA dataset
  2. Evaluate trained model on test set and compare performance metrics with other deep learning models
  3. Visualize predicted burn scars and compare with ground truth to assess fine-grained detail capture and false positive suppression

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How well do proposed methods generalize to different ecosystems and climate zones beyond Mediterranean region?
- Basis in paper: Authors acknowledge FLOGA covers only Greek region with Mediterranean ecosystems
- Why unresolved: Dataset and experiments limited to one geographic region
- What evidence would resolve it: Testing BAM-CD on datasets from different climate zones with ground truth labels

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can spectral indices be effectively thresholded globally across different land cover types and imaging conditions?
- Basis in paper: Authors found global thresholds for spectral indices are difficult to determine
- Why unresolved: Paper shows spectral indices perform inconsistently across land cover types
- What evidence would resolve it: Adaptive thresholding methods accounting for land cover type and imaging conditions

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can spectral confusion with similar surfaces (water, shadows, etc.) be effectively mitigated?
- Basis in paper: Authors note spectral indices and ML models suffer from commission errors due to spectral confusion
- Why unresolved: All approaches still produce false positives when encountering spectrally similar surfaces
- What evidence would resolve it: Methods distinguishing burnt areas from spectrally similar surfaces through improved feature extraction or contextual information

## Limitations
- Evaluation relies on single dataset covering only Greece, limiting generalizability to other geographic regions
- No detailed ablation studies to isolate BAM-CD components contributing to performance gains
- Comparison uses different input representations for traditional ML (differenced indices) vs. deep learning (raw imagery), complicating direct performance attribution

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: BAM-CD achieves state-of-the-art performance on FLOGA dataset (94.03% F1-score, 89.24% IoU)
- **Medium confidence**: Superiority of parallel bitemporal processing over concatenation-based approaches
- **Low confidence**: Generalization claims to geographic regions beyond Mediterranean context
