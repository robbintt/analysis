# AutoRepo: A general framework for multi-modal LLM-based automated construction reporting

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2310.07944
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.07944
- Reference count: 39
- Primary result: Automated construction inspection reporting using unmanned vehicles and multimodal LLMs, reducing inspection/reporting time from 3 hours to 1 hour.

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces AutoRepo, a novel framework for automated construction inspection reporting using unmanned vehicles and multimodal large language models (LLMs). The framework addresses inefficiencies in traditional manual inspections by integrating 3D reconstruction, path planning, and LLM-based report generation. Tested on a real construction site, AutoRepo demonstrated significant improvements in efficiency, reducing inspection and reporting time from three hours (manual) to one hour (automated). While the generated reports captured scene information more comprehensively, minor deficiencies in detail were noted, likely due to limited training data. The study highlights the potential of LLMs to revolutionize construction inspection, though future work will focus on enhancing dataset robustness and multi-vehicle coordination. AutoRepo represents a significant step toward safer, faster, and more accurate construction management.

## Method Summary
AutoRepo is a three-phase framework that automates construction inspection reporting. Phase one involves selecting inspection types and corresponding unmanned vehicles (UAV/UGV). Phase two collects scene data through 3D reconstruction and optimal path planning to ensure comprehensive coverage. Phase three processes the collected data and uses a fine-tuned multimodal LLM to generate inspection reports. The framework leverages 3D reconstruction from images to create site meshes, coverage path planning for unmanned vehicle navigation, and a fine-tuned LLM (MiniGPT-4) to convert visual data into natural language inspection reports. The method reduces manual inspection time from three hours to one hour while improving report comprehensiveness.

## Key Results
- Automated inspection and reporting time reduced from 3 hours (manual) to 1 hour (AutoRepo).
- LLM-generated reports captured scene information more comprehensively than manual reports.
- Minor deficiencies in report detail were observed, likely due to limited training data.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: AutoRepo reduces inspection and reporting time from 3 hours (manual) to 1 hour (automated) by integrating unmanned vehicles with multimodal LLMs.
- Mechanism: Unmanned vehicles (UAV/UGV) autonomously collect scene images, which are processed and fed to a fine-tuned multimodal LLM that generates inspection reports without manual transcription.
- Core assumption: Scene images contain sufficient information for LLM-based report generation when paired with appropriate fine-tuning.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "the framework was applied and tested on a real-world construction site, demonstrating its potential to expedite the inspection process, significantly reduce resource allocation, and produce high-quality, regulatory standard-compliant inspection reports."
  - [section] "Manual inspection team's construction inspection lasted a total of three hours, including the 30 minutes they spent preparing the construction inspection report. In striking contrast, the Unitree AlienGo automated unit was able to successfully execute both the construction inspection and the ensuing generation of the report within the span of a single hour."
- Break condition: Insufficient training data leads to incomplete or inaccurate report generation.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: 3D reconstruction of the construction site enables optimal path planning for unmanned vehicles.
- Mechanism: Images captured during inspection are used to reconstruct a 3D mesh of the site, which informs the planning of coverage paths that maximize inspection completeness and minimize travel distance.
- Core assumption: 3D reconstruction accurately represents the site geometry and inspection targets.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "The inspection vehicles integrate external geographical reference to facilitate global movement and perform a 3D mesh reconstruction of the construction site. Subsequently, an optimal inspection path is devised based on the locations of the required inspection targets."
  - [corpus] Weak correlation with related papers; only one neighbor paper cites 3D reconstruction explicitly.
- Break condition: Poor reconstruction quality or inaccessible areas lead to incomplete coverage.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Fine-tuning a generic LLM on domain-specific construction inspection data improves report accuracy and compliance.
- Mechanism: A pre-trained multimodal LLM (e.g., MiniGPT-4) is further trained on a curated dataset of construction inspection reports to align its output with regulatory standards and domain terminology.
- Core assumption: Sufficient high-quality, labeled construction inspection data is available for fine-tuning.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "The fine-tuning dataset extracted from the second phase, which adheres strictly to the inspection specifications, is used to fine-tune the generic model."
  - [section] "This fine-tuned LLM becomes an integral part of the automated inspection system. It is used to generate a detailed construction inspection report automatically, based on the data collected during the inspection."
- Break condition: Dataset is too small or not representative, causing model to miss key inspection details.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  - Data Collection Layer: Unmanned vehicle (UAV/UGV) with RGB camera, Lidar, 5G/Wi-Fi.
  - Data Processing Layer: 3D reconstruction (SFM + MVS), image preprocessing (grayscale, enhancement), dataset curation.
  - Model Layer: Generic multimodal LLM → fine-tuned domain-specific LLM.
  - Report Generation Layer: LLM outputs structured inspection report.
  - Control Layer: Path planning, vehicle coordination, API for image ingestion.

- Critical path:
  1. Capture images → 2. 3D reconstruction → 3. Path planning → 4. Image preprocessing → 5. LLM fine-tuning → 6. Report generation.

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Single vs. multiple unmanned vehicles: Single reduces coordination complexity but may miss coverage; multiple improves coverage but adds synchronization overhead.
  - Beam search size vs. randomness: Larger beam size increases diversity but may reduce report precision; temperature controls creativity vs. consistency.
  - Fine-tuning dataset size vs. model performance: Larger, higher-quality datasets improve accuracy but increase cost and time.

- Failure signatures:
  - Missing inspection details → likely insufficient training data or poor image quality.
  - Incomplete coverage → path planning failed to account for site geometry or obstacles.
  - Long processing time → inefficient preprocessing or model inference bottleneck.

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Validate image-to-text conversion: Feed a single well-lit construction inspection image into MiniGPT-4 and compare output to manual report.
  2. Test path planning: Use simulated site geometry to verify UAV coverage completeness and path efficiency.
  3. Evaluate fine-tuning impact: Compare LLM-generated reports before and after fine-tuning on a small curated dataset.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How does the accuracy of LLM-generated construction inspection reports compare to human-generated reports across different types of inspections (pre-construction, progress, final)?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper states that LLM-generated reports captured scene information more comprehensively than manual reports, but exhibited some deficiencies in detail, likely due to limited training data.
- Why unresolved: The study only tested one type of inspection (process inspection) and did not provide a quantitative comparison of accuracy across different inspection types.
- What evidence would resolve it: A controlled study comparing LLM-generated and human-generated reports across all three inspection types, using standardized metrics for accuracy and completeness.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What is the optimal size and composition of the training dataset needed to achieve maximum accuracy in LLM-generated construction inspection reports?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions that the deficiencies in detail of the LLM-generated reports could potentially be attributable to an insufficient volume of training data within the construction inspection report dataset.
- Why unresolved: The study did not systematically vary the size or composition of the training dataset to determine the relationship between dataset characteristics and report accuracy.
- What evidence would resolve it: A series of experiments training the LLM with progressively larger and more diverse datasets, measuring the impact on report accuracy and completeness.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can multiple unmanned vehicles be coordinated to eliminate blind spots and ensure comprehensive data collection during automated construction inspections?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper acknowledges that reliance on a singular unmanned device for inspection may inadvertently lead to blind spots in the inspection process and an incomplete collection of scene information.
- Why unresolved: The study used only one unmanned vehicle and did not explore strategies for coordinating multiple vehicles.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development and testing of a multi-vehicle coordination system that demonstrates improved coverage and reduced blind spots compared to single-vehicle inspection.

## Limitations
- The framework's effectiveness is constrained by the quality and size of the fine-tuning dataset, which was not fully specified.
- Reliance on a single unmanned vehicle limits scalability and may result in incomplete coverage in complex construction sites.
- The system does not address potential failures in 3D reconstruction due to poor lighting or occluded areas, which could compromise inspection completeness.

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The reported time reduction (3 hours to 1 hour) is supported by direct comparison in the results section.
- **Medium Confidence**: The claim that the framework produces "high-quality, regulatory standard-compliant inspection reports" is plausible but lacks quantitative validation of report accuracy or completeness.
- **Low Confidence**: The assertion that the system "significantly reduces resource allocation" is vague and unsupported by specific metrics such as labor costs or vehicle operational expenses.
