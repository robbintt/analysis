# Effect of Intensity Standardization on Deep Learning for WML Segmentation in Multi-Centre FLAIR MRI

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2307.03827
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03827
- Reference count: 15
- Key outcome: Intensity standardization techniques like IAMLAB and Ensemble methods improve white matter lesion segmentation in multi-center FLAIR MRI by reducing domain shift between scanners.

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of white matter lesion (WML) segmentation in multi-center FLAIR MRI by investigating intensity standardization techniques. The authors demonstrate that methods like IAMLAB and Ensemble normalization significantly reduce domain shift between different scanners and imaging centers, leading to improved segmentation performance. The proposed approach enables deep learning models to generalize better across unseen clinical datasets, with particular benefits for small lesion detection.

## Method Summary
The study uses a skip-connection U-Net architecture trained on 60 FLAIR volumes from the MICCAI challenge, then evaluates on 128 unseen volumes from three clinical cohorts (ADNI, CAIN, CCNA). Intensity standardization methods (IAMLAB, WhiteStripe, Nyul, Z-score) are applied to reduce domain shift, with an Ensemble model combining predictions from all normalized versions. The model is trained using generalized Dice loss, Adam optimizer, and patch-based training (64x64 patches with 50% overlap), then evaluated using DSC, H95, AVD, F1-score, Recall, and EF metrics.

## Key Results
- IAMLAB and Ensemble normalization methods achieved significantly higher DSC scores across all lesion load categories compared to models trained on original data
- For small lesions (LL<10mL), DSC improved from 0.58 (original) to 0.73 (IAMLAB)
- IAMLAB normalization achieved the lowest KL divergence (0.06) between original and standardized histograms, indicating superior alignment
- Ensemble method showed consistent performance improvements across all clinical cohorts

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: IAMLAB and Ensemble normalization reduce domain shift by aligning intensity histograms across scanners, enabling better generalization.
- Mechanism: These methods map similar tissue intensities to the same numerical ranges across datasets, minimizing scanner-specific intensity variability.
- Core assumption: FLAIR MRI from different scanners/vendors can be aligned to a common intensity distribution without losing diagnostic information.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "Intensity standardization techniques like IAMLAB and Ensemble methods improve white matter lesion segmentation... by reducing domain shift between scanners." [section] "Intensity standardized images are shown in Figure 9. Histograms of original and standardized volumes... IAMLAB normalization has the best alignment (lowest KL) of all the methods, with KL = 0.06, compared to the original data with KL = 0.83."

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Ensemble method combines predictions from multiple normalization approaches to improve robustness and accuracy.
- Mechanism: Pixel-wise majority vote across models trained on different normalized datasets captures complementary information and reduces variance.
- Core assumption: Different normalization methods capture complementary information and combining them improves generalization.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract] "We proposed an Ensemble model that combines predictions from each of these models." [section] "An Ensemble method is considered, which takes pixel-wise majority vote across predictions generated by the five models trained on different intensity standardized images."

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Skip-connection U-Net architecture facilitates better feature propagation and gradient flow, improving segmentation performance.
- Mechanism: Skip connections between encoder and decoder layers allow shallow and deep features to combine, easing training and reducing vanishing gradients.
- Core assumption: Adding skip connections improves information flow in deep networks for medical image segmentation.
- Evidence anchors: [section] "The skip connection (SC) U-Net proposed in (Wu et al., 2019) is used in this work as it was found to be optimal for FLAIR-only WML segmentation." [section] "Skip connections ease training through improved information and back-propagation flow."

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: FLAIR MRI volumes -> Intensity standardization (IAMLAB, WhiteStripe, Nyul, Z-score) -> Skip-connection U-Net with patch-based training -> Generalized Dice Loss -> Adam optimizer -> WML segmentation masks

- Critical path:
  1. Load and skull-strip FLAIR MRI volumes
  2. Apply intensity standardization (or not, for baseline)
  3. Patch the images for training/testing
  4. Train U-Net with generalized dice loss
  5. Evaluate segmentation using DSC, H95, AVD, F1, Recall, EF
  6. Compare models across lesion load categories

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Normalization: Balances between removing scanner variability and preserving pathology-specific intensity patterns.
  - Patch size and overlap: Larger patches may capture more context but increase memory usage.
  - Ensemble vs. single model: Ensemble improves robustness but increases computational cost.

- Failure signatures:
  - High KL divergence after normalization indicates poor alignment.
  - Low DSC or high EF on OOD data suggests domain shift or under/over-segmentation.
  - High coefficient of variation (CoV) in DSC across lesion loads indicates inconsistent performance.

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Train U-Net on original data only and evaluate on all three clinical cohorts (baseline).
  2. Train U-Net on IAMLAB-normalized data and compare DSC to baseline (test main hypothesis).
  3. Train and test the Ensemble model (majority vote across all normalization methods) and analyze improvements over individual models.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How does IAMLAB's performance compare to other state-of-the-art intensity standardization methods specifically designed for FLAIR MRI in clinical settings?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper mentions IAMLAB was specifically developed for FLAIR MRI and shows superior performance compared to other methods, but doesn't compare it to other FLAIR-specific methods.
- Why unresolved: The paper only compares IAMLAB to general normalization methods (White-stripe, Nyul, Z-score) rather than other FLAIR-specific standardization techniques.
- What evidence would resolve it: Comparative studies between IAMLAB and other FLAIR-specific standardization methods on the same dataset, measuring segmentation performance metrics like DSC, EF, H95, AVD, F1-score, and Recall.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What are the long-term clinical implications of using intensity standardization methods like IAMLAB and Ensemble for WML segmentation in terms of patient outcomes and treatment decisions?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper demonstrates improved segmentation performance but doesn't explore how these improvements translate to clinical decision-making or patient outcomes.
- Why unresolved: The study focuses on technical performance metrics without addressing real-world clinical applications or impact on patient care.
- What evidence would resolve it: Longitudinal studies tracking patient outcomes and treatment decisions based on WML segmentations using standardized vs. non-standardized data, correlating segmentation accuracy with clinical outcomes.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How do IAMLAB and Ensemble normalization methods perform when applied to other types of MRI sequences beyond FLAIR, such as T1, T2, or PD-weighted images?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper only evaluates these methods on FLAIR MRI data, leaving their generalizability to other MRI sequences unexplored.
- Why unresolved: The study is limited to FLAIR MRI, and there's no information on how these methods would perform on other common MRI sequences used in clinical practice.
- What evidence would resolve it: Testing IAMLAB and Ensemble on multiple MRI sequences (T1, T2, PD) across various clinical datasets, comparing segmentation performance to original data and other normalization methods.

## Limitations

- The study is limited to FLAIR MRI sequences and does not evaluate performance on other common MRI modalities like T1, T2, or PD-weighted images.
- Results are based on specific scanner types and protocols from three clinical cohorts, raising questions about generalizability to different field strengths or emerging scanner technologies.
- The paper does not explore the clinical implications of improved segmentation performance on patient outcomes or treatment decisions.

## Confidence

- High Confidence: The core finding that intensity standardization reduces domain shift and improves segmentation performance is well-supported by quantitative metrics (KL divergence reduction from 0.83 to 0.06 for IAMLAB).
- Medium Confidence: The superiority of IAMLAB over other standardization methods is demonstrated but based on a single dataset comparison; other methods may perform better with different parameter tuning.
- Low Confidence: The ensemble method's performance gains are reported but the specific contribution of each individual normalization method to the ensemble's success remains unclear.
