# Geodesic Regression Characterizes 3D Shape Changes in the Female Brain During Menstruation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2309.16662
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.16662
- Reference count: 21
- Key outcome: Introduces hybrid geodesic-linear regression with δ-Test and ∆-Test rules for accelerated 3D shape analysis, revealing for the first time that the hippocampal formation shears in response to progesterone increases during menstruation.

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces approximation schemes for geodesic regression on shape spaces of 3D discrete surfaces, motivated by the need to characterize 3D shape changes in the female brain during hormonal fluctuations. The key innovation is a hybrid approach combining geodesic and linear regression, which significantly accelerates computation while maintaining accuracy. The authors propose two rules of thumb - the δ-Test and ∆-Test - for deciding when each approximation method is appropriate based on the curvature of the data manifold relative to measurement noise or data spread. Tested on synthetic data, the approximations yield speed-ups of up to 14 million times with minimal accuracy loss. Applied to real brain MRI data, the method reveals for the first time that the hippocampal formation shears in response to progesterone increases during the menstrual cycle. This work enables practical, comprehensive shape analyses in biomedicine and computer vision, with publicly available implementation.

## Method Summary
The method applies geodesic regression to 3D discrete surface meshes, using a second-order Sobolev metric to measure distances between shapes. Three regression approaches are compared: linear regression (LR), geodesic regression (GR), and geodesic regression with linear residuals (GRLR). The δ-Test determines if linear residuals suffice when measurement noise is less than 1% of mesh diameter, while the ∆-Test determines if linear regression approximates geodesic regression well when deformation is 10-50% of mesh diameter. The approach was tested on synthetic data to validate the approximation rules and then applied to 11 MRI brain scans from a single subject across the menstrual cycle to characterize hippocampal shape changes related to progesterone fluctuations.

## Key Results
- Speed-ups of up to 14 million times with minimal accuracy loss when using approximation methods
- δ-Test: Use linear residuals instead of geodesic residuals when measurement noise < 1% of mesh diameter
- ∆-Test: Use linear regression instead of geodesic regression when deformation is 10-50% of mesh diameter
- First-time discovery that hippocampal formation shears in response to progesterone increases during menstruation

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The δ-Test enables use of linear residuals instead of geodesic residuals when measurement noise is small relative to manifold curvature.
- Mechanism: When the standard deviation of measurement noise is less than 1% of the mesh diameter, the geodesic and linear residuals become nearly identical because the noise-induced displacement is smaller than the local curvature scale.
- Core assumption: Measurement noise displaces vertices by a small fraction (≤1%) of the total mesh diameter.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "...for our δ-Test: when the measurement noise on the vertices of the meshed shapes is expected to be less than 1% of the total mesh diameter, we recommend using linear residuals instead of geodesic residuals."
  - [section] "We expect measurement noise to displace each vertex by around 1% of the total mesh diameter, since MRI images have a good resolution and both segmentation and meshing algorithms are reasonably accurate."
  - [corpus] Weak: No direct corpus evidence about noise displacement thresholds.
- Break condition: If noise exceeds 1% of mesh diameter, the geodesic-linear approximation becomes inaccurate and geodesic regression should be used instead.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: The ∆-Test allows linear regression to approximate geodesic regression when the total deformation between surfaces is small relative to manifold curvature.
- Mechanism: When the maximum deformation between any two meshes in the dataset is 10-50% of the mesh diameter, the nonlinear manifold can be locally approximated as linear, making linear regression sufficiently accurate.
- Core assumption: The data spread (maximum deformation) is within 10-50% of the mesh diameter.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "if the data set's largest deformation between two meshes is 10-50% of the diameter of the mesh...then using linear regression instead of geodesic regression allows them to significantly speed up their pipeline."
  - [section] "Fig. 5 shows that deformation factors of 10-50% yield an error that is less than 10% of the diameter...Consequently, for our ∆-Test..."
  - [corpus] Weak: No corpus evidence about specific deformation thresholds.
- Break condition: If deformation exceeds 50% of mesh diameter, the nonlinear effects dominate and geodesic regression becomes necessary.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: The hybrid geodesic-linear regression (GRLR) provides significant speed-up by avoiding logarithm computations while maintaining accuracy.
- Mechanism: GRLR replaces geodesic residuals (which require expensive logarithm computations) with linear residuals, but retains the exponential map for modeling the manifold trajectory. This reduces computational complexity while preserving the geometric structure.
- Core assumption: Linear residuals provide adequate approximation to geodesic residuals when noise is small.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "We propose geodesic regression with linear residuals (GRLR)...to model the relationship...where the squared geodesic distance of Eq. 7 has been replaced by the squared Euclidean distance, but we keep the exponential map defining the geodesic."
  - [section] "By avoiding the computation of n logarithms, the learning process enjoys a significant speed-up...up to 14M × (for a number of steps of 20)..."
  - [corpus] Weak: No corpus evidence about specific speed-up factors.
- Break condition: If residuals become large relative to manifold curvature, linear residuals no longer approximate geodesic residuals well.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: What is the exact relationship between progesterone levels and hippocampal shape changes during the menstrual cycle?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper states "linear regression reveals hippocampal deformation associated with an increase in progesterone during the menstrual cycle" and "for the first time, the hippocampal formation shears in response to an increase in progesterone."
- Why unresolved: The paper provides a characterization of the shape change but does not quantify the precise mathematical relationship or functional form between progesterone levels and specific shape parameters.
- What evidence would resolve it: A detailed parametric model showing how specific shape descriptors (curvature, thickness, etc.) vary as a function of progesterone concentration, validated across multiple subjects.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How do the proposed approximation schemes (linear regression and geodesic regression with linear residuals) perform on datasets with different noise characteristics and data spread?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper proposes the δ-Test and ∆-Test as rules of thumb for deciding between linear, geodesic, and geodesic-linear regression based on curvature relative to noise or data spread.
- Why unresolved: The paper tests these approximations on synthetic data with specific deformation factors (1%, 10%, 50%, 100%) and real brain MRI data, but does not explore a broader range of noise levels and data spreads.
- What evidence would resolve it: Extensive experiments on diverse datasets with varying noise levels and data spreads to validate and refine the δ-Test and ∆-Test guidelines.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How does the choice of Riemannian metric (e.g., different weighting parameters in the second-order Sobolev metric) affect the results of geodesic regression on surface shape spaces?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper uses a second-order Sobolev metric with specific weighting parameters (a0, a1, a2, b1, c1, d1) but does not explore the sensitivity of results to different metric choices.
- Why unresolved: The paper focuses on a specific metric formulation but does not investigate how alternative metric choices might influence the regression outcomes or computational efficiency.
- What evidence would resolve it: Comparative studies using different Riemannian metrics to assess their impact on geodesic regression accuracy and computational performance.

## Limitations
- The approximation rules (δ-Test and ∆-Test) are validated primarily on synthetic data with limited testing on diverse real-world scenarios
- The biological findings are based on a small sample size (11 brain scans from one subject over 11 consecutive days)
- The specific threshold values (1% noise level, 10-50% deformation) may not generalize across different anatomical structures or imaging modalities

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The mathematical framework for geodesic regression on shape spaces is well-established and the hybrid GRLR approach is theoretically sound.
- **Medium Confidence**: The δ-Test and ∆-Test rules are empirically derived and show strong performance on synthetic data, but require more extensive validation on diverse real-world datasets.
- **Medium Confidence**: The biological findings about hippocampal shape changes during menstruation are novel and methodologically sound, but based on a relatively small sample size (11 brain scans from one subject over 11 consecutive days).
