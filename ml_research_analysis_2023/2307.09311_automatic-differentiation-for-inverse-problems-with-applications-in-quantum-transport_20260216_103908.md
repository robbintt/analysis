# Automatic Differentiation for Inverse Problems with Applications in Quantum Transport

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2307.09311
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.09311
- Reference count: 12
- One-line primary result: Physics-informed neural networks and differentiable simulation successfully engineered quantum transport device characteristics through inverse optimization

## Executive Summary
This paper presents two complementary approaches for inverse problems in quantum transport: physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) and differentiable simulation. The PINN approach directly solves quantum transport equations to optimize internal potentials matching target transmission properties, while the differentiable simulation optimizes barrier parameters to reproduce target current-voltage characteristics. Both methods leverage automatic differentiation for gradient-based optimization, enabling direct computation of transmission properties across energy ranges and systematic device engineering.

## Method Summary
The paper implements two approaches for quantum transport inverse problems. The PINN method uses Fourier features to embed quantum transport equations and boundary conditions into the loss function, optimizing both the wavefunction and internal potential to match target transmission properties. The differentiable simulation approach discretizes the quantum transmitting boundary model on a fixed grid and uses JAX's automatic differentiation capabilities to compute gradients for optimizing barrier parameters to match target I-V curves. Both methods employ gradient descent optimization, with PINNs requiring careful handling of spectral bias and stiffness issues.

## Key Results
- Successfully engineered transmission curves by optimizing internal potentials using PINNs to match target T(E) properties
- Optimized barrier parameters via differentiable simulation to reproduce target current-voltage characteristics for resonant tunneling devices
- Demonstrated direct computation of transmission properties over continuous energy ranges using PINNs
- Showed gradient-based optimization of device parameters using automatic differentiation through the quantum transport simulation pipeline

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) solve quantum transport equations by embedding differential equations and boundary conditions into the loss function, allowing simultaneous learning across the entire domain. This enables solving in parameter space for inverse problems. Core assumption: solutions are sufficiently smooth for network approximation despite potential spectral bias. Break condition: steep gradients or discontinuities cause spectral bias requiring fine step sizes and long training times.

### Mechanism 2
Automatic differentiation computes exact gradients through the simulation pipeline, enabling gradient-based optimization of device parameters for inverse problems. Core assumption: the simulation is differentiable end-to-end. Break condition: non-differentiable operations or numerical noise prevent effective gradient-based optimization.

### Mechanism 3
Differentiable simulation with fixed discretization optimizes barrier parameters via gradient descent to engineer current-voltage characteristics. JAX's vmap enables efficient vectorization over energies and voltages. Core assumption: discretization captures relevant physics and optimization landscape is smooth enough for gradient descent. Break condition: highly non-convex landscape with many local minima prevents finding global optimum.
