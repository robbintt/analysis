# Ambient Technology & Intelligence

## Executive Summary
This paper presents an ambient intelligence system designed to address healthcare worker shortages by embedding sensors into wearable devices that continuously monitor patients' vitals and interact with smart home devices to provide real-time care. The system architecture integrates IoT, sensor technology, and AI to collect and analyze data, send alerts to caregivers, and automate environmental adjustments based on health conditions. The approach was tested through scenarios for patients with high blood pressure, kidney failure, asthma, and infants with Down syndrome. Results show the system can autonomously monitor health metrics, trigger alerts, and coordinate with other devices to improve patient safety and reduce caregiver burden.

## Method Summary
The system uses wearable or embedded sensors to collect physiological data in real-time, which is then analyzed by AI algorithms to detect anomalies. When abnormalities are detected, the system triggers alerts and sends instructions to IoT devices (e.g., windows, humidifiers, alarms) to adjust the environment. The system was tested through scenario-based evaluations for patients with high blood pressure, kidney failure, asthma, and infants with Down syndrome, demonstrating its ability to autonomously monitor health metrics and coordinate with smart devices.

## Key Results
- System autonomously monitors health metrics and triggers alerts in real-time
- Coordinates with IoT devices to automate environmental adjustments
- Addresses privacy, data protection, and security through user consent and TLS 3.0 encryption

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  - Wearable/Embedded Sensors -> Central Processing Unit -> IoT Devices
  - Central Processing Unit -> Secure Storage
  - Central Processing Unit -> User Interface (Mobile App)

- Critical path:
  1. Sensor collects vital data
  2. Data transmitted to central processing unit
  3. Central unit analyzes data and detects anomalies
  4. System triggers alerts and instructs IoT devices
  5. Alerts and data sent to caregivers/patients via mobile app

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Accuracy vs. Latency: Higher accuracy may require more processing time, increasing latency.
  - Privacy vs. Functionality: More data collection improves functionality but increases privacy risks.
  - Cost vs. Coverage: More sensors and IoT devices improve coverage but increase cost.

- Failure signatures:
  - Sensor failure: No data collection, no alerts
  - Communication failure: Data not transmitted, system unaware of vitals
  - Processing failure: Anomalies not detected, no alerts sent
  - IoT device failure: Environmental adjustments not made

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Simulate sensor data collection and transmission to verify accuracy and latency
  2. Test alert triggering and IoT device coordination in a controlled environment
  3. Conduct a privacy and security audit to ensure data protection and encryption compliance
