# Source-free Domain Adaptive Human Pose Estimation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2308.03202
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.03202
- Reference count: 40
- This paper introduces source-free domain adaptation for human pose estimation without access to source data during adaptation

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a new task of source-free domain adaptive human pose estimation, addressing the challenge of adapting human pose estimation models to new target domains without access to source data. The proposed framework employs three models - source, intermediate, and target - to explore the task from both source-protect and target-relevant perspectives. The source-protect module preserves source information while resisting noise, and the target-relevant module reduces the sparsity of spatial representations through a novel spatial probability space. Comprehensive experiments on several domain adaptive HPE benchmarks demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing approaches by a considerable margin.

## Method Summary
The method proposes a three-model framework for source-free domain adaptation in human pose estimation. It consists of source-protect modules that preserve source domain knowledge by fixing feature extractors and updating regressors, and target-relevant modules that reduce spatial representation sparsity through a novel spatial probability space. The framework employs pose-specific contrastive learning and information maximization based on horizontal and vertical projections of heatmaps. The intermediate model serves as a bridge between source and target models, learning simultaneously from both domains through knowledge transfer without explicit distribution matching.

## Key Results
- Achieves 3.4% improvement over SHOT++ baseline on SURREAL → Human3.6M task
- Achieves 3.3% improvement over SHOT++ baseline on SURREAL → LSP task
- Demonstrates effectiveness across multiple domain adaptation benchmarks including hand pose estimation tasks

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The source-protect module prevents catastrophic forgetting while resisting noise by fixing the feature extractor and updating only the regressor
- Mechanism: By freezing the feature extractor in the source model, the spatial representations learned from the source domain are preserved. The regressor is then fine-tuned to reduce domain-specific noise while maintaining the core source knowledge.
- Core assumption: The feature extractor contains the most transferable knowledge between domains, while the regressor is more domain-specific and prone to noise accumulation.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "The source-protect module preserves source information more effectively while resisting noise"
  - [section]: "The feature extractor is fixed so that the source representations continue to be generated, and the regressor is being updated"
- Break condition: If the domain shift is too large, freezing the feature extractor alone may be insufficient, requiring more extensive model adaptation.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: The spatial probability space reduces sparsity in keypoint representations while maintaining completeness
- Mechanism: Instead of working directly with heatmap pixels or treating HPE as a K-classification problem, the method projects heatmaps into horizontal and vertical vectors. This transforms the spatial representation from a high-dimensional sparse space (64×64 heatmap ≈ 4K values) into a compact vector space (64×2 values) that preserves complete spatial information.
- Core assumption: All coordinates can be effectively represented through their horizontal and vertical projections without losing critical spatial relationships between keypoints.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "The target-relevant module reduces the sparsity of spatial representations by building a novel spatial probability space"
  - [section]: "We propose using projected horizontal and vertical vectors of heatmaps as spatial representations"
- Break condition: If keypoint relationships have non-linear dependencies that cannot be captured through simple vector projections, this approach may lose important spatial information.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: The intermediate model serves as an effective bridge that implicitly reduces domain gap through knowledge transfer
- Mechanism: The intermediate model learns simultaneously from both source and target domains. Through the source-protect modules, it receives knowledge from the source model, while through the target-relevant modules, it receives knowledge from the target model. This dual learning process allows it to implicitly align the two domains without explicit distribution matching.
- Core assumption: Learning through bidirectional knowledge transfer is more effective for domain alignment than explicit distribution matching, especially when source data is unavailable.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "The intermediate model interacts with both of them to reduce the domain gap between source and target implicitly"
  - [section]: "It serves as a bridge between source and target as it learns from source and target simultaneously"
- Break condition: If the source and target domains have fundamentally incompatible representations, the intermediate model may fail to find a coherent alignment.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Source model -> Intermediate model (source-protect + target-relevant) -> Target model
- Critical path: Source model pretraining → Source-protect adaptation (Step A) → Target-relevant adaptation (Step B) → Intermediate model inference
- Design tradeoffs:
  - Freezing vs. training feature extractors balances preservation of source knowledge against adaptation to target domain
  - Vector-based spatial representation reduces sparsity but may lose some fine-grained spatial relationships
  - Three-model architecture increases complexity but provides better domain alignment than two-model approaches
- Failure signatures:
  - Source model performance degradation indicates insufficient source protection
  - Target model performance degradation indicates over-reliance on source model
  - Intermediate model not outperforming both source and target models suggests ineffective knowledge transfer
  - High variance across different keypoint types suggests spatial representation issues
- First 3 experiments:
  1. Verify source model pretraining: Measure PCK@0.05 on source domain after pretraining to establish baseline performance
  2. Test source-protect module: Compare intermediate model performance with and without residual loss on a small target dataset
  3. Validate spatial probability space: Replace vector projections with heatmap-based contrastive learning and measure performance impact on a validation split

## Limitations
- Limited ablation studies on individual components to quantify their respective contributions to performance gains
- Spatial probability space effectiveness assumed but not empirically validated against alternative representation methods
- Three-model framework may not directly transfer to other domain adaptation tasks where spatial relationships are less critical
