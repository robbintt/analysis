# From Data to Dialogue: Leveraging the Structure of Knowledge Graphs for Conversational Exploratory Search

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2310.05150
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.05150
- Authors:

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a knowledge-driven dialogue system for exploratory news search that leverages knowledge graphs to enable conversational navigation between related topics. The system integrates a Neo4j knowledge graph of 2,118 news articles with a conversational interface using speech-to-text and text-to-speech models, allowing users to search for entities and explore related content through natural dialogue. A user study with 54 participants demonstrated the effectiveness of entity-based search for discovery, with participants intuitively navigating linked articles, though entity recognition accuracy and speech input presented challenges particularly for older users.

## Method Summary
The system architecture combines a Neo4j knowledge graph containing news articles, categories, entities, and relationships with a conversational interface built on Google Dialogflow and Flask. The knowledge graph was constructed from 2,118 German news articles using named entity recognition and linking to Wikidata. The dialogue system supports three search mechanisms (overview, category search, entity-based search) with navigation capabilities. User evaluation was conducted with 54 participants who completed 17 tasks using the system, with system usability measured using the SUS questionnaire and overall satisfaction assessed on a 5-point Likert scale.

## Key Results
- Entity-based search was the most commonly detected intent (10.2% of dialogue turns), with users intuitively requesting news for specific entities of interest
- The system achieved a high SUS score of 79.4 and was rated positively for relevance and usability by participants
- Entity recognition accuracy was high for cities, countries, and persons, but low for sports, person, and nutrition entities, with foreign language entities presenting particular challenges

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: User -> Speech-to-Text -> Dialogflow -> Flask Service -> Neo4j Knowledge Graph -> Flask Service -> Response Generation -> Text-to-Speech -> User

- Critical path: User speaks -> Speech-to-text transcription -> Intent/entity recognition -> Query construction -> Knowledge graph query -> Response generation -> Text-to-speech conversion -> User hears response

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Using a voice interface provides accessibility but introduces challenges with speech recognition accuracy
  - Entity-based search allows for more precise queries but requires accurate entity recognition and linking
  - The finite state machine dialogue model is simpler to implement but may be less flexible than a more generative model

- Failure signatures:
  - Low speech recognition accuracy leads to misunderstandings and irrelevant responses
  - Incorrect entity recognition or linking results in wrong articles being retrieved or suggested
  - Poor dialogue management causes the system to get confused or stuck in a loop

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Test speech recognition accuracy with a diverse set of user utterances
  2. Evaluate entity recognition and linking performance on a sample of user queries
  3. Conduct user studies to assess the effectiveness and usability of the conversational search interface

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How effective are knowledge graphs for improving entity recognition and linking in conversational search systems?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper discusses challenges with entity recognition and linking, noting that certain entity classes like cities, countries, and persons were recognized with high accuracy, while others like sports, person, and nutrition were frequently unrecognized.
- Why unresolved: The study only used one specific knowledge graph and entity recognition model, so it's unclear how well these results would generalize to other domains or graph structures.
- What evidence would resolve it: Comparing entity recognition accuracy across multiple knowledge graphs and domains would help determine the effectiveness of knowledge graphs for this task.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can knowledge graphs be leveraged to improve the naturalness and diversity of responses in conversational search systems?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions that users desired more diverse responses and better pronunciation of the German text-to-speech models, suggesting that the current system's responses may lack naturalness.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not explore techniques for improving response generation using knowledge graphs.
- What evidence would resolve it: Implementing and evaluating response generation techniques that utilize knowledge graphs to ground responses in factual information would help determine their effectiveness.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can conversational search systems be designed to better support older adults with low technology use?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper notes that older users above the age of 50 reported more difficulties using the system and gave lower usability scores, likely due to lower technical literacy and familiarity with voice assistants.
- Why unresolved: The study does not explore specific design strategies for improving usability for older adults.
- What evidence would resolve it: Conducting usability studies with older adults to identify specific pain points and testing design modifications to address them would help determine effective strategies for supporting this user group.

## Limitations

- The system's performance heavily depends on entity recognition and linking accuracy, which was identified as a significant challenge particularly for foreign language entities, acronyms, and ambiguous terms
- The user study sample was relatively small (54 participants) and may not represent diverse user populations, as older participants reported more difficulties with speech input
- The evaluation focused on immediate usability rather than long-term learning effects or information discovery outcomes

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The system architecture and implementation details are well-documented, with clear descriptions of the knowledge graph construction, dialogue management, and search mechanisms. The user study methodology and results reporting are transparent and reproducible.
- **Medium Confidence**: The findings regarding entity-based search effectiveness and conversational interface suitability are supported by user feedback and interaction data, though the small sample size and single-domain focus reduce generalizability.
- **Low Confidence**: Claims about the superiority of entity-based search over traditional keyword search lack comparative evidence, as no baseline keyword search system was implemented or evaluated alongside the conversational system.

## Next Checks

1. **Entity Recognition Robustness Test**: Conduct systematic evaluation of entity recognition and linking accuracy across diverse entity types (people, organizations, locations, events) and languages to quantify failure rates and identify specific error patterns that could be addressed through improved NLP models.

2. **Cross-Domain Generalization Study**: Replicate the system architecture and user evaluation with knowledge graphs from different domains (e.g., academic publications, product catalogs, medical literature) to assess whether the conversational exploratory search benefits generalize beyond news articles.

3. **Longitudinal Learning Assessment**: Implement a follow-up study tracking the same users over multiple sessions to evaluate whether they develop improved search strategies and whether the system's entity suggestions become more effective as users learn to navigate the graph structure.
