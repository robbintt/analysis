# Personalized Predictions of Glioblastoma Infiltration: Mathematical Models, Physics-Informed Neural Networks and Multimodal Scans

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2311.16536
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.16536
- Reference count: 32
- This paper introduces a method for personalized predictions of glioblastoma infiltration using Physics-Informed Neural Networks and multimodal MRI scans.

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a novel approach for personalized prediction of glioblastoma (GBM) infiltration using Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) combined with a reaction-diffusion PDE model. The method estimates patient-specific tumor growth parameters from a single 3D MRI snapshot by embedding both data and PDE constraints into the loss function. Key innovations include characteristic non-dimensional parameter identification, a pre-training step with patient-specific solutions, and the use of the diffuse domain method to handle complex brain geometry within the PINN framework. The approach shows promise for real-time parametric inference in clinical settings for personalized GBM treatment planning.

## Method Summary
The method combines a Fisher-KPP reaction-diffusion PDE model of GBM growth with PINNs to estimate patient-specific parameters from multimodal MRI scans. It uses a two-step training approach: first estimating characteristic non-dimensional parameters from simplified spherical models, then pre-training the PINN with these parameters before fine-tuning with patient data. The diffuse domain method is employed to handle complex brain geometry by embedding the brain region into a larger computational domain. The PINN jointly minimizes a loss that includes both the PDE residual and the mismatch with observed segmentations, allowing for simultaneous satisfaction of the mathematical model and fit to imaging data.

## Key Results
- PINNs can solve the inverse problem of estimating patient-specific tumor growth parameters from a single MRI snapshot by embedding both data and PDE constraints into the loss function.
- Characteristic non-dimensional parameters and pre-training with patient-specific solutions significantly improve the efficiency and accuracy of PINN training.
- The diffuse domain method allows PINNs to handle complex brain geometry without requiring explicit boundary parameterization.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: PINNs can solve the inverse problem of estimating patient-specific tumor growth parameters from a single MRI snapshot by embedding both data and PDE constraints into the loss function.
- Mechanism: The Physics-Informed Neural Network represents the tumor cell density as a neural network and jointly minimizes a loss that includes both the PDE residual (physics loss) and the mismatch with observed segmentations (data loss). This allows the network to find parameters that simultaneously satisfy the mathematical model and fit the imaging data.
- Core assumption: The reaction-diffusion PDE model adequately captures the essential dynamics of GBM growth, and the neural network has sufficient capacity to represent the solution.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "PINNs embed both the data and the PDE into a loss function, thus integrating theory and data."
  - [section 2.3]: Defines the residual loss function LPDE = 1/Nr * sum of squared PDE residuals.
  - [corpus]: Weak evidence; corpus neighbors discuss similar PINN applications but don't directly validate this specific mechanism.
- Break condition: If the PDE model is fundamentally inadequate for the patient's tumor biology, or if the PINN architecture cannot represent the solution accurately, parameter estimation will fail.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Characteristic non-dimensional parameters and pre-training with patient-specific solutions significantly improve the efficiency and accuracy of PINN training.
- Mechanism: The method identifies characteristic parameters (D, R) for each patient using a simplified spherically symmetric model. These parameters are used to scale the PDE so that the true parameters are of order 1, making the optimization problem easier. The PINN is pre-trained to approximate the characteristic solution before fine-tuning with patient data.
- Core assumption: The simplified 1D model in spherical geometry can reliably estimate the characteristic parameters that capture the essential tumor scale for each patient.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "Key innovations include the identification and estimation of characteristic non-dimensional parameters, a pre-training step that utilizes the non-dimensional parameters and a fine-tuning step to determine the patient specific parameters."
  - [section 2.2]: Details the grid search procedure to estimate characteristic parameters and the pre-training step.
  - [section 3.3]: Discusses the importance of personalized characteristic parameters versus using average values.
- Break condition: If the simplified model fails to capture the true tumor characteristics, or if the characteristic solution is too different from the true solution, pre-training may not help and could even mislead the optimization.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: The diffuse domain method allows PINNs to handle complex brain geometry without requiring explicit boundary parameterization.
- Mechanism: The brain region is embedded into a larger computational domain using a phase field function ϕ. The PDE is modified so that it is naturally zero outside the brain, eliminating the need to sample points exactly on the complex boundary.
- Core assumption: The diffuse interface width (chosen as 3mm) is sufficient to approximate the true boundary conditions accurately.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 2.1.2]: Describes the diffuse domain method and the modified PDE.
  - [section 2.2]: Mentions solving the Cahn-Hilliard equation to obtain the phase field function ϕ.
  - [corpus]: No direct evidence in corpus; this appears to be a novel methodological contribution.
- Break condition: If the diffuse interface is too wide or too narrow relative to the brain geometry, the boundary approximation may be poor, leading to inaccurate solutions near the tumor edges.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

- How can personalized characteristic parameters be pre-trained for all patients efficiently? The paper discusses the use of personalized characteristic parameters for pre-training PINNs, but suggests that using a common pre-trained network for all patients could significantly increase inference speed. This is unresolved because the tumors have different scales and morphologies, making it challenging to use a single pre-trained network for all patients without affecting the accuracy of the results.

- How can the performance of PINNs be improved when the model cannot fully account for the data? The paper notes that when the model cannot fully account for the data, PINNs might sacrifice the PDE loss to fit the data, as long as the total loss is reduced. This is unresolved because the paper suggests that this occurrence is inherent in PINNs and that the estimated parameters remain reliable despite the influence of noisy data, but does not provide a solution to this issue.

- How can the uncertainty quantification be incorporated into PINNs for brain tumor growth modeling? The paper mentions that one of the strengths of Bayesian methods is their ability to quantify uncertainties, while PINNs provide deterministic outputs based on the minimizer of the loss function and do not inherently provide uncertainty estimates. This is unresolved because the paper does not explore the integration of uncertainty quantification into PINNs for brain tumor growth modeling, which could provide valuable information for clinical decision-making.

## Limitations
- The method's performance depends critically on the accuracy of the reaction-diffusion PDE model and the ability of PINNs to solve the inverse problem reliably.
- The diffuse domain method's approximation of complex brain geometry introduces potential errors that are not fully characterized.
- The validation is primarily based on synthetic data and a small patient cohort, limiting generalizability.

## Confidence

- **High confidence**: The PINN framework correctly embeds PDE constraints and data into the loss function (Mechanism 1). The diffuse domain method is correctly implemented as described.
- **Medium confidence**: The characteristic parameter estimation method produces meaningful scaling for the optimization problem (Mechanism 2). The pre-training approach improves PINN training efficiency and accuracy.
- **Low confidence**: The method's performance on real patient data matches synthetic validation results. The diffuse interface width of 3mm provides sufficient accuracy for boundary approximation.
