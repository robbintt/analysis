# Towards Head Computed Tomography Image Reconstruction Standardization with Deep Learning Assisted Automatic Detection

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2307.16440
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.16440
- Reference count: 40
- One-line primary result: YOLOv8-based automatic landmark detection standardizes head CT reconstruction with 92.91% mAP and improved diagnostic quality

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of standardizing head CT image reconstruction to improve diagnostic accuracy and efficiency. The authors propose an automated method that employs a deep learning-based object detection algorithm to identify key landmarks (eyes and external auditory canals) and reformat the images before 3D reconstruction. By comparing ten different object detection algorithms, they identified YOLOv8 as the most suitable for this task, achieving an mAP of 92.91% and demonstrating strong robustness against class imbalance. The qualitative evaluation of the standardized reconstruction results showed clinical practicability and validity, with significant improvements in scores compared to non-standardized reconstructions. The method reduces manual intervention, enhances accuracy and repeatability, and saves time and labor resources.

## Method Summary
The authors developed an automated method for head CT image reconstruction standardization using deep learning-assisted automatic detection of anatomical landmarks. They first converted DICOM CT images to JPEG format while preserving resolution and spatial information, then manually annotated four key landmarks (bilateral eyes and bilateral external auditory canals) using LabelImg. Ten object detection algorithms were systematically compared on the same dataset, with YOLOv8 selected based on highest accuracy (92.91% mAP) and robustness to class imbalance. The YOLOv8 model was trained using Adam optimizer for 100 epochs with specific learning rate scheduling. Detected landmark coordinates were used to calculate Euler angles for 3D head alignment, and SimpleITK was employed to perform 3D rotation, producing reformatted DICOM images. The reconstructed 3D models were then evaluated qualitatively by radiology specialists.

## Key Results
- YOLOv8 achieved 92.91% mAP in landmark detection, outperforming nine other object detection algorithms
- Qualitative evaluation showed standardized reconstructions had significantly higher clinical practicability scores compared to non-standardized reconstructions
- The method successfully reduced manual intervention while maintaining high accuracy and repeatability in head CT reconstruction

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Automatic landmark detection via deep learning eliminates manual re-formatting errors in head CT reconstruction.
- Mechanism: YOLOv8 object detection model identifies bilateral eyes and external auditory canals with high mAP (92.91%) and consistent bounding boxes. These coordinates are then used to calculate rotation angles for 3D head alignment.
- Core assumption: Detected landmarks are anatomically correct and reproducible across diverse head orientations and patient morphologies.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "identify and evaluate orbitomeatal line landmarks to automatically reformat the images prior to reconstruction"
  - [section] "we employ a deep learning-based object detection algorithm, identifying and evaluating orbitomeatal line landmarks to automatically reformat the images prior to reconstruction"
  - [corpus] No direct corpus evidence; method is specific to head CT landmark detection and not found in neighboring papers.
- Break condition: If landmark detection confidence falls below a usable threshold or landmarks are anatomically misplaced, resulting rotations will misalign the head CT.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Selection of the most accurate object detection model reduces downstream reconstruction errors.
- Mechanism: Systematic comparison of ten object detection algorithms (DETR, EfficientDet, Faster R-CNN, RetinaNet, YOLOv3/4/5/7/8, YOLOX) on the same dataset enables selection of YOLOv8 based on highest mAP and robustness to class imbalance.
- Core assumption: Model performance on detection task correlates with reconstruction quality; robustness to class imbalance ensures reliable landmark detection in varied clinical cases.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "By exploring their precision, efficiency, and robustness, we singled out the lightweight YOLOv8 as the aptest algorithm for our task, with an mAP of 92.91%"
  - [section] "Grounded in both theoretical and experimental insights, we discern the most effective and efficient algorithm for this particular task"
  - [corpus] No direct evidence; paper's methodological comparison is unique among cited works.
- Break condition: If other models outperform YOLOv8 under different data distributions or if model selection criteria change (e.g., inference speed prioritized over accuracy), the reconstruction quality may degrade.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Euler angle-based 3D rotation standardizes head CT orientation for consistent reconstruction.
- Mechanism: Landmarks are used to calculate roll, pitch, and yaw angles (via arctan operations), and SimpleITK is employed to perform 3D rotation, producing reformatted DICOM images.
- Core assumption: Calculated Euler angles accurately describe the required 3D rotation to align the orbitomeatal line horizontally and symmetrically.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "To reformat CT images, we conduct a three-dimensional rotation using Euler angles...we calculate the roll angle r, opting for the smaller angle value from both sides"
  - [section] "From the above calculations, we derive three rotation angles, based on which we carry out a three-dimensional rotation to obtain the reformatted DICOM format head CT images"
  - [corpus] No direct evidence; methodology is specific to this paper's landmark-based reformatting.
- Break condition: If landmark coordinates are noisy or detection fails, the calculated Euler angles will be inaccurate, leading to improper head alignment.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: DICOM images -> JPEG conversion -> Manual annotation -> YOLOv8 training -> Landmark detection -> Euler angle calculation -> SimpleITK 3D rotation -> Reformatted DICOM -> 3D reconstruction
- Critical path: Landmark detection -> Euler angle calculation -> 3D rotation -> reconstruction quality
- Design tradeoffs: YOLOv8 chosen for accuracy/robustness over model size; trade-off between inference speed and mAP acceptable in clinical workflow
- Failure signatures: Low landmark detection confidence, inconsistent bounding box sizes, or high reconstruction variance across cases indicate model or preprocessing issues
- First 3 experiments:
  1. Verify landmark detection accuracy on a held-out test set; check AP/mAP per landmark class.
  2. Test Euler angle calculation with synthetic rotated heads; compare reformatted output to ground truth.
  3. Run full pipeline on a small sample set; evaluate qualitative reconstruction scores and quantitative metrics (MSE, PSNR, SSIM).

## Limitations

- Small dataset size (140 CT scans) limits generalizability to diverse clinical populations and scanner types
- Lack of extensive cross-validation across multiple institutions and scanner manufacturers raises questions about real-world robustness
- Qualitative evaluation relies on subjective scoring rather than objective quantitative metrics for final 3D reconstruction outputs

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The methodology for automatic landmark detection using YOLOv8 is well-established and the reported mAP of 92.91% is technically sound. The approach of using anatomical landmarks (eyes and external auditory canals) for head CT standardization is clinically reasonable and aligns with existing radiological practices.
- **Medium Confidence**: The effectiveness of the complete pipeline from landmark detection to 3D reconstruction is supported by qualitative evaluation, but the lack of comprehensive quantitative validation across diverse datasets limits confidence in real-world performance.
- **Low Confidence**: The scalability of this approach to different CT scanner manufacturers, imaging protocols, and patient populations has not been demonstrated, raising questions about clinical applicability beyond the specific Siemens scanner used in this study.
