# Meta-multigraph Search: Rethinking Meta-structure on Heterogeneous Information Networks

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2304.11574
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.11574
- **Reference count**: 40
- **Primary result**: PMMM and PCMM outperform state-of-the-art HGNNs on six medium-scale benchmark datasets and one large-scale dataset, achieving significant performance improvements in node classification and recommendation tasks.

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of searching for effective meta-structures in heterogeneous information networks (HINs) to improve the performance of heterogeneous graph neural networks (HGNNs). The authors propose a novel meta-structure called meta-multigraph and introduce two search algorithms, PMMM and PCMM, to automatically optimize meta-multigraphs for specific HINs and tasks. PMMM uses a partial message search strategy to overcome instability, while PCMM employs a progressive search algorithm to improve consistency and stability. The methods are evaluated on six medium-scale benchmark datasets and one large-scale benchmark dataset across node classification and recommendation tasks.

## Method Summary
The paper proposes a novel meta-structure called meta-multigraph for HINs, which extends traditional meta-paths and meta-graphs by allowing multiple edge types between hyper-nodes. Two search algorithms are introduced: PMMM (Partial Message Meta-Multigraph search) uses partial message sampling to stabilize differentiable search by decoupling architecture parameters, and PCMM (Progressive Complex-to-concise Meta-Multigraph search) employs a progressive search algorithm that reduces activated paths over epochs to improve consistency between the super-net and target-net. Both methods are evaluated on node classification and recommendation tasks across six medium-scale benchmark datasets and one large-scale dataset.

## Key Results
- PMMM achieves Macro-F1 scores of 64.72% on IMDB and 94.86% on DBLP, outperforming state-of-the-art HGNNs.
- PCMM achieves Macro-F1 scores of 64.72% on IMDB and 94.02% on ACM, demonstrating strong performance across different datasets.
- The complex-to-concise (C2C) meta-multigraph structure effectively reduces redundant message propagation while maintaining or improving performance.
- Both PMMM and PCMM demonstrate consistent improvements across node classification and recommendation tasks on diverse HIN datasets.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The partial message sampling in PMMM reduces coupling between architecture parameters, leading to more stable meta-structure search.
- Mechanism: By randomly selecting only a subset of message-passing types in each iteration, the architecture parameters of different paths are decoupled and trained more independently, preventing the greedy nature of differentiable search from misleading the optimization.
- Core assumption: Partial sampling ensures all candidate paths receive adequate training while reducing the coupling that causes instability.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "We propose the first stable differentiable search algorithm on HINs, called PMMM, which can consistently discover promising meta-multigraphs that outperform hand-designed meta-structures."
  - [section] "The greedy nature of the differentiable methods inevitably misleads the architecture search due to the deep coupling [28], especially when the number of candidate paths is large. It motivates us to propose a new partial message meta-multigraph (PMMM) search algorithm to achieve a stable meta-multigraph search as well as overcome the coupling in optimization."
  - [corpus] Weak corpus support - no directly related papers found discussing partial sampling in HIN NAS.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: The progressive search in PCMM improves consistency between the super-net and target-net by gradually reducing the number of activated paths.
- Mechanism: By progressively increasing the threshold for path activation during search, the algorithm implicitly narrows the search space and completes path selection during the search stage, so the activated paths at the end directly form the target-net.
- Core assumption: Reducing activated paths progressively allows the algorithm to focus on the most promising paths while maintaining stability.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "we propose a progressive search algorithm by implicitly narrowing the search space to improve search stability and reduce inconsistency."
  - [section] "To address the inconsistency issue, we need to finish the path selection at the search stage so that the activated paths at the end of the search stage directly make up the target-net in the evaluation stage."
  - [corpus] Weak corpus support - no directly related papers found discussing progressive path reduction in HIN NAS.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: The complex-to-concise (C2C) meta-multigraph structure improves performance by reducing redundant message propagation in deeper layers.
- Mechanism: By allowing more message-passing types in shallow layers for exploration and fewer in deeper layers for exploitation, the C2C structure focuses on relevant information while avoiding task-independent edge types.
- Core assumption: Shallow layers benefit from exploration of multiple message types while deeper layers benefit from focused, concise propagation.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "As the flexibility of meta-multigraphs may propagate redundant messages, we further introduce a complex-to-concise (C2C) meta-multigraph that propagates messages from complex to concise along the depth of meta-multigraph."
  - [section] "Intuitively, for a meta-structure, exploration is more important for the shallow parts while exploitation is more critical for the deep parts. So the message-passing types should be more gathered in deeper parts of the meta-multigraph to avoid task-independent edge types, where the tendency is from complex to concise."
  - [corpus] Weak corpus support - no directly related papers found discussing C2C structures in HIN NAS.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map**: Super-net with candidate message-passing paths → Architecture parameters weighting paths → Binary gates for partial message sampling → Progressive thresholding for path reduction → Downstream task modules (node classification/recommendation)
- **Critical path**: Search stage → progressive threshold adjustment → path selection → evaluation stage → retraining from scratch
- **Design tradeoffs**: More expressive meta-multigraphs vs. computational complexity; partial sampling for stability vs. completeness of training; progressive reduction for consistency vs. risk of premature elimination
- **Failure signatures**: Poor stability (high variance across random seeds), inconsistency between search and evaluation performance, redundant message propagation, or insufficient exploration of semantic relationships
- **First 3 experiments**:
  1. Run PMMM on DBLP with different sampling proportions (p=1,2,3) to observe stability improvements.
  2. Implement PCMM on IMDB to test the progressive search algorithm's consistency benefits.
  3. Compare meta-multigraph vs. C2C meta-multigraph performance on ACM to validate the complex-to-concise approach.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

The paper does not explicitly call out open questions, but several emerge from the research:

1. How do meta-multigraphs compare to other emerging meta-structure approaches (e.g., heterogeneous attention mechanisms, graph transformer networks) in terms of scalability and effectiveness across different HIN domains?

2. Can the progressive search algorithm be extended to dynamically adjust the complexity of meta-multigraphs during search based on task-specific requirements or dataset characteristics?

3. How does the partial message sampling strategy in PMMM affect the exploration of rare but potentially important semantic relationships in HINs compared to full message propagation?

4. What is the theoretical relationship between the depth of meta-multigraphs and their ability to capture long-range dependencies in HINs, and how does this affect performance on different task types?

## Limitations

- The exact implementation details of the differentiable meta-multigraph search framework, including the message-passing function and architecture parameter update rules, are not fully specified, making faithful reproduction challenging.

- The choice of hyperparameters (sampling proportion p, threshold λ, attenuation ratio β) appears dataset-dependent and may require extensive tuning, which could limit the practical applicability of the methods.

- The paper focuses primarily on medium-scale datasets, leaving questions about scalability to larger HINs with more node and edge types unanswered.

## Confidence

- **High confidence**: The core problem statement and motivation (HINs require effective meta-structures for HGNNs) is well-established and clearly articulated.
- **Medium confidence**: The proposed PMMM and PCMM algorithms are logically sound, but their stability benefits rely on assumptions about partial sampling and progressive reduction that would benefit from more extensive empirical validation.
- **Low confidence**: The C2C meta-multigraph concept, while intuitively appealing, lacks theoretical justification and corpus support for its effectiveness.
