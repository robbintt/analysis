# EconAgent: Large Language Model-Empowered Agents for Simulating Macroeconomic Activities

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2310.10436
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.10436
- Authors: 
- Reference count: 40
- One-line primary result: LLM-empowered agents produce more stable and realistic macroeconomic indicators compared to rule-based or RL baselines while reproducing key economic regularities.

## Executive Summary
EconAgent introduces a novel approach to macroeconomic simulation by integrating large language models (LLMs) into agent-based modeling. This framework overcomes traditional challenges in agent heterogeneity and macroeconomic trend modeling by embedding real-world agent profiles, dynamic memory of market trends, and flexible decision prompts. The system demonstrates superior performance in producing stable macroeconomic indicators and correctly reproducing fundamental economic relationships like the Phillips Curve and Okun's Law.

The key innovation lies in replacing rigid rule-based decision-making with LLM-powered agents that can perceive economic environments, reflect on past experiences, and make contextually appropriate decisions. This approach yields more human-like economic behavior patterns and produces simulation results that align with real-world economic observations, addressing long-standing challenges in agent-based macroeconomic modeling.

## Method Summary
EconAgent implements a three-module agent architecture (perception, memory, action) using GPT-3.5-turbo to simulate economic decision-making. The framework initializes 100 agents with U.S. 2018 demographic profiles, runs 20-year simulations incorporating labor markets, consumption, taxation, and price dynamics, and compares results against rule-based and RL baselines. Key innovations include real-world agent heterogeneity, dynamic memory of market trends, and flexible decision prompts that allow agents to consider multiple economic variables without predefined rules.

## Key Results
- EconAgent produces more stable macroeconomic indicators than rule-based or RL baselines (inflation 2-12%, unemployment 2-12%, plausible GDP)
- Correctly reproduces key economic regularities with statistical significance (Phillips Curve correlation -0.619, p<0.01; Okun's Law correlation -0.918, p<0.001)
- Agent decisions align with economic common sense and exhibit human-like decision-making patterns

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Perception (real-world profiles and environment) → Memory (quarterly reflection) → Action (decision prompting) → Market dynamics update → Repeat

- Critical path: Perception → Memory → Action → Market dynamics update → Repeat
  The agent perceives its environment, reflects on past experiences, makes decisions, and the market updates based on these decisions in each simulation step.

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Using LLMs for decision-making provides flexibility and human-like behavior but increases computational cost and potential for unpredictable outputs
  - Incorporating real-world profiles enhances realism but may limit generalizability to other economic contexts
  - Dynamic memory allows for more sophisticated decision-making but adds complexity to the simulation framework

- Failure signatures:
  - Unrealistic macroeconomic indicators (e.g., extremely high inflation or unemployment)
  - Failure to reproduce key economic regularities (Phillips Curve, Okun's Law)
  - Lack of agent heterogeneity leading to uniform decision-making
  - Inability to adapt to changing economic conditions

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Run a baseline simulation with rule-based agents and compare macroeconomic indicators to EconAgent
  2. Test the reproduction of the Phillips Curve and Okun's Law in both EconAgent and baseline simulations
  3. Analyze the impact of different agent profiles on decision-making and macroeconomic outcomes

## Limitations
- Limited evidence of performance across different economic contexts and time periods beyond U.S. 2018 data
- Computational intensity of LLM-based simulations raises scalability concerns for large-scale deployment
- Limited stress-testing under extreme economic conditions to evaluate system robustness

## Confidence

- **High Confidence:** The core claim that EconAgent produces more stable macroeconomic indicators than traditional baselines is well-supported by experimental results showing specific ranges for inflation (2-12%), unemployment (2-12%), and plausible GDP values.

- **Medium Confidence:** The reproduction of economic regularities like the Phillips Curve and Okun's Law is demonstrated through statistical correlation, but the underlying mechanism for why LLMs capture these relationships remains unclear.

- **Medium Confidence:** The claim about human-like decision-making patterns is supported by qualitative observations but lacks systematic comparison with actual human economic behavior data.
