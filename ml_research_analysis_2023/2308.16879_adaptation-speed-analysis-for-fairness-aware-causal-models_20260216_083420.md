# Adaptation Speed Analysis for Fairness-aware Causal Models

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2308.16879
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.16879

## Executive Summary
This paper analyzes the adaptation speed of causal models in the presence of sensitive variables. It considers four domain shift scenarios induced by interventions on the bias, cause, both bias and cause, or effect variable. The core method is to compare the initial parameter distances between causal and anti-causal models after each intervention. Theoretical analysis shows that the causal model always has a speed advantage when the intervention is on the cause or both bias and cause. When the intervention is on the effect, the anti-causal model is faster only under certain conditions. When the intervention is on the bias alone, both models adapt at the same speed. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets confirm these findings. The results provide guidance on which model to use depending on the type of domain shift.

## Method Summary
The method compares adaptation speeds of causal and anti-causal models under domain shifts. For synthetic data, the paper generates prior distributions p(A), p(X|A), and p(Y|A,X) from Dirichlet distributions with K categories, then creates transfer distributions by applying interventions on A, X, both A and X, or Y. ## Key Results
- Causal models adapt faster than anti-causal models when interventions are on the cause or both cause and bias variables
- Anti-causal models adapt faster under certain conditions when interventions are on the effect variable
- Both models adapt at the same speed when interventions are on the bias alone
- Experimental results on synthetic and real datasets confirm theoretical predictions

## Limitations
- Theoretical analysis assumes a specific structural causal model (SCM) with categorical variables and fully parameterized conditional distributions, limiting generalizability to continuous variables or different SCM structures
- The claim that anti-causal models adapt faster under certain effect interventions depends on specific parameter conditions that are not fully characterized
- Experimental validation is limited to synthetic data and two real datasets, which may not capture all possible domain shift scenarios

## Confidence
- High confidence: Claims about causal models adapting faster under interventions on cause or both cause and bias variables, supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental validation
- Medium confidence: Claims about anti-causal models adapting faster under specific conditions when the intervention is on the effect, as these depend on parameter conditions that require further verification
- Medium confidence: Claims about equal adaptation speeds when the intervention is on the bias alone, as this relies on the assumption that bias interventions do not affect conditional distributions
