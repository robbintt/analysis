# The Music Meta Ontology: a flexible semantic model for the interoperability of music metadata

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2311.03942
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.03942
- Reference count: 0
- Primary result: A semantic model that enables music metadata interoperability across genres and stakeholder perspectives through Information-Realisation pattern and RDF* provenance tracking

## Executive Summary
The Music Meta Ontology addresses the challenge of music metadata interoperability by providing a flexible semantic model that harmonizes requirements from diverse stakeholders including musicologists, librarians, and data engineers. The ontology uses the Information-Realisation pattern to create a lean yet expressive structure that can be specialized for different music domains while maintaining alignment with existing music ontologies. Through RDF* support, it enables fine-grained provenance tracking at the triple level, allowing metadata assertions to be attributed to specific sources. The model has been successfully adopted in real-world applications including the integration of 18 music datasets in ChoCo, and is supported by the PyMusicMeta library for automatic RDF conversion with ontology alignments.

## Method Summary
The Music Meta Ontology was developed using the eXtreme Design methodology with ontology design patterns, specifically employing the Information-Realisation pattern to model music metadata at two levels rather than the four levels of FRBR-based approaches. The ontology defines base classes for music entities including artists, compositions, performances, and recordings, with alignments to established ontologies like Music Ontology, DOREMUS, and Wikidata. RDF* is implemented for provenance tracking, embedding source attribution directly into triples. The PyMusicMeta library provides automatic conversion of music metadata to RDF triples with alignment mappings, and the model was evaluated through competency questions translated into SPARQL queries.

## Key Results
- Successfully integrated 18 music datasets in the ChoCo knowledge graph platform
- Demonstrated semantic alignment with existing music ontologies through automatic conversion rules
- Implemented fine-grained provenance tracking at the triple level using RDF* technology
- Provided PyMusicMeta library for automatic conversion and alignment of music metadata to RDF

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The Information-Realisation pattern reduces ontological complexity while maintaining semantic alignment with FRBR-based ontologies
- Mechanism: By modeling music metadata at two levels (Information Object and Realisation) rather than four FRBR levels, the ontology achieves a leaner structure that still maps to existing music ontologies through alignment properties
- Core assumption: Two levels of abstraction are sufficient to capture the essential relationships in music metadata without losing expressiveness
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "The ontology uses the Information-Realisation pattern to create a lean yet expressive model that can be specialized for different domains"
  - [section]: "An information object is a non-physical social object carrying information that can have one or multiple materialisations (information realisations)"
  - [corpus]: Weak evidence - corpus contains related ontology interoperability papers but none specifically address FRBR or Information-Realisation patterns
- Break condition: When domain-specific requirements demand more granular distinctions than the two-level abstraction can provide

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: RDF* support enables fine-grained provenance tracking at the triple level without requiring complex reification
- Mechanism: RDF* allows embedding provenance metadata directly into triples, making each assertion attributable to specific sources while maintaining simple triple structure
- Core assumption: Embedding provenance at the triple level provides sufficient granularity for music metadata integration use cases
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "The model includes provenance tracking at multiple levels through RDF* support, allowing metadata assertions to be attributed to specific sources"
  - [section]: "We propose a pattern based on RDF* [49] to describe the provenance at different levels... The use of RDF* is particularly useful for this purpose, as it allows to embed provenance information to every triple in the dataset"

- Break condition: When performance overhead of RDF* becomes prohibitive for large-scale music knowledge graphs

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Hierarchical design with conversion rules enables domain specialization while maintaining core interoperability
- Mechanism: The ontology provides base classes that can be extended for specific domains (classical, folk, contemporary) while conversion rules enable automatic mapping between levels of specificity
- Core assumption: A hierarchical structure can accommodate both general and domain-specific requirements without compromising interoperability

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How can the Music Meta Ontology be extended to handle non-Western music traditions while maintaining interoperability?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper focuses on Western music metadata but acknowledges the challenge of modeling music across different genres and periods. It mentions that the Information-Realisation pattern makes the model "leaner and flexible" while maintaining alignment with other ontologies.
- Why unresolved: The paper doesn't address whether or how the ontology can be adapted for non-Western music traditions, which may have fundamentally different conceptual frameworks and metadata requirements.
- What evidence would resolve it: Case studies or extensions of Music Meta specifically designed for non-Western music traditions (Indian classical, Arabic maqam, African polyrhythms, etc.) demonstrating successful metadata modeling and interoperability.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What is the actual performance impact of using RDF* for provenance tracking compared to traditional reification approaches in large-scale music knowledge graphs?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper proposes using RDF* for provenance tracking and claims it "simplifies and streamlines the model, eliminating the need for n-ary relations or reification for each triple." However, it doesn't discuss performance implications.
- Why unresolved: While RDF* offers modeling advantages, the paper doesn't address computational efficiency, query performance, or storage overhead compared to traditional approaches when dealing with millions of triples.
- What evidence would resolve it: Comparative benchmarks showing query response times, storage requirements, and computational overhead between RDF* implementations and traditional reification for music knowledge graphs of similar scale.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How does the Music Meta Ontology handle conflicts between different provenance sources when metadata claims contradict each other?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper mentions that provenance is modeled at different levels (claims, links) and includes confidence properties, but doesn't describe conflict resolution mechanisms when different sources provide contradictory information.
- Why unresolved: While the ontology can track provenance and confidence, it doesn't specify how to resolve contradictions between sources (e.g., different birth dates for composers, conflicting attributions, etc.).
- What evidence would resolve it: Implementation of conflict resolution algorithms or heuristics that use the provenance metadata to determine which claims to prioritize or how to present conflicting information to users.

## Limitations
