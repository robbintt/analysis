## Executive Summary

> The rapid advancement of Generative AI has created a critical gap between technological capability and existing ethical frameworks, which were originally designed for static, rule-based systems. This paper addresses the urgent need to update ethical analysis to account for the unique technological affordance of Generative AI: the user's experience of the technology **"as if it were human."**

This phenomenon complicates established concerns regarding responsibility, privacy, and alienation, necessitating a shift away from traditional AI ethics focused solely on transparency and bias toward a framework that can handle the complex socio-technical dynamics of human-machine relationships.

The key innovation of this work is the identification and definition of **"mimetic generativity"**â€”the capacity of AI systems to generate outputs so accurate to human norms that they simulate a human-like presence. Technically, the author grounds this analysis in the architectural shift from Symbolic AI and Supervised Learning to Deep Neural Networks, Self-Supervised Learning, and Transformers.

This research does not present quantitative experimental results but instead yields a comprehensive mapping of the ethical landscape. It demonstrates that Generative AI acts as a **dual-edged sword**, simultaneously aggravating and alleviating concerns across categories such as responsibility, privacy, and exploitation. Consequently, the study establishes a distinct new class of challenges stemming from mimetic generativity, specifically defining the nuances of credit attribution and the manipulative potentials inherent in "as-if" social relationships.

## Key Findings

*   **The "As-If-Human" Affordance:** Generative AI creates a distinct technological affordance where users experience the technology **"as if it were human,"** serving as a focal point for ethical analysis.
*   **Dual Impact on Ethics:** The technology has a dual impact on established ethical issues in AI, simultaneously capable of both **aggravating and alleviating** concerns regarding responsibility, privacy, bias, fairness, alienation, and exploitation.
*   **Mimetic Generativity:** This capacity gives rise to novel ethical disputes regarding **authorship, credit**, and the attribution of creative work.
*   **"As-If" Social Relationships:** The emergence of these relationships introduces new ethical vectors concerning **influence, persuasion, and manipulation**.

## Technical Details

The paper outlines the technical evolution and mechanisms enabling the shift toward "as-if-human" interaction:

*   **Evolutionary Path:**
    *   **Symbolic AI:** Relied on explicit rules.
    *   **Supervised Learning:** Utilized statistical patterns and labeled data.
    *   **Generative AI:** Focuses on learning data structure and probability distributions.
*   **Architectural Enablers:**
    *   Deep Neural Networks
    *   Self-Supervised Learning
    *   Transformers (for large-scale training)
*   **Operational Mechanism:**
    The process involves scaling models to produce **'emergent behaviors'** (such as translation and summarization) without explicit task-specific instructions.
