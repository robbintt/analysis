## Executive Summary

This paper addresses the fundamental instability and information loss in Large Language Models (LLMs) caused by quantization, specifically the **"dead zones"** inherent in standard ternary schemes. In traditional methods, weights are clamped to zero, destroying the gradient signal required for fine-tuning and rendering low-precision models unstable. This issue is critical because pretrained LLM checkpoints exhibit a high density of weights clustering near zero; when these weights fall into the dead zone, learning signals fail to propagate.

The core innovation is **Signed-Zero Ternary (SZT)**, a novel 2-bit quantization scheme that introduces a "fourth state" to preserve gradient determinism without incurring a computational penalty in the forward path. Unlike standard ternary quantization mapping weights to $\{-1, 0, 1\}$, SZT distinguishes between two types of zeros: **$0^+$ and $0^-$**.

*   **Forward Path:** A decode function collapses both signed zeros back to standard zero, ensuring compatibility with existing kernels.
*   **Backward Path:** The distinction between $0^+$ and $0^-$ is retained to provide 1 bit of gradient information, effectively eliminating dead zones.

Analytical findings demonstrate that SZT significantly reduces the expected Mean First-Passage Time (MFPT) for weights to escape the dead zone. It enables models to accommodate **twice the number of parameters** as a comparable 4-bit model within the same memory footprint. The significance lies in challenging the narrative that quantization inherently degrades quality, proposing instead that it enhances information density within fixed hardware constraints.

## Results

**Analytical Metrics**
*   State Change Probability ($\Phi_F$) is derived for Balanced Ternary, showing suppression near threshold boundaries which creates loss landscape flattening.

**Key Findings**
*   **Determinism:** SZT is fully deterministic (unlike stochastic rounding) with a fixed encoder and surrogate gradient.
*   **MFPT Reduction:** It significantly reduces the expected Mean First-Passage Time (MFPT) out of the dead zone by providing gradient feedback for sub-threshold sign flips.
*   **Parameter Efficiency:** It allows models with **twice the number of parameters** of a 4-bit model within the same memory footprint.
*   **Information Density:** Authors argue it maximizes information density.
*   **Empirical Evidence:** Histograms of pretrained LLM checkpoints indicate substantial weight density near zero.
