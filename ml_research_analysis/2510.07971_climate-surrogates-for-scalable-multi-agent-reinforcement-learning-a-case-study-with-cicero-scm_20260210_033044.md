# Climate Surrogates for Scalable Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning: A Case Study with CICERO-SCM

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.07971
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.07971
- Reference count: 40
- Replaces high-fidelity climate model with fast surrogate for scalable MARL experiments

## Executive Summary
This study presents a framework that replaces the climate module of a multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) climate policy game with a fast, learned surrogate of the high-fidelity CICERO-SCM model. The surrogate achieves near-simulator accuracy while delivering over 1000x speed improvements, enabling large-scale MARL experiments previously computationally prohibitive. The approach maintains policy consistency across both tractable and intractable scenarios, demonstrating that high-fidelity climate dynamics can be effectively emulated without sacrificing policy fidelity.

## Method Summary
The framework generates 20,000 perturbed multi-gas emission pathways and trains three RNN variants (LSTM, GRU, TCN) on 1.22 million samples to create a climate surrogate. This surrogate replaces the CICERO-SCM model in MARL training, achieving RMSE ≈ 0.0004 K and R² ≈ 0.99 accuracy while being ~1000× faster per step. The method is validated in both a tractable 4-agent homogeneous scenario and an intractable 10-agent heterogeneous scenario, demonstrating >100× end-to-end speed-ups while preserving policy consistency.

## Key Results
- Surrogate achieves near-simulator accuracy (RMSE ≈ 0.0004 K, R² ≈ 0.99)
- Delivers ~1000× speed-up per step compared to CICERO-SCM
- Maintains policy consistency in MARL training with >100× end-to-end speed-up

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The approach leverages learned surrogate models to approximate complex climate dynamics, enabling rapid simulation without the computational burden of high-fidelity models. By training on perturbed emission pathways, the surrogate captures the essential relationships between emissions and temperature responses, allowing it to maintain accuracy while dramatically reducing computation time. The RNN architectures (LSTM, GRU, TCN) are particularly suited for learning temporal climate dynamics from sequential data.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map**
Climate policy game -> Emission generator -> Surrogate climate model -> MARL agents -> Policy evaluation

**Critical path**
Emission pathway generation → Surrogate prediction → MARL agent decision → Policy evaluation

**Design tradeoffs**
Accuracy vs. speed: High-fidelity models provide accuracy but limit scalability; surrogates sacrifice minimal accuracy for massive speed gains

**Failure signatures**
Surrogate divergence from CICERO-SCM predictions indicates poor generalization; policy inconsistency between surrogate and simulator indicates surrogate inadequacy

**3 first experiments**
1. Compare surrogate vs. CICERO-SCM predictions on held-out emission pathways
2. Train MARL agents with surrogate and evaluate policy similarity to simulator-trained policies
3. Benchmark computational performance (steps/second) of surrogate vs. full model

## Limitations
- Surrogate trained on synthetic trajectories may not capture all real-world complexity or extreme events
- 1000× speed-up claim assumes specific hardware (batch processing, GPU acceleration)
- Limited evaluation of climate variables beyond temperature and CO₂
- Policy consistency contingent on specific MARL algorithms and reward structures

## Confidence
**High**: surrogate accuracy (RMSE ≈ 0.0004 K, R² ≈ 0.99) vs. CICERO-SCM, and ~1000× step-wise speed-up
**Medium**: policy consistency in MARL (4-agent homogeneous and 10-agent heterogeneous scenarios)
**Medium**: computational savings in MARL training (>100× end-to-end speed-up)

## Next Checks
1. Test surrogate robustness on real-world emission pathways and edge-case climate scenarios not present in training data
2. Evaluate surrogate performance across additional climate variables (e.g., precipitation, extreme events) and longer time horizons
3. Validate MARL policy transfer to alternative agent architectures, reward functions, and real-world decision-making contexts
