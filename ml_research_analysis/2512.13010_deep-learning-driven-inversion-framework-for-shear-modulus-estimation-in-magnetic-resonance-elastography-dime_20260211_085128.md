# Deep Learning-Driven Inversion Framework for Shear Modulus Estimation in Magnetic Resonance Elastography (DIME)

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2512.13010
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.13010
- **Reference count:** 0
- **Primary result:** Deep learning framework achieves r=0.99 correlation with ground truth on anatomy-informed simulated liver phantoms

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces DIME, a deep learning-driven inversion framework for shear modulus estimation in Magnetic Resonance Elastography (MRE). Traditional MRE inversion methods are sensitive to noise and rely on simplifying assumptions about tissue homogeneity, limiting their accuracy and clinical utility. DIME addresses these limitations by using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on synthetic displacement fields and stiffness maps generated via Finite Element Modeling (FEM), enabling direct learning of the mapping from displacement fields to shear modulus distributions.

The framework demonstrates superior performance on simulated anatomy-informed liver phantoms with near-perfect correlation to ground truth values (r=0.99, R²=0.98), outperforming traditional Multimodal Direct Inversion (MMDI) methods. While in vivo validation shows visually similar patterns to MMDI with reduced directional filtering bias, the clinical validation is limited to a small cohort of 15 subjects. The approach represents a promising advancement in MRE quantification but requires further validation across diverse tissue types and larger clinical populations.

## Method Summary
The DIME framework employs a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on synthetic displacement fields and stiffness maps generated through Finite Element Modeling (FEM). The CNN learns to map input displacement fields directly to shear modulus distributions, bypassing the need for iterative inversion algorithms used in traditional MRE methods. Training data consists of synthetic liver phantoms with varying anatomical structures and material properties, generated using FEM to simulate wave propagation and displacement patterns. The network architecture includes multiple convolutional layers with skip connections to preserve spatial information, followed by fully connected layers for output prediction. During inference, the trained model takes MRE displacement field data as input and outputs estimated shear modulus maps without requiring additional processing steps or assumptions about tissue homogeneity.

## Key Results
- DIME achieved r=0.99 correlation and R²=0.98 with ground truth on anatomy-informed simulated liver phantoms
- Superior performance compared to Multimodal Direct Inversion (MMDI) on the same simulated dataset
- In vivo validation on 15 subjects showed visually similar patterns to MMDI with reduced directional filtering artifacts

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The DIME framework works by leveraging deep learning's ability to learn complex, non-linear mappings from displacement fields to underlying tissue stiffness. Traditional MRE inversion methods rely on iterative algorithms that make simplifying assumptions about tissue homogeneity and are sensitive to noise, particularly at tissue boundaries. By training on a diverse dataset of synthetic displacement fields and corresponding stiffness maps generated through FEM, the CNN learns to capture the complex relationships between observed displacements and true tissue properties without these constraints. The architecture's use of skip connections helps preserve spatial information critical for accurate boundary detection, while the direct mapping approach eliminates the need for noise-sensitive iterative procedures.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map:** MRE displacement fields -> CNN -> Shear modulus distribution

**Critical path:** Input displacement data undergoes normalization and preprocessing, passes through convolutional layers with skip connections, processes through fully connected layers, and outputs estimated shear modulus map. The FEM-generated training data provides ground truth for supervised learning.

**Design tradeoffs:** The framework trades computational resources during training for faster inference times compared to iterative methods. Using synthetic data avoids privacy concerns and enables large-scale training but may introduce simulation biases. The CNN architecture prioritizes spatial resolution through skip connections at the cost of increased parameter count.

**Failure signatures:** Performance degradation occurs when input displacement fields contain significant noise beyond training distribution, when tissue boundaries are not well-represented in training data, or when wave propagation deviates significantly from FEM assumptions. The model may produce oversmoothed outputs in low-signal regions or amplify artifacts in high-noise regions.

**First experiments:** 1) Test network sensitivity to displacement field resolution by evaluating performance across different voxel sizes. 2) Assess training data diversity requirements by measuring performance degradation with reduced anatomical variation. 3) Validate generalization to different organs by testing on cross-tissue type datasets.

## Limitations
- Clinical validation limited to small cohort of 15 subjects, insufficient for robust statistical conclusions
- Generalizability to organs beyond liver and to different pathologies remains unproven
- Reliance on FEM-generated training data may introduce biases if simulated properties don't fully capture biological complexity

## Confidence
- **High** confidence in framework's effectiveness for anatomy-informed simulations given quantitative metrics and direct ground truth comparison
- **Medium** confidence in clinical applicability due to limited in vivo validation and small cohort size
- **Low** confidence in generalizability to other tissue types based on current evidence
