# Retrieval-Augmented Water Level Forecasting for Everglades

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2508.04888
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.04888
- **Reference count:** 27
- **Primary result:** RAF framework improves water level forecast accuracy in Everglades, particularly for extreme events and long-term predictions

## Executive Summary
This study introduces Retrieval-Augmented Forecasting (RAF) for water level prediction in the Everglades, addressing the challenge of limited context in traditional time series forecasting. RAF retrieves historically analogous multivariate hydrological episodes from an external archive to enrich the model input before forecasting. The framework explores both similarity-based and mutual information-based retrieval methods, along with three augmentation strategies. Comprehensive experiments on real-world Everglades data demonstrate that RAF substantially improves water level forecast accuracy, particularly for extreme events and long-term predictions (21–28 days).

## Method Summary
The Retrieval-Augmented Forecasting framework addresses the context limitation in traditional time series forecasting by integrating historical memory retrieval with pre-trained models. The method retrieves historically analogous multivariate hydrological episodes from an external archive and uses these to augment the model input before forecasting. The framework employs similarity-based retrieval (using Pearson correlation) and mutual information-based retrieval to identify relevant historical episodes, which are then incorporated through augmentation strategies. The approach was tested on Everglades water level forecasting data, comparing against baseline models including Chronos, GRU, and LSTNet.

## Key Results
- RAF substantially improves water level forecast accuracy compared to baselines
- For extreme events and long-term predictions (21–28 days), RAF shows particular effectiveness
- Sim-RAF and MI-RAF achieve MAE and RMSE improvements of up to 13.5% and 7.4% respectively compared to strongest baseline (Chronos)
- RAF demonstrates superior performance in extreme value forecasting as measured by SEDI metric

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
RAF works by expanding the contextual window available to forecasting models through retrieval of historically analogous episodes. Traditional time series models often struggle with long-term dependencies and extreme events due to limited historical context in their training data. By retrieving and incorporating relevant historical multivariate hydrological patterns, RAF provides the model with richer contextual information that captures complex hydrological relationships and extreme event precursors that may not be present in the immediate historical window.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Historical Archive -> Retrieval Engine -> Augmentation Module -> Forecasting Model -> Forecast Output

**Critical Path:** Data Collection → Historical Archive Construction → Retrieval Engine (Similarity/Mutual Information) → Augmentation (Data/Feature/Hybrid) → Forecasting Model → Forecast Evaluation

**Design Tradeoffs:** 
- Similarity metric choice (Pearson vs Mutual Information) balances computational efficiency vs information capture
- Augmentation strategy selection balances model complexity vs performance gain
- Archive size vs retrieval speed tradeoff affects operational feasibility
- Model architecture choice balances accuracy vs interpretability

**Failure Signatures:** 
- Poor retrieval quality leading to irrelevant historical episodes
- Overfitting to historical patterns that don't generalize
- Computational bottlenecks in retrieval operations
- Degradation in forecast accuracy for short-term predictions

## Limitations
- Effectiveness depends on availability of comprehensive historical archive of multivariate hydrological data
- Similarity metrics (Pearson correlation and mutual information) may not capture all relevant patterns in complex hydrological systems
- Results focus specifically on Everglades water level forecasting, limiting generalizability to other hydrological variables or geographic regions
- Computational overhead of retrieval operations and impact on real-time operational forecasting not evaluated
