# ReGraM: Region-First Knowledge Graph Reasoning for Medical Question Answering

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2601.09280
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.09280
- Reference count: 35
- Primary result: 8.04% accuracy gain on multiple-choice medical QA questions

## Executive Summary
ReGraM introduces a region-first approach to knowledge graph reasoning for medical question answering, addressing the challenge of semantic drift and hallucination accumulation in multi-hop biomedical reasoning. The framework constructs a query-aligned subgraph and performs stepwise reasoning constrained to this localized region, effectively bounding the reasoning space. This approach achieves significant improvements over strong baselines, demonstrating 8.04% accuracy gain on multiple-choice questions and 42.9% reduction in hallucination inconsistency scores across seven medical QA benchmarks.

## Method Summary
ReGraM employs a novel region-first strategy that constructs a query-aligned subgraph before performing any reasoning operations. The method performs stepwise reasoning constrained within this localized region, which structurally bounds the knowledge graph traversal space and prevents semantic drift. The framework integrates multi-hop decomposition to break down complex reasoning chains into manageable steps, each operating within the constrained region. This architectural choice enables more focused and factually consistent reasoning compared to traditional KG-based approaches that traverse the entire knowledge graph without early restrictions.

## Key Results
- Achieves 8.04% accuracy gain on multiple-choice medical QA questions
- Demonstrates 4.50% accuracy gain on short-answer medical QA questions
- Reduces hallucination inconsistency scores by 42.9% compared to baseline KGARevion

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The region-first approach works by creating a structurally bounded reasoning space that prevents the knowledge graph traversal from wandering into semantically irrelevant areas. By constraining reasoning to a query-aligned subgraph, ReGraM reduces the search space and eliminates potential pathways that could lead to factual inconsistencies or hallucinations. The multi-hop decomposition breaks complex reasoning chains into smaller, more manageable steps that can be validated within the constrained region, preventing error accumulation that typically occurs in long reasoning chains.

## Foundational Learning

**Knowledge Graph Traversal**: Understanding how to navigate relationships in biomedical knowledge graphs like MeSH
- Why needed: Forms the basis for extracting relevant medical concepts and relationships
- Quick check: Can identify path between two medical concepts in MeSH

**Multi-hop Reasoning**: Ability to chain multiple inference steps in knowledge graphs
- Why needed: Medical QA often requires connecting multiple pieces of biomedical knowledge
- Quick check: Can trace 3-4 step reasoning paths without losing context

**Semantic Drift**: Phenomenon where reasoning chains gradually move away from relevant concepts
- Why needed: Understanding this problem motivates the region-first approach
- Quick check: Can identify when a reasoning path has become semantically irrelevant

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Query -> Region Construction -> Subgraph Pruning -> Stepwise Reasoning -> Answer Generation
**Critical Path**: Region construction and stepwise reasoning within the constrained subgraph are the core innovations that differentiate ReGraM from baseline approaches
**Design Tradeoffs**: The region-first approach trades computational efficiency (smaller search space) for potential completeness (may miss some valid paths outside the region)
**Failure Signatures**: Performance degradation occurs when relevant knowledge lies outside the constructed region or when the region construction fails to capture query intent
**First Experiments**: 1) Test region construction with simple medical queries to verify subgraph alignment, 2) Evaluate stepwise reasoning accuracy on single-hop queries, 3) Measure hallucination reduction on controlled test cases with known factual inconsistencies

## Limitations
- Relies on a single biomedical knowledge graph (MeSH) without cross-knowledge-base validation
- May be brittle to concept distribution shifts not captured in current benchmarks
- Limited interpretability of the subgraph pruning criteria
