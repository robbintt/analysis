# Learning Concave Bid Shading Strategies in Online Auctions via Measure-valued Proximal Optimization

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.10693
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.10693
- Reference count: 36
- This paper introduces a new approach to bid shading in first-price auctions by formulating the problem as a convex optimization over the space of joint probability distributions of shading parameters.

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a novel approach to bid shading in first-price auctions that treats the problem as a convex optimization over probability distributions of shading parameters, rather than finding point estimates for predefined segments. The authors develop an entropy-regularized Wasserstein-proximal update that adapts the parameter distribution based on observed auction outcomes. The method demonstrates effectiveness in tracking budget constraints, adapting to seasonal patterns, and self-correcting after abrupt budget changes in simulated campaign settings.

## Method Summary
The paper introduces a measure-valued optimization approach for bid shading in first-price auctions. The key innovation is an entropy-regularized Wasserstein-proximal update that adapts the distribution of shading parameters based on observed auction outcomes. This update shifts probability mass toward parameter values with higher expected surplus, where both win probability and value gap are large. The authors derive a closed-form solution for this update, making it practical for online implementation. The approach models the parameter distribution as a joint probability distribution that can adapt to changing market conditions while following budget constraints.

## Key Results
- The proposed method effectively tracks non-stationary budget targets and adapts to changing market conditions
- The approach demonstrates self-correcting behavior after abrupt budget changes in simulated 408-hour campaign experiments
- The method successfully follows seasonal patterns while maintaining budget constraints
- Closed-form solution for the entropy-regularized Wasserstein-proximal update enables practical online implementation

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The approach works by optimizing over the space of joint probability distributions of shading parameters rather than finding point estimates. The entropy-regularized Wasserstein-proximal update mechanism shifts probability mass toward parameter values with higher expected surplus, where both win probability and value gap are large. This distribution-based approach allows for more robust adaptation to changing market conditions compared to traditional point-estimate methods.

## Foundational Learning
- **First-price auction mechanism**: Understanding how bidding works in first-price auctions is essential, as the shading strategy directly impacts win probability and expected surplus
- **Wasserstein distance**: This metric measures the distance between probability distributions and is crucial for the proximal update mechanism
- **Entropy regularization**: Helps smooth the optimization process and prevent overfitting to specific auction outcomes
- **Convex optimization over measures**: The mathematical framework that enables treating bid shading as a distribution optimization problem
- **Measure-valued optimization**: Allows the method to adapt to non-stationary environments by optimizing over entire distributions rather than point estimates
- **Budget pacing**: The constraint that the method must satisfy while optimizing for surplus

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Bid shading distribution -> Auction outcome observation -> Wasserstein-proximal update -> New shading distribution

Critical path: The core computation involves the entropy-regularized Wasserstein-proximal update that shifts probability mass based on observed auction outcomes. This update requires computing expected surplus for different parameter values and applying the closed-form solution derived by the authors.

Design tradeoffs: The method trades computational complexity for adaptability by optimizing over entire distributions rather than point estimates. This allows better handling of non-stationary environments but requires more sophisticated mathematical machinery.

Failure signatures: The method may fail if the assumed parametric form for the log-shift function Ψ is misspecified, leading to bias in the optimal distribution. Performance may degrade in highly competitive or rapidly changing auction environments where the distribution cannot adapt quickly enough.

Three first experiments:
1. Test the closed-form solution computation on synthetic data with known optimal distributions

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
The paper acknowledges that non-parametric forms might be more appropriate for complex, non-stationary environments, though it currently assumes a parametric form for the log-shift function Ψ. The performance under different market conditions, particularly in highly competitive or rapidly changing auction environments, remains an open question requiring further empirical validation.

## Limitations
- The method assumes a parametric form for the log-shift function Ψ, which may introduce bias if the true optimal distribution deviates significantly from the assumed family
- Performance in highly competitive or rapidly changing auction environments is not fully validated
- The approach's computational efficiency and scalability for large-scale, high-frequency auctions typical in modern RTB systems require further evaluation
