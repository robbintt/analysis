# Ontology-based knowledge representation for bone disease diagnosis: a foundation for safe and sustainable medical artificial intelligence systems

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2506.04756
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.04756

## Executive Summary
This study proposes an ontology-based framework for bone disease diagnosis that aims to bridge structured clinical knowledge with artificial intelligence systems. The framework introduces three theoretical contributions: a hierarchical neural network architecture guided by bone disease ontology for segmentation-classification tasks, an ontology-enhanced Visual Question Answering (VQA) system for clinical reasoning, and a multimodal deep learning model integrating imaging, clinical, and laboratory data through ontological relationships. The methodology emphasizes clinical interpretability through systematic knowledge digitization, standardized medical terminology mapping, and modular architecture design. While the theoretical foundations are well-established, experimental validation remains pending due to current dataset and computational resource limitations.

## Method Summary
The proposed framework integrates ontology-based knowledge representation with deep learning architectures for bone disease diagnosis. The approach employs systematic knowledge digitization to convert clinical expertise into structured ontological representations, which guide the design of neural network architectures and enhance clinical reasoning capabilities. The methodology includes three main components: (1) a hierarchical neural network architecture for segmentation-classification tasks that incorporates Visual Language Models (VLMs), (2) an ontology-enhanced VQA system for clinical decision support, and (3) a multimodal deep learning model that integrates imaging, clinical, and laboratory data through ontological relationships. The framework maintains clinical interpretability through standardized medical terminology mapping and modular design principles, allowing for potential extension beyond bone diseases through its standardized structure.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by systematically encoding clinical knowledge into structured ontological representations that guide AI system design and enhance interpretability. The hierarchical neural network architecture leverages ontological relationships to improve segmentation and classification accuracy by incorporating domain-specific knowledge directly into the model structure. The VQA system enhances clinical reasoning by grounding visual understanding in formalized medical concepts and relationships, enabling more accurate and interpretable clinical decision support. The multimodal integration approach combines diverse data sources through ontological relationships, creating a unified representation that captures the complex interdependencies between imaging findings, clinical symptoms, and laboratory results in bone disease diagnosis.

**Critical Path**: Ontology Construction → Neural Network Architecture Design → Multimodal Integration
The critical path follows knowledge representation to model design to data integration, ensuring that ontological guidance is maintained throughout the AI system development.

**Design Tradeoffs**: 
- Complexity vs. Interpretability: More complex ontological structures provide richer guidance but reduce model transparency
- Computational Efficiency vs. Clinical Accuracy: Deeper hierarchical architectures may improve accuracy but increase resource requirements
- Generalization vs. Specialization: More specific ontological knowledge improves domain performance but reduces transferability

**Failure Signatures**: 
- Inconsistent ontological reasoning leading to contradictory clinical recommendations
- Over-reliance on visual features without proper clinical context integration
- Computational bottlenecks preventing real-time clinical deployment
- Poor generalization when extending beyond training domain
