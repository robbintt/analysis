# Robust Adverse Weather Removal via Spectral-based Spatial Grouping
## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2507.22498
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.22498
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Novel transformer-based SSGformer architecture achieves state-of-the-art performance in removing rain, snow, and raindrop degradations through spectral decomposition and group-wise attention

## Executive Summary
SSGformer introduces a transformer-based architecture that addresses the limitations of global filtering approaches for adverse weather removal. The method leverages spectral decomposition to separate edge and low-frequency features, then fuses them using multi-head linear attention. A novel grouping-mask clusters regions based on spatial similarity and texture, enabling group-wise attention mechanisms that operate both spatially and across channels. Extensive experiments demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing methods across multiple weather conditions.

## Method Summary
The paper proposes a spectral-based spatial grouping approach that combines edge-preserving techniques with group-wise attention mechanisms. By extracting high-frequency edge features through the Sobel operator and low-frequency information via Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), the method creates a more robust feature representation for weather degradation removal. The grouping-mask, generated through clustering based on spatial similarity and texture, enables targeted attention mechanisms that preserve important structural details while removing weather artifacts.

## Key Results
- Achieves state-of-the-art PSNR and SSIM metrics across rain, snow, and raindrop removal tasks
- Demonstrates superior performance on both synthetic and real-world adverse weather datasets
- Significantly outperforms existing methods in terms of visual quality and quantitative metrics

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The effectiveness stems from combining spectral decomposition with spatial grouping. By separating edge and low-frequency features, the method preserves important structural information while targeting weather artifacts specifically. The group-wise attention mechanism allows for localized processing that adapts to different weather patterns and textures within the same image, addressing the limitations of global filtering approaches that may blur important details.

## Foundational Learning
- Spectral decomposition (why needed: separates edge from low-frequency information; quick check: verify SVD and Sobel outputs preserve key image features)
- Group-wise attention mechanisms (why needed: enables localized processing for diverse weather patterns; quick check: confirm grouping-mask creates meaningful spatial clusters)
- Multi-head linear attention (why needed: efficient feature fusion across multiple scales; quick check: validate attention weights correspond to relevant weather features)

## Architecture Onboarding
Component map: Input Image -> Spectral Decomposition (Sobel + SVD) -> Feature Fusion -> Grouping-Mask Generation -> Group-wise Attention -> Output
Critical path: The spectral decomposition and grouping-mask generation stages are critical, as they directly impact the quality of the group-wise attention mechanism and final output.
Design tradeoffs: Balances computational efficiency with restoration quality by using linear attention instead of standard self-attention, while maintaining effectiveness through spectral decomposition.
Failure signatures: Poor performance on extreme weather conditions or when weather patterns don't align with grouping-mask clusters; potential blurring of fine details in complex textures.
First experiments:
1. Test spectral decomposition on synthetic weather patterns to verify edge preservation
2. Validate grouping-mask clustering on images with varying weather distributions
3. Evaluate group-wise attention performance on simple rain removal before scaling to complex conditions
