# SEA: Spectral Edge Attack on Graph Neural Networks

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2512.08964
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.08964
- **Authors**: Yongyu Wang
- **Reference count**: 19
- **Primary result**: SEA attack causes 1.23-1.04-0.62% accuracy drops on Cora dataset across GCN, GAT, and GraphSAGE models

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces SEA (Spectral Edge Attack), a novel attack method targeting graph neural networks by exploiting spectral properties of graph structures. Unlike traditional attacks that modify graph topology through edge additions or deletions, SEA focuses on subtly adjusting edge weights of the most vulnerable edges identified through spectral adversarial robustness evaluation. The method constructs a k-NN graph from latent representations learned by the GNN and uses spectral embedding to rank edge vulnerabilities based on their Spade scores. Experiments demonstrate SEA's effectiveness at degrading GNN performance while maintaining stealth through preservation of graph connectivity.

## Method Summary
SEA operates by first obtaining latent representations from a trained GNN, then constructing a k-NN graph based on these representations. The method uses spectral embedding techniques to identify edges with high vulnerability scores (Spade scores), which are then subtly reweighted to maximize adversarial impact. The attack preserves graph connectivity while causing significant accuracy degradation, distinguishing it from more detectable topological modifications. The approach leverages spectral properties to identify critical edges without requiring extensive modifications to the graph structure.

## Key Results
- SEA causes accuracy drops of 1.23% on GCN, 1.04% on GAT, and 0.62% on GraphSAGE models using Cora dataset
- SEA outperforms random edge reweighting attacks in effectiveness
- The attack maintains stealth by preserving graph connectivity while achieving significant performance degradation

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
SEA exploits the spectral properties of graph structures to identify vulnerabilities that are not apparent through topological analysis alone. By analyzing latent representations through spectral embedding, the method can pinpoint edges whose weight modifications will have the most significant impact on model performance. The k-NN graph construction from latent representations provides a more meaningful basis for identifying vulnerable edges compared to random selection or purely topological approaches.

## Foundational Learning
- **Spectral Graph Theory**: Understanding eigenvalues and eigenvectors of graph adjacency/Laplacian matrices - needed for analyzing graph structure properties and vulnerabilities
- **k-NN Graph Construction**: Creating neighborhood graphs from high-dimensional data - essential for identifying local graph structures
- **Latent Representations**: Hidden layer outputs of neural networks that capture semantic information - used as basis for vulnerability analysis
- **Adversarial Robustness**: Measures of model resistance to adversarial examples - central to evaluating attack effectiveness
- **Spade Scores**: Spectral-based vulnerability metrics for edges - the core metric for identifying attack targets
- **Graph Neural Networks**: Neural networks designed for graph-structured data - the target of the attack

## Architecture Onboarding
- **Component Map**: Latent Representations -> k-NN Graph Construction -> Spectral Embedding -> Spade Score Calculation -> Edge Weight Modification
- **Critical Path**: The attack flow from obtaining latent representations through to edge modification represents the core pipeline
- **Design Tradeoffs**: Stealth (preserving connectivity) vs. attack effectiveness (identifying truly vulnerable edges)
- **Failure Signatures**: If the attack fails to degrade accuracy, it may indicate the model has strong spectral robustness or the latent representations don't capture vulnerability-relevant information
- **First Experiments**: 1) Test on synthetic graphs with known vulnerabilities, 2) Compare Spade scores against random edge selection, 3) Evaluate detection resistance through statistical analysis of edge weight distributions

## Limitations
- Scalability concerns for extremely large graphs due to computational complexity of spectral embedding
- Reliance on access to model's latent representations limits applicability in black-box scenarios
- Potential detectability through statistical analysis of edge weight distributions despite connectivity preservation

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: Attack methodology and experimental results on Cora dataset
- **Medium confidence**: Generalizability to larger datasets and different GNN architectures
- **Medium confidence**: Stealthiness claims under various detection methods
