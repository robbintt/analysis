# CDER: Collaborative Evidence Retrieval for Document-level Relation Extraction

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2504.06529
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.06529
- Authors: Khai Phan Tran; Xue Li
- Reference count: 26
- Key outcome: CDER achieves 84.75% Evi F1 score on DocRED, improving overall DocRE performance

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of evidence retrieval in document-level relation extraction (DocRE) by proposing CDER, a collaborative evidence retrieval framework. Traditional approaches retrieve evidence sentences independently for each entity pair, missing the collaborative relationships among semantically similar entity pairs within documents. CDER explicitly models these collaborative patterns through a bipartite graph architecture with entity pair and sentence nodes, organized into three sub-graphs: Entity Pair, Entity Pair-Sentence, and Sentence.

The framework employs Entity Pair-aware Graph Attention Networks (EP-GAT) to capture collaborative patterns and refine representations of both entity pairs and sentences. A dynamic sub-structure further enhances robustness by removing edges between irrelevant entity pairs based on relational similarity. Experimental results on the DocRED dataset demonstrate that CDER significantly outperforms existing evidence retrieval systems, achieving an Evi F1 score of 84.75% and corresponding improvements in overall DocRE performance.

## Method Summary
CDER introduces a collaborative evidence retrieval framework that addresses the limitations of independent retrieval methods in document-level relation extraction. The approach constructs a bipartite graph with entity pair and sentence nodes, divided into three sub-graphs: Entity Pair, Entity Pair-Sentence, and Sentence. This architecture enables the model to capture collaborative patterns among semantically similar entity pairs within the same document. The framework employs Entity Pair-aware Graph Attention Networks (EP-GAT) to refine entity pair and sentence representations while considering their collaborative relationships. Additionally, a dynamic sub-structure mechanism identifies and removes edges between irrelevant entity pairs based on relational similarity, enhancing the robustness of evidence retrieval. The model is evaluated on the DocRED dataset, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing evidence retrieval systems.

## Key Results
- CDER achieves an Evi F1 score of 84.75% on the DocRED dataset
- The collaborative evidence retrieval approach outperforms independent retrieval methods
- Improved evidence retrieval translates to enhanced overall DocRE task performance

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The collaborative approach works by recognizing that semantically similar entity pairs within a document often share evidence patterns. By modeling the relationships between entity pairs through a bipartite graph structure, CDER can leverage information from related entity pairs to improve evidence identification. The Entity Pair-aware Graph Attention Networks (EP-GAT) mechanism allows the model to dynamically weigh the importance of collaborative signals while refining representations. The dynamic sub-structure further enhances performance by filtering out irrelevant relationships between entity pairs based on relational similarity, reducing noise in the collaborative learning process.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:**
Entity Pair Nodes <-> Entity Pair-Sentence Edges <-> Sentence Nodes
Entity Pair Nodes <-> Entity Pair Edges <-> Entity Pair Nodes

**Critical Path:**
1. Input document with entity pairs and sentences
2. Bipartite graph construction (Entity Pair, Entity Pair-Sentence, Sentence sub-graphs)
3. EP-GAT processing for representation refinement
4. Dynamic sub-structure for edge filtering
5. Evidence sentence prediction

**Design Tradeoffs:**
The bipartite graph approach trades computational complexity for improved collaborative modeling. Alternative designs might use simpler attention mechanisms but would miss the rich relational structure. The three-sub-graph division balances granularity with computational efficiency.

**Failure Signatures:**
- Poor performance on documents with sparse entity relationships
- Over-reliance on collaborative signals leading to incorrect evidence identification
- Computational bottlenecks with large numbers of entity pairs
- Sensitivity to noise in the dynamic sub-structure filtering

**3 First Experiments:**
1. Compare CDER performance with and without the dynamic sub-structure component on a validation set
2. Test the impact of different graph attention mechanisms on evidence retrieval accuracy
3. Evaluate performance on documents with varying numbers of entity pairs to assess scalability

## Limitations
- Evaluation primarily on DocRED dataset limits generalizability to other domains
- Computational complexity not thoroughly analyzed for large-scale applications
- Dynamic sub-structure contribution not isolated through detailed ablation studies
- Performance on documents with different structures not extensively explored

## Confidence

**High confidence:** The claim that collaborative evidence retrieval improves over independent retrieval methods is well-supported by experimental results showing improved Evi F1 scores and overall DocRE performance.

**Medium confidence:** The assertion that the bipartite graph construction with three sub-graphs is essential for capturing collaborative patterns among entity pairs. While results demonstrate effectiveness, alternative architectures could potentially achieve similar improvements.

**Low confidence:** The claim that the dynamic sub-structure specifically for removing edges between irrelevant entity pairs is a critical component for robust evidence retrieval. The paper presents this as important but lacks sufficient ablation evidence to isolate its individual contribution.

## Next Checks
