## Executive Summary
This work introduces OSIRIS, a unified Bayesian framework that simultaneously addresses three major challenges in multimodal clinical data: structured missingness, limited sample sizes, and the need for interpretability. OSIRIS integrates generative and discriminative latent spaces within a single architecture: a generative space enables missing-data imputation and semi-supervised learning, while a discriminative space is optimized for task-specific classification. Sparsity-inducing priors allow automatic feature selection, enhancing interpretability. The model uses mean-field variational inference to approximate posterior distributions, and is validated on diverse multimodal datasets, achieving state-of-the-art classification performance and robust imputation accuracy. In a real clinical application on Alzheimer's disease data, OSIRIS successfully identified stable discriminative and generative factors linked to relevant biomarkers, demonstrating its capacity to disentangle complex multimodal relationships while providing interpretable insights. OSIRIS thus offers a principled, interpretable, and scalable solution for multimodal clinical data analysis.

## Method Summary
OSIRIS is a Bayesian framework that unifies generative and discriminative learning for multimodal clinical data with missingness. The model learns two distinct latent spaces: a generative space for data imputation and semi-supervised learning, and a discriminative space for classification. Sparsity-inducing priors enable automatic feature selection for interpretability. Mean-field variational inference is used to approximate posterior distributions, allowing efficient optimization. The framework is validated on multiple multimodal datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art classification performance and robust imputation accuracy. In a clinical application on Alzheimer's disease, OSIRIS identified interpretable discriminative factors linked to relevant biomarkers.

## Key Results
- Achieved state-of-the-art classification performance on multimodal datasets
- Demonstrated robust imputation accuracy for structured missing data
- Identified interpretable discriminative factors in Alzheimer's disease application, linking to relevant biomarkers

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
OSIRIS works by integrating generative and discriminative learning within a unified Bayesian framework. The generative component handles missing data imputation and semi-supervised learning through learned latent representations, while the discriminative component optimizes for task-specific classification. Sparsity-inducing priors automatically select relevant features, enhancing interpretability. Mean-field variational inference enables efficient posterior approximation, making the model scalable. By jointly optimizing both spaces, OSIRIS captures complex multimodal relationships while providing interpretable insights through identified discriminative factors.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**
OSIRIS -> Generative Space -> Latent Representation -> Imputation + Semi-supervised Learning
                \-> Discriminative Space -> Classification + Feature Selection

**Critical Path**
Data -> Multimodal Encoder -> Generative Latent Space + Discriminative Latent Space -> Variational Inference -> Imputation/Classification/Interpretation

**Design Tradeoffs**
- Generative vs. discriminative focus: Joint optimization balances reconstruction accuracy with classification performance
- Sparsity vs. completeness: L1 priors enable interpretability but may miss subtle associations
- Variational approximation: Mean-field assumption enables scalability but may oversimplify posterior dependencies

**Failure Signatures**
- Poor imputation quality indicates insufficient generative capacity or inappropriate missingness assumptions
- Low classification accuracy suggests inadequate discriminative feature learning or suboptimal latent space alignment
- Uninterpretable latent factors indicate need for stronger sparsity priors or alternative feature selection methods

## Limitations
- Performance gains demonstrated on relatively small datasets (hundreds of samples), raising scalability questions
- Mean-field variational approximation may oversimplify posterior dependencies, limiting capture of complex multimodal interactions
- Interpretability claims rely on post-hoc analysis requiring domain expert validation
- Computational complexity may challenge real-time clinical deployment
- Assumes structured missingness patterns that may not generalize to all clinical scenarios
