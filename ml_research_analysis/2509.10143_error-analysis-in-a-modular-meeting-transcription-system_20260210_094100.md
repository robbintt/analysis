# Error Analysis in a Modular Meeting Transcription System

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.10143
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.10143
- Reference count: 28
- Primary result: Leakage from primary speaker to cross channel in CSS-AD pipeline has minimal impact on final WER because VAD largely ignores leaked regions; missing speech segments are the dominant error source

## Executive Summary
This paper analyzes error sources in a modular meeting transcription system combining continuous speech separation (CSS), energy-based voice activity detection (VAD), ASR, and diarization. The key finding is that while significant cross-channel leakage occurs when only the primary speaker is active, this leakage does not substantially degrade performance because the dual-channel VAD effectively filters out these leaked regions. The analysis reveals that missing speech segments (VAD false negatives) are the primary contributor to the performance gap versus oracle segmentation, while advanced ASR-supported diarization can reduce this gap by approximately one-third compared to simple energy-based VAD alone.

## Method Summary
The system uses TF-GridNet continuous speech separation with 4-second windows and 3-second shifts to produce two overlap-free channels. An energy-based VAD operates on both channels using energy ratios to identify speech segments. A hybrid HMM ASR with 12 conformer blocks (87M parameters) processes these segments, with sMBR fine-tuning. Diarization uses word-level timestamps from the first ASR pass to detect speaker changes, extracting speaker embeddings from 3-second context windows and clustering with k-means. The leakage analysis framework aligns clean LibriSpeech forced alignments to LibriCSS utterances and computes coincidence rates (CR) between separated channels to quantify frame-level word matches.

## Key Results
- Cross-channel leakage in single-speaker regions (8.5% CR) has minimal impact on final WER because VAD filters leaked regions
- Missing speech segments (VAD false negatives) are the dominant error source, accounting for >50% of the oracle gap
- ASR-supported diarization reduces the gap to oracle segmentation by approximately one-third compared to energy-based VAD alone
- System achieves 4.3% cpWER on LibriCSS using only LibriSpeech training data with transformer language model and refined diarization

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Primary-to-cross channel leakage does not substantially degrade final ASR performance despite measurable significance
- Mechanism: Dual-channel energy-based VAD uses energy ratios across both separated channels to identify active speech; leaked regions in the cross channel typically lack sufficient energy to trigger segment creation
- Core assumption: VAD robustness to separation artifacts; leaked audio has lower energy than true speech regions in the primary channel
- Evidence: 8.5% cross-channel leakage CR but minimal WER impact (Table 4 shows eliminating leaks yields negligible improvement); dual-channel VAD more robust than single-channel approach

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: ASR-supported diarization closes approximately one-third of the performance gap between simple VAD and oracle segmentation
- Mechanism: Word-level timestamps from first ASR pass enable speaker change detection within VAD-defined segments; speaker embeddings from 3-second context windows around word boundaries are compared, with low similarity triggering segment splits
- Core assumption: ASR provides accurate word boundaries; speaker embeddings are discriminative for speaker identity
- Evidence: ORC WER improves from 5.6% (VAD only) to 5.2% (CSS-AD); gap to oracle (4.3%) reduced by ~1/3

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Missing speech segments are the dominant contributor to remaining performance gap versus oracle segmentation
- Mechanism: VAD occasionally fails to detect speech regions entirely, causing content loss that cannot be recovered downstream
- Core assumption: VAD thresholds optimized for precision sacrifice recall; missing speech is more costly than spurious segments
- Evidence: Eliminating "missing" errors improves ORC WER by 0.5-0.7% (largest single improvement in Table 4); "leaks," "merges," and "boundaries" yield negligible improvement

## Foundational Learning

- Concept: Voice Activity Detection (VAD)
  - Why needed: VAD is the segmentation bottleneck; understanding energy-based vs. ASR-informed VAD tradeoffs is essential for interpreting diarization improvements and error analysis
  - Quick check: Given two separated channels with potential leakage, why would dual-channel energy ratio be more robust than single-channel thresholding?

- Concept: Coincidence Rate (CR) and Graph Coincidence Rate (GCR)
  - Why needed: Leakage analysis framework uses CR/GCR to quantify frame-level word matches across channels; these metrics reveal leakage extent without conflating effects via aggregate WER
  - Quick check: Why is GCR (lattice-based) considered "optimistic" compared to word CR (1-best hypothesis)?

- Concept: ORC WER vs. cpWER
  - Why needed: Paper uses ORC WER to isolate segmentation effects from diarization errors, while cpWER evaluates full system performance including speaker labeling
  - Quick check: If system transcribes all words correctly but assigns every segment to wrong speaker, how would ORC WER and cpWER differ?

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Single-channel audio input → Continuous Speech Separation (CSS) → Two separated channels → VAD Segmentation → ASR (first pass) → Diarization → Refined Segmentation → ASR (second pass) → Final transcription with speaker labels

- Critical path: VAD → First ASR → Diarization → Refined ASR. Errors in VAD propagate directly; missing segments are unrecoverable downstream

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Energy-based VAD is simple and fast but sacrifices recall (missing segments); ASR-supported diarization adds compute but recovers ~1/3 of oracle gap
  - Hybrid HMM ASR provides accurate word-level timestamps (enabling diarization) but requires more engineering than end-to-end models
  - Training ASR only on LibriSpeech (no domain adaptation) yields competitive results but may not generalize to unconstrained meetings

- Failure signatures:
  - High WER with low leakage CR: suspect VAD missing segments or ASR errors, not separation leakage
  - Significant gap between ORC WER and cpWER: diarization speaker attribution errors
  - Leakage CR elevated but WER unchanged: VAD successfully filtering leaked regions (expected behavior)

- First 3 experiments:
  1. VAD recall ablation: Lower VAD energy threshold incrementally and measure ORC WER vs. cpWER; identify point where missing speech errors are minimized without introducing excessive false positives
  2. Leakage tolerance test: Synthetically inject controlled leakage into cross channel (varying energy levels) and measure when VAD begins to segment leaked regions; quantify robustness margin
  3. Diarization-only vs. full pipeline: Run diarization on oracle segmentation (skip VAD) to isolate diarization's speaker labeling contribution from its segmentation refinement effect; compare to Table 2 results

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

- Future work might therefore concern improved VAD to address these errors. (Section 6)
- The analysis shows that leakage artifacts do not substantially degrade performance, but this finding may not generalize to end-to-end systems that process audio directly without explicit separation or VAD modules

## Limitations

- Leakage analysis framework relies on strong assumptions about forced alignment quality between clean and overlapped speech
- Energy-based VAD parameters (thresholds, ratio computation) are not fully specified, making exact reproduction challenging
- ASR-supported diarization assumes word-level timestamps are sufficiently accurate for speaker change detection, which may not hold in noisy or overlapping speech conditions
- Generalization to meeting domains outside LibriCSS (different acoustic conditions, languages) is untested

## Confidence

- High confidence: Missing speech segments are the dominant error source (well-supported by quantitative leakage analysis showing minimal WER improvement when eliminating leakage artifacts)
- Medium confidence: Dual-channel VAD robustness preventing leakage transcription is theoretically sound but lacks direct ablation studies isolating VAD vs. leakage effects
- Low confidence: Claims about relative error contributions beyond missing segments (leaks, merges, boundaries) are based on oracle heuristics that may not reflect realistic conditions

## Next Checks

1. Perform ablation study varying VAD energy thresholds to quantify tradeoff between missing speech segments and spurious leakage transcription, measuring exact point where leaked regions begin appearing in final ASR output

2. Run diarization independently on oracle segmentation to isolate speaker labeling accuracy from segmentation refinement effects, comparing to current results where diarization must first correct VAD errors

3. Test leakage analysis framework on synthetic mixtures with controlled leakage energy levels to validate framework's sensitivity and determine minimum leakage threshold that VAD can reliably filter out
