# PID-Guided Partial Alignment for Multimodal Decentralized Federated Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2601.10012
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10012
- Authors: Yanhang Shi; Xiaoyu Wang; Houwei Cao; Jian Li; Yong Liu
- Reference count: 30
- Primary result: Feature fission approach prevents gradient conflicts in multimodal decentralized federated learning without central coordination

## Executive Summary
PARSE introduces a novel approach to multimodal decentralized federated learning where agents with different modalities and architectures collaborate over peer-to-peer networks without central coordination. The framework employs feature fission to decompose latent representations into redundant, unique, and synergistic slices based on partial information decomposition. This allows agents to share only semantically alignable knowledge slices through P2P communication, preventing gradient conflicts while maintaining collaborative learning capabilities.

## Method Summary
The PARSE framework addresses the challenge of multimodal decentralized federated learning by implementing a feature fission mechanism that decomposes each agent's latent representation into three distinct slices: redundant (shared across modalities), unique (modality-specific), and synergistic (emerges only from multimodal observation). P2P knowledge sharing occurs at the slice level, where only semantically alignable branches are exchanged between agents with corresponding modalities. This design prevents gradient conflicts without requiring server orchestration or gradient surgery. The approach maintains compatibility with standard DFL constraints while enabling effective collaboration among heterogeneous agents.

## Key Results
- PARSE consistently outperforms task-, modality-, and hybrid-sharing baselines by 0.3-2.8% accuracy
- Benefits both unimodal and multimodal agents across various agent ratios
- Demonstrates effectiveness across four benchmarks (KU-HAR, ModelNet-40, AVE, IEMOCAP)
- Maintains compatibility with standard DFL constraints while preventing gradient conflicts

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The feature fission mechanism works by decomposing latent representations into three slices based on partial information decomposition principles. The redundant slice captures information shared across modalities, enabling cross-modal collaboration. The unique slice preserves modality-specific knowledge that cannot be transferred, while the synergistic slice captures information that emerges only from multimodal observation. By sharing only alignable slices between corresponding modalities, PARSE prevents gradient conflicts that typically arise from incompatible feature spaces in heterogeneous agent networks.

## Architecture Onboarding
**Component Map:** Input Modalities -> Feature Fission -> Slice Decomposition (Redundant/Unique/Synergistic) -> P2P Slice Sharing -> Model Update

**Critical Path:** Raw multimodal inputs → Feature extractor → Partial information decomposition → Slice alignment → P2P exchange → Gradient aggregation → Model update

**Design Tradeoffs:** The framework trades some potential information transfer (by not sharing all slices) for gradient stability and compatibility. This prevents catastrophic forgetting and gradient conflicts at the cost of potentially slower convergence compared to unrestricted sharing.

**Failure Signatures:** Gradient explosion or vanishing, poor convergence, or accuracy degradation indicate slice misalignment or inappropriate sharing patterns. Monitoring slice-wise gradient norms can identify these issues.

**First Experiments:**

3. Evaluate gradient stability under various network topologies and agent ratios

## Limitations
- Evaluation focuses primarily on classification tasks, leaving scalability to complex multimodal tasks uncertain
- P2P topology assumptions may not fully capture real-world network dynamics including delays and packet loss
- Partial information decomposition relies on feature independence assumptions that may not hold in all scenarios

## Confidence
- Performance claims (0.3-2.8% accuracy improvements): High - Multiple benchmarks with statistical comparisons against established baselines
- Gradient conflict prevention mechanism: Medium - Theoretical soundness demonstrated, but empirical validation of conflict avoidance in extreme scenarios needed
- Scalability to larger agent networks: Low - Current evaluations limited to specific agent ratios without stress testing at scale

## Next Checks
1. Stress test the feature fission mechanism under extreme non-IID conditions where modality distributions are highly skewed across agents
2. Evaluate robustness to communication failures and delays in the P2P topology to assess real-world deployment viability
3. Extend experiments to multimodal tasks beyond classification, particularly generation or continuous control, to validate general applicability
