# Quantum Properties Trojans (QuPTs) for Attacking Quantum Neural Networks

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2507.08202
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.08202
- **Reference count**: 11
- **Primary result**: First comprehensive study of Trojan attacks on fully quantum neural networks (QNNs) using quantum properties

## Executive Summary
This work introduces Quantum Properties Trojans (QuPTs), the first comprehensive study of Trojan attacks targeting fully quantum neural networks without classical-quantum hybrid architectures. The authors exploit fundamental quantum computing properties—unitary transformations for noise injection and Hadamard gates for interference-based state manipulation—to create three distinct Trojan attack classes that can severely compromise QNN performance. Tested on a binary MNIST classifier, the attacks demonstrate dramatic accuracy degradation, reducing performance from 70% to 50% in ideal simulations and from 65% to 50% in noisy IonQ Aria-1 simulations, while completely destroying precision, recall, and F1-scores.

The study establishes a novel threat model based on configuration file manipulation in quantum compilation pipelines, highlighting critical vulnerabilities that emerge as quantum machine learning systems become more prevalent. By demonstrating that fundamental quantum properties can be weaponized to create effective Trojan attacks, the research reveals that QNNs face unique security challenges distinct from classical neural networks, necessitating new defensive strategies specific to quantum computing architectures.

## Method Summary
The authors present three classes of Quantum Properties Trojans (QuPTs) that exploit fundamental quantum computing properties to attack fully quantum neural networks. Class-A QuPTs use repeated unitary operations to accumulate noise in quantum states, Class-B introduces controlled triggering via ancilla qubits for targeted attacks, and Class-C leverages Hadamard-induced interference to maximally distort quantum states. The attacks are tested on a quantum neural network binary classifier for MNIST digits 0 and 1, demonstrating severe performance degradation across both ideal and noisy simulation environments using IonQ Aria-1 hardware. The threat model assumes vulnerability in quantum compilation configuration files, allowing malicious manipulation of the QNN's operational parameters.

## Key Results
- Class-C QuPTs reduce QNN accuracy from 70% to 50% in ideal simulations (28.57% drop) and from 65% to 50% in noisy IonQ Aria-1 simulations (23.08% drop)
- All three QuPT classes completely destroy precision, recall, and F1-score metrics in both ideal and noisy simulation environments
- The attacks demonstrate that fundamental quantum properties can be weaponized to create effective Trojan attacks without requiring classical-quantum hybrid architectures

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The attacks exploit quantum computing's fundamental properties: unitary transformations that can systematically accumulate errors through repeated operations, and Hadamard gates that create quantum interference patterns capable of distorting state representations. Class-A leverages noise accumulation through iterative unitary operations, Class-B uses controlled ancilla qubits to trigger attacks only under specific conditions, and Class-C exploits Hadamard interference to maximally scramble quantum information. These mechanisms work because quantum states are exponentially sensitive to perturbations, and small systematic distortions can propagate through the network's quantum operations to completely corrupt classification outputs.

- **Unitary Transformations**: Reversible quantum operations that preserve inner products between states. Needed because QuPTs exploit these to systematically inject noise while maintaining quantum mechanical consistency. - **Hadamard Gate Interference**: Quantum gate that creates superposition states and enables interference patterns between quantum amplitudes. - **Configuration File Manipulation**: Modifying compilation parameters that control quantum circuit execution. Needed because the threat model assumes this as the attack vector for injecting QuPTs. Quick check: Confirm configuration changes persist through quantum compilation and execution pipeline.
- **Ancilla Qubits**: Auxiliary quantum bits used for control operations and intermediate computations. Needed because Class-B QuPTs use these for conditional triggering mechanisms. Quick check: Verify ancilla qubit states can be manipulated without affecting main computation until triggering conditions are met.

## Limitations
- The study focuses exclusively on binary classification tasks, limiting generalizability to multi-class or regression problems
- Trojan methodology relies on configuration file manipulation in specific quantum compilation pipelines, which may not generalize across platforms
- Experimental validation uses relatively small QNN architectures without exploring scaling effects on Trojan effectiveness
- No defensive mechanisms or mitigation strategies are proposed to address the demonstrated vulnerabilities

## Confidence
- **High Confidence**: Theoretical framework for QuPT attacks exploiting unitary transformations and Hadamard interference is mathematically sound
- **Medium Confidence**: Experimental results showing 28.57% accuracy degradation in ideal simulations are reproducible within tested MNIST context
- **Medium Confidence**: Threat model based on configuration file manipulation is plausible but requires broader validation across diverse quantum platforms
