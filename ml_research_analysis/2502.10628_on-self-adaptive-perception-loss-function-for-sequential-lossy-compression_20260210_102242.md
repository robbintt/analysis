# On Self-Adaptive Perception Loss Function for Sequential Lossy Compression

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2502.10628
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.10628
- **Authors:** Sadaf Salehkalaibar; Buu Phan; Likun Cai; Joao Atz Dick; Wei Yu; Jun Chen; Ashish Khisti
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Proposed self-adaptive PLF avoids error permanence and exploits temporal correlation better than existing metrics

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of sequential lossy video compression by introducing a self-adaptive perception loss function (PLF-SA) that jointly considers the current source frame and previous reconstructions. The authors identify limitations in existing PLF approaches - PLF-JD suffers from error permanence while PLF-FMD fails to exploit temporal correlation. Their proposed solution adapts based on the quality of previous reconstructions, achieving superior performance in both distortion and perceptual quality metrics.

The work establishes theoretical rate-distortion-perception bounds for first-order Markov sources and provides detailed Gaussian model analysis. Experimental validation on MovingMNIST and UVG datasets demonstrates that PLF-SA can recover from errors when the first frame is compressed at low bitrate, while maintaining temporal consistency when the first frame is compressed at high bitrate.

## Method Summary
The paper proposes a self-adaptive perception loss function (PLF-SA) for sequential lossy video compression that addresses the limitations of existing metrics. PLF-SA considers the joint distribution between the current source frame and previous reconstructions, allowing it to adapt its behavior based on reconstruction quality. The authors establish theoretical rate-distortion-perception bounds for first-order Markov sources and analyze the Gaussian model in detail. The proposed metric is evaluated against PLF-JD (which suffers from error permanence) and PLF-FMD (which fails to exploit temporal correlation) on MovingMNIST and UVG datasets, demonstrating superior performance in both distortion and perceptual quality metrics.

## Key Results
- PLF-SA simultaneously avoids error permanence and better exploits temporal correlation compared to existing metrics
- When first frame is compressed at low bitrate, PLF-SA recovers from errors like PLF-FMD but unlike PLF-JD
- When first frame is compressed at high bitrate, PLF-SA maintains temporal consistency unlike PLF-FMD while avoiding error propagation unlike PLF-JD

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The self-adaptive perception loss function works by considering the joint distribution between the current source frame and previous reconstructions, rather than treating frames independently. This joint consideration allows the metric to adapt its behavior based on the quality of previous reconstructions. When previous reconstructions are poor (low bitrate initial frame), the metric can recover from errors by not being overly constrained by past reconstructions. When previous reconstructions are good (high bitrate initial frame), the metric maintains temporal consistency by leveraging the correlation between frames. This adaptive behavior addresses the fundamental trade-off between error recovery and temporal consistency that plagues existing PLF approaches.

## Foundational Learning

**First-order Markov sources**: Video frames where each frame depends only on the immediately preceding frame. Why needed: The theoretical framework assumes this structure to establish rate-distortion-perception bounds. Quick check: Verify that the video sequences used in experiments exhibit approximately Markovian behavior.

**Rate-distortion-perception bounds**: Theoretical limits that characterize the fundamental trade-off between compression rate, distortion, and perceptual quality. Why needed: Provides the theoretical foundation for understanding the performance limits of sequential compression systems. Quick check: Confirm that the derived bounds match empirical performance trends.

**Joint distribution modeling**: Capturing the statistical relationship between current source frames and previous reconstructions. Why needed: Enables the self-adaptive behavior by considering how current decisions affect future frames. Quick check: Verify that the joint distribution accurately captures temporal dependencies in the data.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map**: Source frame → Joint distribution modeling → Self-adaptive PLF → Rate allocation → Encoder → Reconstruction → Feedback to joint distribution

**Critical path**: The joint distribution modeling component is critical as it enables the self-adaptive behavior. The rate allocation must balance between current distortion and future perceptual quality based on this joint distribution.

**Design tradeoffs**: The self-adaptive mechanism trades computational complexity for improved performance. The joint distribution modeling requires additional computation but enables better exploitation of temporal correlation. The system must balance between being conservative (avoiding error propagation) and aggressive (recovering from past errors).

**Failure signatures**: If the joint distribution is poorly estimated, the system may oscillate between error-recovery and temporal-consistency modes inappropriately. If the rate allocation is suboptimal, the system may waste bits on perceptual quality at the expense of distortion, or vice versa.

## Limitations
- Theoretical framework assumes first-order Markov sources, which may not fully represent complex video dependencies in practice
- Experimental validation is limited to synthetic MovingMNIST and UVG datasets, lacking real-world video content diversity
- Lack of extensive ablation studies on how the self-adaptive mechanism behaves under different bitrate conditions and source characteristics
