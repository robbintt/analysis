# Mispronunciation Detection and Diagnosis Without Model Training: A Retrieval-Based Approach

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2511.20107
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.20107
- **Reference count**: 0
- **Primary Result**: Retrieval-based MDD achieves 69.60% F1 and 4.43% FRR on L2-ARCTIC

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a training-free retrieval-based approach for mispronunciation detection and diagnosis (MDD) that leverages pretrained ASR embeddings to construct a phoneme embedding pool from training data. During inference, the method retrieves similar embeddings to predict phoneme sequences, which are then aligned with reference text for detection and diagnosis. The approach eliminates the need for model training, reducing complexity and data scarcity issues. Experimental results on the L2-ARCTIC dataset demonstrate competitive performance with an F1 score of 69.60% and an FRR of 4.43%, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines while addressing the limitations of traditional supervised approaches.

## Method Summary
The retrieval-based approach uses a pretrained ASR model to generate phoneme embeddings from training data, creating a phoneme embedding pool for inference. When processing new speech input, the method retrieves similar embeddings from this pool to predict phoneme sequences. These predicted sequences are then aligned with reference text to detect and diagnose mispronunciations. The method operates without task-specific training, relying entirely on the pretrained ASR model and retrieval mechanisms to identify pronunciation errors.

## Key Results
- Achieves F1 score of 69.60% and FRR of 4.43% on L2-ARCTIC dataset
- Outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in MDD performance
- Successfully eliminates model training complexity while maintaining competitive accuracy

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method leverages the semantic richness of pretrained ASR embeddings to capture pronunciation patterns without requiring task-specific training. By constructing a comprehensive phoneme embedding pool from training data, the retrieval mechanism can effectively match incoming speech to known pronunciation patterns. The alignment of retrieved phoneme sequences with reference text enables accurate detection and diagnosis of mispronunciations.

- **Sequence Alignment**: The process of comparing retrieved phoneme sequences with reference text. Why needed: Identifies specific mispronunciations by highlighting discrepancies. Quick check: Evaluate alignment accuracy for different types of pronunciation errors.

## Architecture Onboarding
**Component Map**: ASR Model -> Embedding Extraction -> Phoneme Pool -> Retrieval Engine -> Sequence Alignment -> Diagnosis Output

**Critical Path**: Input Speech → ASR Embedding Extraction → Pool Retrieval → Sequence Prediction → Text Alignment → Mispronunciation Detection

**Design Tradeoffs**: The method trades computational efficiency for accuracy by avoiding model training but requiring comprehensive embedding pools. The retrieval-based approach eliminates the need for labeled training data but may struggle with novel pronunciations not represented in the pool.

**Failure Signatures**: Poor performance on insertion errors indicates limitations in handling phonemes not present in the reference pool. The method may also struggle with highly accented speech that deviates significantly from training data patterns.

## Limitations
- Struggles with insertion errors, resulting in higher phoneme error rates
- May not effectively handle novel pronunciations not represented in the embedding pool
- Performance depends heavily on the quality and diversity of the phoneme embedding pool

## Confidence
- **High Confidence**: The retrieval-based approach achieves the reported F1 and FRR scores on the L2-ARCTIC dataset.
- **Medium Confidence**: The claim that the method avoids model training complexity is valid, but the extent of this advantage in real-world deployment scenarios is uncertain.
- **Low Confidence**: The assertion that the method can effectively handle all mispronunciation types is questionable due to its struggle with insertion errors.
