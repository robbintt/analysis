# High-accuracy and dimension-free sampling with diffusions
## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2601.10708
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.10708
- Reference count: 40
- This paper introduces a high-accuracy, dimension-free sampling algorithm for diffusion models that achieves polylogarithmic dependence on the inverse accuracy parameter.

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a novel diffusion-based sampling algorithm that achieves high accuracy with polylogarithmic complexity in the inverse accuracy parameter while being dimension-free. The key innovation is leveraging the observation that score function time derivatives along the reverse process are bounded, allowing effective low-degree polynomial approximation. This enables efficient simulation of the reverse process using the collocation method, with complexity depending on the effective radius R rather than ambient dimension d. The algorithm represents the first diffusion sampler to achieve high-accuracy guarantees (polylogarithmic in 1/ϵ) while only requiring access to approximate scores.

## Method Summary
The proposed algorithm exploits the fact that score function time derivatives along the reverse process are bounded, enabling low-degree polynomial approximation of the score in time. The collocation method is used with this low-degree approximation to simulate the reverse process efficiently. The approach achieves total variation distance ϵ from the target distribution in O((R/σ)² · polylog(1/ϵ)) iterations, where R is the effective radius and σ is the noise level. This is particularly efficient for distributions with low intrinsic dimension since complexity depends on R rather than ambient dimension d.

## Key Results
- Achieves total variation distance ϵ from target distribution in O((R/σ)² · polylog(1/ϵ)) iterations
- Complexity depends on effective radius R rather than ambient dimension d
- First diffusion-based sampler to achieve high-accuracy guarantees (polylogarithmic in 1/ϵ)
- Only requires access to approximate scores, not exact scores

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The algorithm works by exploiting the smoothness properties of the score function along the reverse diffusion process. Specifically, the time derivatives of the score function are bounded, which means the score can be well-approximated by low-degree polynomials in time. This polynomial approximation property enables the use of the collocation method, which can efficiently simulate the reverse process without requiring high-dimensional operations. The dimension-free nature arises because the complexity depends on the effective radius R of the distribution rather than the ambient dimension d, making it particularly suitable for distributions with low intrinsic dimension.

## Foundational Learning
- **Score function**: The gradient of the log probability density function. Needed to guide the reverse diffusion process. Quick check: Verify score function bounds for your target distribution.
- **Reverse diffusion process**: The time-reversed stochastic process that denoises samples. Needed to transform from Gaussian noise back to the target distribution. Quick check: Ensure noise schedule is appropriate for your application.
- **Polynomial approximation**: Technique to approximate functions using polynomials of bounded degree. Needed to efficiently represent the time-varying score function. Quick check: Validate approximation error bounds for your score function.
- **Collocation method**: Numerical method for solving differential equations by enforcing constraints at specific points. Needed to simulate the reverse process efficiently. Quick check: Verify stability and convergence of the collocation scheme.
- **Effective radius R**: The intrinsic scale of the distribution, distinct from ambient dimension d. Needed to characterize the complexity of sampling. Quick check: Estimate R empirically for your target distribution.
- **Total variation distance**: A measure of the difference between probability distributions. Needed to quantify sampling accuracy. Quick check: Compute TV distance between samples and target distribution.

## Architecture Onboarding
**Component Map:** Score function approximator -> Time polynomial approximation -> Collocation solver -> Sample generator
**Critical Path:** Approximate score → Compute polynomial coefficients → Solve collocation equations → Generate sample
**Design Tradeoffs:** Lower-degree polynomials reduce computation but increase approximation error; higher-degree polynomials do the opposite. The noise schedule must balance between signal preservation and smoothness of score evolution.
**Failure Signatures:** Poor score approximations lead to inaccurate polynomial fits; high noise levels may violate bounded derivative assumptions; distributions with complex geometry may have large effective radii.

## Limitations
- Assumption of bounded score function time derivatives may not hold for all realistic distributions
- Effective radius R may be difficult to estimate or may not be small for distributions with complex geometry
- Practical performance and numerical stability of the collocation method in high-noise regimes remains uncertain
- The method's behavior near distribution boundaries or in the presence of heavy tails requires further investigation

## Confidence
- High: The basic framework of using polynomial approximations for score functions in time
- Medium: The claimed polylogarithmic complexity bound and its dependence on R vs d
- Low: The practical implementability and numerical stability of the proposed method
