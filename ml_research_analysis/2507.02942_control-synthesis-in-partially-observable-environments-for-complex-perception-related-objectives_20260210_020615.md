# Control Synthesis in Partially Observable Environments for Complex Perception-Related Objectives

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2507.02942
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.02942
- Authors: Zetong Xuan; Yu Wang
- Reference count: 22
- Primary result: Introduces sc-iLTL for perception-related objectives in POMDPs, achieving 87% success rate in drone-probing task

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a novel approach to synthesizing optimal policies for complex perception-related objectives in partially observable environments. The authors introduce co-safe linear inequality temporal logic (sc-iLTL), which enables specification of objectives as linear inequalities over belief space, making it particularly suited for perception-related tasks. The method transforms the control problem into a reachability problem using product construction between belief MDP and DFA, then employs Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to find optimal policies. The approach is validated on a drone-probing case study where the drone must locate a ground target and reach a landing zone.

## Method Summary
The method introduces sc-iLTL as an extension of linear temporal logic that can express complex perception-related objectives through linear inequalities over belief space. The authors construct a product MDP between the belief MDP and a deterministic finite automaton (DFA) that encodes the sc-iLTL objective. To address scalability challenges from this product construction, they employ Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) that focuses only on beliefs reachable from the initial belief, achieving scalability independent of the full belief space size. The approach proves convergence in probability and demonstrates that augmenting the belief space with DFA states does not increase sample complexity.

## Key Results
- The sc-iLTL method achieved an 87% success rate across 100 independent runs in a drone-probing case study
- Successful runs completed the task in an average of 40.71 steps
- The approach demonstrates scalability through MCTS focusing on reachable beliefs rather than full belief space
- Proven convergence in probability of the MCTS method for finding optimal policies

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The approach works by leveraging the mathematical structure of POMDPs and temporal logic to create a tractable solution method. sc-iLTL provides a natural way to express perception-related objectives as linear inequalities over belief space, which captures the uncertainty inherent in partial observability. The product construction transforms the complex temporal logic objective into a reachability problem in a higher-dimensional space. MCTS then efficiently explores this space by focusing computational resources on reachable beliefs rather than exhaustively searching the entire belief space, making the approach scalable to larger problems.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map:** POMDP -> Belief MDP -> Product MDP (Belief MDP × DFA) -> MCTS Planner

**Critical path:** Initial belief state → MCTS tree expansion → Belief updates → DFA state transitions → Goal satisfaction check → Policy extraction

**Design tradeoffs:** The product construction increases state space dimensionality but enables formal specification of complex objectives. MCTS sacrifices optimality guarantees for computational efficiency and scalability.

**Failure signatures:** Poor performance when observation models are highly uncertain, when belief space becomes too large despite MCTS optimization, or when sc-iLTL specifications are overly complex.

**3 first experiments:**
1. Verify belief update correctness on simple POMDP examples
2. Test MCTS performance on small belief MDPs with known optimal policies
3. Validate DFA construction for simple sc-iLTL specifications

## Limitations
- Validation restricted to a single case study with a 4×4 grid world and 256 hidden states
- 87% success rate leaves 13% of runs unsuccessful, raising reliability concerns
- Assumes known transition probabilities and observation models, limiting applicability to unknown environments
- sc-iLTL specification language may struggle with highly complex perception-related objectives requiring nuanced spatial-temporal reasoning

## Confidence

**High confidence in the theoretical foundation of sc-iLTL and its relationship to belief space inequalities**
**Medium confidence in the MCTS implementation and its scalability claims, given limited empirical validation**
**Low confidence in the method's performance on larger, more complex environments with higher dimensional state and observation spaces**
