# AWARE: Audio Watermarking with Adversarial Resistance to Edits

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.17512
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.17512
- Reference count: 29
- High audio quality (PESQ: 4.08, STOI: 0.97) with low bit error rates under edits

## Executive Summary
AWARE is an audio watermarking method that achieves high robustness against edits like cuts, resampling, and compression without relying on handcrafted distortion simulations. Instead, it uses adversarial optimization in the time-frequency domain with a level-proportional perceptual budget. The key innovation is a time-order-agnostic detector with a Bitwise Readout Head (BRH) that aggregates temporal evidence into one score per watermark bit, enabling robust decoding even under desynchronization and temporal cuts. The method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches like WavMark and AudioSeal, especially under neural vocoder resynthesis and spectral edits.

## Method Summary
AWARE embeds watermarks through an iterative adversarial process that minimizes signal distortion while maximizing robustness to targeted attacks. It uses a Mel-frequency representation and a level-proportional perceptual budget based on signal amplitude. The detector employs a Bitwise Readout Head (BRH) that aggregates evidence across time for each watermark bit, enabling time-order-agnostic decoding. The system is trained end-to-end with an adversarial attack simulator, allowing it to resist both common audio edits and sophisticated adversarial perturbations.

## Key Results
- Achieves PESQ score of 4.08 and STOI of 0.97, indicating high audio quality
- Consistently low bit error rates across various attacks, outperforming WavMark and AudioSeal
- Demonstrates superior robustness under neural vocoder resynthesis and spectral edits

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method's robustness stems from adversarial training that simulates real-world distortions during embedding optimization. By optimizing directly against a suite of attacks in the time-frequency domain, the watermark learns to survive edits without relying on predefined distortion models. The Bitwise Readout Head (BRH) enables robust decoding by aggregating evidence across time, making the system resilient to temporal cuts and desynchronization. The Mel front-end captures perceptually relevant features while the level-proportional budget ensures imperceptibility without sacrificing robustness.

## Architecture Onboarding
- **Component map**: Audio signal → Mel front-end → Embedding network (iterative) → Watermarked audio → Adversarial attack simulator → Detector (BRH) → Bit scores
- **Critical path**: Embedding optimization loop (500 iterations) → Adversarial attack simulation → BRH-based detection
- **Design tradeoffs**: Iterative embedding ensures robustness but increases computational cost vs. single-pass methods
- **Failure signatures**: High BER under unseen distortions, temporal warping artifacts, perceptual quality degradation
- **First experiments**: 1) Benchmark against WavMark and AudioSeal on standard attacks, 2) Test robustness under neural vocoder resynthesis, 3) Evaluate perceptual quality (PESQ/STOI) on clean and attacked audio

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
### Open Question 1
- Question: Does integrating a full psychoacoustic model (e.g., simultaneous masking) improve the imperceptibility-robustness trade-off compared to the current level-proportional budget?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors state, "In future work the tolerance $\tau_{dB}$ can be replaced by frequency-dependent thresholds or full psychoacoustic models."
- Why unresolved: The current method uses a level-proportional budget based solely on signal amplitude, which ignores frequency-dependent masking effects.
- What evidence would resolve it: Comparative experiments measuring PESQ/STOI and BER between the current level-proportional method and a version utilizing threshold-in-quiet and simultaneous masking models.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can the adversarial embedding procedure be accelerated or replaced by a feed-forward generator while maintaining the same resistance to unseen distortions?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The method requires 500 iterations of optimization (NAdam) per sample, which is computationally intensive compared to single-pass neural watermarking methods like AudioSeal.
- Why unresolved: The paper establishes robustness via this iterative process but does not benchmark the latency or propose a method to distill this into a faster, non-iterative embedder.
- What evidence would resolve it: A latency benchmark comparing AWARE to baselines, or the successful training of a generator network that mimics the adversarial embeddings in a single pass.

### Open Question 3
- Question: Can the detector architecture be modified to achieve near-zero Bit Error Rates under Time-Scale Modification (TSM) without sacrificing performance on spectral attacks?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The results (Table 2) show that AWARE incurs a 5.53% BER under time-stretch attacks, which the authors identify as "the hardest case" and "temporal edits remain the hardest case."
- Why unresolved: While the Bitwise Readout Head handles cuts well, the global warping of time axes in TSM appears to degrade the temporal evidence aggregation.
- What evidence would resolve it: Experiments testing additional temporal invariant layers (e.g., dilated convolutions or specialized pooling) specifically targeting $\pm 20\%$ time-stretch scenarios.

## Limitations
- Evaluation conducted on relatively small dataset (100 utterances, 10s each), which may not capture full real-world variability
- Generalizability to unseen distortions remains untested despite reported superiority over baselines
- Perceptual quality metrics measured only on clean audio; impact after edits not reported
