# Fairness Through Matching

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2501.02793
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.02793
- Authors: Kunwoong Kim; Insung Kong; Jongjin Lee; Minwoo Chae; Sangchul Park; Yongdai Kim
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Proposes Matched Demographic Parity (MDP) and Fairness Through Matching (FTM) algorithm for group fairness using transport maps

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to group fairness in machine learning by establishing a connection between group-fair models and transport maps between input spaces of protected groups. The authors propose Matched Demographic Parity (MDP) as a new fairness measure that quantifies prediction gaps between matched individuals from different groups. Their Fairness Through Matching (FTM) algorithm learns group-fair models by constraining MDP with user-specified transport maps, specifically optimal transport maps in either the input space (marginal OT) or the joint input-output space (joint OT).

## Method Summary
The authors develop a theoretical framework linking group fairness to transport maps, introducing MDP as a measure of fairness based on matched pairs. They propose FTM, an algorithm that constrains MDP through a user-specified transport map to learn fair models. Two specific transport maps are considered: the marginal OT map in input space and the joint OT map in product space of input and output. The method is evaluated on benchmark datasets, showing improved fairness and prediction performance compared to existing approaches.

## Key Results
- Successfully trains group-fair models using transport maps
- Shows improved fairness on subsets compared to existing methods
- Demonstrates better prediction performance while maintaining fairness

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The approach works by leveraging the mathematical relationship between group-fair models and transport maps. By constraining the model to minimize prediction differences between matched individuals from different groups, the method ensures fairness while maintaining prediction quality. The transport maps provide a structured way to compare individuals across groups based on their similarity in feature space.

## Architecture Onboarding
Component map: Input Data -> Transport Map Generator -> FTM Algorithm -> Group-Fair Model

Critical path: The core workflow involves computing transport maps between groups, then training the model while constraining MDP based on these maps.

Design tradeoffs: Choice between marginal OT (simpler, faster) and joint OT (more comprehensive) maps; computational complexity vs. fairness quality.

Failure signatures: Poor transport map quality leads to unfair matching; overly restrictive constraints may degrade prediction performance.

## Limitations
- Theoretical claims about sufficiency conditions need more rigorous proof
- MDP may not capture all aspects of complex fairness requirements
- Choice between transport maps lacks clear guidance

## Confidence
High confidence: The mathematical framework connecting transport maps to group fairness is sound and provides valuable theoretical insights. The basic algorithmic approach is technically valid.

Medium confidence: Experimental results are promising but may be sensitive to dataset characteristics. The choice between marginal and joint OT maps needs more empirical justification.

Low confidence: Generalizability to complex fairness scenarios and multi-class problems is uncertain. Scalability to large-scale real-world applications requires further investigation.
