# NAICS-Aware Graph Neural Networks for Large-Scale POI Co-visitation Prediction: A Multi-Modal Dataset and Methodology

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2507.19697
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.19697
- Reference count: 40
- 157% R-squared improvement (0.243 to 0.625) and 32% NDCG@10 improvement in POI co-visitation prediction

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces NAICS-aware GraphSAGE, a novel graph neural network for predicting population-scale POI co-visitation patterns. The method integrates business taxonomy knowledge through learnable NAICS embeddings, enabling the model to capture semantic relationships between business categories that pure spatial models miss. The approach scales to massive datasets (4.2 billion potential venue pairs) through state-wise decomposition while combining spatial, temporal, and socioeconomic features in an end-to-end framework.

## Method Summary
The paper presents a graph neural network architecture that incorporates NAICS (North American Industry Classification System) taxonomy knowledge into GraphSAGE through learnable business category embeddings. The model processes co-visitation data by constructing graphs where nodes represent POIs and edges represent observed co-visitation patterns. To handle the massive scale (4.2 billion venue pairs), the approach decomposes the problem by state, enabling distributed processing while maintaining global context through shared NAICS embeddings. The architecture combines spatial proximity, temporal patterns, and socioeconomic indicators as multimodal inputs alongside the taxonomy-aware graph structure.

## Key Results
- R-squared value increased from 0.243 to 0.625 (157% improvement) over state-of-the-art baselines
- NDCG@10 ranking quality improved by 32% compared to previous methods
- Evaluated on a multi-modal POI-Graph dataset containing 94.9 million co-visitation records across 92,486 brands and 48 US states

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The NAICS-aware approach works by explicitly encoding semantic relationships between business categories into the graph neural network. Traditional co-visitation prediction models rely primarily on spatial proximity and historical visitation patterns, missing the underlying business category relationships that drive human behavior. By learning NAICS embeddings that capture the semantic distance between different business types (e.g., restaurants vs. shopping centers), the model can generalize better to new venue pairs and capture complementary business relationships that pure spatial models miss.

## Architecture Onboarding
**Component map:** NAICS Embeddings -> GraphSAGE Aggregator -> Multimodal Fusion -> Prediction Layer
**Critical path:** Business category nodes → NAICS embedding lookup → GraphSAGE message passing → Feature concatenation → MLP prediction
**Design tradeoffs:** State-wise decomposition enables scalability but may miss cross-state patterns; learnable NAICS embeddings capture semantics but require more parameters than fixed encodings
**Failure signatures:** Poor performance on cross-category predictions suggests inadequate NAICS embedding learning; state decomposition issues manifest as boundary artifacts in predictions
**Three first experiments:**
1. Compare NAICS-aware vs. non-NAICS-aware GraphSAGE on small dataset to verify taxonomy contribution
2. Test different NAICS embedding dimensions to find optimal semantic representation size
3. Evaluate state-wise vs. full-graph training to quantify decomposition impact

## Limitations
- Proprietary dataset prevents independent verification of results and methodology
- State-wise decomposition effectiveness depends on specific geographic distribution patterns
- Unclear whether improvements stem primarily from taxonomy awareness or multimodal feature combination

## Confidence
**High:** The core architectural contribution of NAICS-aware GraphSAGE and its ability to incorporate business taxonomy knowledge into graph neural networks.
**Medium:** The scalability claims through state-wise decomposition, as this depends on specific data characteristics not fully disclosed.
**Low:** The absolute performance improvements without independent dataset validation.

## Next Checks
1. Release a subset of the dataset or create a public benchmark with similar characteristics to enable reproducibility of the 157% R-squared improvement.

3. Test the state-wise decomposition approach on alternative geographic partitioning schemes (e.g., county-level or custom clusters) to verify its robustness beyond the 48-state implementation.
