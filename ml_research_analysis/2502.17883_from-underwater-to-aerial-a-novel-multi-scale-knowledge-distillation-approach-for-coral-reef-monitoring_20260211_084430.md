# From underwater to aerial: a novel multi-scale knowledge distillation approach for coral reef monitoring

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2502.17883
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.17883
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Multi-scale deep-learning approach integrating underwater and aerial imagery for coral reef monitoring, achieving AUC of 0.9251

## Executive Summary
This study presents a novel multi-scale deep-learning approach for coral reef monitoring that bridges the gap between fine-scale underwater imagery and broader aerial views. The method uses transformer-based models trained on underwater imagery collected by autonomous surface vehicles to detect 31 coral morphotypes, fauna, and habitats. These predictions are then transferred to aerial imagery using a weighted footprint method that accounts for partial overlaps between image tiles. The approach enables scalable classification without manual aerial annotations, significantly reducing annotation time while maintaining high accuracy across different spatial scales.

## Method Summary
The methodology employs a two-stage process: first, underwater imagery collected by autonomous surface vehicles is used to train a transformer-based deep learning model that classifies 31 different coral morphotypes, fauna, and habitats. Second, these underwater-trained predictions are transferred to aerial imagery through a weighted footprint method that addresses partial overlap issues between image tiles. This knowledge distillation approach eliminates the need for manual aerial annotations by leveraging the detailed underwater classifications. The system is designed to be adaptable and can potentially be extended to satellite imagery for even broader monitoring applications.

## Key Results
- Achieved AUC score of 0.9251 in aligning underwater-derived annotations with ground truth data
- Successfully extended fine-scale classification from underwater imagery to larger reef areas through aerial imagery
- Demonstrated reduced annotation time while maintaining classification precision across scales
- Validated approach on Hayman Island in the Great Barrier Reef with 31 coral morphotype categories

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The approach works by leveraging the complementary strengths of underwater and aerial imagery. Underwater images provide high-resolution, detailed views of coral structures and biodiversity, enabling precise classification of 31 different morphotypes. These detailed classifications serve as a knowledge base that can be transferred to aerial imagery, which offers broader spatial coverage but lacks the resolution for fine-scale identification. The weighted footprint method effectively handles the geometric and scale differences between the two imaging modalities, allowing knowledge transfer without requiring manual annotations on the aerial images.

## Architecture Onboarding
**Component map**: ASV imagery acquisition -> Transformer model training -> Weighted footprint mapping -> Aerial image prediction -> Multi-scale classification

**Critical path**: The most critical sequence is ASV imagery acquisition → Transformer model training → Weighted footprint mapping → Aerial image prediction. This path ensures that the knowledge transfer from underwater to aerial domains is properly executed.

**Design tradeoffs**: The approach trades computational complexity for reduced manual annotation requirements. While the transformer model and weighted footprint calculations require significant processing power, this is offset by eliminating the need for manual aerial image annotation. The method also balances spatial resolution against coverage area, using underwater imagery for detail and aerial imagery for scale.

**Failure signatures**: 
- Poor alignment between underwater and aerial predictions (AUC drops below 0.85)
- Inconsistent morphotype classifications across scales
- Weighted footprint method fails to properly handle partial overlaps
- Transformer model overfitting to specific underwater conditions

3. Compare classification results between direct aerial annotation and the knowledge distillation approach on a small test area

## Limitations
- Results are based on a single study site (Hayman Island, Great Barrier Reef), limiting generalizability
- Performance in different environmental conditions and reef systems remains untested
- Scalability and cost-effectiveness of ASV-based data collection for widespread implementation are unclear
- Knowledge distillation effectiveness from underwater to aerial domains requires further validation

## Confidence
- Transformer model performance on underwater imagery: High
- Weighted footprint method effectiveness: Medium
- Scalability of approach across different reef systems: Low
- Time savings compared to traditional methods: Low
- Extension to satellite imagery: Low
