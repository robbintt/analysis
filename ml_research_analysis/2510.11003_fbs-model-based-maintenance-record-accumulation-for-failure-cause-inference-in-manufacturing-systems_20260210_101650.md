# FBS Model-based Maintenance Record Accumulation for Failure-Cause Inference in Manufacturing Systems

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.11003
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.11003
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: FBS model-based method improves failure-cause inference by structuring knowledge and creating causal chains

## Executive Summary
This study addresses the critical challenge of knowledge-based failure-cause inference in manufacturing systems by proposing a Function-Behavior-Structure (FBS) model-based method for maintenance-record accumulation. The approach explicitly structures knowledge about target systems and failures while creating sufficiently long causal chains, meeting two critical requirements for effective failure-cause inference. An ontology-based approach was developed to integrate deep system knowledge with shallow failure knowledge, and maintenance records were accumulated using this framework. Experimental evaluation using a LEGO car assembly line demonstrated that the proposed method significantly outperformed a baseline approach, particularly in difficult cases with limited data and vocabulary differences.

## Method Summary
The proposed method utilizes Function-Behavior-Structure (FBS) models to explicitly structure knowledge about manufacturing systems and their failures. An ontology-based approach integrates deep system knowledge with shallow failure knowledge, creating a framework for accumulating maintenance records. The method focuses on creating sufficiently long causal chains that connect system functions to failure causes, addressing the knowledge gap between design and maintenance phases. The approach was implemented using a vocabulary-based evaluation method that leverages terminology differences between design and maintenance phases to assess the effectiveness of failure-cause inference.

## Key Results
- The proposed FBS model-based method significantly outperformed baseline approaches in failure-cause inference
- Performance improvements were particularly notable in difficult cases with limited data availability
- The method effectively bridged vocabulary differences between design and maintenance phases, improving precision and recall metrics

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by explicitly structuring knowledge about manufacturing systems and failures through FBS models, which creates a framework for building sufficiently long causal chains. The ontology-based integration approach allows deep system knowledge from the design phase to be combined with shallow failure knowledge from maintenance records. This structured knowledge representation enables more effective inference of failure causes by providing context and relationships that would otherwise be implicit or missing. The approach addresses the critical gap between design and maintenance phases by creating a common knowledge framework that can be used across the engineering chain.

## Foundational Learning
- **Function-Behavior-Structure (FBS) models**: Provide explicit knowledge structuring for system and failure relationships; quick check: verify that all system components have defined functions, behaviors, and structures
- **Ontology-based integration**: Enables combination of deep and shallow knowledge sources; quick check: confirm that ontology relationships are consistent and complete
- **Causal chain construction**: Creates long chains connecting functions to failure causes; quick check: verify chain length and completeness meet requirements
- **Vocabulary-based evaluation**: Leverages terminology differences between phases; quick check: validate that vocabulary differences are meaningful and measurable

- Vocabulary-based evaluation vs. semantic richness
- LEGO system simplicity vs. industrial applicability
- Ontology comprehensiveness vs. maintenance overhead

- Vocabulary mismatch between design and maintenance
- Incomplete system knowledge representation
- Ontology integration inconsistencies

## Limitations
- Experimental validation was limited to a LEGO car assembly line, raising questions about scalability to industrial systems
- Vocabulary-based evaluation may not capture full semantic richness of failure-cause relationships
- Performance on rare failure types and edge cases was not thoroughly examined
- Transferability to complex manufacturing environments with diverse failure modes remains uncertain

## Confidence

**High confidence:**
- Core methodology of using FBS models for knowledge structuring is technically sound
- Ontology-based integration approach is well-founded in existing research

**Medium confidence:**
- Experimental results demonstrating improved performance are convincing within the LEGO assembly line context
- Claim of bridging design and maintenance phases is supported by results

**Low confidence:**
- Generalizability to industrial-scale manufacturing systems
- Long-term performance across evolving system configurations

## Next Checks
1. Test the method on an industrial-scale manufacturing system with diverse failure modes and complex interdependencies to assess scalability and real-world applicability
2. Conduct a longitudinal study to evaluate the method's performance over extended operational periods with evolving system configurations and maintenance practices
3. Perform a comparative analysis using alternative semantic similarity measures and natural language processing techniques to validate the vocabulary-based evaluation approach
