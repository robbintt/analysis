# Multi-agent Multi-armed Bandit with Fully Heavy-tailed Dynamics

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2501.19239
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.19239
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Sublinear regret bounds for multi-agent bandits with heavy-tailed rewards and sparse power-law graphs

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the multi-agent multi-armed bandit problem under fully heavy-tailed dynamics, where both the communication graph (following power-law degree distributions) and reward distributions (with potentially infinite variance) exhibit heavy tails. The authors propose novel algorithms that exploit the unique hub structures in heavy-tailed graphs for the homogeneous case and neighbor aggregation for the heterogeneous case. The approach enables clients to reduce noise through aggregation mechanisms while maintaining communication efficiency in sparse network topologies.

## Method Summary
The authors develop two distinct algorithms based on graph and reward heterogeneity. For the homogeneous setting, they leverage hub-like structures characteristic of heavy-tailed graphs, where central nodes (hubs) aggregate rewards from multiple clients to construct more stable UCB indices. In the heterogeneous setting, clients communicate with neighbors and aggregate exchanged estimators to synchronize their decisions. Both algorithms incorporate power-law assumptions into their regret analysis, showing improved performance compared to existing methods in the heavy-tailed regime.

## Key Results
- In homogeneous setting with α > 1: regret bound of (almost) O(M^(1-1/α) log T)
- In heterogeneous setting: regret bound of O(M log T)
- Both results demonstrate sublinear regret growth
- The homogeneous algorithm improves upon existing work in the heavy-tailed regime

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The proposed algorithms work by exploiting the structural properties of heavy-tailed networks. In heavy-tailed graphs, hub nodes connect to many clients, creating natural aggregation points that reduce variance in reward estimation. This hub-based aggregation is particularly effective when rewards are heavy-tailed with finite variance (α > 1), as the law of large numbers applies to the aggregated rewards. For heterogeneous rewards, the neighbor-based aggregation ensures all clients have access to a common information pool despite not knowing the global optimal arm, enabling coordinated exploration.

## Foundational Learning

**Heavy-tailed distributions**: Why needed - to model real-world phenomena with extreme events and potential infinite variance; Quick check - verify tail decay follows power law and compute α parameter.

**Power-law graphs**: Why needed - captures sparse network topologies common in real-world systems; Quick check - measure degree distribution and confirm power-law behavior.

**UCB algorithms**: Why needed - provides principled exploration-exploitation tradeoff in bandit problems; Quick check - verify concentration bounds hold under heavy-tailed conditions.

**Graph aggregation**: Why needed - enables noise reduction through collective information sharing; Quick check - measure variance reduction when aggregating across multiple sources.

**Regret analysis**: Why needed - quantifies cumulative performance loss relative to optimal policy; Quick check - verify sublinear growth of regret bound with time horizon.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map**: Client nodes -> Communication graph (power-law) -> Reward observations -> Aggregation mechanism -> UCB index computation -> Arm selection

**Critical path**: Observation → Aggregation → Index Computation → Selection → Reward → Feedback → Aggregation

**Design tradeoffs**: The algorithms trade communication bandwidth (sparse graph constraints) for statistical efficiency (aggregation benefits). The homogeneous algorithm requires hubs to be identifiable and stable, while the heterogeneous algorithm assumes reliable neighbor communication but needs more rounds for convergence.

**Failure signatures**: In the homogeneous case, failure occurs when hub nodes become unstable or when the power-law assumption breaks down. In the heterogeneous case, failure manifests as poor neighbor synchronization or when reward distributions are too heavy-tailed for reliable aggregation.

## Limitations
- Theoretical guarantees assume specific power-law distributions that may not hold in practice
- Results for α ≤ 1 (infinite variance) are less comprehensive than for α > 1
- Communication constraints represent a specific sparse graph model that may not capture all practical topologies

## Confidence
- Homogeneous setting (α > 1): High
- Heterogeneous setting: High
- α ≤ 1 regime: Medium
