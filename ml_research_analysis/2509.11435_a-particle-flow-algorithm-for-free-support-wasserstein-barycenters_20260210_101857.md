# A Particle-Flow Algorithm for Free-Support Wasserstein Barycenters

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.11435
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.11435

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel particle-flow algorithm for computing Wasserstein barycenters with free support, which avoids entropic regularization by leveraging the formal Riemannian geometry of Wasserstein space. The method updates barycenter atoms as particles advected by averaged optimal-transport displacements, using barycentric projections of optimal transport plans in place of Monge maps when the latter do not exist. Theoretical guarantees include consistency of barycentric projections, monotone descent and convergence to stationary points, stability with respect to perturbations of inputs, and resolution consistency as the number of atoms increases. Empirical studies on averaging distributions, Bayesian posterior aggregation, image prototypes and classification, and large-scale clustering demonstrate accuracy and scalability, positioning the approach as a principled alternative to both linear programming and regularized solvers.

## Method Summary
The particle-flow algorithm for Wasserstein barycenters with free support operates by treating barycenter atoms as particles that move according to averaged optimal-transport displacements. When Monge maps do not exist, the algorithm uses barycentric projections of optimal transport plans. This approach leverages the formal Riemannian geometry of Wasserstein space to avoid entropic regularization, which is common in other methods. The algorithm iteratively updates the positions of the barycenter atoms, ensuring convergence to stationary points and stability with respect to input perturbations.

## Key Results

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The particle-flow algorithm works by leveraging the formal Riemannian geometry of Wasserstein space, which allows for the computation of barycenters without the need for entropic regularization. By treating barycenter atoms as particles that move according to averaged optimal-transport displacements, the algorithm can effectively capture the geometric structure of the data. The use of barycentric projections of optimal transport plans, in place of Monge maps when they do not exist, ensures that the method is applicable in a wide range of scenarios. The iterative update of barycenter atom positions, combined with the theoretical guarantees of convergence and stability, makes the algorithm both effective and robust.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Input Distributions -> Barycentric Projections -> Particle Flow Updates -> Wasserstein Barycenter

**Critical Path**: The critical path involves the computation of barycentric projections from the input distributions, followed by the iterative update of particle positions based on averaged optimal-transport displacements. The algorithm then computes the Wasserstein barycenter as the final output.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The algorithm trades off computational complexity for the avoidance of entropic regularization, which can introduce bias in the barycenter computation. By leveraging the formal Riemannian geometry of Wasserstein space, the method achieves a balance between accuracy and scalability.

**Failure Signatures**: Potential failure modes include the inability to compute barycentric projections when the input distributions are too dissimilar, or the particle flow updates leading to numerical instability. The algorithm's convergence to stationary points may also be affected by the choice of initial barycenter atoms.
