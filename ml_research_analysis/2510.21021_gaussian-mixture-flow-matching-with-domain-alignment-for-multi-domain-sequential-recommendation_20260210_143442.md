# Gaussian Mixture Flow Matching with Domain Alignment for Multi-Domain Sequential Recommendation

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2510.21021
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.21021
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** GMFlowRec achieves up to 44% improvement in NDCG@5 over baselines for multi-domain sequential recommendation

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses Multi-Domain Sequential Recommendation (MDSR) by proposing GMFlowRec, a generative framework that combines Gaussian Mixture Flow Matching with domain-aligned priors. The method tackles challenges of domain heterogeneity, transition sparsity, and scalability in cross-domain recommendation systems. GMFlowRec uses a unified dual-masked Transformer to disentangle domain-invariant and domain-specific user intents, while modeling transitions through a Gaussian Mixture flow field guided by informative priors.

## Method Summary
GMFlowRec is a generative framework for multi-domain sequential recommendation that leverages Gaussian Mixture Flow Matching with domain-aligned priors. The core innovation is a unified dual-masked Transformer architecture that simultaneously captures domain-invariant and domain-specific user intents. The framework models user behavior transitions using a Gaussian Mixture flow field, which provides more flexible and accurate representations compared to single distribution approaches. The domain-aligned priors help guide the flow matching process to better handle cross-domain transitions while maintaining computational efficiency.

## Key Results
- Achieves up to 44% improvement in NDCG@5 over state-of-the-art baselines on JD and Amazon datasets
- Demonstrates superior performance under high transition rates and few-shot conditions
- Maintains high efficiency and scalability while delivering SOTA results

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
GMFlowRec works by addressing the fundamental challenges of MDSR through a generative approach that explicitly models the complex transition patterns between domains. The Gaussian Mixture flow field captures the multi-modal nature of user behavior transitions, while the domain-aligned priors provide informative guidance for the flow matching process. The dual-masked Transformer architecture effectively separates shared user preferences from domain-specific patterns, allowing the model to leverage cross-domain information without being confused by domain heterogeneity.

## Foundational Learning

**Flow Matching**: A generative modeling technique that learns continuous transformations between distributions. *Why needed:* Traditional autoregressive methods struggle with long sequences and complex dependencies. *Quick check:* Verify the flow field produces smooth, invertible transformations between distributions.

**Gaussian Mixture Models**: Probabilistic models that represent complex distributions as weighted combinations of Gaussian components. *Why needed:* Single Gaussian distributions cannot capture the multi-modal nature of user behavior across domains. *Quick check:* Confirm the mixture components adequately represent the observed transition patterns.

**Dual-masked Transformers**: Transformer architectures with separate attention mechanisms for different types of information. *Why needed:* Standard transformers cannot easily separate domain-invariant from domain-specific features. *Quick check:* Validate that the two attention masks produce distinct, meaningful representations.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map**: User behavior sequences -> Dual-masked Transformer -> Gaussian Mixture Flow Field -> Domain-aligned Priors -> Next item prediction

**Critical path**: Input sequences → Domain-invariant and domain-specific encoding → Gaussian mixture transition modeling → Posterior distribution → Item prediction

**Design tradeoffs**: The Gaussian mixture approach provides flexibility in modeling complex transitions but adds computational overhead compared to single-distribution methods. The dual-masked architecture enables better separation of shared and domain-specific features but requires careful masking strategy design.

**Failure signatures**: Poor performance on domains with very different item distributions, degraded accuracy when transition patterns are highly sparse, increased computational cost compared to simpler sequential models.

## Limitations
- Experimental validation limited to only two datasets (JD and Amazon), restricting generalizability
- Computational overhead of Gaussian Mixture flow field components not fully analyzed
- Claims about effectiveness under high transition rates lack detailed statistical significance testing

## Confidence

- **High confidence**: Core methodology (GMFlowRec framework with Gaussian Mixture Flow Matching and domain-aligned priors) is technically sound and well-defined
- **Medium confidence**: Performance claims on tested datasets are credible but may not generalize beyond experimental conditions
- **Low confidence**: Claims about scalability to extremely large systems and effectiveness across diverse domain transition patterns lack sufficient empirical backing

## Next Checks

1. Test GMFlowRec on additional datasets from different domains (e.g., social media, streaming platforms) to verify cross-domain generalization and assess performance consistency across varied transition patterns.

2. Conduct runtime and memory complexity analysis specifically for the Gaussian Mixture flow field components, comparing against simpler flow matching approaches to quantify the efficiency trade-offs.

3. Perform ablation studies with varying numbers of mixture components and different prior distributions to understand the sensitivity of performance to these architectural choices and identify optimal configurations for different domain characteristics.
