# Rescuing the Unpoisoned: Efficient Defense against Knowledge Corruption Attacks on RAG Systems

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2511.01268
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.01268
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary Result:** RAGDefender reduces attack success rates from 0.89 to 0.02 against Gemini model when adversarial passages outnumber legitimate ones by 4x

## Executive Summary
RAGDefender is an efficient defense system designed to protect Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems from knowledge corruption attacks. The system operates during the post-retrieval phase, employing lightweight machine learning techniques to detect and filter adversarial passages without requiring additional model training or inference. Through a two-stage approach involving passage grouping and semantic similarity analysis, RAGDefender significantly outperforms existing defenses across multiple models and adversarial scenarios.

## Method Summary
RAGDefender operates by first grouping retrieved passages using either clustering-based or concentration-based strategies, then identifying potentially adversarial content based on semantic similarity metrics. The system analyzes retrieved passages to determine which ones are likely to be adversarial without requiring additional model training or inference overhead. This post-retrieval filtering approach allows for efficient defense against knowledge corruption attacks while maintaining system performance.

## Key Results
- RAGDefender significantly reduces attack success rates from 0.89 to 0.02 against the Gemini model when adversarial passages outnumber legitimate ones by 4x
- The system demonstrates strong performance across multiple models and adversarial scenarios
- Outperforms existing defense mechanisms while maintaining computational efficiency

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
RAGDefender leverages post-retrieval filtering to identify adversarial passages based on semantic similarity patterns. By grouping passages and analyzing their semantic relationships, the system can effectively distinguish between legitimate and malicious content without requiring additional model training or inference overhead.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Retrieval System -> RAGDefender (Grouping + Filtering) -> Generation Model

**Critical Path:** Retrieved passages → Grouping strategy (clustering or concentration) → Semantic similarity analysis → Adversarial passage filtering → Clean passages to generation model

**Design Tradeoffs:** Post-retrieval defense vs. real-time overhead, semantic similarity accuracy vs. computational cost, clustering vs. concentration grouping strategies

**Failure Signatures:** High false positive rates in benign scenarios, missed adversarial passages in complex attack scenarios, performance degradation with domain-specific content

**First 3 Experiments:**
1. Baseline evaluation: Measure attack success rates without RAGDefender across multiple models
2. Controlled attack test: Evaluate RAGDefender performance when adversarial passages outnumber legitimate ones by 2x, 3x, and 4x
3. Cross-model comparison: Test RAGDefender effectiveness across different RAG models (Gemini, GPT, Claude) under identical attack scenarios

## Limitations
- Evaluation primarily focuses on controlled environments, with potential challenges in real-world deployment
- Clustering and concentration-based grouping strategies may have varying effectiveness across different domain-specific retrieval scenarios
- Reliance on semantic similarity assumes adversaries cannot easily mimic legitimate content semantics

## Confidence

**High Confidence:** Core defensive mechanism (post-retrieval filtering using semantic similarity) and effectiveness against known attack patterns

**Medium Confidence:** Generalization to unseen adversarial strategies and performance across diverse domain-specific retrieval scenarios

**Medium Confidence:** Assumption that adversaries cannot easily bypass semantic similarity detection through content manipulation
