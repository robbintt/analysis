# Causal Discovery for Linear DAGs with Dependent Latent Variables via Higher-order Cumulants

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.14780
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.14780
- Authors: Ming Cai; Penggang Gao; Hisayuki Hara
- Reference count: 40
- This paper proposes a novel algorithm to identify causal directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) in linear non-Gaussian acyclic models with latent confounders (LvLiNGAM), where causal structures exist among both latent and observed variables.

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel causal discovery algorithm for linear DAGs with dependent latent variables, addressing limitations in existing LvLiNGAM approaches. The method leverages higher-order cumulants of observed data to identify causal structures when both observed and latent variables have causal relationships. By relaxing the pure observed children assumption and handling models with causal edges among observed variables, the algorithm provides a more general solution for causal discovery in the presence of latent confounders. The approach combines Triad constraints with higher-order cumulant analysis to first identify over-segmented causal clusters, then recursively determines the causal order among latent variables, and finally estimates the complete causal structure in a bottom-up manner.

## Method Summary
The proposed algorithm operates through a three-stage process. First, it estimates over-segmented causal clusters using Triad constraints and higher-order cumulants of observed data, which helps group variables with direct causal relationships. Second, it recursively identifies the causal order among latent variables by eliminating the influence of identified sources, allowing the algorithm to build up the causal structure systematically. Finally, it estimates the complete causal structure among latent variables in a bottom-up manner, refining the initial cluster estimates to produce the final causal graph. The method specifically addresses the challenge of dependent latent variables by using higher-order cumulants, which can capture non-Gaussian features that traditional linear methods miss, making it particularly effective when causal relationships exist among both latent and observed variables.

## Key Results
- The method correctly recovers causal structures for all tested models with up to 6 variables
- Achieves F1-scores above 0.94 for latent edges and 0.8-0.97 for observed ancestral relationships
- Successfully recovers the known causal structure in the Political Democracy dataset application

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by exploiting higher-order cumulants of observed data to break symmetries and identify causal directions in the presence of latent confounders. Higher-order cumulants capture non-Gaussian features that remain informative even when latent variables influence multiple observed variables. By combining these cumulant-based techniques with Triad constraints, the algorithm can distinguish between different causal configurations that would appear identical under purely Gaussian assumptions. The recursive approach to identifying causal order among latent variables allows the method to handle the complex dependencies that arise when both latent and observed variables have causal relationships, effectively peeling away layers of influence to reveal the underlying causal structure.

Design Tradeoffs:
The method trades computational complexity for generality, allowing it to handle models where observed variables have causal relationships among themselves. This comes at the cost of increased computational requirements compared to methods that assume pure observed children. The use of higher-order cumulants provides robustness to non-Gaussian data but requires sufficient non-Gaussianity to be effective. The recursive approach ensures systematic structure building but may become computationally intensive for systems with many latent variables.

Failure Signatures:
Performance degradation occurs when latent variables have weak influences on observed variables, making higher-order cumulants difficult to estimate reliably. The method may fail or produce unreliable results with small sample sizes where cumulant estimates are unstable. If the data is approximately Gaussian, the higher-order cumulant-based approach loses its distinguishing power. Computational time increases significantly with the number of latent variables due to the recursive nature of the algorithm.

## Limitations
- Effectiveness depends on sufficient non-Gaussianity in the data, as higher-order cumulants must be non-zero
- Performance may degrade with smaller sample sizes or when latent variables have weak influences on observed variables
- Assumes linear relationships among variables, which may not hold in many real-world scenarios
