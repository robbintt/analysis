# A Unified Deep Reinforcement Learning Approach for Close Enough Traveling Problem

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.03065
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.03065
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: UD3RL outperforms conventional methods (LKH, OR-Tools, PyVRP, GA, POMO-SOCP) on CETSP in solution quality and runtime

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces UD3RL, a unified deep reinforcement learning framework for the Close-Enough Traveling Salesman Problem (CETSP), where agents must visit neighborhoods around targets rather than specific points. The method uses a dual-decoder architecture: one decoder selects the next node to visit, and another determines the precise waypoint within the chosen neighborhood. A k-nearest neighbors subgraph interaction strategy enhances spatial reasoning. UD3RL is trained with a customized REINFORCE algorithm and demonstrates strong generalization across different problem sizes and radius types. Experimental results show superior performance compared to conventional solvers in both solution quality and runtime.

## Method Summary
UD3RL formulates CETSP as a Markov Decision Process and employs a dual-decoder architecture. The first decoder selects the next node to visit, while the second decoder determines the precise waypoint within the chosen neighborhood. The method incorporates a k-nearest neighbors subgraph interaction strategy to enhance spatial reasoning. Training is performed using a customized REINFORCE algorithm. The approach demonstrates generalization capabilities across different problem sizes and radius types (constant and random), with experimental results showing superior performance compared to conventional methods.

## Key Results
- UD3RL outperforms conventional methods (LKH, OR-Tools, PyVRP, GA, POMO-SOCP) on CETSP in both solution quality and runtime
- Demonstrates strong generalization to larger problem scales, alternative node distributions, and varying radius ranges
- Shows superior performance across different radius types (constant and random)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The dual-decoder architecture enables separate reasoning for node selection and waypoint determination within neighborhoods. The k-nearest neighbors subgraph interaction strategy enhances spatial reasoning by focusing on local neighborhoods rather than the entire graph. The customized REINFORCE algorithm allows for effective policy learning in the CETSP environment. The unified framework's ability to handle varying problem sizes and radius types during training enables strong generalization capabilities.

## Foundational Learning
- **Markov Decision Process**: Framework for modeling sequential decision problems; needed for CETSP formulation
- **Dual-decoder architecture**: Separates node selection from waypoint determination; needed for precise neighborhood navigation
- **k-nearest neighbors subgraph**: Local graph representation; needed for efficient spatial reasoning
- **Customized REINFORCE algorithm**: Policy gradient method; needed for training in CETSP environment
- **Graph attention networks**: Processing graph-structured data; needed for node and edge representation learning
- **Generalization in RL**: Ability to perform on unseen problem scales; needed for practical deployment

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Graph Encoder -> Dual Decoder (Node Selector, Waypoint Selector) -> Reward Computation -> Policy Update

**Critical Path**: Input graph -> Graph encoder embedding -> Node selector decoder -> Waypoint selector decoder -> Action execution -> Reward calculation -> Policy update

**Design Tradeoffs**: Dual-decoder provides precise control but increases model complexity; k-NN subgraph improves efficiency but may miss long-range dependencies; customized REINFORCE allows flexible reward shaping but may suffer from high variance

**Failure Signatures**: Poor performance on irregular neighborhoods, failure to generalize beyond training scales, high variance in policy updates, inability to handle asymmetric cost structures

**First Experiments**: 1) Validate dual-decoder separation on simple grid environments; 2) Test k-NN subgraph effectiveness on varying neighborhood densities; 3) Evaluate generalization on 3x larger problem sizes

## Limitations
- Results focus on 2D Euclidean instances with circular neighborhoods
- Runtime comparisons do not report variability across problem instances
- Claims of outperforming conventional solvers rely on specific problem generation methods
- Limited ablation studies for the k-NN subgraph strategy

## Confidence
- High confidence in core algorithmic framework and training pipeline
- Medium confidence in comparative runtime and solution quality results
- Low confidence in generalization claims beyond tested scales and distributions

## Next Checks
1. Test generalization on problem sizes at least 3x larger than the training regime and with heterogeneous radius types not seen during training.
2. Conduct ablation studies removing the k-NN subgraph strategy and the dual-decoder architecture to quantify their individual contributions.
3. Evaluate performance on non-circular, irregular neighborhoods (e.g., elliptical or polygonal) to assess real-world applicability.
