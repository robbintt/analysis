# Frequency-Based Hyperparameter Selection in Games

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2601.18409
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.18409
- Authors: Aniket Sanyal; Baraah A. M. Sidahmed; Rebekka Burkholz; Tatjana Chavdarova
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Introduces MoLA method that uses frequency-domain analysis to adaptively select LookAhead hyperparameters, achieving O(1/T) convergence for monotone games

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of hyperparameter selection for variational inequality methods in games, where standard tuning approaches fail due to complex rotational dynamics. The authors propose Modal LookAhead (MoLA), a principled approach that leverages frequency-domain analysis of oscillatory modes to adaptively select LookAhead hyperparameters. By analyzing the spectrum of the Jacobian, MoLA chooses the depth k and averaging factor α to maximize stability and damp oscillations. The method demonstrates significant improvements in convergence speed and stability over standard methods like Extragradient, Optimistic Gradient, and LookAhead with random hyperparameters.

## Method Summary
MoLA introduces a novel frequency-based approach to hyperparameter selection for variational inequality methods in games. The method analyzes the Jacobian spectrum of the game's operator to identify oscillatory modes and their frequencies. Based on this spectral analysis, MoLA adaptively selects the LookAhead depth k and averaging factor α parameters to optimize damping of these oscillations. The approach combines theoretical analysis of monotone and Lipschitz operators with practical spectral estimation techniques to provide both convergence guarantees and effective hyperparameter tuning. The method is evaluated on bilinear and strongly convex–strongly concave games, demonstrating superior performance compared to baseline methods.

## Key Results
- MoLA achieves O(1/T) convergence rate for monotone and Lipschitz operators
- Experimental results show significant improvements in convergence speed over standard LookAhead methods
- MoLA provides better stability and damping of oscillatory behavior compared to Extragradient and Optimistic Gradient methods

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
MoLA works by leveraging frequency-domain analysis to understand and control the oscillatory dynamics inherent in game optimization problems. The key insight is that the Jacobian spectrum reveals the frequencies of dominant oscillatory modes in the system. By analyzing these frequencies, MoLA can select hyperparameters that specifically target and dampen the most problematic oscillations. The LookAhead depth k determines how far ahead the method looks in the optimization trajectory, while the averaging factor α controls how much weight is given to this lookahead information. The frequency-based selection ensures these parameters are chosen optimally for the specific game dynamics rather than through random or grid search approaches.

## Foundational Learning
- **Variational inequalities**: Mathematical framework for modeling game dynamics and equilibrium problems; needed to understand the optimization setting
- **Jacobian spectral analysis**: Technique for identifying dominant frequencies in system dynamics; needed to detect oscillatory modes
- **LookAhead methods**: Optimization techniques that use future gradient information; needed as the base method to be enhanced
- **Monotone operators**: Mathematical property ensuring convergence of optimization methods; needed for theoretical guarantees
- **Lipschitz continuity**: Smoothness condition on operators; needed for establishing convergence rates
- **Frequency-domain optimization**: Approach to selecting hyperparameters based on system dynamics; needed for adaptive tuning

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Jacobian spectrum analysis -> Frequency identification -> Hyperparameter selection -> LookAhead optimization

Critical path: The method first computes or estimates the Jacobian of the game operator, then performs spectral analysis to identify dominant frequencies, uses these frequencies to select optimal LookAhead parameters, and finally applies the LookAhead method with these parameters to achieve improved convergence.

Design tradeoffs: The method trades computational overhead from spectral analysis against improved convergence and stability. While frequency analysis provides better hyperparameter selection, it requires additional computation that may be prohibitive for very large-scale problems.

Failure signatures: The method may fail when the Jacobian spectrum cannot be accurately estimated, when the game dynamics are highly non-monotonic, or when computational resources are insufficient for spectral analysis.
