# Theoretical Closed-loop Stability Bounds for Dynamical System Coupled with Diffusion Policies

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.15520
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.15520
- Authors: Gabriel Lauzier; Alexandre Girard; François Ferland
- Reference count: 17
- Primary result: Partial diffusion policies are equivalent in stability to full diffusion policies when expert demonstration variance is below a threshold determined by system response and diffusion parameters

## Executive Summary
This work addresses the challenge of using diffusion policies in real-time robotics applications by investigating the stability of partially executed denoising processes. The core idea is to treat the plant dynamics and denoising diffusion process as coupled dynamical systems rather than decoupled ones, allowing for faster inference by taking actions before full denoising completion. The primary result establishes theoretical stability bounds showing that partial diffusion policies are equivalent in stability to full diffusion policies when the variance of expert demonstrations is below a threshold determined by system response and diffusion parameters. This yields a practical metric (σ < g√(α/A)) for ensuring stability and provides a framework for trading denoising depth against latency, making diffusion policies more viable for real-time applications.

## Method Summary
The paper proposes a novel theoretical framework that couples dynamical system modeling with denoising diffusion processes. Instead of treating the plant dynamics and denoising process as separate entities, the authors model them as interconnected dynamical systems where the state evolution depends on both the plant response and the ongoing denoising. The stability analysis is conducted using Lyapunov methods, deriving bounds that characterize when partial execution of the denoising process remains stable. The key insight is that by considering the system's response time and damping characteristics, one can determine safe operating regions where taking actions before full denoising completion doesn't compromise stability.

## Key Results
- Partial diffusion policies are theoretically equivalent in stability to full diffusion policies under certain variance conditions
- The stability threshold is characterized by the condition σ < g√(α/A), where σ is expert variance, g is the diffusion process parameter, α is related to the denoising schedule, and A is the damping coefficient
- The analysis provides a principled way to trade denoising depth against latency in real-time control applications

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanism works by recognizing that in real-time robotics applications, waiting for complete denoising completion introduces unacceptable latency. By treating the plant and diffusion process as coupled systems, the authors can analyze how partial denoising affects overall system stability. The key insight is that when the expert demonstration variance is sufficiently low relative to system damping and diffusion parameters, taking intermediate actions doesn't destabilize the system. This creates a theoretical foundation for understanding when and how much denoising can be safely skipped while maintaining control stability.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:**
Diffusion Policy Process -> Coupled System Dynamics -> Stability Analysis -> Variance Threshold Condition

**Critical Path:**
1. Expert demonstrations with variance σ
2. Diffusion process with parameters α, g
3. Plant dynamics with damping coefficient A and response time τ
4. Coupling analysis to derive stability conditions

**Design Tradeoffs:**
- Latency vs. stability: partial execution reduces latency but requires careful variance management
- Model complexity vs. tractability: coupled system model provides better accuracy but requires more complex analysis
- Theoretical guarantees vs. practical applicability: assumptions of Lipschitz continuity and noise-free conditions may not hold in real systems

**Failure Signatures:**
- Instability when σ exceeds the threshold g√(α/A)
- Oscillatory behavior if damping coefficient A is too low
- Excessive overshoot when system response time τ is not properly accounted for

**First 3 Experiments:**

2. Test stability bounds with varying damping coefficients A

## Limitations
- Assumes deterministic system dynamics with no noise corruption
- Requires Lipschitz continuous system dynamics, which may not hold for all robotic systems
- The variance threshold condition may be difficult to verify in practice without precise system identification

## Confidence
Theoretical analysis: Medium
- Assumes idealized conditions that may not hold in practice
- Relies on accurate estimation of system parameters
- Doesn't account for real-world uncertainties and noise

Practical applicability: Low
- Requires empirical validation on physical robotic systems
- May be sensitive to modeling errors in system identification
- Needs demonstration of performance in complex, real-world tasks
