# Scalable Quantum Error Mitigation with Neighbor-Informed Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2512.12578
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.12578
- Authors: Zhenyu Chen; Bin Cheng; Minbo Gao; Xiaodie Lin; Ruiqi Zhang; Zhaohui Wei; Zhengfeng Ji
- Reference count: 40
- The paper proposes Neighbor-Informed Learning (NIL), a scalable quantum error mitigation framework that unifies ZNE and PEC methods, achieving superior performance on noisy quantum circuits.

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the critical challenge of quantum noise in quantum computing by proposing Neighbor-Informed Learning (NIL), a versatile framework that unifies and strengthens existing quantum error mitigation methods. NIL learns to predict ideal quantum circuit outputs by leveraging structurally related "neighbor" circuits, using a novel 2-design training method that generates more effective training data than conventional approaches. The framework demonstrates superior accuracy and efficiency compared to existing QEM techniques through extensive numerical simulations.

The key innovation lies in the 2-design training method, which enables logarithmic scaling of training set size with the number of neighbor circuits. This makes NIL particularly suitable for large-scale quantum circuits, achieving significant performance improvements over traditional methods. The authors validate their approach on various quantum algorithms, including VQE circuits for molecular simulations, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in mean squared error at comparable training costs.

## Method Summary
NIL operates by learning a mapping from noisy outputs of structurally similar "neighbor" circuits to the ideal output of a target circuit. The framework employs a 2-design training method that generates training data by sampling from specific gate sequences rather than uniformly random Clifford gates. This structured approach creates more representative training examples that capture the essential features of quantum noise. The learning model is trained to minimize the discrepancy between predicted and actual outputs across the training set. The logarithmic scaling of training set size is achieved through careful analysis of the relationship between circuit structure and noise characteristics, enabling efficient learning even for large-scale quantum circuits.

## Key Results
- NIL achieves four-order-of-magnitude improvement in MSE for VQE circuits on LiH molecule compared to random Clifford training at same training cost
- The framework demonstrates logarithmic scaling of training set size with number of neighbor circuits
- NIL is validated on circuits with over 100 qubits and more than 20 layers, showing scalability
- Superior performance compared to various existing QEM techniques across multiple quantum algorithms

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
NIL works by exploiting the structural similarities between quantum circuits to learn noise patterns that generalize across related circuits. The 2-design training method creates more informative training data by sampling from structured gate sequences that better represent the actual noise landscape. By training on outputs from neighbor circuits, the model learns to predict ideal outputs through pattern recognition rather than direct error cancellation. The logarithmic scaling arises from the fact that the learning model can effectively interpolate between a relatively small number of strategically chosen training examples, rather than requiring exhaustive sampling of all possible circuit variations.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Quantum circuits -> Noisy outputs -> Learning model -> Ideal output prediction

Critical path: Circuit generation -> Noise injection -> Data collection -> Model training -> Prediction deployment

Design tradeoffs: NIL trades classical computational overhead for reduced quantum resource requirements, choosing structured 2-design sampling over random sampling to improve learning efficiency. The framework balances training data quality against quantity, leveraging circuit structure to minimize required samples.

Failure signatures: Poor performance on circuits with highly unstructured noise, failure to generalize when neighbor circuits are not sufficiently similar to target, and breakdown of logarithmic scaling assumptions for extremely large circuits with complex noise patterns.
