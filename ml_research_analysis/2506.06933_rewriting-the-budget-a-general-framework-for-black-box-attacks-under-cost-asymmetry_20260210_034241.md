# Rewriting the Budget: A General Framework for Black-Box Attacks Under Cost Asymmetry

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2506.06933
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.06933
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: Introduces a general framework for decision-based black-box adversarial attacks under asymmetric query costs, achieving up to 40% reduction in total query cost while producing smaller adversarial perturbations.

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a general framework for black-box adversarial attacks under cost asymmetry, where different query types incur different costs. The authors propose two key modifications—Asymmetric Search (AS) and Asymmetric Gradient Estimation (AGREST)—that can be integrated into existing black-box attack methods with minimal changes. The framework demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple attack baselines, reducing query costs and producing smaller perturbations while maintaining attack effectiveness.

## Method Summary
The framework introduces two complementary modifications to existing black-box attacks. Asymmetric Search (AS) is a cost-aware search strategy that reduces reliance on high-cost queries by splitting intervals in proportion to the cost ratio during the search process. Asymmetric Gradient Estimation (AGREST) shifts the sampling region toward low-cost query space and reweights queries to improve gradient estimation efficiency. These modifications are designed to be broadly compatible with existing black-box attack methods, requiring minimal structural changes while delivering improved performance under asymmetric cost settings.

## Key Results
- Up to 40% reduction in total query cost compared to prior approaches across standard image classification benchmarks
- Consistently smaller adversarial perturbations, potentially improving stealthiness of attacks
- Framework validated across various cost regimes and model architectures, including CLIP
- Robust performance under extreme asymmetry conditions

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by optimizing query allocation under asymmetric cost structures. AS reduces expensive query usage by proportionally adjusting search intervals based on cost ratios, while AGREST improves gradient estimation efficiency by strategically sampling in low-cost query space and applying appropriate reweighting. Together, these modifications ensure that the attack process remains effective while minimizing the use of high-cost queries, leading to reduced overall query costs and improved attack efficiency.

## Limitations
- Scalability to high-dimensional or non-image domains remains unproven as experiments are confined to standard image classification benchmarks
- Theoretical analysis establishes asymptotic equivalence but does not quantify finite-sample performance gaps or convergence rates
- Some improvements may stem from hyperparameter tuning rather than intrinsic algorithmic advantages, given the wide variation in reported gains across different attacks and cost settings

## Confidence
**High confidence**: The core claim that AS and AGREST modifications reduce query costs under asymmetric settings is well-supported by empirical results across multiple attack baselines. The compatibility with existing black-box methods is demonstrated and theoretically justified.

**Medium confidence**: Claims about robustness across extreme cost asymmetries and CLIP model validation are supported but based on limited experimental diversity. The assertion that smaller perturbations directly translate to improved stealthiness requires further validation in targeted attack scenarios.

**Low confidence**: The framework's performance in non-image domains (text, audio, graphs) is not evaluated, making claims about general applicability speculative. The theoretical bounds assume idealized conditions that may not hold in practice.

## Next Checks
1. Evaluate the framework on high-dimensional non-image domains (e.g., text classification, audio) to test scalability claims

3. Test performance under non-linear cost functions and dynamic cost regimes beyond simple proportional ratios
