# $O(\sqrt{T})$ Static Regret and Instance Dependent Constraint Violation for Constrained Online Convex Optimization

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2502.05019
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.05019
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Achieves $O(\sqrt{T})$ static regret and instance-dependent constraint violation in constrained online convex optimization

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses constrained online convex optimization (COCO) where the goal is to minimize both static regret and cumulative constraint violation (CCV) while choosing actions before observing convex cost and constraint functions. The authors propose Algorithm 2 that exploits geometric properties of nested constraint sets through projection-based online gradient descent. They demonstrate that Algorithm 2 achieves $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret and CCV bounded by a geometry-dependent quantity $V$. For specific constraint set geometries like spheres and axis-aligned cuboids, $V = O(1)$, while for general cases, $V$ depends on the shape of the constraint sets.

## Method Summary
The paper introduces two main algorithms: Algorithm 2 and Algorithm Switch. Algorithm 2 performs online gradient descent with projections onto nested constraint sets, leveraging the geometric structure to bound both regret and constraint violation. The key insight is that by carefully analyzing the projection steps onto these sets, one can achieve bounded CCV that depends on the specific geometry. Algorithm Switch combines Algorithm 2 with a previous best algorithm to achieve the optimal balance between regret and constraint violation, obtaining $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret and $\min\{V, O(\sqrt{T}\log T)\}$ CCV. For the special case of $d=2$ with monotonic projection angles, Algorithm 2 achieves $O(1)$ CCV. Additionally, for the online constraint satisfaction (OCS) problem, Algorithm 2 achieves $O(1)$ CCV.

## Key Results
- Algorithm 2 achieves $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret and CCV bounded by geometry-dependent quantity $V$
- For "nice" constraint sets (spheres, axis-aligned cuboids), $V = O(1)$; for general sets, $V$ depends on geometry
- Algorithm Switch achieves $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret and $\min\{V, O(\sqrt{T}\log T)\}$ CCV
- For $d=2$ with monotonic projection angles, Algorithm 2 achieves $O(1)$ CCV
- For OCS problem, Algorithm 2 achieves $O(1)$ CCV

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanism exploits the geometric properties of nested constraint sets. By projecting online gradient descent steps onto these sets, the algorithm can bound both regret and constraint violation. The key insight is that the constraint violation is directly related to the geometric properties of the projection operation onto the constraint sets. For well-behaved geometries, this violation remains bounded, while for general cases, it depends on how the constraint sets change over time.

## Foundational Learning
- **Online Convex Optimization**: Optimization where decisions are made sequentially without knowing future cost functions; needed for understanding the online nature of the problem; check by verifying understanding of regret minimization
- **Projected Gradient Descent**: Gradient descent with projections onto constraint sets; crucial for maintaining feasibility; verify by understanding projection properties
- **Static Regret**: Cumulative difference between algorithm's performance and best fixed decision in hindsight; key performance metric; confirm by calculating regret for simple examples
- **Cumulative Constraint Violation**: Total amount by which constraints are violated over time; another key metric; validate by tracking violations in example scenarios
- **Nested Constraint Sets**: Sequence of constraint sets where each contains the previous; enables geometric analysis; test by examining projection properties in nested structures

## Architecture Onboarding

### Component Map
Online Gradient Descent -> Projection onto Constraint Sets -> Regret Calculation -> Constraint Violation Tracking

### Critical Path
The critical path involves: (1) receiving cost and constraint functions, (2) computing gradient descent step, (3) projecting onto current constraint set, (4) updating cumulative regret and constraint violation, (5) receiving next round's functions.

### Design Tradeoffs
- Complexity vs. Performance: More sophisticated geometric analysis enables better bounds but increases computational complexity
- Generality vs. Tightness: Results for general constraint sets are less tight than for specific geometries
- Algorithm Simplicity vs. Switching Mechanism: Algorithm 2 is simpler but Algorithm Switch achieves better overall performance

### Failure Signatures
- High constraint violation indicates poorly behaved constraint set geometry
- Suboptimal regret suggests inappropriate step size or projection issues
- Switching mechanism failures occur when switching thresholds are not properly calibrated

## Limitations
- Constraint violation bound $V$ depends on constraint set geometry, which may be large for general cases
- The switching mechanism in Algorithm Switch adds complexity to the analysis
- The $O(1)$ CCV results for $d=2$ with monotonic projection angles apply to restricted scenarios
