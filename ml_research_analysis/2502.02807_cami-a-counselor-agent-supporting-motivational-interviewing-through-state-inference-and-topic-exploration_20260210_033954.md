# CAMI: A Counselor Agent Supporting Motivational Interviewing through State Inference and Topic Exploration

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2502.02807
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.02807
- Authors: Yizhe Yang; Palakorn Achananuparp; Heyan Huang; Jing Jiang; Kit Phey Leng; Nicholas Gabriel Lim; Cameron Tan Shi Ern; Ee-peng Lim
- Reference count: 40
- CAMI outperforms state-of-the-art methods in MI competency, success rate (53.1% overall), topic exploration focus, and client experience

## Executive Summary
CAMI introduces a novel counselor agent that integrates client state inference, motivation topic exploration, and strategy selection for motivational interviewing. The system employs a topic tree constructed from counseling sessions and Wikipedia, enabling exploration of diverse motivation topics to evoke change talk. CAMI demonstrates superior performance in simulated environments compared to existing methods, with particular strengths in topic exploration and maintaining counselor-like behavior.

## Method Summary
CAMI combines client state inference using language models with a topic exploration mechanism based on a hierarchical topic tree. The system uses a Graph Attention Network to infer client states from utterances and employs a policy network for strategy selection. The topic tree is constructed by extracting topics from counseling sessions and enriching them with Wikipedia content, organized hierarchically with parent-child relationships. CAMI's architecture includes an RL-based policy for response selection that balances topic exploration with counseling effectiveness.

## Key Results
- CAMI achieves 53.1% overall success rate in simulated counseling sessions
- Superior performance in MI competency compared to state-of-the-art methods
- Expert assessments show CAMI maintains realistic counselor-like behavior while achieving better topic exploration focus

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
CAMI's effectiveness stems from its integration of state inference with topic exploration. By maintaining an inferred understanding of client states and systematically exploring diverse motivation topics through a hierarchical topic tree, CAMI can more effectively evoke change talk. The combination of language model-based state inference with RL-based strategy selection allows for adaptive, context-aware counseling responses that balance exploration with therapeutic effectiveness.

## Foundational Learning
- Motivational Interviewing principles - Why needed: Core framework for counselor-agent interaction; Quick check: Can the system correctly identify and respond to change talk vs. sustain talk
- State inference using Graph Attention Networks - Why needed: To understand client emotional and motivational states from conversation; Quick check: Can the system accurately classify client statements into relevant state categories
- Hierarchical topic tree construction - Why needed: To organize and explore diverse motivation topics systematically; Quick check: Are parent-child relationships in the topic tree semantically meaningful
- RL-based policy for strategy selection - Why needed: To balance exploration and exploitation in counseling responses; Quick check: Does the policy converge to consistent, effective response patterns

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Client Utterances -> State Inference (GAT) -> Topic Tree Exploration -> Strategy Selection (RL Policy) -> Counselor Response

Critical Path: The essential sequence is Client Utterance → State Inference → Strategy Selection → Response Generation. State inference must complete before strategy selection can occur, and the selected strategy determines the response format.

Design Tradeoffs: CAMI prioritizes topic exploration over purely reactive responses, which may increase session length but improves therapeutic outcomes. The hierarchical topic tree adds complexity but enables more systematic exploration compared to flat topic structures.

Failure Signatures: Poor state inference accuracy leads to inappropriate strategy selection. Overly aggressive topic exploration can make responses feel unnatural. Insufficient exploration may result in repetitive counseling patterns that fail to evoke change talk.

## Limitations
- Evaluation relies entirely on simulated clients rather than human participants
- Topic tree construction depends heavily on Wikipedia-derived structures with limited clinical validation
- Results may not generalize to real-world counseling scenarios with unpredictable human behavior
