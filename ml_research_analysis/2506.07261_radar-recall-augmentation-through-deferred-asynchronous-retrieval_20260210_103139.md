# RADAR: Recall Augmentation through Deferred Asynchronous Retrieval

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2506.07261
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.07261
- **Reference count**: 0
- **Primary result**: +0.8% engagement lift and +6% unique item consumption via offline pre-ranking on 50X larger candidate set

## Executive Summary
RADAR introduces an offline asynchronous pre-ranking pipeline that uses the full ranking model on a 50X larger candidate set, storing top results per user for online retrieval. This approach significantly boosts recall from 8.1% to 16.5% at k=200 versus standard two-tower retrieval. Online A/B testing showed +0.8% lift in engagement and +6% unique item consumption, with minimal latency impact. RADAR is most effective for moderately active users, less so for highly active users who exhaust cached lists quickly, and ineffective for dormant users due to stale content. The method complements real-time retrieval by specializing in stable, long-term user interests.

## Method Summary
RADAR operates by running the full ranking model offline on an expanded candidate set (50X larger than standard two-tower retrieval) to generate precomputed user-specific recommendation lists. These lists are stored and retrieved online with minimal latency impact. The system focuses on stable, long-term user interests rather than real-time trends, making it particularly effective for moderately active users while showing limited benefits for highly active users who quickly exhaust cached results and dormant users with stale content.

## Key Results
- Recall improved from 8.1% to 16.5% at k=200 versus standard two-tower retrieval
- Online A/B testing demonstrated +0.8% engagement lift and +6% unique item consumption
- Minimal latency impact despite processing 50X larger candidate sets offline

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
RADAR leverages the full ranking model offline on an expanded candidate pool, capturing items that standard two-tower retrieval misses due to computational constraints. By precomputing and caching these results, the system delivers high-quality recommendations with minimal online latency. The approach assumes user preferences are relatively stable over time, allowing for effective offline processing and caching strategies.

## Foundational Learning
- **Two-tower retrieval models**: Fast online retrieval but limited recall due to computational constraints - needed because standard methods sacrifice quality for speed
- **Offline pre-ranking**: Running full ranking models on expanded candidate sets offline - needed to capture items missed by online methods
- **User segmentation**: Different user activity levels require different recommendation strategies - needed because effectiveness varies significantly across user types
- **Latency optimization**: Maintaining low online latency while improving recall - needed to preserve user experience during recommendations
- **Cache management**: Handling stale content for dormant users - needed because precomputed lists degrade over time for inactive users
- **Preference stability assumption**: Offline methods work best when user interests don't shift rapidly - needed for effective caching strategies

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map**: Candidate Generator -> Full Ranking Model -> Cache Storage -> Online Retriever

**Critical path**: Offline candidate generation and ranking -> Cache population -> Online retrieval from cache

**Design tradeoffs**: Quality vs latency (full model offline vs two-tower online), coverage vs freshness (larger candidate sets vs stale content), computation vs storage (50X candidates vs cache size)

**Failure signatures**: Highly active users exhausting cached lists quickly, dormant users receiving stale recommendations, trending content missing from cached results

**First 3 experiments**:
1. Compare recall rates between standard two-tower and RADAR approaches across different k values
2. Measure latency impact when switching from online retrieval to cached RADAR results
3. Analyze user segment effectiveness by activity level to identify optimal user groups

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
The study focuses on a single large-scale system, raising questions about generalizability to different recommendation domains. The effectiveness disparity between user segments (moderately active vs highly active vs dormant users) suggests domain-specific adaptations may be necessary. The offline pre-computation approach assumes relatively stable user preferences, but the impact of rapid preference shifts or trending content discovery remains unclear.

## Limitations
- Performance gains may not translate to different recommendation domains beyond the tested platform
- Highly active users quickly exhaust precomputed lists, limiting effectiveness
- Dormant users receive stale content due to the offline caching approach
- Rapid preference shifts or trending content may be poorly captured by static precomputed lists

## Confidence
- **High**: Offline pre-ranking mechanism and computational efficiency benefits
- **Medium**: Online A/B test results showing engagement and consumption metric improvements
- **Medium**: User segment effectiveness analysis and behavioral distinctions
