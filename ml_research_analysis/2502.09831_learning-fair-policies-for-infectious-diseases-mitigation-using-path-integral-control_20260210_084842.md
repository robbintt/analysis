# Learning Fair Policies for Infectious Diseases Mitigation using Path Integral Control

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2502.09831
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09831
- Authors: Zhuangzhuang Jia; Hyuk Park; Gökçe Dayanıklı; Grani A. Hanasusanto
- Reference count: 23
- Key outcome: Framework achieves 20% fairness improvement in disease mitigation by integrating unfairness measures into path integral control for stochastic multi-group SIR models

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for designing fairness-aware infectious disease mitigation policies using path integral control. The authors address the challenge of sequential decision-making under uncertainty by integrating various unfairness measures into disease mitigation strategies. A case study on COVID-19 demonstrates that the proposed region-specific policies significantly improve fairness across different socioeconomic regions compared to conventional homogeneous approaches, with fairness-aware policies suggesting prioritizing vaccination efforts in lower-income regions.

## Method Summary
The method formulates disease mitigation as a stochastic optimal control problem using a multi-group SIR model where each group represents different socioeconomic regions. The path integral control algorithm is employed to solve the resulting nonlinear stochastic optimal control problem by transforming it into a linear PDE through a specific relationship between control cost and noise covariance. The algorithm computes optimal vaccination and lockdown policies through Monte Carlo sampling without requiring complex optimization, generating M=1000 uncontrolled trajectories and computing optimal control as a weighted average of noise encountered.

## Key Results
- Path integral control framework achieves 20% improvement in relative fairness compared to homogeneous policies
- Fairness-aware policies shift vaccination priority to lower-income regions as unfairness penalty increases
- The approach balances disease control with equitable resource allocation while maintaining minimal additional costs

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- **Claim:** If the control cost matrix and noise covariance satisfy a specific proportionality relationship, the nonlinear Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation transforms into a linear Partial Differential Equation (PDE).
- **Mechanism:** The authors apply a logarithmic transformation to the value function ($V = -\lambda \ln \phi$). This substitution, combined with the condition $\lambda R^{-1} = \Sigma$, cancels out the nonlinear quadratic term in the HJB equation, resulting in the linear backward Chapman-Kolmogorov PDE.
- **Core assumption:** The control cost ($R$) is inversely proportional to the noise variance ($\Sigma$) via a scalar $\lambda$. This implies that controlling directions with low uncertainty is infinitely expensive.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [Section 4.1, Eq. 14-15]: Explicitly states the condition $\lambda R^{-1} = \Sigma$ and the resulting linear PDE.
  - [Remark 2]: Justifies the assumption in the context of disease control (high precision is costly).
  - [corpus]: Corpus evidence for this specific mathematical derivation is weak; related papers focus on fairness outcomes rather than the control theory derivation.
- **Break condition:** If the noise does not scale with control cost (violating $\lambda R^{-1} = \Sigma$), the PDE remains nonlinear and the path integral solution scheme fails.

### Mechanism 2
- **Claim:** The optimal control policy can be approximated as a weighted expectation of random noise trajectories, avoiding iterative gradient-based optimization.
- **Mechanism:** Using the Feynman-Kac formula, the solution to the linear PDE is represented as an expectation over trajectory costs. The algorithm samples $M$ uncontrolled trajectories (forward sampling), evaluates the cost (including fairness penalties) for each, and computes the optimal control as a weighted average of the noise encountered, where weights are $e^{-\text{Cost}/\lambda}$.
- **Core assumption:** The Monte Carlo sample size $M$ is sufficiently large to approximate the continuous expectation integral accurately.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [Section 4.1, Eq. 17]: Defines optimal control via the expectation of weighted perturbations.
  - [Section 4.2, Eq. 20]: Describes the discrete-time Monte Carlo approximation.
  - [corpus]: "Optimization of Infectious Disease Intervention Measures" [arXiv:2505.04161] uses Reinforcement Learning (RL) for similar problems, highlighting that this paper's "optimization-free" sampling is a distinct mechanism.
- **Break condition:** If the state space is high-dimensional and costs are sparse, variance in the Monte Carlo estimate may cause control instability (high variance in $u^*$).

### Mechanism 3
- **Claim:** Incorporating an economic disparity penalty into the state cost function shifts vaccination priority to lower-income regions without requiring explicit hard constraints.
- **Mechanism:** The unfairness measure $U(x(t))$ (max difference in unemployment rates) is added to the running cost. Trajectories that result in high disparity generate high costs. The path integral controller naturally deprioritizes these "expensive" trajectories, resulting in control actions (vaccination) that preemptively target regions with higher baseline risk to minimize the penalty.
- **Core assumption:** The "economic loss" parameter $w_j$ and infection/mortality rates ($\beta, \delta$) accurately reflect real-world socioeconomic disparities.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [Section 3.1, Eq. 5]: Defines the cost function with the unfairness penalty $\eta U(x(t))$.
  - [Section 5, Figure 2]: Shows vaccination shifting to lower-income regions as $\eta$ increases.
  - [corpus]: "Counterfactually Fair Reinforcement Learning" [arXiv:2501.06366] similarly addresses socioeconomic bias, supporting the premise that standard optimization exacerbates disparities without explicit penalties.
- **Break condition:** If the penalty weight $\eta$ is set too high, the controller may implement excessively strict lockdowns or resource allocations that, while "fair," cause total economic collapse (Pareto frontier trade-off).

## Foundational Learning

- **Concept: Stochastic Optimal Control & HJB Equation**
  - **Why needed here:** The paper formulates disease mitigation as a stochastic control problem. Understanding the HJB equation is necessary to understand why the problem is initially "hard" (nonlinear PDE) and why the "path integral" transformation is required.
  - **Quick check question:** Can you explain why solving a nonlinear PDE becomes intractable as the state dimension (number of regions * 4) increases?

- **Concept: Control-Affine Systems**
  - **Why needed here:** The SIR dynamics (Eq. 2) are nonlinear in state but affine in control ($f(x) + G(x)u$). This structure is a prerequisite for applying the path integral control derivation.
  - **Quick check question:** Identify the term in the dynamics that makes the system nonlinear in the state (hint: interaction of Susceptible and Infected).

- **Concept: Monte Carlo Integration & Importance Sampling**
  - **Why needed here:** The core solution relies on approximating an expectation (integral) using random samples (Eq. 20).
  - **Quick check question:** How does the "importance" of a trajectory relate to its cost in this specific formulation (hint: exponential weighting)?

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:** Parameter Store -> Dynamics Engine -> Cost Evaluator -> Aggregator
- **Critical path:** The calculation of the exponentiated cost $\exp(-\frac{1}{\lambda}\hat{J}_t)$ is the numerical bottleneck. If $\lambda$ is small or costs are high, exponentials can underflow; numerical stability handling (e.g., log-sum-exp trick) is critical.
- **Design tradeoffs:**
  - **Sample Count ($M$) vs. Latency:** Higher $M$ yields smoother, more optimal control but linearly increases compute time per step (Appendix B).
  - **Fairness ($\eta$) vs. Efficiency:** Increasing $\eta$ improves the fairness metric but increases total economic loss (Fig 3).
- **Failure signatures:**
  - **Controller passivity:** If $\lambda$ is misconfigured relative to the cost scale, the exponential weights become uniform, resulting in near-zero control (random drift).
  - **Bias amplification:** If the unfairness penalty $\eta$ is 0 and initial infections are skewed, the optimal policy may "sacrifice" high-risk regions to save the economy of productive regions (greedy cost minimization).
- **First 3 experiments:**
  1. **Validation of $\lambda$ constraint:** Run the simulator with varying $\lambda$ and noise $\Sigma$ to confirm the control law holds only when $\lambda R^{-1} \approx \Sigma$ (sanity check of Eq. 14).
  2. **Sensitivity Analysis ($M$):** Sweep the number of trajectories (e.g., $M \in \{100, 500, 1000\}$) to find the point of diminishing returns for control stability.
  3. **Fairness/Efficiency Frontier:** Reproduce the Pareto frontier (Fig 3) by sweeping $\eta \in [0, 0.1]$ to observe the trade-off between total cost and the unfairness metric.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- **Question:** How does the framework perform with alternative unfairness measures (e.g., Concentration Index, Theil Index) beyond the pairwise comparison measure used in the case study?
- **Basis in paper:** [explicit] The authors state: "this paper focuses solely on the unfairness measure defined in Definition 1... Nonetheless, our framework is flexible to accommodate other unfairness measures."
- **Why unresolved:** Different unfairness measures capture distinct aspects of inequality; their impact on optimal policy structure and computational tractability under path integral control remains unexplored.
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Comparative experiments using alternative unfairness measures, analyzing resulting policy differences and computational costs.

### Open Question 2
- **Question:** How do non-zero cross-group infection rates (β_{jk} > 0) affect the fairness-cost trade-off and optimal vaccination prioritization?
- **Basis in paper:** [explicit] "For simplicity, cross-group infection rates are simplified to β_{ij} = 0 (no interactions between regions)."
- **Why unresolved:** Real-world populations have significant inter-region contact; ignoring this may underestimate spillover effects where vaccinating one group protects others, potentially altering fairness-optimal allocations.
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Sensitivity analysis varying cross-group infection rates, measuring changes in optimal policies and fairness outcomes.

### Open Question 3
- **Question:** Can the assumption λR^{-1} = Σ be relaxed while maintaining computational tractability, and how does violating this condition affect solution quality?
- **Basis in paper:** [inferred] The assumption (14) enables the linearization critical to path integral control, but Remark 2 notes it implies "in directions with low noise, control is expensive, with the cost approaching infinity as the variance of noise tends to zero."
- **Why unresolved:** This constraint couples noise and control cost structures arbitrarily, potentially limiting realistic parameter choices where noise variance and control costs are independently determined.
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Theoretical analysis or empirical tests with decoupled parameters using alternative solution methods as benchmarks.

## Limitations
- The path integral control framework critically relies on the proportionality condition between control cost and noise covariance, which is difficult to validate in real-world epidemic settings
- The model assumes perfect observability of region-specific infection and mortality states, which is rarely achievable in practice
- The assumption that economic loss weights and infection parameters accurately reflect real-world disparities may not hold across different contexts

## Confidence
- **High confidence:** The mathematical derivation of the path integral control solution and its application to the stochastic multi-group SIR model is well-founded and internally consistent
- **Medium confidence:** The claim of achieving "20% fairness improvement" is supported by the presented simulations, but robustness across different conditions is not extensively explored
- **Low confidence:** The assumption that economic loss weights and infection parameters accurately reflect real-world disparities may limit generalizability

## Next Checks
