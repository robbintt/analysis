# The Social Cost of Intelligence: Emergence, Propagation, and Amplification of Stereotypical Bias in Multi-Agent Systems

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.10943
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.10943
- Authors: Thi-Nhung Nguyen; Linhao Luo; Thuy-Trang Vu; Dinh Phung
- Reference count: 22
- Multi-agent systems exhibit vulnerability to bias emergence, propagation, and amplification, but cooperative protocols and robust underlying LLMs can mitigate these effects

## Executive Summary
This study systematically examines how stereotypical bias emerges, propagates, and amplifies in multi-agent systems where multiple large language models interact and collaborate. The research reveals that while MAS are generally less robust than single-agent systems, with bias often emerging early through in-group favoritism, cooperative and debate-based communication protocols can effectively mitigate bias amplification. The findings demonstrate that social groups in MAS exhibit both in-group and out-group stereotypes, expressing them more strongly under competitive conditions, similar to human societies.

## Method Summary
The study employs simulation-based experiments using Vicuna LLM architectures to examine bias dynamics in multi-agent systems. Researchers create controlled social contexts where agents represent different social groups and analyze how inherent LLM biases evolve through inter-agent communication. The methodology systematically evaluates bias emergence, propagation, and amplification under various communication protocols, including cooperative and debate-based scenarios. Bias measurement relies on the Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count (LIWC) tool, and the study tests vulnerability to bias injection attacks while evaluating potential defense mechanisms.

## Key Results
- MAS are generally less robust than single-agent systems, with bias often emerging early through in-group favoritism
- Cooperative and debate-based communication protocols can effectively mitigate bias amplification
- Social groups exhibit both in-group and out-group stereotypes, expressing them more strongly under competition
- MAS show vulnerability to bias injection attacks, but adding more neutral agents improves resilience

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The emergence and propagation of bias in MAS occurs through inter-agent communication where individual LLM biases interact and compound. In-group favoritism emerges as agents preferentially share and reinforce biases about their own social groups. Cooperative protocols work by encouraging agents to consider multiple perspectives before forming judgments, while debate-based protocols force critical examination of biases through adversarial discussion. The vulnerability to bias injection attacks stems from the interconnected nature of MAS, where a single compromised agent can influence the entire network.

## Limitations
- Simulation-based methodology may not capture real-world multi-agent interaction complexity
- Findings based on specific LLM architectures (Vicuna) may not generalize to all systems
- Bias measurement relies on LIWC tool, potentially missing subtle or context-dependent biases
- Focus on English-language LLMs limits applicability to multilingual contexts

## Confidence
- High confidence: Cooperative and debate-based communication protocols effectively mitigate bias amplification
- Medium confidence: Vulnerability to bias injection attacks and effectiveness of adding neutral agents as defense
- Medium confidence: Observation that social groups exhibit both in-group and out-group stereotypes
- Low confidence: Broad generalization that MAS are "generally less robust than single-agent systems"

## Next Checks
