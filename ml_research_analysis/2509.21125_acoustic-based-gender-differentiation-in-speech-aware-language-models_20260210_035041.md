# Acoustic-based Gender Differentiation in Speech-aware Language Models

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2509.21125
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21125
- **Reference count**: 37
- **Key outcome**: SpeechLMs exhibit male-oriented responses in gender-stereotypical contexts while showing gender-agnostic responses in gender-dependent contexts where gender consideration would be appropriate

## Executive Summary
This study systematically analyzed acoustic-based gender differentiation in SpeechLMs using a dataset of 9,208 speech samples across three categories: Gender-Independent, Gender-Stereotypical, and Gender-Dependent. Results revealed a paradoxical pattern where all models consistently exhibited male-oriented responses in Gender-Stereotypical questions (statistically significant p < 0.001), while showing gender-agnostic responses in Gender-Dependent questions where gender consideration would be contextually appropriate. Through experiments with neutral response options, gender-neutralized speech inputs, and comparisons with backbone LLMs, the study confirmed that these biases primarily stem from male-oriented acoustic tokens generated by the Whisper encoder. This indicates current SpeechLMs prioritize general fairness principles over contextual appropriateness, highlighting the need for more sophisticated techniques to properly utilize gender information in speech technology.

## Method Summary
The study employed a three-category question framework (Gender-Independent, Gender-Stereotypical, Gender-Dependent) to evaluate gender bias in SpeechLMs. Researchers conducted experiments using 9,208 speech samples with various interventions including neutral response options, gender-neutralized speech inputs, and comparisons with backbone LLMs. The analysis focused on identifying patterns of male-oriented responses and determining whether bias originated from the Whisper encoder's acoustic token generation. Statistical significance testing (p < 0.001) was used to validate findings across different model types and experimental conditions.

## Key Results
- All tested SpeechLMs exhibited statistically significant male-oriented responses in Gender-Stereotypical questions (p < 0.001)
- SpeechLMs showed gender-agnostic responses in Gender-Dependent questions where gender consideration would be contextually appropriate
- Experiments confirmed that gender bias primarily stems from male-oriented acoustic tokens generated by the Whisper encoder

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanism underlying gender differentiation in SpeechLMs operates through the interaction between acoustic input processing and language generation. The Whisper encoder converts speech signals into acoustic tokens, which then influence the language model's understanding of speaker characteristics, including gender. When these acoustic tokens carry gender biases, particularly male-oriented patterns, the subsequent language generation process propagates these biases into the model's responses. The study demonstrates that this acoustic-to-text pathway is the primary vector for gender differentiation, rather than biases in the language model architecture itself or in the training data distribution.

   - Quick check: Verify that different speakers produce distinct acoustic token patterns in the encoder output

   - Why needed: The architecture determines how acoustic information influences text generation decisions
   - Quick check: Confirm that acoustic tokens flow through to the language model's attention mechanisms

   - Quick check: Test whether neutralizing acoustic tokens reduces gender bias in responses

   - Why needed: Distinguishing between situations where gender-neutral vs. gender-aware responses are appropriate

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Speech Input -> Whisper Encoder -> Acoustic Tokens -> SpeechLM -> Generated Response

**Critical Path**: The acoustic tokenization process represents the critical path for gender bias introduction. When Whisper generates male-oriented acoustic tokens, this information flows directly into the SpeechLM's language generation process, creating downstream bias effects that cannot be easily corrected without addressing the source.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The study reveals a fundamental tension between general fairness principles (promoting gender neutrality) and contextual appropriateness (recognizing when gender information should be utilized). Current SpeechLMs appear to err on the side of neutrality, even when context suggests gender-aware responses would be more appropriate. This conservative approach may avoid overt stereotyping but fails to capture nuanced gender-related contexts.

**Failure Signatures**: Male-oriented responses in gender-stereotypical contexts and gender-agnostic responses in gender-dependent contexts serve as clear indicators of bias propagation through the acoustic tokenization pathway. These patterns suggest the model is not properly utilizing gender information from acoustic inputs.

**First Experiments**:
1. Compare gender bias patterns across different Whisper model versions to isolate version-specific acoustic token generation differences
2. Test SpeechLMs with manually corrected acoustic tokens to determine if bias reduction is possible without architectural changes
3. Evaluate cross-linguistic gender bias patterns to determine if the phenomenon is language-specific or universal

## Limitations
- The study's dataset of 9,208 speech samples may not fully represent the complexity of real-world gender-related speech interactions
- The three-category question framework may oversimplify the nuanced ways gender information should be handled in different contexts
- Attribution of bias primarily to Whisper encoder acoustic tokens may overlook other contributing factors in the SpeechLM architecture

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The experimental observation that all tested SpeechLMs exhibited male-oriented responses in Gender-Stereotypical questions (p < 0.001) is well-supported by the statistical analysis provided. The consistency across different models strengthens this claim.

- **Medium Confidence**: The conclusion that SpeechLMs prioritize general fairness principles over contextual appropriateness is supported by the experimental design but relies on the assumption that gender-neutral responses in Gender-Dependent contexts are universally inappropriate. This may vary based on cultural and contextual factors not fully explored in the study.

- **Medium Confidence**: The attribution of gender bias primarily to male-oriented acoustic tokens from the Whisper encoder is supported by the neutral response and gender-neutralization experiments, but the study does not fully explore alternative sources of bias or conduct ablation studies to isolate the Whisper encoder's specific contribution.

## Next Checks

1. **Dataset Expansion and Diversity Testing**: Replicate the study using a significantly larger and more diverse dataset of speech samples, including varied accents, languages, and cultural contexts to assess whether the observed gender bias patterns hold across broader real-world conditions.

2. **Cross-Model Encoder Comparison**: Conduct controlled experiments comparing SpeechLMs using different speech encoders (e.g., Whisper vs. other ASR models) to isolate the specific contribution of the Whisper encoder to the observed male-oriented acoustic token generation and resulting gender bias.

3. **Contextual Appropriateness Validation**: Perform human evaluation studies where participants assess the appropriateness of SpeechLM responses in Gender-Dependent contexts, comparing model outputs against expert-determined gold standards to validate whether the models' gender-agnostic responses are indeed contextually inappropriate.
