# There Is More to Refusal in Large Language Models than a Single Direction

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2602.02132
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.02132
- Reference count: 39
- Primary result: Linear steering interventions flatten mechanistic diversity in LLM refusal behaviors into uniform behavioral control

## Executive Summary
This paper investigates whether distinct types of refusal and non-compliance behaviors in large language models are mediated by a single activation-space direction or multiple distinct mechanisms. Through contrastive activation analysis and sparse autoencoder (SAE) latent space interventions across 11 refusal categories, the authors demonstrate that while refusal directions are geometrically distinct, steering along any of them produces nearly identical trade-offs between refusal and over-refusal rates. This reveals that linear interpretability methods collapse mechanistic diversity into a shared one-dimensional control knob, highlighting their limitations for understanding alignment-relevant behaviors.

## Method Summary
The authors compute refusal directions for 11 categories of non-compliance using contrastive activation differences at position -2 (token before assistant response) in the residual stream. They apply both linear steering (adding scaled refusal directions to activations) and sparse autoencoder-based interventions to test causal roles. Four datasets with 11 balanced splits (32/32 non-compliant/benign prompt pairs each) are used, with Gemma-2-9b-it and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct models. The SAE analysis identifies a structured internal representation: a small core of shared refusal latents supplemented by style- and domain-specific latents, explaining why geometrically distinct directions behave similarly under linear intervention.

## Key Results
- Refusal directions across 11 categories are geometrically distinct but produce identical trade-offs between refusal and over-refusal rates under linear steering
- SAE analysis reveals a structured internal representation: small reusable core of shared refusal latents + long tail of style-specific latents
- Linear steering interventions collapse mechanistic diversity into uniform behavioral control, flattening the structured representation

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Linear steering interventions act as a shared one-dimensional control knob that uniformly increases or decreases refusal behavior across all categories. The geometrically distinct refusal directions in activation space are actually different linear combinations of the same underlying refusal features identified by SAE. This explains why steering along any direction produces similar behavioral effects - it's activating the shared core refusal latents regardless of the geometric path taken.

## Foundational Learning
- **Activation-space direction computation**: Needed to quantify geometric differences between refusal behaviors. Quick check: Compute cosine similarity between directions from different categories.
- **Sparse autoencoder latents**: Required to identify underlying feature structure. Quick check: Verify SAE reconstruction accuracy > 0.8 for test activations.
- **Contrastive activation differences**: Essential for isolating refusal-specific patterns. Quick check: Confirm refusal vs benign mean activation differences are statistically significant.
- **Linear steering intervention**: Core experimental manipulation. Quick check: Validate that α=0 produces baseline behavior and α=60 maximizes refusal rate.
- **Refusal classification**: Needed to measure behavioral effects. Quick check: Test WildGuard classifier accuracy on held-out labeled responses.

## Architecture Onboarding

### Component Map
Gemma-2-9b-it -> Residual stream (layer 20, pos -2) -> Refusal direction computation -> Steering intervention -> WildGuard classification

### Critical Path
Activation extraction at position -2 → Direction computation via mean differences → Linear steering with parameter α → Response generation → WildGuard classification for behavioral measurement

### Design Tradeoffs
- **Position selection**: -2 chosen as best indicator of refusal intent vs. earlier layers capturing more generic refusal patterns
- **Steering strength**: Multiple α values tested to map full behavioral spectrum, with α=60 providing strongest effect
- **Dataset balancing**: 32/32 splits ensure equal representation but may limit statistical power for rare refusal types

### Failure Signatures
- Steering produces incoherent outputs → Reduce α or verify direction normalization
- No increase in refusal rates → Check benign prompts are truly benign, verify steering applied at every token
- SAE poor reconstruction → Confirm correct layer/position and JumpReLU activation logic

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: Do the identified multi-directional refusal mechanisms and the shared core of latent features persist across larger model scales, base (non-instruction-tuned) models, or models trained with substantially different alignment procedures?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors state in the Limitations section: "We do not evaluate whether the observed refusal mechanisms persist in larger models, base (non–instruction-tuned) models, or systems trained with substantially different alignment procedures."
- Why unresolved: The study restricts its analysis to two specific instruction-tuned architectures (Gemma-2-9b-it and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct).
- What evidence would resolve it: Replicating the activation-space geometry and SAE latent overlap analysis on base models or larger-scale architectures (e.g., 70B+ parameters).

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can non-linear or highly granular interventions successfully decouple refusal rate from refusal style?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper concludes that linear steering "flattens mechanistic differences" into a uniform behavioral control, failing to separate the decision to refuse from the style of refusal.
- Why unresolved: The authors demonstrate a limitation of *linear* interpretability and steering but do not propose or test methods that might navigate the structured internal representation more delicately.
- What evidence would resolve it: Developing an intervention method that targets specific "long-tail" style latents identified via SAE without activating the "core" refusal latents, thereby changing the refusal tone without altering the refusal rate.

### Open Question 3
- Question: What is the causal relationship between the "shared refusal core" latents and the "long tail" of style-specific latents during generation?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper identifies a structural composition of refusal (a small core vs. a long tail) but notes that linear interventions collapse this structure, leaving the interaction dynamics between core and style features unclear.
- Why unresolved: While the paper establishes correlation and overlap, it does not fully map the causal circuit determining if the core latents trigger the style latents or if they operate in parallel.
- What evidence would resolve it: Ablating specific style-latents while retaining core refusal latents to see if the model defaults to a generic refusal style, confirming the functional hierarchy.

## Limitations
- Analysis restricted to two specific instruction-tuned architectures (Gemma-2-9b-it and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct)
- Does not evaluate persistence of mechanisms across larger models, base models, or different alignment procedures
- Linear steering interventions may not capture full complexity of refusal mechanisms

## Confidence
- Geometric distinctiveness of refusal directions → High
- Similar behavioral effects under steering → High
- SAE-based explanation for observed phenomena → High
- Exact quantification of "small core" vs "long tail" → Medium
- Generalizability to other model architectures → Low
