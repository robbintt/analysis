# Multimodal Representation-disentangled Information Bottleneck for Multimodal Recommendation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2509.20225
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.20225
- Authors: Hui Wang; Jinghui Qin; Wushao Wen; Qingling Li; Shanshan Zhong; Zhongzhan Huang
- Reference count: 27
- Primary result: Introduces MRdIB framework achieving 8.47% average improvement in recall@5 and 8.27% in NDCG@5 over six baselines

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces MRdIB, a novel framework for multimodal recommendation systems that addresses the challenge of filtering irrelevant information and disentangling complex information structures from multiple modalities. The framework combines a Multimodal Information Bottleneck (MIB) to compress input representations and remove noise with Partial Information Decomposition (PID) to decompose the remaining information into unique, redundant, and synergistic components. By optimizing specific learning objectives for each component type, MRdIB preserves modality-unique signals, minimizes redundant information overlap, and captures synergistic information that emerges only when combining modalities.

The proposed approach demonstrates significant improvements over six baseline models across three benchmark datasets, with an average 8.47% improvement in recall@5 and 8.27% in NDCG@5. The framework shows particular effectiveness on simpler models like VBPR, achieving up to 27.23% gains. This represents a substantial advancement in multimodal recommendation systems by providing a theoretically grounded method for managing information flow and representation quality across different modalities.

## Method Summary
MRdIB employs a two-stage approach to improve multimodal recommendation. First, it applies a Multimodal Information Bottleneck (MIB) to compress input representations and remove noise, effectively filtering out irrelevant information. Second, it uses Partial Information Decomposition (PID) to decompose the remaining information into three components: unique information specific to each modality, redundant information shared across modalities, and synergistic information that emerges only from the combination of modalities. The framework then optimizes distinct learning objectives for each component type, preserving modality-unique signals while minimizing redundant overlap and capturing synergistic relationships. This systematic decomposition and optimization process enables more effective utilization of multimodal information in recommendation tasks.

## Key Results
- Achieved an average 8.47% improvement in recall@5 across six baseline models
- Achieved an average 8.27% improvement in NDCG@5 across six baseline models
- Demonstrated up to 27.23% performance gains on simpler models like VBPR
- Consistent improvements observed across three benchmark datasets

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The MRdIB framework works by addressing the fundamental challenge of information redundancy and noise in multimodal recommendation systems. By applying the Information Bottleneck principle, it compresses input representations to retain only the most relevant information for the recommendation task. The Partial Information Decomposition then systematically separates information into modality-specific, shared, and synergistic components, allowing the model to optimize each type differently. This prevents the model from being overwhelmed by redundant information while ensuring it captures the complementary aspects that emerge when combining modalities. The optimization objectives are designed to preserve unique modality characteristics, minimize unnecessary information overlap, and maximize the synergistic benefits of multimodal fusion.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Raw Input -> Multimodal Information Bottleneck (MIB) -> Partial Information Decomposition (PID) -> Component-specific Optimization -> Recommendation Output

Critical Path: The framework's critical path flows from raw multimodal inputs through the MIB for compression, then through PID for decomposition into three information components, followed by separate optimization objectives for each component type, ultimately producing the final recommendation output. The MIB and PID stages are particularly crucial as they determine the quality and structure of information available for the downstream optimization and recommendation tasks.

Design Tradeoffs: The framework balances computational complexity against information preservation, as the PID decomposition and multiple optimization objectives increase computational overhead. The choice to decompose information into three components rather than using simpler fusion approaches trades off some efficiency for more nuanced control over information flow. The MIB's compression level must be carefully tuned to avoid losing task-relevant information while still filtering noise effectively.

Failure Signatures: Potential failures include over-compression in the MIB stage leading to loss of critical information, incorrect decomposition in the PID stage resulting in misclassification of information types, or imbalanced optimization objectives causing one component type to dominate. The framework may also struggle with highly imbalanced modality contributions or when synergistic information is minimal across the input modalities.

Three First Experiments:
1. Conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (MIB, PID, and optimization objectives) to overall performance, quantifying the marginal benefit of each innovation.
2. Test the framework on datasets with varying levels of modality imbalance to assess robustness when one modality dominates or is sparse.
3. Evaluate the framework's sensitivity to hyperparameter choices, particularly the compression level in MIB and the balance between optimization objectives for different information components.

## Limitations
- Evaluation focuses on relatively simple recommendation tasks without considering real-world complexity factors like data sparsity, cold-start scenarios, or temporal dynamics
- Reported improvements may be partially attributed to specific experimental setup rather than fundamental advantages of the MRdIB approach
- Framework's computational overhead and scalability with larger datasets and more complex modalities remain unexplored
