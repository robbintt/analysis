## Method Summary
The paper presents a learning-based method for learning a hierarchy of complex locomotion controllers for a four-legged robot. The method involves two phases: pretraining with a large dataset of motion capture data to produce a kinematic trajectory generator, followed by training a low-level controller to track the kinematic motion while handling environmental constraints. The final phase involves learning a high-level controller to adapt to the environment, such as stepping over gaps.

## Key Results
The key results include the robot's ability to run over challenging terrain, including stepping over gaps and stairs, without prior knowledge of the gap locations. The robot can also adapt its gait to match the terrain, such as running up stairs with the same gait as running over gaps. The method achieves a success rate of 90% in traversing 0.2m gaps.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by leveraging a large dataset of motion capture data to learn a kinematic trajectory generator. This generator is then used to train a low-level controller that can track the kinematic motion while handling environmental constraints. The high-level controller adapts to the environment by adjusting the kinematic motion to suit the terrain. The use of a separate trajectory generator allows for easier tuning of the gait and ensures stability during learning.

## Limitations
The limitations of the method include the need for a large dataset of motion capture data, the computational cost of training the controllers, and the potential for overfitting to the training data. Additionally, the method may not generalize well to different robot morphologies or dynamic environments.

## Confidence
The confidence in the method is moderate. While the results are promising, the method relies heavily on a large dataset of motion capture data, which may not be readily available for all robots or environments. Additionally, the computational cost of training the controllers may be prohibitive for some applications.
