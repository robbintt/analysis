# Interpretable Machine Learning Model for Early Prediction of Acute Kidney Injury in Critically Ill Patients with Cirrhosis: A Retrospective Study

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2508.10233
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.10233
- Reference count: 40
- Developed LightGBM model with AUROC 0.808 and NPV 0.911 for AKI prediction in cirrhotic ICU patients

## Executive Summary
This study developed a machine learning model to predict acute kidney injury (AKI) in critically ill patients with cirrhosis using data from the first 48 hours of ICU admission. The model identified key predictors including prolonged partial thromboplastin time, absence of outside-facility 20G placement, low pH, and altered pO2. With high performance metrics, the model enables early AKI detection and risk stratification, supporting timely nephroprotective interventions in ICU settings.

## Method Summary
The study applied a robust pipeline incorporating missingness filtering, LASSO feature selection, and SMOTE balancing to a cohort of 1,240 patients from the MIMIC-IV database. LightGBM achieved the highest performance with an AUROC of 0.808 (95% CI: 0.741-0.856) and NPV of 0.911. The approach combined interpretable feature analysis with strong predictive performance, identifying clinically relevant predictors that align with known cirrhosis-AKI pathophysiology.

## Key Results
- LightGBM achieved AUROC of 0.808 (95% CI: 0.741-0.856) and NPV of 0.911
- Identified prolonged partial thromboplastin time, absence of outside-facility 20G placement, low pH, and altered pO2 as key predictors
- Model supports early AKI detection and risk stratification for safe de-escalation of low-risk patients

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The model leverages interpretable machine learning to identify clinically meaningful predictors of AKI in cirrhotic patients, enabling actionable interventions through early detection and risk stratification.

## Limitations
- Single-center data source (MIMIC-IV) may limit generalizability to other populations
- Absence of external validation cohorts prevents assessment of broader applicability
- Retrospective design prevents causal inference and requires prospective verification
