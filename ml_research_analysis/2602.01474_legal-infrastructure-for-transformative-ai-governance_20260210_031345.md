# Legal Infrastructure for Transformative AI Governance

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2602.01474
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.01474
- Authors: Gillian K. Hadfield
- Reference count: 0
- Primary result: Proposes AI model registration, AI agent registration, and regulatory markets as legal infrastructure for AI governance

## Executive Summary
The paper addresses the challenge of governing transformative AI by arguing that traditional regulatory approaches are inadequate for this rapidly evolving technology. The author proposes three key innovations: AI model registration regimes to enhance state oversight, AI agent registration regimes to ensure accountability in legal and economic transactions, and regulatory markets that leverage private sector expertise to translate government-set outcomes into technical compliance standards. These proposals aim to build effective AI governance infrastructure in the face of technological uncertainty and rapid change.

## Method Summary
The paper presents a theoretical analysis of AI governance challenges and proposes novel legal infrastructure solutions. It synthesizes existing regulatory frameworks and governance literature to develop the three-pronged approach of model registration, agent registration, and regulatory markets. The proposals are presented as conceptual frameworks rather than tested implementations, focusing on the structural requirements for effective AI oversight in a transformative AI context.

## Key Results
- Traditional regulatory approaches are insufficient for transformative AI governance
- Three infrastructure innovations proposed: model registration, agent registration, and regulatory markets
- Registration regimes would require developers to disclose information about frontier models and agents
- Regulatory markets would license private regulators to set technical standards aligned with government goals
- Infrastructure-focused proposals address both technical and democratic deficits in AI governance

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The proposed legal infrastructure works by creating multiple layers of oversight and accountability for AI systems. Model registration regimes provide governments with visibility into frontier AI development, while agent registration ensures accountability for AI systems that interact with legal and economic systems. Regulatory markets address the expertise gap by allowing governments to set outcome-based goals while private regulators translate these into technically feasible standards. This multi-pronged approach balances government oversight with technical feasibility and democratic legitimacy.

## Foundational Learning
- **Model Registration Regimes**: Government oversight of AI development; needed because state agencies lack technical expertise to directly regulate complex AI systems; quick check: assess current capabilities for tracking frontier AI models
- **AI Agent Registration**: Accountability for AI systems in legal/economic transactions; needed because AI agents blur lines of legal responsibility; quick check: examine existing frameworks for autonomous agent oversight
- **Regulatory Markets**: Private sector standard-setting under government oversight; needed because government agencies lack technical capacity for detailed technical standards; quick check: analyze existing regulatory market examples in other sectors
- **Legibility Gap**: The challenge of governments understanding complex AI systems; needed to bridge the technical expertise gap; quick check: evaluate current state of AI transparency requirements
- **Outcome-Based Regulation**: Government sets goals, private entities determine technical means; needed to leverage private sector expertise while maintaining democratic control; quick check: review existing examples of outcome-based regulation

## Architecture Onboarding
- **Component Map**: Model Registration -> Agent Registration -> Regulatory Markets
- **Critical Path**: Government goal-setting -> Private regulator licensing -> Technical standard development -> Compliance verification
- **Design Tradeoffs**: Democratic oversight vs. technical expertise; centralized vs. distributed governance; mandatory vs. voluntary compliance
- **Failure Signatures**: 
  - Registration evasion through jurisdictional arbitrage
  - Regulatory capture of private regulators
  - Technical standards that fail to achieve intended safety outcomes
- **First Experiments**:
  1. Pilot model registration regime for frontier AI labs
  2. Test agent registration framework with autonomous vehicle companies
  3. Simulated regulatory market for AI safety standards development

## Limitations
- Technical standards for model registration remain undefined and may be difficult to establish
- Practical challenges of maintaining accurate, up-to-date registries of rapidly evolving AI systems
- Democratic legitimacy concerns regarding outsourcing regulatory standard-setting to private entities
- Limited evidence from real-world implementations or pilot programs to validate effectiveness

## Confidence
- **Medium** for identification of governance challenges (well-supported by existing literature)
- **Medium-Low** for regulatory markets proposal (largely theoretical with limited validation)
- **Medium** for general concept of registration regimes (supported by precedent but untested for AI)
