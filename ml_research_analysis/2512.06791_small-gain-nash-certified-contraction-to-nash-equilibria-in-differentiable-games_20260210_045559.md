# Small-Gain Nash: Certified Contraction to Nash Equilibria in Differentiable Games

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2512.06791
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.06791
- **Reference count**: 40
- **Primary result**: Introduces Small-Gain Nash (SGN), a structural condition certifying convergence to Nash equilibria in differentiable games by designing a block-weighted metric where the pseudo-gradient is strongly monotone.

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces Small-Gain Nash (SGN), a structural condition that certifies convergence to Nash equilibria in differentiable games by designing a block-weighted metric in which the pseudo-gradient becomes strongly monotone. SGN converts player-wise curvature and cross-player coupling bounds into a tractable matrix inequality that guarantees contraction in a tailored geometry. The method provides explicit step-size bounds for both projected Euler and RK4 discretizations, and identifies a finite "timescale band" where a single-step-size dynamics is provably contractive—contrasting with asymptotic TTUR approaches. Empirically, SGN successfully certifies convergence in non-monotone quadratic games where Euclidean analysis fails, and extends to mirror/Fisher geometries for entropy-regularized policy gradient in Markov games.

## Method Summary
Small-Gain Nash (SGN) introduces a block-weighted metric that transforms the pseudo-gradient dynamics into a strongly monotone system, guaranteeing convergence to a Nash equilibrium. The method leverages player-wise curvature (Hessian bounds) and cross-coupling constants to construct a metric matrix whose positivity certifies global contraction. SGN yields explicit step-size bounds for both projected Euler and RK4 discretizations and identifies a finite timescale band for safe single-step-size updates. An offline pipeline estimates local curvature, coupling, and Lipschitz parameters, then optimizes block weights to maximize the SGN margin, producing a computable convergence certificate.

## Key Results
- SGN certifies convergence in non-monotone quadratic games where Euclidean analysis fails.
- Provides explicit step-size bounds for projected Euler and RK4 discretizations.
- Identifies a finite "timescale band" for provably contractive single-step-size dynamics, contrasting with asymptotic TTUR methods.
- Extends to mirror/Fisher geometries for entropy-regularized policy gradient in Markov games.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
SGN works by transforming the multi-agent dynamics into a geometry where the pseudo-gradient is strongly monotone, leveraging player-wise curvature and coupling bounds. By designing a block-weighted metric, SGN converts the game's non-monotonicity into a contraction mapping, ensuring global convergence to Nash equilibria. The method exploits structural properties of differentiable games—specifically, the block-separability of the pseudo-gradient—to certify contraction even when the Euclidean geometry is insufficient.

## Foundational Learning

- **Strongly monotone operators**: Key for proving convergence; needed to ensure the pseudo-gradient drives the system to a unique equilibrium.
- **Block-weighted metrics and metric design**: Allows tailoring the geometry to the game's structure, enabling contraction where standard metrics fail.
- **Matrix inequalities and SGN condition**: The tractable matrix inequality encodes the SGN condition; quick check: verify matrix positivity for given game parameters.
- **Discretization schemes (Euler, RK4)**: Needed to translate theoretical guarantees into practical algorithms; quick check: confirm step-size bounds are respected in implementation.

## Limitations
- Empirical validation is limited to a few non-monotone quadratic games; scalability to high-dimensional or non-convex settings is untested.
- Relies on accurate estimation of bounded parameters (curvature, coupling, Lipschitz), which may be brittle under noise or incomplete data.
- The SGN matrix inequality may be conservative, especially with heterogeneous player timescales or sparse coupling.
- Extension to mirror/Fisher geometries is theoretically grounded but not thoroughly validated beyond entropy-regularized policy gradient.

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: Mathematical derivation of SGN condition and its equivalence to strong monotonicity in the block-weighted metric.
- **Medium confidence**: Step-size bounds for projected Euler and RK4 schemes, and the identification of the timescale band.
- **Medium confidence**: Offline certification pipeline and empirical success in non-monotone quadratic games.
