# Foundation Models for Scientific Discovery: From Paradigm Enhancement to Paradigm Transition

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2510.15280
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.15280
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Foundation models are catalyzing a transition from enhancing scientific paradigms to enabling autonomous scientific discovery across three stages: integration, hybrid co-creation, and autonomous agents.

## Executive Summary
This paper proposes a conceptual framework for understanding how foundation models (FMs) are transforming scientific discovery beyond mere task enhancement. The authors argue that FMs are catalyzing a paradigm transition from traditional scientific methods toward a new era where FMs become autonomous scientific agents. They present a three-stage framework tracking this evolution: Meta-Scientific Integration (FMs as infrastructure), Hybrid Human-AI Co-Creation (FMs as collaborators), and Autonomous Scientific Discovery (FMs as independent agents). The paper reviews current FM applications across experimental, theoretical, computational, and data-driven paradigms while identifying key risks including bias, hallucination, reproducibility challenges, and ethical concerns around accountability and authorship.

## Method Summary
The paper presents a conceptual framework based on literature review and analysis of current foundation model applications in scientific discovery. The authors systematically examine how FMs are being integrated across different scientific paradigms (experimental, theoretical, computational, and data-driven) and identify patterns of adoption and transformation. They analyze the trajectory of FM development from simple task automation to potential autonomous scientific agents, supported by examples from various scientific domains. The framework is developed through synthesis of existing research on FM capabilities, limitations, and emerging applications in scientific contexts.

## Key Results
- Foundation models are evolving through three distinct stages: from enhancing traditional workflows as infrastructure, to becoming active collaborators in scientific reasoning, to potentially operating as autonomous discovery agents
- Current FM applications span all four scientific paradigms, with increasing integration across traditional disciplinary boundaries
- Key risks include bias propagation, hallucination in generated scientific content, reproducibility challenges, and ethical concerns around accountability and authorship in human-AI collaborative science
- Future directions include embodied agents, closed-loop autonomy systems, and continual learning approaches for scientific discovery

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by mapping the evolution of foundation models from passive tools to active scientific participants. As FMs develop increasingly sophisticated reasoning capabilities, multimodal understanding, and autonomous planning abilities, they transition from being mere computational infrastructure to genuine scientific collaborators and potentially independent agents. This transformation is driven by FMs' ability to process vast scientific literature, generate hypotheses, design experiments, and integrate knowledge across traditionally separate scientific paradigms. The mechanism operates through progressive integration where FMs move from supporting roles to central positions in scientific workflows, enabled by advances in few-shot learning, reasoning capabilities, and cross-domain knowledge transfer.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Scientific Paradigms → Foundation Model Capabilities → Integration Patterns → Risk Assessment → Governance Framework
- Foundation Models (LLMs, Multimodal Models) -> Integration Infrastructure (APIs, Tools, Datasets)
- Integration Infrastructure -> Scientific Workflows (Experimental, Theoretical, Computational, Data-driven)
- Scientific Workflows -> Risk Analysis (Bias, Hallucination, Reproducibility)
- Risk Analysis -> Governance and Ethical Framework

**Critical Path:** Model Capabilities → Integration Infrastructure → Scientific Workflow Enhancement → Risk Assessment → Governance Framework
The critical path focuses on developing robust FM capabilities, integrating them into existing scientific workflows, assessing associated risks, and establishing appropriate governance structures.

**Design Tradeoffs:** Autonomy vs. Human Oversight, Performance vs. Explainability, Innovation vs. Safety, Integration vs. Specialization
- High autonomy enables faster discovery but increases risk of unreproducible results
- Deep integration across paradigms enables novel insights but complicates validation
- Autonomous agents promise efficiency but raise accountability questions

**Failure Signatures:** Hallucinated scientific facts, biased hypothesis generation, unreproducible results, attribution ambiguity
- Systematic bias in generated hypotheses due to training data limitations
- Unverifiable claims that appear scientifically plausible but lack empirical support
- Difficulty attributing credit or responsibility for scientific discoveries

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
The paper raises several critical open questions about the future of foundation models in scientific discovery, particularly around the transition to autonomous scientific agents. Key questions include how to establish accountability frameworks when FMs generate novel scientific knowledge, what governance structures are needed for human-AI co-created research, and how to ensure reproducibility when FMs are central to discovery processes. The paper also questions how traditional scientific paradigms will evolve as FMs enable cross-paradigm integration and whether current evaluation metrics are sufficient for assessing FM contributions to scientific discovery.

## Limitations
- The three-stage framework remains largely theoretical with limited empirical validation across diverse scientific domains
- Claims about paradigm transition are speculative, as most current applications demonstrate enhancement rather than autonomous discovery
- Risk assessment focuses on qualitative concerns without quantitative evaluation or systematic measurement approaches
- Future directions toward embodied agents and closed-loop autonomy lack demonstrated feasibility in real scientific contexts
