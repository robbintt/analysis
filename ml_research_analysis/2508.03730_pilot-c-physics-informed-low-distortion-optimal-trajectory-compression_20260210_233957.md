# PILOT-C: Physics-Informed Low-Distortion Optimal Trajectory Compression

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2508.03730
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.03730
- Authors: Kefei Wu; Baihua Zheng; Weiwei Sun
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: Physics-informed DCT-based compression achieves 19.2% better ratios than state-of-the-art while reducing average error by 32.6%

## Executive Summary
PILOT-C introduces a novel trajectory compression framework that treats movement paths as physical signals rather than geometric curves. By applying Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) to each spatial dimension independently, the method separates meaningful low-frequency motion patterns from high-frequency noise. This physics-informed approach achieves superior compression performance compared to traditional line simplification methods while maintaining computational efficiency and extending naturally to 3D and higher-dimensional trajectories.

## Method Summary
The framework models trajectories as multi-dimensional time-series signals using DCT, transforming spatial coordinates into frequency components. Low-frequency components representing significant motion patterns are retained while high-frequency noise is discarded. The method processes each spatial dimension independently, applies physics-informed truncation based on frequency significance, and includes optimized storage structures with post-compression validation to ensure error bounds are met. This approach fundamentally differs from geometric line simplification by focusing on the physical nature of motion rather than curve approximation.

## Key Results
- Achieves 19.2% better compression ratios than CISED-W (state-of-the-art SED-based line simplification)
- Reduces average error by 32.6% compared to competing methods
- For 3D trajectories, outperforms SQUISH-E by 49% in compression ratios while maintaining identical computational complexity

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The physics-informed approach works by recognizing that real-world trajectories consist of smooth, low-frequency motion patterns (intentional movement) overlaid with high-frequency noise (measurement error, micro-movements). DCT naturally separates these components by decomposing the signal into frequency domains where low frequencies represent the underlying motion and high frequencies represent noise. By truncating high-frequency components while preserving low-frequency ones, PILOT-C retains the essential trajectory information while achieving significant compression. This mathematical separation is more principled than geometric simplification because it directly addresses the physical nature of the data.

## Foundational Learning
- **Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT)**: Mathematical transform that converts spatial/time data into frequency components; needed because it efficiently separates smooth motion patterns from noise in trajectories; quick check: verify that low-frequency DCT coefficients capture major trajectory features
- **Frequency-domain signal processing**: Framework for analyzing signals in terms of their frequency components rather than time/space; needed because it provides the theoretical foundation for separating meaningful motion from noise; quick check: confirm that high-frequency truncation preserves trajectory semantics
- **Multi-dimensional time-series modeling**: Approach to treating trajectories as signals across multiple spatial dimensions; needed because it enables independent processing of each dimension while maintaining their relationships; quick check: ensure that joint reconstruction maintains spatial coherence
- **Physics-informed truncation**: Method of determining which frequency components to retain based on their physical significance; needed because it provides a principled way to balance compression ratio against error bounds; quick check: validate that truncation thresholds preserve motion patterns
- **Compression error metrics**: Quantitative measures of fidelity loss during compression; needed because they provide objective comparison between different compression approaches; quick check: verify that error metrics align with practical usability requirements

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Raw Trajectory -> DCT Transform -> Frequency Component Analysis -> Physics-Informed Truncation -> Optimized Storage -> Post-Compression Validation -> Compressed Trajectory

**Critical Path**: The critical path is the DCT transformation and physics-informed truncation sequence, as these operations directly determine the compression ratio and error characteristics. The transform must complete before truncation decisions can be made, and truncation must complete before storage optimization can occur.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The primary tradeoff is between compression ratio and reconstruction fidelity. More aggressive high-frequency truncation yields better compression but increases error. The physics-informed approach attempts to optimize this tradeoff by retaining components that contribute meaningfully to the physical motion. Storage optimization adds implementation complexity but provides significant space savings.

**Failure Signatures**: Compression failures manifest as either excessive error (when too many low-frequency components are discarded) or insufficient compression (when too many high-frequency components are retained). The post-compression validation step catches both conditions by enforcing error bounds. Datasets with highly erratic, noise-like motion patterns may not compress well because meaningful information is distributed across many frequencies.

**First 3 Experiments**: 1) Apply PILOT-C to synthetic trajectories with known frequency characteristics to verify that low-frequency components capture intended motion patterns; 2) Test compression performance on 2D GPS trajectory datasets with varying noise levels to validate error bound enforcement; 3) Extend to 3D trajectory compression and compare against SQUISH-E on benchmark datasets to verify the 49% improvement claim.

## Limitations
- Relies heavily on the assumption that DCT-based frequency decomposition adequately captures all meaningful trajectory patterns across diverse datasets
- Comparison with only one SED-based method (CISED-W) and one 3D-specific method (SQUISH-E) may not fully represent the state-of-the-art landscape
- The 19.2% and 49% improvement metrics require independent verification across different dataset types and sizes

## Confidence

**Confidence Levels:**
- High confidence: The DCT-based compression methodology and its mathematical foundations are well-established and correctly applied
- Medium confidence: The experimental results and comparative performance metrics, as they are based on a limited number of datasets and comparison methods
- Medium confidence: The claim of maintaining computational complexity while improving compression ratios, as this depends on implementation details not fully disclosed

## Next Checks

3. Perform ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component (DCT transformation, physics-informed truncation, storage optimization) to the overall compression performance
