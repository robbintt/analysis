# Acquisition of interpretable domain information during brain MR image harmonization for content-based image retrieval

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.14535
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.14535
- Reference count: 23
- Primary result: Proposed PL-SE-ADA framework improves brain MR image harmonization with interpretable disentanglement of domain-invariant and domain-specific components

## Executive Summary
This study addresses the challenge of domain shifts in brain MR images caused by scanner and protocol differences, which degrade machine learning performance in medical imaging tasks. The authors propose Pseudo-Linear-Style Encoder Adversarial Domain Adaptation (PL-SE-ADA), a framework that disentangles brain MR images into domain-invariant and domain-specific components to enhance interpretability. The framework improves upon existing methods by separately reconstructing these components and linearly combining them, enabling visualization of both brain features and domain-specific variations. The approach demonstrates improved performance in image reconstruction, disease classification, and domain recognition while providing high interpretability across the entire framework.

## Method Summary
The PL-SE-ADA framework employs a novel disentanglement approach using pseudo-linear-style encoders to separate brain MR images into domain-invariant (brain anatomy) and domain-specific (scanner/protocol) components. The architecture uses adversarial training to ensure the domain-invariant component contains only anatomical information while the domain-specific component captures scanner-related variations. These components are then linearly combined (x_u + αx_d) to reconstruct the original image. The framework is trained end-to-end using reconstruction loss, adversarial loss, and classification loss to optimize both harmonization and interpretability. The model uses gradient reversal layers to align the domain-invariant features across different scanner types while maintaining separation of domain-specific information.

## Key Results
- Image reconstruction achieves RMSE of 0.0991 and SSIM of 0.729
- Disease classification performance reaches F1-score of 0.875
- Domain recognition capability achieves F1-score of 0.512
- Framework provides interpretable visualization of disentangled components

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework succeeds by explicitly separating anatomical information from scanner-specific variations through adversarial training and pseudo-linear-style encoding. By forcing the domain-invariant component to contain only brain anatomy while the domain-specific component captures scanner characteristics, the model can effectively harmonize images across different domains. The linear combination of these components allows for controlled reconstruction while maintaining interpretability of each component's contribution.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Input Image -> Pseudo-Linear-Style Encoder -> Domain-Invariant & Domain-Specific Components -> Linear Combination -> Decoder -> Reconstructed Image
                          ↓
                      Domain Classifier (for adaptation)

**Critical Path**: The critical path flows through the pseudo-linear-style encoder for disentanglement, followed by linear combination and decoder for reconstruction, with adversarial training applied to the domain-invariant component.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The framework trades some reconstruction fidelity (RMSE 0.0991 vs baseline 0.086) for interpretability and domain disentanglement. The linear combination approach (α=0.2) provides interpretability but may not capture complex nonlinear relationships between components.

**Failure Signatures**: Poor reconstruction quality indicates inadequate disentanglement or suboptimal α weighting. Low domain recognition F1-score (0.512) suggests the domain-specific component may not capture sufficient scanner information. Inconsistent disease classification performance indicates the domain-invariant component may retain unwanted domain-specific information.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
### Open Question 1
- Question: Does the PL-SE-ADA framework generalize effectively to datasets with significantly higher domain heterogeneity or a larger number of acquisition sites?
- Basis in paper: The authors explicitly note the "current evaluation used a limited dataset with few domains" and identify "broader experiments" as a necessary focus for future work.
- Why unresolved: The validation was restricted to only two specific datasets (ADNI2 and ADNI3), limiting claims of robustness against diverse scanner types.
- What evidence would resolve it: Successful harmonization results on a multi-center dataset comprising more than three distinct scanner protocols or manufacturers.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can the image reconstruction fidelity be improved to match baseline levels without sacrificing the model's domain disentanglement capability?
- Basis in paper: The authors list "improving image reconstruction quality" as a future focus; Table II shows PL-SE-ADA underperforms the Baseline and SE-ADA in RMSE (0.0991) and SSIM (0.729).
- Why unresolved: The proposed linear combination of latent components appears to introduce a trade-off that lowers reconstruction accuracy compared to standard autoencoders.
- What evidence would resolve it: A modification to the decoder or loss function that achieves RMSE comparable to the baseline (e.g., < 0.086) while maintaining a domain classification F1-score near 0.5.

### Open Question 3
- Question: Is the weighting hyperparameter α stable across different datasets, or does it require re-tuning for specific scanner combinations?
- Basis in paper: The study fixes α=0.2 based on preliminary tests, but Table III shows performance metrics fluctuate notably as α changes.
- Why unresolved: It is unclear if α=0.2 is a universal constant or if it merely fits the specific intensity distributions of the ADNI2/ADNI3 pair used in the study.
- What evidence would resolve it: A sensitivity analysis demonstrating that a single value of α yields near-optimal performance across multiple independent domain adaptation tasks.

## Limitations
- Limited dataset size with only 350 subjects from two cohorts restricts generalizability
- Focus exclusively on T1-weighted MRI scans, excluding other important modalities
- Image reconstruction quality (RMSE 0.0991, SSIM 0.729) remains below baseline levels

## Confidence
- Performance improvements over baseline methods: Medium confidence - reported metrics show improvement but limited comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods
- Interpretability of disentangled components: Low confidence - visualizations provided but lack quantitative validation of interpretability
- Applicability to medical applications: Medium confidence - framework shows promise but needs validation on larger, more diverse datasets
