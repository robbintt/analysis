# MasHost Builds It All: Autonomous Multi-Agent System Directed by Reinforcement Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2506.08507
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.08507
- Authors: Kuo Yang; Xingjie Yang; Linhui Yu; Qing Xu; Yan Fang; Xu Wang; Zhengyang Zhou; Yang Wang
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: 5.86% absolute improvement on GSM8K benchmark

## Executive Summary
MasHost introduces a reinforcement learning-based framework for fully autonomous construction of multi-agent systems (MAS). The key innovation lies in formulating MAS design as a graph search problem, where agent roles and their interactions are simultaneously determined through a joint probabilistic sampling mechanism. This approach eliminates the need for manual MAS design while maintaining cost efficiency and structural rationality through the Hierarchical Relative Policy Optimization (HRPO) algorithm.

The framework demonstrates consistent outperformance across six benchmarks, with up to 5.86% absolute improvement on GSM8K. HRPO combines group-relative advantages for balancing accuracy and efficiency with action-wise rewards for structural rationality. The system's ability to autonomously discover effective MAS architectures while ensuring role and structure rationality represents a significant advancement in the field of automated multi-agent system design.

## Method Summary
MasHost treats MAS construction as a graph search problem where nodes represent potential agent roles and edges represent possible interactions. The framework employs a joint probabilistic sampling mechanism that simultaneously selects both agent roles and their interconnections, guided by reinforcement learning objectives. The Hierarchical Relative Policy Optimization (HRPO) algorithm is central to this process, combining group-relative advantages to balance exploration and exploitation across different agent configurations, while incorporating action-wise rewards to ensure the resulting MAS structures maintain rational agent interactions and role assignments.

## Key Results
- Achieved 5.86% absolute improvement on GSM8K benchmark
- Outperformed state-of-the-art baselines across all six tested benchmarks
- Maintained cost efficiency while ensuring role and structure rationality
- Demonstrated effectiveness on diverse task types including GSM8K, MATH, MMLU, GPQA, MBPP, and HumanEval

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
MasHost's effectiveness stems from its graph-based formulation of MAS construction combined with hierarchical policy optimization. By treating agent role selection and interaction design as a unified graph search problem, the framework can explore the full space of possible MAS architectures simultaneously rather than sequentially optimizing individual components. The HRPO algorithm's dual approach—using group-relative advantages for efficient exploration while maintaining action-wise rewards for structural validity—enables the discovery of both effective and interpretable MAS designs.

## Foundational Learning
- **Graph Search in MAS Design**: Why needed: Traditional MAS construction requires manual specification of agent roles and interactions, limiting scalability and adaptability. Quick check: Can the graph representation capture all valid MAS configurations for a given task?
- **Probabilistic Sampling Mechanism**: Why needed: Deterministic selection methods can get trapped in local optima when exploring complex MAS design spaces. Quick check: Does the sampling mechanism maintain sufficient diversity across multiple construction episodes?
- **Hierarchical Relative Policy Optimization**: Why needed: Standard RL approaches struggle to balance global MAS structure optimization with local interaction rationality. Quick check: Are group-relative advantages improving convergence speed compared to standard policy gradients?
- **Action-wise Reward Design**: Why needed: Ensures that discovered MAS structures are not just effective but also interpretable and maintainable. Quick check: Do learned structures exhibit meaningful agent role specialization?
- **Multi-Task Benchmarking**: Why needed: Validates that the framework generalizes across different problem domains rather than overfitting to specific task types. Quick check: Is performance improvement consistent across all six benchmark categories?

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Graph Search -> Probabilistic Sampling -> HRPO -> MAS Construction

Critical Path: Input Task Specification -> Graph Search Initialization -> Joint Role/Interaction Sampling -> HRPO Policy Update -> MAS Structure Output

Design Tradeoffs: The framework prioritizes autonomy and generality over construction speed, accepting higher computational overhead during the design phase to eliminate manual intervention and enable adaptation to new task types.

Failure Signatures: Construction failures typically manifest as either overly complex MAS structures with redundant agents or overly simplistic structures that cannot capture task requirements. Performance degradation often indicates insufficient exploration of the graph search space.

Three First Experiments:
1. Task Specification Test: Provide the framework with a simple decomposition task and verify it can identify appropriate agent roles without prior knowledge
2. Structure Validation: Run the probabilistic sampling mechanism on a known optimal MAS structure to confirm it can rediscover established designs
3. Baseline Comparison: Construct MAS for a simple task using both MasHost and a manual design approach to quantify automation benefits

## Limitations
- Evaluation limited to six benchmarks, potentially missing edge cases in real-world MAS design
- Graph search formulation may face exponential complexity as agent role and interaction spaces grow
- Computational overhead during construction phase not thoroughly analyzed
- Limited analysis of how learned MAS structures generalize to tasks outside training distribution
