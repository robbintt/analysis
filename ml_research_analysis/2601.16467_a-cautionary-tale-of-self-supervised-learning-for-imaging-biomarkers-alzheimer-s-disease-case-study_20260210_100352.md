# A Cautionary Tale of Self-Supervised Learning for Imaging Biomarkers: Alzheimer's Disease Case Study

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2601.16467
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.16467
- Authors: Maxwell Reynolds; Chaitanya Srinivasan; Vijay Cherupally; Michael Leone; Ke Yu; Li Sun; Tigmanshu Chaudhary; Andreas Pfenning; Kayhan Batmanghelich
- Reference count: 40
- One-line primary result: R-NCE outperforms traditional FreeSurfer features and existing SSL methods for Alzheimer's disease classification, conversion prediction, and amyloid status prediction

## Executive Summary
This study demonstrates that existing self-supervised learning (SSL) methods underperform traditional FreeSurfer-derived imaging biomarkers for Alzheimer's disease classification, conversion prediction, and amyloid status prediction. To address this limitation, the authors introduce Residual Noise Contrastive Estimation (R-NCE), a new SSL framework that integrates auxiliary FreeSurfer features while maximizing additional augmentation-invariant information. R-NCE outperforms both traditional features and existing SSL methods across multiple benchmarks, including AD conversion prediction (p < 0.0001 for 2- and 3-year prediction). The derived Brain Age Gap measures show high heritability and associations with MAPT and IRAG1 genes, indicating sensitivity to neurodegenerative and cerebrovascular processes.

## Method Summary
The study compares SSL methods against FreeSurfer features for Alzheimer's disease biomarkers using T1-weighted MRI data from UK Biobank (30,815 subjects), OASIS-3 (1,316 subjects), and ADNI (2,431 subjects). R-NCE extends noise contrastive estimation by applying contrastive learning in a residual space orthogonal to auxiliary FreeSurfer features. The method uses 18 region-specific 3D CNN patch encoders followed by a Transformer aggregator. Training occurs in two stages: patch-level first, then image-level aggregator. The R-NCE loss combines feature reconstruction and contrastive learning in the residual space. For evaluation, frozen representations are used with linear probes on ADNI tasks, and Brain Age Gap measures are derived for genetic association studies.

## Key Results
- R-NCE outperforms traditional FreeSurfer features and existing SSL methods for AD conversion prediction (p < 0.0001 for 2- and 3-year prediction)
- R-NCE-BAG shows high heritability (h² = 0.42) and associations with MAPT and IRAG1 genes
- BAG variants map to 153 genes with enrichment in astrocytes and oligodendrocytes, indicating sensitivity to neurodegenerative and cerebrovascular processes

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
R-NCE outperforms generic SSL by explicitly partitioning representation space into auxiliary-aligned and residual subspaces. The architecture enforces that FreeSurfer features are first reconstructed from the representation, then applies contrastive learning in the orthogonal residual space. This guarantees FS information is preserved while allowing discovery of complementary signals. If FS features are noisy or poorly harmonized across scanners, the reconstruction loss may lock in artifacts rather than signal.

### Mechanism 2
Generic contrastive SSL underperforms FS because standard augmentations do not enforce disease-relevant invariances. SimCLR and Barlow Twins maximize mutual information between random augmentations, but these augmentations are agnostic to AD-relevant anatomical changes. The learned invariances may discard subtle atrophy patterns that FS thickness/volume measures capture explicitly. If domain-specific augmentations (e.g., simulated atrophy patterns) were designed, generic SSL might recover FS-equivalent sensitivity—this is not tested in the paper.

### Mechanism 3
R-NCE-derived Brain Age Gap captures genetically heritable neurodegeneration signal enriched in non-neuronal cell types. R-NCE representations predict chronological age; the residual (BAG = predicted - true age) indexes accelerated brain aging. GWAS on R-NCE-BAG identifies loci whose open chromatin overlaps with astrocyte and oligodendrocyte regulatory elements, suggesting glial involvement in brain aging. If age prediction models overfit to dataset-specific biases (e.g., scanner effects), BAG heritability could reflect artifact rather than biology.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:** T1w MRI -> 18 patch encoders (3D CNN, 13 conv layers, ELU, batch norm, 64-dim) -> Transformer aggregator (3 blocks, 2 heads) -> R-NCE loss

- **Critical path:** 1) Preprocess T1w MRI with FreeSurfer recon-all → extract patches + FS features 2) Train patch encoders with R-NCE (FS features per region as auxiliary) 3) Freeze patch encoders → train aggregation transformer with image-level R-NCE 4) For downstream tasks: extract frozen representations → fit linear classifier/regressor

- **Design tradeoffs:** Separate patch encoders vs. shared allows parallel training and region-specific specialization but increases parameter count; frozen evaluation vs. fine-tuning uses frozen representations for fair comparison; linear probe vs. nonlinear chosen for interpretability and direct comparison with FS features

- **Failure signatures:** If R-NCE underperforms FS on MCI vs. CN (p=0.003) but not AD vs. CN: Suggests residual contrastive signal is more relevant to advanced disease; if BAG heritability drops significantly on external dataset: Possible overfitting to UKB population characteristics; if patch-level FS feature alignment fails: Check harmonization (ComBat) and region-to-feature mapping consistency

- **First 3 experiments:** 1) Reproduce generic SSL underperformance: Train SimCLR on UKB+OASIS, evaluate on ADNI with linear probe for AD vs. CN; confirm AUROC gap vs. FS features 2) Ablate residual contrastive component: Train R-NCE variant with only reconstruction loss (no L_NCE on R'); expect performance drop to near-FS levels 3) Validate BAG heritability claim: Compute R-NCE-BAG on held-out UKB subjects; estimate h² using GCTA and compare to reported 0.42

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

- **Open Question 1:** Can the Residual Noise Contrastive Estimation (R-NCE) framework improve biomarker sensitivity for non-Alzheimer's neurological conditions like Parkinson's disease or psychosis? The study restricted its evaluation to Alzheimer's disease datasets (ADNI). What evidence would resolve it: Application of R-NCE to structural MRI datasets containing Parkinson's or psychosis labels, comparing performance against FreeSurfer features.

- **Open Question 2:** Does the R-NCE framework provide performance benefits when applied to alternative imaging modalities such as diffusion MRI (dMRI) or PET? The current experiments only utilized T1-weighted structural MRI. What evidence would resolve it: Pre-training R-NCE on dMRI or PET data using modality-specific auxiliary features (e.g., mean diffusivity, uptake values) and evaluating downstream task performance.

- **Open Question 3:** Does end-to-end fine-tuning of R-NCE representations yield significant performance improvements over the frozen, linear-probe setting? The study utilized frozen representations to provide a fair assessment of generalizability and avoid overfitting. What evidence would resolve it: A comparison of transfer learning performance between frozen linear probes and full fine-tuning strategies on the disease classification tasks.

- **Open Question 4:** What is the functional mechanism driving the association between the IRAG1 gene variant, astrocytes, and brain aging? The paper provides statistical association (GWAS) but lacks molecular validation of the pathway. What evidence would resolve it: Molecular or cellular studies investigating the expression and functional impact of the IRAG1 variant in astrocytes and its interaction with platelets.

## Limitations

- Dataset Composition and Bias: The SSL pretraining combines UKB (healthy middle-aged adults) with OASIS-3 (older adults with dementia), creating a heterogeneous age distribution that may influence learned invariances.

- Augmentation Domain Specificity: While R-NCE incorporates FS features, the augmentations remain generic. The paper does not test whether domain-specific augmentations could achieve similar FS feature sensitivity.

- Heritability Interpretation: The reported BAG heritability (h² = 0.42) is based on UKB subjects. Without external validation, it's unclear whether this reflects true biological signal or dataset-specific confounding.

## Confidence

- **High:** SSL methods underperform FS features on ADNI classification tasks; R-NCE outperforms both on 2- and 3-year conversion prediction (p < 0.0001).
- **Medium:** R-NCE-BAG shows significant heritability and genetic associations; FS features contain disease-relevant information not captured by generic augmentations.
- **Low:** BAG associations with astrocyte/oligodendrocyte cell types definitively indicate glial involvement in neurodegeneration; generic SSL cannot achieve FS-equivalent sensitivity even with domain-specific augmentations.

## Next Checks

1. External Validation of BAG Heritability: Compute R-NCE-BAG on an independent dataset (e.g., AIBL, ENIGMA-AD) and replicate heritability estimates using identical GCTA pipeline.

2. Ablation of Reconstruction Loss: Train R-NCE variants with only reconstruction loss (no L_NCE on R') or only contrastive loss (no reconstruction) to quantify each component's contribution to performance gains.

3. Domain-Specific Augmentation Test: Implement disease-relevant augmentations (simulated atrophy, ventricle expansion) in a SimCLR baseline and evaluate whether generic SSL can match FS performance when trained with AD-specific invariances.
