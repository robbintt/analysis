# Hierarchical Vision-Language Reasoning for Multimodal Multiple-Choice Question Answering

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2508.16148
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.16148
- Reference count: 40
- Best ensemble model achieves Public Score of 0.59 on Japanese PDF understanding benchmark

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of multimodal multiple-choice question answering on Japanese PDF documents with complex layouts and lengthy content. The authors propose a hierarchical vision-language reasoning framework that combines ColQwen-based document retrieval with sub-question decomposition and semantic verification. The approach retrieves relevant pages, decomposes questions into sub-questions with structured reasoning traces, and employs multilingual prompts to mitigate language bias. Experimental results on a Japanese PDF understanding benchmark show that the proposed method achieves superior performance compared to existing models.

## Method Summary
The proposed method employs a hierarchical vision-language reasoning framework that processes Japanese PDF documents through a multi-stage pipeline. It begins with ColQwen-based document retrieval to identify relevant pages, followed by sub-question decomposition that breaks down complex questions into manageable components with structured reasoning traces. The framework then performs semantic verification to ensure consistency across answers and uses multilingual prompts to address language bias issues. The entire system is designed to handle the unique challenges of Japanese PDF documents, including complex layouts and extensive textual content.

## Key Results
- Proposed method achieves superior performance compared to existing models on Japanese PDF understanding benchmark
- Best ensemble model reaches Public Score of 0.59
- Demonstrates improved accuracy and robustness for complex document understanding tasks

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The hierarchical approach works by systematically breaking down complex document understanding tasks into manageable components. ColQwen-based retrieval efficiently identifies relevant document sections, while sub-question decomposition allows the model to focus on specific aspects of complex questions. The semantic verification step ensures that answers are consistent and coherent across different parts of the document, preventing contradictory conclusions. Multilingual prompts help mitigate language-specific biases that could affect the model's reasoning process.

## Foundational Learning

1. **Multimodal multiple-choice QA** - Why needed: Handles questions requiring both visual and textual understanding from PDF documents. Quick check: Model can correctly answer questions that combine layout information with textual content.

2. **ColQwen-based retrieval** - Why needed: Efficiently locates relevant document sections among lengthy PDF content. Quick check: Retrieval precision and recall metrics on document sections.

3. **Sub-question decomposition** - Why needed: Breaks complex questions into simpler components for better reasoning. Quick check: Accuracy improvement when using decomposed vs. full questions.

4. **Semantic verification** - Why needed: Ensures consistency across answers from different document sections. Quick check: Reduction in contradictory or inconsistent answers.

5. **Multilingual prompt engineering** - Why needed: Mitigates language bias specific to Japanese documents. Quick check: Performance stability across different Japanese dialects or writing styles.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** PDF Document -> ColQwen Retrieval -> Sub-question Decomposition -> Semantic Verification -> Answer Generation

**Critical Path:** Document retrieval and sub-question decomposition form the core pipeline, with semantic verification providing quality control before final answer generation.

**Design Tradeoffs:** The framework prioritizes accuracy through hierarchical processing over computational efficiency, potentially limiting real-time applications. The use of ColQwen-based retrieval balances precision with computational cost.

**Failure Signatures:** Performance degradation on documents with extremely dense layouts, failure to correctly decompose questions with ambiguous intent, and potential language bias issues despite multilingual prompts.

**Three First Experiments:**
1. Evaluate retrieval precision on a subset of documents with known relevant sections
2. Test sub-question decomposition accuracy on questions of varying complexity
3. Assess semantic verification effectiveness by introducing controlled inconsistencies
