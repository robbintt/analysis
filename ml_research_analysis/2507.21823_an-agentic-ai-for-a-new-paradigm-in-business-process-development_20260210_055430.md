# An Agentic AI for a New Paradigm in Business Process Development

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2507.21823
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.21823
- Authors: Mohammad Azarijafari; Luisa Mich; Michele Missikoff
- Reference count: 10
- Primary result: Agent-based approach shifts business process development from task-oriented to goal-driven models using autonomous AI agents

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces an agent-based approach to business process development using Agentic AI, shifting from traditional task-oriented workflows to goal-driven models. Instead of fixed task sequences, business processes are structured around business goals, information objects, and autonomous AI agents. The method enables more modular and context-aware automation by allowing agents to independently pursue goals, collaborate when needed, and adapt dynamically to changing environments. Goals are represented as states of business objects, with agents defined by their goals, trigger objects, capabilities (CRUDA operations), and final outputs.

## Method Summary
The paper presents a formal model for agent-based business process development where processes are specified declaratively through goals and objects rather than imperative task sequences. Agents are autonomous entities defined by their capabilities (CRUDA operations), trigger objects that activate them, resource objects they consume, final objects they produce, and the goals they pursue. Precedence relations between goals are derived inductively from object dependencies, eliminating the need for explicit control-flow specification. The model supports split goals (one-to-many agent activation) and merge goals (many-to-one aggregation) with AND/OR/XOR semantics, enabling parallel execution and collaborative goal achievement.

## Key Results
- Goal-driven business processes enable workflows to emerge from agent interactions rather than being pre-designed
- Agent autonomy allows dynamic adaptation to changing environments through context-aware decision making
- Merge goals facilitate multi-agent collaboration by aggregating outputs through union operations
- The formal model provides a foundation for modular and flexible business process automation

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Goal-object coupling enables adaptive workflow emergence without predefined task sequences.
- Mechanism: Agents are bound to goals (desired states) and business objects (information artifacts), not tasks. When an agent's trigger objects become available, it autonomously determines appropriate actions to produce final objects. Workflows emerge from object dependencies rather than explicit process diagrams.
- Core assumption: Agents possess sufficient reasoning capability to select context-appropriate actions from multiple alternatives.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "workflows emerge from agent interactions rather than being predesigned"
  - [section 3] "The agent will be able to analyse the context and make the most convenient choice"
  - [corpus] Neighbor papers on agentic BPM (arXiv:2504.03693, arXiv:2601.18833) similarly emphasize declarative goal specification, but empirical validation remains limited across this research thread.
- Break condition: If agents lack domain knowledge or reasoning capacity, goal achievement becomes non-deterministic or fails; object dependencies alone cannot compensate.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Trigger object availability creates implicit precedence without explicit orchestration.
- Mechanism: Each agent specifies trigger objects ($O_T^a$) required for activation. Precedence relations between goals are derived inductively by analyzing which agent's final objects serve as another agent's triggers. This eliminates explicit control-flow specification.
- Core assumption: Object dependencies form a directed acyclic structure; circular dependencies are detectable during specification validation.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 3] "We do not need to explicitly provide such relations; they are inductively derived analysing the trigger objects of agents"
  - [section 4] Formal definition: agent activates when "$O_T^a$ is ready"
  - [corpus] Weak corpus signalâ€”neighbor papers do not validate this derivation mechanism empirically.
- Break condition: If trigger objects are incorrectly specified or shared across competing agents, activation becomes ambiguous; deadlocks possible in merge scenarios.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Merge goals coordinate multi-agent collaboration through object union operations.
- Mechanism: When no single agent can fulfill a goal, merge goals aggregate outputs from multiple incoming agents. The goal's object set ($O_G$) equals the union of all incoming agents' final objects ($O_G = \cup_i O_F^i$). AND/OR/XOR semantics determine satisfaction conditions.
- Core assumption: Agents produce compatible, mergeable object formats; conflict resolution is trivial or handled externally.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "merge goal that can be achieved through the collaboration of multiple agents"
  - [section 4] "In case of a merge goal, the objects associated with the goal, $O_G$, will be obtained by the union of all the incoming agents"
  - [corpus] Neighbor paper on compound AI architecture (arXiv:2504.08148) mentions agent orchestration but does not address object merging specifically.
- Break condition: If incoming objects are semantically incompatible or temporally misaligned, union semantics fail; no conflict resolution mechanism is specified in the paper.

## Foundational Learning

- Concept: **Declarative vs. Imperative Process Modeling**
  - Why needed here: The paper explicitly contrasts its declarative "what" (goals/objects) approach with imperative "how" (task sequences) workflows. Understanding this paradigm shift is prerequisite.
  - Quick check question: Can you explain why a declarative specification might produce different runtime behaviors across executions?

- Concept: **CRUDA Operations (Extended CRUD)**
  - Why needed here: Agent capabilities are defined in terms of Create, Read, Update, Delete, Archive operations on business objects. This vocabulary is central to capability specification.
  - Quick check question: Why might "Archive" be semantically distinct from "Delete" in business process contexts?

- Concept: **Multi-Agent Coordination Patterns (Split/Merge)**
  - Why needed here: The formal model relies on split goals (one-to-many agent activation) and merge goals (many-to-one aggregation) with AND/OR/XOR semantics.
  - Quick check question: What is the difference between AND-merge and OR-merge in terms of goal satisfaction conditions?

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Start Objects ($O_S$) -> Agents ($A$) -> Goals ($G$) -> End Objects ($O_E$)
- Critical path:
  1. Identify business goals and decompose into sub-goals
  2. Define business objects associated with each goal
  3. Specify agents with trigger objects, required capabilities, and final objects
  4. Derive precedence relations from trigger object dependencies
  5. Validate specification: orphan triggers (no producer) and unreachable objects (no consumer) raise red flags
  6. Execute: agents activate on trigger availability, produce outputs, merge goals aggregate

- Design tradeoffs:
  - **Flexibility vs. Predictability**: Declarative goals allow adaptive execution but produce non-deterministic workflows; audit and debugging harder than task-based flows
  - **Autonomy vs. Control**: High agent autonomy reduces human oversight; paper acknowledges safety/ethics concerns without proposing solutions
  - **Modularity vs. Coordination Overhead**: Split/merge patterns enable parallelism but require object compatibility and synchronization semantics

- Failure signatures:
  - **Orphan trigger objects**: Agent cannot activate; trigger object has no producer agent
  - **Redundant objects**: Object produced but never consumed by any agent's $O_T$
  - **Circular precedence**: Derived `pre` relation contains cycles; process cannot start
  - **Merge incompatibility**: Incoming agents produce semantically conflicting objects; union undefined
  - **Capability gaps**: Agent's required capabilities exceed $C$ set available in ABP specification

- First 3 experiments:
  1. **Single-path validation**: Implement the pizza delivery example (Figure 1, Table 1) using a simple LLM-backed agent framework; verify object flow matches specification and identify where agent reasoning deviates from expected paths.
  2. **Split scenario test**: Extend the example with a parallel split (e.g., simultaneous kitchen notification and inventory check); validate AND-split activation and measure whether both agents trigger correctly on shared trigger objects.
  3. **Merge conflict injection**: Create a merge goal where two incoming agents produce conflicting object states (e.g., different pricing calculations); observe failure mode and document what conflict resolution is missing from the formal model.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: What specific safeguards and alignment protocols are required to prevent unintended consequences when autonomous agents operate at scale within business processes?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The conclusion explicitly asks, "How do we ensure these systems align with human intentions? What safeguards are needed to prevent unintended consequences, especially when agents operate at scale...?"
- Why unresolved: The paper establishes the theoretical model for agent interaction but does not define the specific control mechanisms or safety layers required to govern agent autonomy in complex scenarios.
- What evidence would resolve it: A proposed governance framework or simulation results demonstrating that agents can detect and avoid unsafe actions in edge cases.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can transparency and auditability be effectively implemented in a non-deterministic, goal-driven workflow to ensure accountability?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors state that "ensuring transparency in how agents make decisions, maintaining human oversight, and creating mechanisms for audit and correction are essential steps toward responsible deployment."
- Why unresolved: While the paper provides a formal model for workflow structure (goals, objects), it does not detail a mechanism for logging agent decision-making paths to satisfy audit requirements.
- What evidence would resolve it: An extension of the formal model that includes an audit trail definition, or a prototype demonstrating explainable decision logs for the pizza delivery example.

### Open Question 3
- Question: Does the proposed agent-based paradigm quantitatively improve efficiency and adaptability in dynamic environments compared to traditional task-based Business Process Management (BPM)?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The prompt notes "no quantitative performance metrics or empirical results are presented," while the paper claims the method "enables flexible and context-aware automation."
- Why unresolved: The paper relies on a theoretical "running example" (pizza delivery) without benchmarking the agent-based approach against traditional static workflows.
- What evidence would resolve it: Comparative experiments measuring execution time, success rates, and resource consumption between the proposed model and traditional BPM in a simulated dynamic environment.

## Limitations
- No empirical validation of agent autonomy and collaboration effectiveness
- Critical gaps in conflict resolution mechanisms for merge goals
- Theoretical framework assumes agents can reliably interpret goals and select appropriate actions from CRUDA capabilities without empirical demonstration
- Scalability to complex industrial processes remains unproven

## Confidence
- High confidence in the formal model definitions and theoretical framework
- Medium confidence in mechanism 1 (goal-object coupling enabling workflow emergence) - conceptually sound but untested
- Medium confidence in mechanism 2 (implicit precedence derivation) - valid in theory, potential edge cases not explored
- Low confidence in mechanism 3 (merge goal coordination) - critical gaps in conflict resolution and semantic compatibility
