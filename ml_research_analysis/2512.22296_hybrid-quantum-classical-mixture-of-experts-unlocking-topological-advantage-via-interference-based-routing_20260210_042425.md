# Hybrid Quantum-Classical Mixture of Experts: Unlocking Topological Advantage via Interference-Based Routing

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2512.22296
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.22296
- **Reference count:** 10
- **Primary result:** Quantum Router achieves 94% accuracy vs 65% for classical linear routing on Two Moons dataset with fewer parameters

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a Hybrid Quantum-Classical Mixture of Experts (QMoE) architecture that uses a Quantum Router for gating decisions while retaining classical experts, isolating the source of quantum advantage. The key innovation is the Interference Hypothesis: quantum interference via angle embedding enables the Quantum Router to model complex, non-linear decision boundaries more efficiently than classical linear routers. Ablation studies on non-linearly separable datasets (Two Moons) show that the Quantum Router achieves 94% accuracy versus 65% for classical linear routing, with significantly fewer parameters (24 vs. 12) and better parameter efficiency (7.14 vs. 2.79 efficiency ratio). The architecture maintains its advantage under simulated quantum noise up to 2% error rates, confirming NISQ feasibility.

## Method Summary
The QMoE architecture combines a quantum router with classical experts. The quantum router uses Angle Embedding to map classical inputs to quantum states, followed by variational layers with RY rotations and CZ entangling gates. The router's measurement probabilities determine gating weights for classical expert networks. Training uses adjoint differentiation for quantum gradients, Adam optimizer, and batch size 32. The ablation study compares three models: classical linear router (12 parameters), quantum router (24 parameters), and deep classical router (240 parameters) on non-linearly separable datasets.

## Key Results
- Quantum Router achieves 94% accuracy on Two Moons dataset vs 65% for classical linear routing
- Quantum advantage persists under simulated noise up to 2% error rates
- Quantum Router demonstrates 1.5-2.5× parameter efficiency advantage over deep classical alternatives

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1: Interference-Based Decision Boundary Formation
Quantum interference enables non-linear topological separation that classical linear routers cannot achieve efficiently. Quantum amplitudes undergo constructive/destructive interference, controlled by learnable parameters θ. The probability includes an interference term: P = |A₁|² + |A₂|² + 2|A₁||A₂|cos(φ₁ - φ₂). This allows the router to create complex, data-dependent decision boundaries without deep networks.

### Mechanism 2: Implicit Kernel Embedding via Hilbert Space Mapping
Angle Embedding implements an implicit kernel function operating in exponentially large feature space (2ⁿ dimensions for n qubits). Classical input x is encoded via |ψ(x)⟩ = ⊗cos(xᵢ/2)|0⟩ + sin(xᵢ/2)|1⟩. The kernel K(x, x') = |⟨ψ(x)|ψ(x')⟩|² enables linear separability in Hilbert space for non-linearly separable classical data.

### Mechanism 3: Parameter Efficiency Through Quantum Inductive Bias
Each quantum parameter contributes 1.5-2.5× more expressive power than classical parameters due to quantum circuit's built-in non-linearity. Variational layer with RY(θ) rotations + CZ entangling gates produces non-linear transformations inherently. Parameter count P = L × n vs O(n²) for classical networks of comparable expressivity.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map**: Input preprocessing -> Quantum Router (Angle Embedding → variational layers → measurement) -> Classical Experts (linear networks) -> Output aggregation (weighted sum)
- **Critical path**: Input encoding fidelity → circuit depth vs. noise tradeoff → measurement statistics → gradient flow through quantum-classical boundary
- **Design tradeoffs**: Qubit count n vs. noise sensitivity; circuit depth L vs. barren plateaus; expert count N vs. measurement outcomes
- **Failure signatures**: Linear decision boundary on non-linear data; random expert selection; training divergence at epoch ~30-50; performance drops with noise >2%

## Limitations

- Small problem scale limits scalability conclusions (Two Moons 2D, reduced MNIST 8D)
- Topological advantage mechanism relies heavily on Angle Embedding's expressiveness
- Quantum noise simulations differ substantially from real-device noise characteristics
- Parameter efficiency claims lack broader corpus validation

## Confidence

- Interference-based routing mechanism: **Medium** - Theoretical derivation is clear, but corpus lacks direct support for interference-specific advantages
- NISQ feasibility up to 2% noise: **High** - Systematic noise sweep provides strong evidence
- Parameter efficiency advantage: **Medium** - Well-quantified in this work but limited external validation
