# Improving the evaluation of samplers on multi-modal targets

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2504.08916
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.08916
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Local MCMC methods fail on multi-modal targets while annealed samplers and diffusion-based methods show promise but require careful tuning

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in the evaluation of sampling methods for multi-modal distributions by proposing a systematic framework centered on mode separation and dimensionality challenges. The authors argue that traditional evaluation metrics fail to capture the fundamental difficulty of correctly identifying and weighting multiple modes in complex distributions. Through a synthetic bi-modal Gaussian mixture setup, they demonstrate that standard local MCMC methods perform poorly on multi-modal targets, while annealed approaches like Sequential Monte Carlo and Replica Exchange show better performance but degrade with increasing dimensionality and mode separation. The study introduces mode weight estimation as a key metric for evaluating sampler effectiveness on multi-modal distributions.

## Method Summary
The authors propose a synthetic experimental setting using bi-modal Gaussian mixtures to test samplers' ability to recover mode relative importance. They evaluate multiple sampling approaches including MCMC, Importance Sampling, Variational Inference, Sequential Monte Carlo, Replica Exchange, and diffusion-based methods (SLIPS and DDS). The experimental design systematically varies dimensionality and mode separation to assess sampler performance under controlled conditions. Mode weight estimation serves as the primary metric for evaluating sampler effectiveness, providing an interpretable measure of whether samplers can correctly identify the relative importance of different modes in the target distribution.

## Key Results
- Local MCMC methods consistently fail to properly explore multi-modal distributions and recover mode weights
- Annealed samplers (SMC, RE) perform better but show degradation as dimensionality and mode separation increase
- Diffusion-based methods demonstrate promising results but require careful hyperparameter tuning for optimal performance
- Mode weight estimation proves to be a challenging yet interpretable metric for assessing sampler performance on multi-modal distributions

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The paper's approach works because it isolates the fundamental challenges of multi-modal sampling through controlled synthetic experiments. By focusing on mode weight estimation, the evaluation directly measures whether samplers can correctly identify the relative importance of different modes rather than just finding them. The systematic variation of dimensionality and separation creates a clear gradient of difficulty that reveals fundamental limitations of different sampling approaches.

## Limitations
The synthetic bi-modal Gaussian mixture setup represents a simplified view that may not capture the complexity of real-world multi-modal distributions with non-Gaussian modes and asymmetric weights. Diffusion-based methods show promise but with notable variability across parameter settings, and the paper provides limited systematic exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity. The practical implications for method selection in real applications remain unclear given the controlled nature of the experimental design.

## Confidence

**High confidence**: Local MCMC failure on multi-modal targets and mode weight estimation as meaningful metric

**Medium confidence**: Relative performance of annealed samplers across dimensions and separations due to synthetic experimental setup

**Medium confidence**: Characterization of diffusion-based methods as promising but requiring careful tuning based on limited parameter exploration
