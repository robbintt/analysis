# The Batch Complexity of Bandit Pure Exploration

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2502.01425
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.01425
- Authors: Adrienne Tuynman; Rémy Degenne
- Reference count: 40
- One-line primary result: Introduces Phased Explore then Track (PET) algorithm with instance-dependent bounds on sample and batch complexity for pure exploration in multi-armed bandits

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses pure exploration in multi-armed bandits with a fixed-confidence setting, focusing on batched algorithms that can only change sampling behavior a limited number of times. The authors establish an instance-dependent lower bound on the number of batches required by any sample-efficient algorithm and introduce the Phased Explore then Track (PET) algorithm. They prove upper bounds on both expected sample complexity and batch complexity, demonstrating that PET's performance is close to optimal under mild conditions.

## Method Summary
The authors propose a general batched algorithm called Phased Explore then Track (PET) for pure exploration tasks in multi-armed bandits. The algorithm operates in phases, alternating between exploration and tracking phases to efficiently identify the correct answer to queries about arm distributions. PET maintains confidence intervals for arm means and adaptively adjusts the number of pulls based on observed gaps between arms. The theoretical analysis provides instance-dependent bounds on both sample complexity and batch complexity, with the latter being close to the established lower bound under certain conditions.

## Key Results
- Establishes an instance-dependent lower bound on batch complexity for any sample-efficient pure exploration algorithm
- Introduces PET algorithm with provable upper bounds on both sample and batch complexity
- Demonstrates PET's performance is close to optimal in the high confidence regime
- Shows empirical superiority of PET over state-of-the-art algorithms in best-arm identification settings

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The paper leverages information-theoretic arguments to establish fundamental limits on batch complexity in pure exploration problems. By combining exploration phases that gather information about all arms with tracking phases that focus on the most promising candidates, PET efficiently balances between exploration and exploitation while respecting the batch constraint. The adaptive nature of the algorithm allows it to respond to observed gaps between arms, reducing unnecessary sampling in less promising directions.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map**: Input parameters → PET algorithm → Exploration phases → Tracking phases → Output decision

**Critical path**: Initialization → Exploration phase (gather initial samples) → Track phase (refine estimates) → Decision phase (output answer with confidence)

**Design tradeoffs**: Batch complexity vs sample complexity - more batches allow finer adaptation but increase communication/computation costs; Conservative vs aggressive exploration - balancing between sample efficiency and batch efficiency.

**Failure signatures**: Poor gap estimation leading to incorrect decisions; Excessive batch count due to conservative stopping criteria; High sample complexity when gap estimates are inaccurate.

- Focus on fixed-confidence settings may not capture all practical scenarios

- Theoretical bounds may not directly translate to practical performance across all problem instances
