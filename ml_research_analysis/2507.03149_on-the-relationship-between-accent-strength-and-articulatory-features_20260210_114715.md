# On the Relationship between Accent Strength and Articulatory Features

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2507.03149
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.03149
- Reference count: 0

## Executive Summary
This study investigates the relationship between accent strength and articulatory features in American and British English by analyzing tongue positioning patterns. Using articulatory inversion techniques on a corpus of read speech from 53 speakers, the research identifies significant correlations between specific articulatory parameters and accent strength measures. The findings reveal that tongue dorsum raising and advancement in rhotic vowels show the strongest correlations with dialect distinctions, providing insights for automated accent analysis and articulatory modeling applications.

## Method Summary
The study employs articulatory inversion techniques to derive tongue positioning parameters from acoustic speech data. A corpus of read speech from 53 speakers is analyzed, with accent strength proxies computed through phoneme-level difference measures. Statistical analysis is conducted to examine correlations between derived articulatory parameters and these accent strength measures, focusing on distinguishing features between American and British English dialects.

## Key Results
- Tongue positioning patterns, particularly for rhotic and low back vowels, distinguish American and British English dialects
- Strongest correlations observed between tongue dorsum raising and advancement in rhotic vowels and accent strength
- Significant correlations found between certain articulatory features and accent strength proxies

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanism underlying the relationship between accent strength and articulatory features operates through systematic differences in tongue positioning patterns between dialects. These articulatory variations directly influence the acoustic realization of speech sounds, creating distinguishable phonetic characteristics that can be measured and correlated with accent strength metrics. The inversion process effectively maps acoustic signals back to their underlying articulatory configurations, revealing the physical basis for dialectal differences.

## Foundational Learning
- Articulatory inversion: Converts acoustic speech signals back to estimated articulatory movements; needed to extract tongue positioning data from speech recordings; quick check: validate inversion accuracy against ground truth articulatory measurements
- Accent strength proxies: Phoneme-level difference measures used to quantify dialectal variations; needed to create measurable metrics for comparing accent characteristics; quick check: ensure proxy measures capture relevant accent distinctions
- Tongue dorsum positioning: Movement of the back part of the tongue; needed to identify key articulatory features distinguishing dialects; quick check: verify correlation between dorsum movements and specific vowel contrasts

Critical Path:
Acoustic recordings → inversion processing → parameter extraction → statistical correlation → interpretation

Design Tradeoffs:
- Inversion accuracy vs. computational efficiency
- Proxy measure specificity vs. generalizability
- Feature selection comprehensiveness vs. statistical power

Failure Signatures:
- Poor inversion quality leading to unreliable articulatory estimates
- Inadequate proxy measures failing to capture accent variations
- Statistical noise overwhelming meaningful correlations

First Experiments:

2. Test correlation stability across different speaking styles and genres
3. Compare results using alternative proxy measures for accent strength

## Limitations
- Reliance on articulatory inversion from acoustic data may introduce inaccuracies in derived tongue positioning parameters
- Proxy measures based on phoneme-level differences may not capture all aspects of accent variation
- Corpus may not fully represent diversity within each dialect group
- Focus on specific articulatory features may overlook other relevant parameters

## Confidence
- The correlation between articulatory features and accent strength: Medium confidence
- The specific articulatory patterns for rhotic and low back vowels: Medium confidence
- The applicability to automated accent analysis: Low-Medium confidence
