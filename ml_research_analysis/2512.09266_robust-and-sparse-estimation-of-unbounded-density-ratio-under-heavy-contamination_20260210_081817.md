# Robust and Sparse Estimation of Unbounded Density Ratio under Heavy Contamination

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2512.09266
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.09266
- Authors: Ryosuke Nagumo; Hironori Fujisawa
- Reference count: 40
- First non-asymptotic analysis of robust density ratio estimation under heavy contamination with sparse consistency guarantees

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces the first non-asymptotic analysis of robust density ratio estimation under heavy contamination. The authors propose a weighted density ratio estimation (Weighted DRE) method that achieves doubly strong robustness by requiring the weighted density ratio to be bounded rather than the density ratio itself. This approach allows for better handling of outliers while maintaining estimation accuracy. The theoretical framework relaxes previous assumptions by requiring only that either contamination ratios or outlier weights are small.

## Method Summary
The method employs Weighted Density Ratio Estimation (Weighted DRE) that introduces a weight function to down-weight contaminated samples. Instead of requiring the density ratio itself to be bounded, the method requires only the weighted density ratio to be bounded, providing doubly strong robustness. The approach leverages sparsity assumptions on the true density ratio and uses convex optimization techniques to estimate the density ratio from contaminated samples. The weight function is designed to assign small values to outliers while preserving the contribution of clean samples.

## Key Results
- Establishes non-asymptotic error bounds of O(√(k log d / n)) for density ratio estimation
- Achieves sparse consistency under heavy contamination conditions
- Provides theoretical guarantees requiring only one of: (i) small contamination ratios or (ii) small outlier weights
- Introduces doubly strong robustness by bounding weighted rather than raw density ratios

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by introducing a weight function that attenuates the influence of contaminated samples during estimation. By requiring the weighted density ratio to be bounded rather than the density ratio itself, the method can handle unbounded density ratios while still maintaining robustness to outliers. The sparsity assumption on the true density ratio enables efficient estimation even in high-dimensional settings. The relaxation of assumptions to require only small contamination ratios or small outlier weights makes the method more broadly applicable than previous approaches.

## Limitations
- Theoretical guarantees rely heavily on assumptions about small contamination ratios or outlier weights
- Sparsity requirement on true density ratio may limit applicability
- Practical implementation challenges when contamination patterns are unknown
- Limited empirical validation across diverse real-world scenarios
